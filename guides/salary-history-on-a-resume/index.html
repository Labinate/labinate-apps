Salary history on a resume

Should you list what you were paid at your last jobs? No — and this one isn't close. Salary history exists to serve the employer, not you: it quietly anchors your next offer to your last paycheck instead of to the market rate for the new role. If you were underpaid before, disclosing it carries the underpayment forward. In much of the US it's now illegal for an employer to even ask. Here's why past pay never touches the page, and exactly how to handle a form that demands it.

The one-line version: keep your salary history off the resume — always. What you were paid before is backward-looking information that can only lower your next offer. A resume proves your value going forward; it should never price that value against your own history. If a form forces the question, redirect to a researched range for the new role, and know that in many places the question itself may be unlawful.

Why salary history never belongs on a resume

There is only one direction disclosing your past pay can move your next offer, and it isn't up:

  • It anchors the offer to your last paycheck. The moment an employer knows your old number, the new offer forms around it — a small "raise" on top of it — instead of around the market budget for the role. You want the offer anchored to what the job is worth, not to what you happened to earn last.
  • It carries underpayment forward. If you were paid below market before — through a low starting offer, a lean employer, or a pay gap — revealing that history locks the gap in for another job. This is exactly the cycle that salary-history bans exist to break.

Either way, you've handed over information the employer would otherwise have to work without, and you've done it on the one document whose entire purpose is to make you look worth interviewing. The strongest position in any pay conversation belongs to the person who makes the other side commit to wanting them before money comes up — and a past-salary line on page one throws that away.

In many places, they can't even ask

Salary-history disclosure isn't just a bad idea — it's increasingly against the law to require. More than twenty US states and a long list of cities have passed salary-history bans that prohibit employers from asking about, or setting pay based on, a candidate's previous compensation.

What a salary-history ban typically does: bars the employer from asking you (or a prior employer) what you were paid, and bars them from using that figure to set your offer — even if they learn it. Coverage, wording, and exceptions vary by state and city and can change, so check your own location. But the direction of travel is one-way, and the reason is consistent: tying new offers to old salaries perpetuates gender and racial pay gaps.

The practical upshot: even where it's still legal to ask, you're not obligated to volunteer the number, and it never belongs on the resume. Where a ban applies, a required salary-history field may itself be unlawful — which makes declining to answer entirely reasonable.

Salary history vs. salary requirements — not the same question

These two get confused constantly, and the confusion costs money. They point in opposite directions:

Salary historyWhat you were paid before — a backward-looking record of past compensation.Decline, redirect, or mark "negotiable." Never disclose the actual figure. Never on the resume.
Salary requirementsWhat you want going forward for the new role — a forward-looking expectation.If forced, give a researched range for the target role. Still never on the resume — see salary requirements on a resume.
The trap

Answering a "salary expectations" field with a number based on your current salary. That quietly turns a forward-looking question back into salary history — and locks in any underpayment all over again.

→ Base any number you give on market data for the new role, level, and location. Anchor to the job, never to your last paycheck.

When an application forces a salary-history field

Some online forms have a required "previous salary" or "current salary" field and won't let you submit without it. When you can't skip it, in order of preference:

  1. If text is allowed, decline gracefully. Enter "Negotiable," "Prefer to discuss based on the role," or "Open — targeting market rate for this position." This answers the field without handing over a number.
  2. If it demands a number, redirect to the target range. Enter a researched range (or its midpoint) for the new role rather than your old salary. You're answering "what would make sense here," not "what did I used to earn."
  3. Know your rights. If you're in a state or city with a salary-history ban, a required past-pay field may be non-compliant. You're on solid ground marking it negotiable or contacting the recruiter to ask what the role budgets.
  4. Never enter your real past salary. Once it's in the system, it's the anchor for every number that follows.

Say this

  • "Negotiable" / "Prefer to discuss based on the role."
  • A range for the target role: "Targeting $X–$Y, in line with the market for this position."
  • In conversation: "I'd rather anchor to the value of this role than to my last one — what range are you working with?"

Not this

  • Your actual previous salary, anywhere.
  • Your current pay as the answer to "expectations."
  • A salary-history line on the resume itself.
  • Inflating an old number — it collapses on verification and can end an offer.

What to put on the page instead

The resume real estate people are tempted to spend on pay is far better spent on evidence of value. A line that says you "grew revenue 30%," "cut processing time in half," or "managed a team of 8" does more to justify a strong offer than any salary figure — because it makes the employer want you before price is on the table. That's the real work of the page:

  • Lead bullets with results, not duties — see responsibilities vs. accomplishments.
  • Attach numbers to your wins — see how to quantify achievements.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put your salary history on a resume?

No. Never put what you were paid at previous jobs on your resume. Salary history serves the employer, not you: it anchors your next offer to your last paycheck instead of to the market rate for the new role, so if you were underpaid before, disclosing it carries that underpayment forward. A resume exists to prove your value and earn an interview, not to price you against your own history. In a growing number of US states and cities it is also now illegal for an employer to ask for your salary history at all — so there is no version of listing it that helps you.

Is it illegal for an employer to ask for salary history?

In many places, yes. More than twenty US states and numerous cities have passed salary-history bans that prohibit employers from asking about or relying on a candidate's past pay when setting an offer. The exact rules vary by location and can change, so check your state and city, but the trend is clear and the reason is consistent: tying new offers to old salaries perpetuates the gender and racial pay gaps. Even where it is still legal to ask, you are not obligated to volunteer the number, and it never belongs on the resume itself.

How do you answer salary history when an application form requires it?

First, know that in many jurisdictions the field itself may be unlawful. When you must respond: if the field accepts text, enter 'Negotiable' or 'Prefer to discuss based on the role.' If it demands a number and won't accept text or zero, redirect to what you are targeting going forward — a researched salary range for the new role, based on market data, not what you earned before. Never enter your actual past salary; it becomes a permanent anchor beneath your value. If a required numeric field blocks submission, some applicants enter a placeholder like 0 or 000 to signal 'will discuss,' but a range for the target role is the stronger move.

What is the difference between salary history and salary requirements?

Salary history is what you were paid in the past — a backward-looking record of previous compensation. Salary requirements (or expectations) are what you want to be paid going forward for the new role. They are opposite directions: history anchors you to your old paycheck, while requirements should be anchored to the market rate for the job you are applying for. Neither belongs on the resume, but they are handled differently. If a form or posting asks for expectations, give a researched range for the target role; if it asks for history, decline, redirect, or note it is negotiable — and never carry an old underpayment into a new offer.

What should go on the resume instead of salary information?

Evidence of value. The space people are tempted to spend on numbers about pay is far better spent on quantified achievements — revenue grown, costs cut, time saved, teams led. Those numbers justify a strong offer without ever naming a price, because they make the employer want you before compensation is discussed. Keep every mention of money about the impact you delivered, never about what you were paid to deliver it.
