PDF or Word for your resume? What to send, and when

The short version: send a PDF unless the posting asks for Word. But the file type matters far less than most advice suggests — what actually gets a resume screened out is a layout the parser can't read, in either format.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

It's one of the most-asked resume questions, and it's been muddied by a myth that refuses to die: "an ATS can't read a PDF, so always send Word." That advice was reasonable a decade ago. It isn't now. Here's the current reality, the one real exception, and the thing that actually determines whether software can read your resume at all.

The default answer: PDF

For most applications, PDF is the right choice, for two reasons:

It preserves your layout

A PDF renders identically on every device and in every reader. A Word file reflows based on the recipient's fonts and settings — your careful spacing can collapse, and a one-page resume can spill onto two.

Modern ATS read it fine

Today's applicant tracking systems parse text-based PDFs without trouble. The "PDFs break the ATS" warning is out of date; a clean PDF with real text is a safe, standard submission.

The one real exception: when they ask for Word

If a job posting, application form, or recruiter explicitly requests a .docx file, send Word. Full stop. Some older systems and many staffing agencies re-format or annotate your resume before it reaches the hiring manager, and they need an editable file to do it. Ignoring a stated format instruction is one of the easiest, most avoidable reasons to get filtered out — so read the posting and follow it exactly.

Rule of thumb. No format specified → send PDF. Format specified → send exactly that. When a recruiter emails asking for "your resume in Word," they mean it — reply with the .docx.

The thing that actually matters: is the text readable?

Here's the part most PDF-vs-Word debates miss. Whether a parser can read your resume has almost nothing to do with the file extension and almost everything to do with the layout inside it. These break parsing in both formats:

  • Text saved as an image. A resume "designed" in Canva or Photoshop and exported as a picture has zero readable text. The parser sees a blank page. This is the single most common self-inflicted wound.
  • Tables and text boxes. Parsers read columns and boxes in an unpredictable order, scrambling your experience into nonsense.
  • Multiple columns. A two-column layout often gets read straight across, interleaving your skills sidebar into the middle of a job description.
  • Headers and footers. Contact details tucked into the document header are frequently ignored entirely.

Fix those and either format works. Leave them in and neither does. If you want the full list, see our ATS resume checklist.

How to check your file in 20 seconds

Open your finished resume (PDF or Word) and try to select the text with your cursor and copy it. If it highlights and copies as normal, editable text, a parser can read it. If nothing selects — if it behaves like a photo — the file is an image and no ATS will read a word of it, no matter which format you chose. Paste what you copied into a blank document: if it comes out jumbled or out of order, your layout (columns, tables) is the problem, not the file type.

Frequently asked questions

Should a resume be a PDF or a Word document?

PDF by default — it locks your layout and modern ATS read it fine. The exception is when a posting or recruiter explicitly asks for a Word (.docx) file, in which case send Word.

Can applicant tracking systems read PDF resumes?

Yes. The "ATS can't read PDFs" warning is outdated. What breaks parsing is the layout — tables, columns, text boxes, or text saved as an image — not the file type. A clean, single-column PDF with real text reads correctly.

When should I send a Word resume instead?

Whenever the application asks for it. Some older systems and recruiting agencies re-format your resume and need an editable file. If a format is named, follow it exactly.

Why does my resume look different after uploading?

Word documents reflow on the reader's machine, so margins and line breaks shift. A PDF renders identically everywhere — the main reason to prefer it unless you're told otherwise.
