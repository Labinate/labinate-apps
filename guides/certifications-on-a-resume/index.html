How to list certifications on a resume

A certification is proof someone else has vouched for your skill — which makes it some of the most credible space on your resume. But only if it's placed right and formatted so both a recruiter and an applicant tracking system can read it. Here's exactly where certifications go, how to format each one, and which ones are worth the line.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

Certifications punch above their weight because they're externally verified — anyone can claim "proficient in Python," but "AWS Certified Solutions Architect" means Amazon tested and signed off. The mistake most people make isn't leaving them out; it's burying a job-critical license at the bottom, or padding the section with completion certificates for two-hour webinars. This guide covers placement, the exact entry format, and the judgment call of what's worth listing.

Where certifications go

Placement depends on one question: is the certification required for the job, or does it just support you?

Required or defining → near the top

A nursing license (RN), CPA, bar admission, CDL, PMP, or an active security clearance often is the qualifying credential. Put it in your summary or a short "Licenses & Certifications" block right under it — a recruiter should see it in the first five seconds without scrolling.

Supporting → a dedicated section lower down

Nice-to-have credentials (a Google Analytics cert, a Scrum badge, a language certificate) belong in their own "Certifications" section after experience and education — present and credible, but not competing with your main story.

Just one → fold it into skills or education

If you have a single relevant certification, don't create a whole section for it. Add one line under skills or beneath your degree in the education section. A section of one looks thin.

The format of a single entry

Every certification entry needs three parts — and, when it applies, a fourth. Keep them consistent down the list.

  • Full official name — spell it out at least once, even if you also use the acronym, so both a human and the ATS match it.
  • Issuing organization — who granted it (Amazon Web Services, PMI, CompTIA).
  • Date earned — month and year.
  • Expiration date — only if the credential expires and is still valid. This reassures the reader it's current.
Dedicated section Certifications
  • AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate · Amazon Web Services · 2025 (valid through 2028)
  • Certified ScrumMaster (CSM) · Scrum Alliance · 2024
  • Google Data Analytics Professional Certificate · Google · 2024

Don't add credential ID numbers, badge links, or logos on the resume itself — they clutter the line and can trip a parser. Save verification details for the interview or a reference sheet.

Which certifications are worth listing

The test is simple: would a hiring manager in your field recognize it and care? If yes, it earns a line. If not, that space is better spent on results in your experience section.

List these
  • ✓ Required for the role — licenses, clearances, regulated credentials.
  • ✓ Industry-recognized — the ones a posting names or that are standard in your field.
  • ✓ Skill-backing — a cert that proves a skill the job asks for.
Leave these off
  • ✗ Expired credentials with no current value.
  • ✗ "Completion" certificates for short videos or generic webinars.
  • ✗ Anything unrelated to the job you're applying for.

Tricky cases

In-progress certifications

List them if they're relevant — it shows direction. Write the name followed by "In progress" or an expected date: "PMP Certification — expected Sept 2026." Never list it as earned before you've passed.

Expired certifications

If a lapsed cert still shows relevant knowledge and you plan to renew, you can keep it with the earned date and a note ("renewal in progress"). If it's expired and irrelevant, drop it — an out-of-date credential can read as neglect.

A long list of them

If you hold many, list only the ones relevant to this job, most impressive first, and tailor the set to the posting. A wall of every badge you've ever earned dilutes the ones that matter.

Formatting details that trip people up

  • Keep it real text, single-column — no badge images, tables, or icons an ATS will scramble. See the ATS checklist.
  • Spell out acronyms once. "Certified Information Systems Security Professional (CISSP)" so both the acronym and the full term get matched as keywords.
  • Be consistent in date style and ordering across every entry, matching the rest of your resume.
  • Name the issuer. "Certified" alone means little; "Certified — Scrum Alliance" is verifiable.
The whole section in one breath. Put required or defining credentials near the top; give supporting ones their own section after experience and education; fold a lone certification into skills. Each entry: full name, issuer, date earned, and expiry only if it still applies. List in-progress ones honestly, drop dead weight, and tailor a long list to the job.

Frequently asked questions

Where do certifications go on a resume?

If a certification is required or central to the job — a nursing license, CPA, CDL, security clearance — put it near the top, in the summary or a short "Licenses & Certifications" block. Supporting credentials go in a dedicated section after experience and education. With only one, a single line under skills or education is enough.

How do I format a certification entry?

List the full official name, the issuing organization, and the date earned (month and year). Add an expiration date only if it expires and is still valid. Spell out the name at least once alongside the acronym so both a human and an ATS match it — e.g. "AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate, Amazon Web Services, 2025."

Should I list certifications that are in progress?

Yes, if relevant — it signals initiative. Write the name plus "In progress" or an expected completion date, e.g. "PMP Certification — expected Sept 2026." Be honest about status; don't list it as earned until you've passed.

Which certifications are worth putting on a resume?

Ones required for the role, recognized in your industry, or that directly back up a skill the job asks for. Skip expired credentials with no value, generic completion certificates, and anything unrelated. If a hiring manager in your field wouldn't recognize or care about it, the line is better spent elsewhere.
