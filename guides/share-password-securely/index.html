How to share a password securely — the safe way, and what to never do

You need to send a colleague the Wi-Fi password, a client the login, a family member a code. The obvious ways — email, Slack, a text — quietly leave that secret sitting in a dozen places forever. Here's the safe way instead.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

The problem with sharing a password isn't the sending — it's the lingering. When you paste a password into email or a chat app, that message doesn't disappear when it's read. It stays in your Sent folder, the recipient's inbox, both sides' backups, and often a company server. A secret you needed for thirty seconds can survive for years, waiting for the day one of those accounts is compromised.

The fix is simple: make the secret readable once, encrypt it so no server can see it, and leave no permanent copy behind.

What not to do

  • Don't email it. Email is stored in plaintext on servers and in backups on both ends. It's the single most common way credentials leak long after they were sent.
  • Don't post it in Slack, Teams or Discord. Chat history is searchable forever and often retained by the company. "I'll delete it after" almost never happens — and deletion doesn't touch backups.
  • Don't text it. SMS is unencrypted and syncs to phone-cloud backups. A stolen or resold phone hands over the whole message history.
  • Don't put it in a shared doc or spreadsheet. Access sprawls over time; six months later nobody remembers who can open that sheet.
  • Don't reuse the same password across the people you share with. One leak then unlocks everything.

What to do instead

  • Use a one-time, end-to-end encrypted link. The secret is encrypted in your browser, opens once, and self-destructs. There's no inbox copy to leak later.
  • Split the channels for high-stakes secrets. Send the link one way (e.g. Slack) and any extra passphrase another (e.g. a quick call), so no single compromised channel reveals everything.
  • For credentials you share repeatedly, use a team password manager. One-time links are perfect for a hand-off; a manager is better for a login five people need every day.
  • Rotate after sharing anything sensitive. Once a secret has travelled, treat it as slightly exposed and change it on a sensible schedule.
  • Prefer zero-backend tools. If the service never stores the secret at all, there is literally no database that can be breached.

Frequently asked questions

Is it safe to send a password over email or Slack?

Not really. Email and chat keep a permanent copy in both sides' history, in backups, and often on a company server. Anyone who later gains access to either account — or the server — can read a password you sent months ago.

What is the safest way to share a password?

Use a one-time, end-to-end encrypted link that self-destructs after it's read. The secret is encrypted in your browser, opens only once, and leaves no lasting copy. For credentials you share repeatedly, a team password manager is best.

What do "end-to-end encrypted" and "zero-backend" mean?

End-to-end encrypted means only the sender and recipient can read the content — not the service in the middle. Zero-backend means the secret is never stored on a server at all; the key lives in the link, so there's no database of secrets to breach.

Should I text a password to someone?

Avoid it for anything sensitive. SMS is unencrypted and sits in both phones' history indefinitely, and phone backups often sync those messages to the cloud. A self-destructing encrypted link is a much safer default.
