How to list a zero-hours contract on a resume

A zero-hours contract is real, ongoing employment — so treat it like a job, not a footnote. List it as one continuous role with a title, employer, and date range, lead with what you did and delivered, and leave the words "zero-hours" off the page entirely.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

A zero-hours contract has a simple definition and an unfairly bad reputation. You're on one employer's books, working on an as-needed or casual basis, with no guaranteed minimum hours. That's it — it's a genuine, direct, ongoing employment relationship, just without a fixed schedule. The problem is that the phrase sounds marginal, and so people mishandle the entry at two opposite extremes. The first is underplaying it: writing "zero-hours" next to the role, hiding it, or breaking it into a scatter of disconnected shifts, so a genuinely steady stretch of experience reads as insignificant — or worse, as a gap. The second is overstating it: dressing it up as a guaranteed, salaried, full-time role you never actually held, which is the kind of thing that unravels the moment an employer calls for a reference. The fix for both is the same, and it's liberating: treat a zero-hours role as the ordinary job it was.

The core idea: a recruiter is reading for what you did and whether it was continuous — not for the terms of your contract. Nobody puts a guaranteed-hours figure on a resume for a salaried job, and you shouldn't for a zero-hours one either. Give it a title, an employer, a clean date range, and quantified results, and it reads exactly like any other role. The contract type is a detail for the application form and the interview — never the page.

Move 1 — List it as one continuous role, not scattered shifts

The single most damaging mistake is treating a zero-hours job as a series of separate engagements — a list of dates, a handful of one-line "worked shifts at…" entries, or a role chopped into fragments. That's what actually makes it look unstable. The through-line of a zero-hours contract is the ongoing relationship with one employer, so present it as one block with a single date range.

Fragmented — looks marginal

  • Casual staff, ACME Ltd — various shifts
  • Broken into disconnected dates
  • Flagged as "zero-hours / bank"
  • No sense of a continuous role

Continuous — reads as steady

  • Retail Assistant, ACME Ltd — Mar 2023–Present
  • One employer block, one date range
  • Contract type not mentioned
  • Reads as an ongoing role

Use a normal date range exactly as you would for any job — "Mar 2023 – Present" or "Jun 2022 – Nov 2023." The reader doesn't need to know you worked twelve hours one week and thirty the next; they need to know the relationship was continuous over that span. Getting the dates right is what quietly signals stability, so a period of casual work never reads as a gap in your timeline.

Move 2 — Lead with responsibilities and results, not the contract type

The words "zero-hours" never need to appear on a resume. A resume lists what you did, not the terms under which you did it — and the arrangement adds nothing a hiring manager can use, while quietly inviting doubt. Delete the label and spend that space on the work.

Contract-led — weak

"Zero-hours contract at ACME. Worked flexible shifts covering staff shortages as required." This describes your contract and your availability. It says nothing about what you were actually good at or what changed because you were there.

Result-led — strong

"Served 100+ customers per shift across tills, floor, and stockroom; trained 3 new starters; maintained a 98% accuracy rate on cash handling."

Same job, but now it's a set of concrete contributions — and none of them depend on how many hours you were guaranteed.

This is the same discipline that makes any resume strong: every line is a quantified achievement, not a duty or a disclaimer, and each role reads outcome-first rather than task-first. If you're unsure which points earn a place, that's the broader question of what to put on a resume.

Move 3 — Handle the hours question honestly, only if it's asked

Because a resume never states hours for any job, a zero-hours role has nothing to disclose by default — there's simply no line where "how much did you work" belongs. Don't invent one. The variable nature of the contract is a fact for the places that actually ask.

  • Application form. If a form asks for employment type or average hours, answer plainly and neutrally there — "casual / variable" — where it's expected. That's the right home for the detail, not the resume.
  • Only note it when it earns its place. A short neutral tag like "(casual/variable basis)" is worth adding only when the variability explains something useful — for instance, that it ran alongside full-time study or another job. Otherwise it's a caveat nobody asked for.
  • Interview. If it comes up, describe it forwards and without apology: "It was a casual contract, which suited me while I was studying — I picked up regular shifts and often covered at short notice." Calm, factual, done.
  • Never apologise for it. Over-explaining the contract is what makes a perfectly solid role sound flimsy. Silence about the hours isn't hiding anything; it's the norm.

Move 4 — Turn real variability into a concrete strength

Zero-hours work genuinely does build things worth naming — but only if you show them as facts, not slogans. "Flexible team player," "adaptable," "reliable" are empty; every recruiter skips them. The reliability and range that as-needed work produces are real, so state them with evidence, once.

Slogan — proves nothing

  • "Flexible and adaptable"
  • "Reliable team player"
  • "Willing to work any hours"

Concrete — demonstrates it

  • "Regularly covered shifts at <24 hours' notice"
  • "Cross-trained across 4 departments; deployed wherever short-staffed"
  • "Kept a 3-year record with zero missed booked shifts"

The breadth is often the best part: casual staff frequently work across more of an operation than fixed-schedule colleagues, because they're the ones sent wherever cover is needed. Name that range concretely — the departments, the systems, the situations — and it becomes evidence of versatility, not a euphemism for "no set job." Then tailor which strength you lead with to what the target role actually values.

The bright line

Present a zero-hours role as the continuous, credible job it was — title, employer, date range, quantified results — and leave the contract mechanics off the page. List it as one block with a clean date range, not a scatter of shifts. Lead with responsibilities and outcomes, and never write "zero-hours" on the resume. Keep the hours question for the application form and the interview, where a neutral word settles it. Turn genuine variability into a concrete strength — reliability at short notice, cross-training, breadth — stated once as a fact. Never apologise for the arrangement, and never inflate it into a guaranteed full-time salaried role you didn't hold.

Zero-hours vs. the things it's confused with

"Zero-hours" gets tangled with several adjacent arrangements. They're different in what they are — but on the resume, reassuringly, they all follow the same rule: title, employer, dates, results, contract type left off.

The situationWhat it really is — and how it differs
Part-time jobGuaranteed reduced hours on a set schedule. The defining difference is the guarantee: a part-time contract commits to a minimum number of hours; a zero-hours contract commits to none. The test: is there a contracted minimum (part-time) or no minimum at all (zero-hours)?
Temp jobA fixed-term assignment with an end date, often via an agency. A temp role is bounded and time-limited; a zero-hours contract is open-ended and direct with one employer. The test: fixed end / agency-placed (temp) vs. ongoing / directly employed (zero-hours)?
Gig workSelf-employed, discrete tasks across platforms or clients. On a gig you're your own boss taking jobs; on a zero-hours contract you're an employee of a single company (on their payroll), just without guaranteed hours. The test: self-employed across many (gig) vs. employed by one (zero-hours)?
Seasonal jobBounded to a peak period, then it ends. Seasonal work is tied to a defined stretch (summer, holidays) and finishes; a zero-hours role continues year-round on an as-needed basis. The test: bounded to a season that ends (seasonal) vs. ongoing but variable (zero-hours)?
Per-diem workThe close cousin — as-needed, day-rate work, common in US healthcare and staffing. "Per diem" is essentially the American analog of the UK/Commonwealth "zero-hours." Both are on-call, as-needed employment, and both are listed the same way. The test: mostly terminology and region — treat the entry identically.

Quick reference

  • One continuous role → a single employer block with one date range, not a scatter of shifts.
  • Never write "zero-hours" → a resume lists what you did, not your contract terms or hours.
  • Lead with results → quantified contributions, outcome-first, exactly as for any job.
  • Hours question → application form and interview → a neutral word there; a caveat on the resume only if the variability genuinely explains something.
  • Variability → concrete strength → "covered at <24 hours' notice," "cross-trained across 4 departments," stated once as fact — never "flexible team player."
  • Never → apologise for the contract, or inflate it into a guaranteed full-time salaried role you didn't hold.

Frequently asked questions

How do you list a zero-hours contract on a resume?

List it exactly like any other job — title, employer, location, a continuous date range, and quantified results — and don't label it "zero-hours." A zero-hours contract is real, ongoing employment; you were on one employer's books, just without guaranteed hours. So give it a single employer block with one date range (e.g. "Mar 2023 – Present"), not a scatter of individual shifts, and describe what you were responsible for and what you delivered. The contract mechanics — how many hours you were guaranteed — belong on the application form or in the interview, never on the resume, which never states hours anyway.

Should you write "zero-hours" on your resume?

No. A resume lists what you did, not the terms of your contract, and "zero-hours" only invites doubt about how substantial the role was. Recruiters don't put your guaranteed-hours number on the page for salaried jobs either. Present the title, employer, dates, and results plainly. If the variable nature is genuinely relevant — for example it explains how you also studied full-time or held another role in the same period — a short neutral note like "casual/variable basis" is enough. Default to saying nothing about the contract type; let the work speak.

How is a zero-hours contract different from a part-time or temp job?

The defining difference from part-time is the guarantee: a part-time job has a contracted minimum number of hours on a set schedule, while a zero-hours contract guarantees none — you work as needed. It differs from a temp job in permanence: a temp assignment is fixed-term with an end date, often through an agency, whereas a zero-hours contract is an open-ended, direct relationship with one employer. And it differs from gig work in employment status: gig work is self-employed, discrete tasks across platforms or clients, while on a zero-hours contract you're an employee of a single company. On the resume, though, all of them follow the same rule: title, employer, dates, results — contract type left off.

Won't a zero-hours job look unstable or insignificant to employers?

Only if you frame it that way. Listed as a scatter of disconnected shifts, or flagged as "zero-hours," it can read as marginal. Listed as one continuous role with a clean date range and concrete results, it reads as steady, credible experience — which it is. The way to make it look substantial is to lead with responsibilities and outcomes and to name any genuine strength the arrangement produced: reliability at short notice, cross-training across several departments, breadth from handling varied situations. Continuity plus results is what signals stability, not the contract type.

Do you have to mention that a zero-hours role was variable or casual?

Not on the resume, and usually not at all until it's asked. You don't state hours for any job on a resume, so there's nothing to disclose by default. If an application form asks for employment type or hours, answer honestly and neutrally there; if a role's variability is what let you do something else worth showing (study, another job, caregiving), a brief note can add useful context. But don't volunteer a caveat nobody asked for — over-explaining the contract makes a solid role sound apologetic. Keep the resume to the role and its results, and let the details surface in the interview if they matter.
