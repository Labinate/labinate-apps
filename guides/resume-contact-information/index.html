Resume contact information

It's the smallest section on your resume and the easiest to get wrong. Your contact header is the one part a recruiter has to use — and a stray typo, a work email, or details buried in a header field can cost you the interview before anyone reads a word. Here's exactly what to include, how to format it, and what to leave off.

Updated July 2026 · ~4 min read · by Labinate

Every resume needs a contact block, and it's the first of the five sections every resume must have. Most people treat it as an afterthought — which is exactly why a surprising number of good candidates go unreachable, screened out by an applicant tracking system that never found their phone number, or quietly passed over for a habit like an old party-nickname email. Get these six lines right once and you never think about them again.

What to include

The complete list is short. Five essentials, plus one optional line when it's relevant.

  • Full name — the name you go by professionally, on the largest line. This is also your document's headline for keyword matching, so no nicknames in quotes.
  • Phone number — one reliable number, with a professional voicemail set up. Include a country code if you're applying internationally.
  • Professional email — based on your name (firstname.lastname@gmail.com), never a work address.
  • Location — city and state, or city and country. Not your full street address. Seeking remote work? You can add "Open to remote" here — see how to put remote work on a resume.
  • LinkedIn URL — if your profile is current and complete. Use a clean custom URL (linkedin.com/in/yourname); see how to add LinkedIn to your resume.
  • Portfolio or site (optional) — a GitHub, Behance, or personal site, only when it's relevant to the role.
A clean header Jordan Rivera
  • jordan.rivera@gmail.com · (415) 555-0182
  • San Francisco, CA · linkedin.com/in/jordanrivera · jordanrivera.dev

That's the whole section — one bold name line and one or two lines of details beneath it. Everything else that follows is your summary, experience, education, and skills.

What to leave off

Just as important as the fields you add is the clutter you remove. Each of these either dates your resume, risks bias, or exposes information you shouldn't share on a document you email to strangers.

Cut these
  • ✗ Full street address — city and state is enough; a home address is outdated and a privacy risk.
  • ✗ Your work email — it signals job-hunting on the clock and vanishes when you leave.
  • ✗ Photo, age, date of birth, marital status, nationality — in the US, UK, Canada, and Australia these invite bias and are left off by convention.
  • ✗ Multiple phone numbers or emails — pick the one you actually answer.
  • ✗ Social media that isn't professional — no personal Instagram, Facebook, or an inactive account.
  • ✗ The label "Resume" or "Curriculum Vitae" as a title — the file obviously is one.
A note on norms outside the US. In parts of Europe, Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America, a photo and a date of birth are still expected on a CV. Match the convention of the country you're applying in — but when in doubt for US/UK/CA/AU roles, leave them off. See CV vs resume for the wider differences.

Format details that matter

Keep it in the body, not the page header

The single most damaging mistake: putting your name and contact details inside your word processor's header/footer area to save space. Many resume parsers skip that region entirely — so the ATS reads a resume with no name and no way to reach you. Always keep contact details in the normal body of the document. See the ATS checklist for the full list of parser traps.

Make links plain text, not buttons

Write out linkedin.com/in/yourname as readable text rather than hiding it behind a linked icon. A parser reads the text; an icon it may drop. The same goes for your email — spell it out, don't bury it in a "Contact me" button.

Match the styling to the rest of the resume

Use the same font as the body, size the name a couple of points larger, and don't wrap the whole block in a colored bar or graphic. Keep it in the same font and size scheme as everything else so it reads as one document.

Double-check every character

One transposed digit in a phone number or a dropped letter in an email address quietly makes you unreachable — and you'll never know why the callback didn't come. Read it aloud, or test the email by sending yourself a message. It's the highest-stakes proofread on the page.

The section in one breath. Name on the top line; email, phone, city and state, and a current LinkedIn beneath it; a portfolio link only if it's relevant. Leave off your street address, work email, photo, and personal details. Keep it in the document body — never a header field — with plain-text links, and proofread every character.

Frequently asked questions

What contact information should go on a resume?

Your full name, a professional email, a phone number, your city and state (or region), and a current LinkedIn URL — plus a portfolio link only if it's relevant. That's five or six lines. You don't need a full street address, and you should leave off age, marital status, a photo (for US/UK/CA/AU roles), and personal IDs.

Should I put my full address on a resume?

No. City and state — or city and country — is enough. It tells an employer your general location and whether you'd relocate, without exposing your home address. Applying out of area? Write "Open to relocation" instead of a location — see relocation on a resume for how to say it so it beats the location filter.

What email address should I use on a resume?

A simple, professional address based on your name, like firstname.lastname@gmail.com. Avoid nicknames, birth-year numbers, and outdated providers. Never use your work email — it signals job-hunting on company time and disappears when you leave. Make a free dedicated address if you need one.

Where does the contact section go on a resume?

At the very top, in the header of the page — the first thing a recruiter and an ATS read. Name on the largest line, details beneath it. Keep it in the document body, not a header/footer field or text box, or a parser may miss your details entirely.
