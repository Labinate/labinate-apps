How to list education on a resume

The education section is short, but people overthink it — and a few small choices (order, GPA, what to leave off) genuinely change how it reads. Here's the format, the exceptions, and how to handle the awkward cases like no degree or study still in progress.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

For most jobs, education is a credential the recruiter confirms in two seconds, not the reason you get hired. So the goal is simple: state it cleanly, put it in the right place, and don't pad it. This guide covers the standard format, then the situations that actually cause anxiety — a strong GPA vs. a weak one, an unfinished degree, no degree at all.

The standard format

Each entry has a required core and optional extras. The core, always: degree and field, institution, graduation year. That's a complete entry on its own.

Standard entry B.S. in Computer Science
University of Michigan — Ann Arbor, MI · 2024

Optional lines you can add when they help: a strong GPA, honors (cum laude, Dean's List), relevant coursework (useful for students light on experience), and academic awards. Add them if they strengthen the case; leave them off if they don't. More isn't better here.

Where the section goes

Experienced professionals → near the bottom

After your work experience. Your track record is your strongest card, so lead with it and let the degree confirm the foundation.

Students & recent grads → near the top

Above or just below your summary. When your degree is your most relevant recent achievement, give it the prime real estate. See how to write a resume with no experience.

Should you include your GPA?

Only if two things are true: it's strong (usually 3.5+) and you're a recent graduate. A high GPA early in your career is a useful signal; once you have a couple of years of real work behind you, drop it — nobody hiring a mid-career professional is weighing their sophomore-year transcript. GPA is optional, so a mediocre one simply doesn't appear, and that's not dishonest.

The awkward cases

A degree in progress

List it with an expected date so it reads as on-track, not abandoned:

B.A. in Economics (expected May 2027)
Boston University — Boston, MA

Coursework you didn't finish

You can still list attendance honestly — the institution, the dates, and what you completed — without claiming a degree you didn't earn. "Completed 60 credits toward a B.A. in Marketing" is truthful and gives credit for the work. The one rule that's non-negotiable: never imply you graduated if you didn't. It's the single fastest way to lose an offer at the background-check stage.

No degree

No degree is not a hole to hide. Many roles don't require one, and where it isn't required, your experience and skills carry the resume. If you have certifications, bootcamps, or relevant training, an "Education & Training" or "Certifications" section can do the same job — showcasing that you've built the skills, credential or not.

Formatting details that trip people up

  • Reverse-chronological order — most recent degree first, matching the rest of your resume (see the format guide).
  • Skip high school once you have any college — listing it alongside a degree reads as padding. (High school alone is fine if that's your highest level.)
  • Consistency — match the date style and layout you use in your experience section so the page reads as one document.
  • Keep it real text — no icons, columns, or graphics an ATS will scramble. See the ATS checklist.

Frequently asked questions

What should the education section include?

At minimum your degree and field, the institution's name, and the graduation year. Add location, honors, relevant coursework, or a strong GPA when they help — but the degree, school, and year are the non-negotiable core.

Where should education go on a resume?

Experienced professionals put it near the bottom, after work experience — your track record leads. Students and recent graduates put it near the top, because the degree is their strongest recent credential.

Should I include my GPA?

Only if it's strong (generally 3.5+) and you're a recent graduate. Once you have a few years of experience, drop it. GPA is optional, so leaving off a low one isn't dishonest.

How do I list an unfinished or in-progress degree?

For a degree in progress, use an expected graduation date: "B.A. in Economics, expected May 2027." For coursework you didn't finish, list the institution, dates, and credits completed — without claiming a degree. Never imply you graduated if you didn't.
