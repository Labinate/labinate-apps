How to list a company that changed names or was acquired

Your employer got acquired, merged, renamed, or spun off — and now the company you worked at doesn't exist under the name you knew it by. This feels like a career problem. It isn't. It's a verification problem: a recruiter needs to recognize where you worked, and a background check needs to confirm your dates against the entity that holds the records today. Both break if the name on your resume doesn't connect to the company that can vouch for you. The fix is one line and one rule.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

You spent four good years at a company. Then it got bought, and the name on the door changed. Now, on your resume, you face a small but real dilemma: write the name you knew and a recruiter Googles it and finds nothing — or write the new name and it doesn't match the payroll records a background check will query. Left unhandled, a name change makes an ordinary, honest job look inconsistent. Handled in one line, it disappears as an issue entirely. This guide shows the naming convention and the one bright line that keeps it honest.

The core idea: a name change is a problem of connecting names, not of explaining a career move. Two readers matter — the recruiter who must recognize the company, and the background check that must confirm your dates against the entity that holds the records now. Show both names in one line, lead with the one that verifies you, and you've solved it. There's nothing to apologize for and nothing to explain.

First, know what this is not

A name change looks like a few neighboring situations, but it calls for its own handling. Mixing it up with them is how a non-issue turns into a weak entry.

SituationHow it differs from a company name change
Listing datesThat's about the time you worked somewhere. A name change is about the identity of the employer — the company is the same continuous entity, but what it's called changed. You handle the name in the employer line, independent of the dates.
Job-hoppingThat's many separate employers in a short span. A rename is the opposite risk: one continuous job that looks like two employers because the name changed. Done wrong (splitting it into two entries) it manufactures a job-hopping impression that isn't real.
Your own business closingThat's your company ending. Here, someone else's company changed identity while you were an employee — you didn't own it, you just need its current and former names to line up so you can be verified.
Multiple roles at one employerThat's one company, several titles — a vertical-movement question. A rename is one company, several names — a verification question. They're different axes and can both be true at once.
General work experienceThe overall formatting of your history. The name change is a narrow fix inside one entry's header — everything else about that job is listed exactly as normal.

Step 1 — Show both names in one line

The whole fix lives in the employer line. Put both names there so that whichever one a reader searches — the name you knew it by, or the name it's called today — lands on the same job. The standard convention is recognizable name first, the other in parentheses.

ScenarioEmployer line
Acquired / renamed; new name is what people know nowGlobex (formerly Acme Corp)
Old name still carries the brand equityAcme Corp (now part of Globex)
Merger of two firms into a new nameNorthwind (formed by the merger of Acme & Contoso)
Your division was spun off into its own companyAcme Cloud (spun off from Acme Corp, 2024)

Keep it to a parenthetical — this is a label, not a narrative. Your title, dates, and bullets stay exactly as they were; only the employer line carries the extra name. Because applicant tracking systems and recruiters both search by keyword, having both names as plain, selectable text means a search for either one finds you.

Step 2 — Lead with the name that verifies you

When you're unsure which name goes first, use this test: which name will confirm your employment if someone calls? Background checks and reference calls reach whoever holds the employment records now — almost always the current or successor entity. Leading with the name that matches those records keeps the verification path clean.

Default: current entity first, former name in parentheses

The successor company holds payroll and HR records and answers verification requests. Putting it first — "Globex (formerly Acme Corp)" — means the name a checker queries is the name they see first. This is the right choice in most cases.

Exception: lead with the name that carries the brand

If you worked somewhere well-known that was later folded into an obscure holding company, leading with the recognizable name earns you the credit — "Acme Corp (now part of NWH Holdings)." You still show the current name so verification resolves; you just don't bury the name that makes a recruiter sit up.

Either way, both names are present. The only question is order, and the answer is: whichever one does more work for the reader in front of you.

Step 3 — Keep one continuous job as one entry

If the name changed while you were there and your role didn't stop, it's still one job. The instinct to split it — an "Acme Corp" block for the first two years and a "Globex" block for the next two — is a mistake: it chops one solid tenure into two shorter-looking stints and manufactures the appearance of job-hopping out of a company event you had nothing to do with.

Split — looks like two short jobs

  • Acme Corp — 2019–2021
  • Globex — 2021–2024
  • Reads as two employers, two shorter tenures, a possible move

One entry — the tenure it actually was

  • Globex (formerly Acme Corp) — 2019–2024
  • One continuous five-year role, correctly credited
  • Name change noted, not dramatized

If you were also promoted or changed roles during those years, handle the role changes the usual way — that's a separate axis stacked inside the same single employer block.

The bright line — don't misstate when you were there versus when the name changed. The tempting move is to claim a famous acquirer's name for your whole tenure — "Worked at Globex, 2019–2024" — when Globex didn't own the company until 2023. That's not a formatting choice; it's a factual claim that your start date can't support, and it's exactly what a background check surfaces when it compares your dates to the acquisition's close date. Show the transition honestly instead — Acme Corp (acquired by Globex, 2023), dates spanning both — and you get full credit for the recognizable name with zero risk. You may use the new name for the period you were actually there under it; never for the period before it existed.

Quick reference

  • It's verification, not career → the job is fine; you're just making sure both names connect to you.
  • Both names, one line → "Current (formerly Old)" in the employer line; keep it a parenthetical, not a story.
  • Lead with the verifying name → usually the current entity that holds your records; lead with the old name only when it carries the brand.
  • One job, one entry → a mid-tenure rename stays a single continuous block; splitting it fakes job-hopping.
  • Make both names searchable → plain selectable text so a keyword or ATS search on either name finds you.
  • Bright line → never claim the new name for a period before the change actually happened; show the transition and the real dates.

Keep building the resume

Once the employer line is honest and searchable, the rest of the entry is ordinary craft: turn duties into accomplishments, quantify them, lead with strong action verbs, and get your dates clean and consistent. Frequently asked questions

How do you list a company that was acquired or changed its name on a resume?

Show both names in the employer line so verification can't break, then move on — it's one line, not a story. The standard format is the current or better-known name first with the former name in parentheses: "Globex (formerly Acme Corp)". If the old name is the one people still recognize, invert it: "Acme Corp (now part of Globex)". Keep your title, dates, and bullets exactly as they were. The goal is simple: whichever name a recruiter or background checker searches — the one you knew it as, or the one it's called today — they land on the same job.

Which name should I lead with — the old one or the new one?

Lead with whichever name a reader is more likely to recognize and can verify you against today. Usually that's the current entity, because it's the one that holds your employment records and answers the background check — put it first and show the former name in parentheses. But if you worked at a well-known company that was later absorbed into an obscure holding company, lead with the name that carries brand equity and note the change second. The test is: which name makes the reader immediately understand where you worked, and which one will confirm your dates if someone calls.

Do I even need to mention the name change?

Mention it whenever the two names could cause confusion or a failed lookup — which is almost always. If a recruiter Googles the company on your resume and finds nothing (because it's since been renamed), or a background check queries the old name and the successor company has no record under it, you look inconsistent through no fault of your own. One parenthetical fixes it. The only time you can safely use just one name is when that single name is unambiguous and still verifiable — e.g., a rename so minor or so recent that both the old records and the current entity resolve to it.

I worked at a startup that was acquired by a big company — can I put the big company's name?

You can use the acquirer's name for the period you actually worked there under that ownership — and it's smart to, because it carries weight. What you can't do is backdate it. If you joined the startup in 2020 and the acquisition closed in 2023, you were an employee of the big company only from 2023 on; claiming you "worked at [FamousCo] since 2020" is false and unravels the moment a background check compares your start date to the deal's close date. The honest, still-impressive framing shows the transition: "Acme (acquired by Globex, 2023)", with your dates spanning both. You get full credit for the name without inviting a discrepancy.

My employer was renamed while I worked there — does that make it two jobs?

No — it's one continuous role, and you should present it as one entry so it doesn't read as job-hopping. Splitting a single uninterrupted job into two employer blocks because the name changed makes your tenure look shorter and your history choppier than it was. Keep one entry with continuous dates and fold the name change into the employer line: "Globex (formerly Acme Corp), 2019–2024". If you were also promoted or changed roles during that time, handle the role changes the normal way — that's a separate axis from the company's name.
