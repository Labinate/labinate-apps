How to list a portfolio on a resume

A portfolio on a resume is a link, not a section — and that one fact settles almost every question people agonize over. It isn't a block of the page to design; it's a single line in your header whose only job is to be the one link a recruiter actually clicks. So the real work isn't formatting. It's making sure the thing behind the link is live, curated, relevant — and cutting the link entirely when it isn't.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Most portfolio advice tells you how to place the link and stops there. But the link is the easy part. The hard truth is that a portfolio link is a promise — "there's proof of my work at this address" — and the recruiter cashes that promise in one click. If the click lands on strong, on-target work, the link did more for you than any bullet on the page. If it lands on a 404, a "coming soon" page, or a pile of half-finished experiments, it did the opposite: it turned your strongest asset into evidence of carelessness. This guide is about earning the click, not just adding the URL.

The core idea: the value of a portfolio link is entirely what's behind it. A live, curated, relevant destination is worth more than a paragraph of description. A broken, empty, or off-target one is worse than no link at all. So: put it in the header, format it to survive an ATS, deep-link to your best relevant work — and if the destination isn't ready to be judged, leave the link off.

Where the link goes: the header, with your contact info

A portfolio is a way to reach and verify you, so it lives with the other ways to reach you — in the header block, next to your email, phone, and LinkedIn. That's the spot a recruiter's eye lands in the first three seconds, and it's where they look when they've read enough to want proof. It does not get its own section, its own heading, or a footer slot on page two where nobody scrolls.

The clean header format

Jane Doe · Product Designer
jane@email.com · (555) 018-2245 · janedoe.design · linkedin.com/in/janedoe

The portfolio sits inline with the rest of your contact information, as plain readable text. One glance gives the reader every way to reach you and the single address that proves your work — which is exactly the job of a header.

If you have more than one relevant link — a personal site and a GitHub, say — keep them together in the same block and hold the line at two. Two links read as "here's my work"; four read as clutter and dilute the one that matters. Pick the strongest two for this role and let the rest go.

Format it to survive both a human and an ATS

Your resume gets read twice: once by an applicant tracking system that only sees text, and once by a person who clicks. A portfolio URL has to work for both at the same time, which rules out a few common mistakes.

Do thisNot this
Write the URL as visible text and make it a live link — github.com/janedoeA "Portfolio" word hyperlinked with no visible URL — an ATS can't read the hidden link, and a printed copy loses it entirely
Drop the https:// and www. to keep it short and cleanThe full https://www.janedoe.design/portfolio/index.html eating half the line
Use the real destination so it's legible even if every link is stripped outA shortener like bit.ly/3xK9 — it hides where you're sending them and reads as spammy
A custom domain or a clean profile URL you controlA raw Google Drive / Dropbox share link, or a URL with a login wall the recruiter can't get past

The single rule underneath all of this: the destination should be readable even if the document is printed and every hyperlink disappears. If your link only works as a click, it doesn't work in an ATS and it doesn't work on paper.

Earn the click: what's behind the link matters most

This is the part most guides skip, and it's the part that decides whether the link helps or hurts. A recruiter gives your portfolio seconds, not minutes. Where the link lands has to make your case immediately.

A link worth clicking

  • Lands on your strongest, most relevant work — up top, no hunting
  • Curated: your best 4–8 pieces, not everything you've ever made
  • Live, loads fast, works on a phone, no login wall
  • On-target for this role, not a generic grab-bag

A link that costs you

  • 404, lapsed domain, or a "coming soon" splash page
  • A file dump where the good work is three clicks deep
  • Years out of date, or full of work unrelated to the job
  • A GitHub of only forks and tutorial follow-alongs

When one project is a near-perfect match for the role, deep-link straight to it instead of making the reader dig through a homepage. And because the ideal link changes with the job, which portfolio piece you feature is a tailoring decision — the same way you'd tailor a summary or reorder your skills. Point the link at whatever proves your case fastest for the job in front of you.

Which link, by field

"Portfolio" means different things in different fields, and the right destination is the one your industry already expects to see.

Engineers & developers

A github.com/you with real, pinned repositories — projects you built, readable READMEs, actual commits — beats a slide deck. A personal site works too if it links out to the code. Skip it if your GitHub is only forks and course exercises; an empty or copycat profile reads worse than none.

Designers, artists & UX

A personal site, Behance, or Dribbble showing finished work with context — the problem, your role, the outcome. Lead with the pieces closest to the target role. Visual fields are where a curated portfolio does the most work, because the resume can only describe what the link can show.

Writers, marketers & content

A clips page, personal site, or a clean doc of published pieces with live links. Feature the formats the job wants — long-form, landing pages, email — and put the best-performing, most on-brand work first.

Everyone else

Most roles don't expect a portfolio, and forcing one is worse than omitting it. If your work genuinely lives online — a research page, a product you shipped, a public talk — link it. If it doesn't, don't manufacture a portfolio to have a link; a strong experience section carries the resume on its own.

Portfolio link vs. projects section — you often want both

These get confused, but they do different jobs. Your projects section is words on the page: a title, a one-line what-and-why, the impact, the tools. The portfolio link is the evidence behind those words — the code, the designs, the live product a reader can actually inspect. The resume makes the claim; the portfolio proves it. The cleanest pattern is to describe a project as an entry and, where it helps, link that specific project straight to its portfolio page, so the claim and the proof sit next to each other. Don't paste a wall of raw links into the body of the resume — the header holds your main link; individual project links attach to the projects they belong to.

The bright line — no link beats a bad link. A missing portfolio link is neutral; the recruiter simply moves on. A broken, empty, stale, or off-target link is a negative — it reads as carelessness and quietly taints everything else on the page. So the honest test before you add the URL: if a recruiter clicks this right now, does it help my case? If yes, put it in the header. If no, cut it, ship the resume, and add it back the day the destination is worth the click. Never link to something you're hoping they won't actually open.

Quick reference

  • It's a link, not a section → one line in the header, next to your contact info; never its own heading or a page-two footer.
  • Make it ATS-proof → visible URL that's also a live link, no shorteners, no "click here," no login walls; readable even when links are stripped.
  • Earn the click → land on curated, relevant, live work; deep-link to the best match; cap it at two links.
  • Match your field → GitHub for devs, a visual portfolio for designers, clips for writers; skip it where the role doesn't expect one.
  • Bright line → no link beats a bad link; if it's broken, empty, or off-target, leave it off until it's ready.

Frequently asked questions

Where do you put a portfolio link on a resume?

In the header, on the same line or block as the rest of your contact information — next to your email, phone, and LinkedIn. A portfolio is a way to reach and verify you, so it lives with the other ways to reach you, at the very top where a recruiter sees it in the first three seconds. It does not get its own section, its own heading, or a spot buried at the bottom of page two. If you have several relevant links (a personal site plus a GitHub, say), keep them in the same header block and don't let them sprawl — two links is comfortable, four starts to look like clutter.

How do you format a portfolio URL so it works in both a PDF and an ATS?

Write the URL as plain, readable text and also make it a live hyperlink. Applicant tracking systems read the text, so a clean "janedoe.design" or "github.com/janedoe" is parseable; a human reading the PDF clicks the same text because it's linked. Avoid link-shorteners (bit.ly hides the destination and looks spammy), avoid "click here" labels with no visible URL (an ATS can't read the hidden link, and a printed copy loses it entirely), and drop the "https://" and "www." to keep it short. The rule: the destination should be legible even if every link in the document is stripped out.

Should you link to your homepage or to specific work?

Link to whatever proves your case fastest for this job. For most people that's a curated portfolio homepage where the strongest, most relevant pieces are on top. But if one project is the perfect match for the role, deep-link straight to it rather than making the reader hunt — a recruiter gives your link seconds, not minutes. The failure mode is linking to a generic landing page, a login wall, or a file dump where the relevant work is three clicks deep; if they have to search for the good stuff, they won't. Put the best, most on-target work exactly where the link lands.

Is a portfolio link the same as listing projects on your resume?

No — they do different jobs and you often want both. Projects on the resume are described entries on the page: a title, a one-line what-and-why, the impact, the tools. The portfolio link is the place a reader goes to see the actual work — the code, the designs, the writing, the live product. The resume makes the claim in words; the portfolio is the evidence behind it. The cleanest pattern is to describe a project as a bullet or entry and, where it helps, link that specific project to its portfolio page, so the claim and the proof sit next to each other.

When should you leave the portfolio link off entirely?

Whenever the destination isn't ready to be judged. Leave it off if the link is broken or the domain has lapsed, if the site is empty or "coming soon," if the work is years out of date or off-target for the role, or if it's a GitHub with only forks and tutorial follow-alongs. A recruiter who clicks a dead or empty link doesn't think "no big deal" — they read it as carelessness, and it taints the rest of the resume. No link is neutral; a bad link is a negative. If the portfolio isn't ready, cut the link, ship the resume, and add the link back the day the destination is worth the click.
