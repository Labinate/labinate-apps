The chronological resume: the format most people should use

Reverse-chronological is the default resume format for a reason — it's what recruiters expect and what every applicant tracking system reads best. Here's the exact section order, a copy-ready template, and the rare cases where another format serves you better.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

When people say "resume," they almost always mean a chronological resume — more precisely, reverse-chronological: your most recent job first, then the one before it, back through your history. It's the format roughly nine in ten applicants should use, because it answers the two questions a recruiter asks first — where have you worked, and when? — without making them dig.

This guide covers what goes in it, in what order, with a template you can copy. For the wider picture of all three formats, see the resume format guide.

The section order

  1. Header — name, one-line title, email, phone, city, and a link (LinkedIn or portfolio).
  2. Summary — two or three lines framing who you are and what you're targeting. See resume summary examples.
  3. Work experience — the heart of the format: each role in reverse-chronological order, newest first.
  4. Education — degree, institution, year (drop the year if it's far back).
  5. Skills — a compact, relevant list. See skills to put on a resume.
  6. Optional extras — certifications, projects, or volunteer work, if they earn their space.

How each job entry is built

Every entry in the work-experience section follows the same shape, so a reader can scan them like rows in a table:

One correctly formatted job entry

Senior Product Designer — Acme SaaS, Austin TX · Mar 2022 – Present

• Led the redesign of the checkout flow, cutting drop-off 22% and adding $1.4M in annual recurring revenue.
• Built and documented a 40-component design system now used by 6 product teams.
• Mentored 3 junior designers; 2 were promoted within a year.

  • Title, employer, location, dates on one line — dates right-aligned or at the end, and consistent across every entry (see how to handle dates and gaps).
  • Three to five bullets per recent role, fewer as you go back in time.
  • Achievements, not duties — start with a verb and put a number in as many as you can. See resume action verbs and quantifying achievements.
Reverse-chronological, not forward. "Chronological resume" is shorthand — you always list newest first. Your current or most recent role is the one a recruiter cares about most, so it goes at the top of the experience section, right under your summary.

Chronological vs. functional vs. combination

  • Chronological (this one): organised by job, newest first. Transparent, expected, and the most ATS-reliable. Use it unless you have a specific reason not to.
  • Functional: organised by skill group, dates downplayed. Reads as an attempt to hide a gap or short tenure — recruiters distrust it. Avoid it.
  • Combination: a skills summary up top, then a dated work-history section below. A reasonable choice for a big career change where your titles don't match the new role — but keep the dated history.

The through-line: keep a dated work history. Even in a combination resume, a timeline reassures; a resume with no dates at all raises exactly the questions you were hoping to dodge.

When chronological is the clear winner

  • You have a steady work history in one or related fields.
  • You're applying to a role similar to your current or recent one.
  • Your progression shows growth — bigger scope, better titles — which the format puts on display.
  • You want the safest bet with applicant tracking systems, which is nearly always. See is my resume ATS-friendly?

Frequently asked questions

What is a chronological resume?

A chronological resume lists your work history in reverse-chronological order — your most recent job first, working backwards — with each role showing the employer, title, dates, and a few bullet points of achievements. It's the standard, expected resume format and the one applicant tracking systems parse most reliably.

What is the difference between a chronological and a functional resume?

A chronological resume organises your experience by job, newest first, so a reader sees where and when you worked. A functional resume groups skills instead and downplays dates. Recruiters trust chronological because it's transparent; functional resumes read as an attempt to hide a gap or a short tenure, so most applicants should use chronological or a combination format.

Is a chronological resume ATS-friendly?

Yes — the reverse-chronological format is the most ATS-friendly layout there is. Its clear job-title, employer, and date structure maps directly to the fields applicant tracking systems extract, so your experience is parsed accurately. Keep it single-column with standard section headings for the best results.

When should you not use a chronological resume?

Consider a combination format if you're making a big career change and your job titles don't match the new role, or if you have very fragmented experience. Even then, keep a dated work-history section — a purely skills-based functional resume with no timeline raises more suspicion than it solves. For most people, chronological is the right choice.
