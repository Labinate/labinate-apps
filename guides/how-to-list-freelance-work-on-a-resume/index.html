How to list freelance work on a resume

Freelancing is real employment, and it belongs in your work experience section — not tucked into an "other" list at the bottom. The trick is deciding who the employer is, whether to bundle your clients under one heading, and how to write bullets that turn scattered gigs into a coherent track record. Here's how to do all three.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

Most freelancers make their work look smaller than it is. They label it "Self-Employed" with no dates, hide it below their salaried jobs, or list twelve one-off clients as twelve tiny entries. A recruiter skimming that sees fragments. The fix is to treat your freelance practice the way you'd treat any job: a clear title, real dates, and results — presented as one deliberate story.

Be your own employer

The first question a resume answers for every job is where did you work. For freelance work, the answer is you. Put a real job title in the role slot and something concrete in the employer slot — a registered business name if you have one, or simply "Freelance" / "Self-Employed" if you don't. What you should not do is leave the employer blank or bury the whole thing in a footnote.

Lead with the skill, not the arrangement. "Freelance UX Designer" tells a recruiter — and an applicant tracking system scanning for keywords — exactly what you do. A bare "Self-Employed" makes them guess.

✓ Title it clearly

  • Freelance Web Developer
  • Independent Marketing Consultant
  • Freelance Copywriter — Jane Doe Studio
  • Contract Data Analyst

✕ Leave it vague

  • Self-Employed (no role, no skill)
  • Various projects
  • Odd jobs / gig work
  • Freelancer (with no dates)

Where freelance work goes

In your main work experience section, in normal reverse-chronological order among your other roles. If you freelanced full-time for a year between salaried jobs, that year sits in the timeline exactly where it happened — not demoted to a separate "freelance" block. Recruiters read work experience top to bottom expecting a continuous story; a paid freelance stretch is that story for those months. If that work was also remote, that's a separate field — mark the location "Remote"; see how to put remote work on a resume.

Why placement matters

A freelance entry sitting in the main timeline reads as "this is what I was doing." The same entry pushed into an "Additional Experience" box at the bottom reads as "this barely counts." Same work, opposite impression. Keep it in the main section unless it truly was a minor side project.

One umbrella entry vs. one entry per client

This is the decision most freelancers get wrong, and it has a simple rule.

Default to a single umbrella entry. Give your practice one title and one continuous date range, then name your best clients inside the bullet points as proof. This keeps a long client list from fragmenting your resume into a dozen two-line stubs.

Umbrella entry — the default Freelance Content Strategist · Self-Employed · Mar 2023 – Present
  • Delivered content strategy and SEO audits for 15+ B2B SaaS clients, including Acme Analytics and Northwind CRM.
  • Grew one client's organic traffic 3× in six months by rebuilding their blog taxonomy and briefing a 40-article content plan.
  • Retained 80% of clients on repeat engagements through 2024–2025.

Break a client out into its own entry only when the engagement earns it — it was long-term, senior, a recognizable name, or squarely relevant to the job you're targeting. A six-month embedded role as "Interim Head of Growth" at a known startup deserves its own line; a two-hour logo tweak does not.

Standalone entry — a notable engagement Interim Growth Lead (Contract) · Northwind CRM · Jan 2024 – Jul 2024
  • Owned the full acquisition funnel for a 6-month contract, reporting directly to the CEO.
  • Lifted trial-to-paid conversion from 4% to 9% by rebuilding onboarding emails and the pricing page.

Use freelancing to close employment gaps

Here's the quiet advantage: continuous freelance work fills what would otherwise look like a hole. A stretch between salaried jobs that would read as a blank employment gap instead reads as active, paid, self-directed work — as long as the dates are there and unbroken. Put the date range on your freelance entry the same way you would any job, using "Present" if you're still taking clients, and the gap disappears on its own.

Dates are what make this work. A freelance entry with no dates raises the exact suspicion you're trying to avoid. "Freelance Designer · 2022 – 2024" bridges a gap; "Freelance Designer" floating with no timeline does the opposite.

Quantify like it's a business — because it is

Freelancers have better numbers than most employees and rarely use them. You know your client count, your revenue, your retention, your delivery record. Put those to work the same way you'd quantify any achievement: number of clients, results delivered, revenue managed, repeat-business rate. "Served 20+ clients" and "generated $80K in project revenue over 18 months" are the kind of hard evidence a salaried peer often can't offer.

Write the bullets with strong action verbs and outcomes, not a list of services. "Designed logos and websites" is a menu; "Redesigned a retailer's storefront, lifting checkout completion 22%" is a result.

Common mistakes

  • Labeling it "Self-Employed" with no role. Name the skill — "Freelance Software Engineer" — so a recruiter and an ATS both know what you did.
  • Listing every micro-gig as its own entry. Bundle them under one umbrella entry and feature the best clients in bullets.
  • Dropping the dates. Missing dates both weaken the entry and forfeit the gap-filling benefit. Always include a range.
  • Hiding freelance work at the bottom. If it's real experience, put it in the main timeline, not an afterthought section.
  • Describing services instead of results. Clients hired you for outcomes; show the outcomes, with numbers where you have them.
  • Name-dropping clients you can't disclose. If an engagement is under NDA, describe the client by type ("a Fortune 500 retailer") rather than by name.
The whole thing in one breath. Treat freelancing as a job: a clear "Freelance [Role]" title, a real employer slot, and a dated range in your main work experience. Default to one umbrella entry with your best clients named in the bullets; break out a client only when the engagement is big enough to stand alone. Keep the dates unbroken to cover any gap, and quantify everything — clients, revenue, retention, results.

Frequently asked questions

How do you list freelance work on a resume?

Put it in your work experience section like any other job. Use a job title such as "Freelance Graphic Designer" or your business name as the employer, give the date range (using "Present" if you're still active), and write results-focused bullet points naming client outcomes. Freelancing is real employment — it belongs alongside your full-time roles, not in a lesser "other" section.

What job title should I use for freelance work?

Use the role you actually performed, prefixed with "Freelance" or "Independent" — for example "Freelance Web Developer" or "Independent Marketing Consultant". This is clearer to a recruiter and to an ATS than a vague "Self-Employed", because it names the skill. You can list a registered business name as the employer, or simply write "Self-Employed" or "Freelance" in the employer slot.

Should I list every freelance client on my resume?

No. If you've had many clients, create one umbrella entry for your freelance practice and name two or three notable clients inside the bullet points as evidence. List clients as separate entries only when a single engagement was long, senior, or directly relevant to the job you're targeting — otherwise a wall of tiny gigs reads as fragmented rather than impressive.

Does freelance work count as employment on a resume?

Yes. Freelance and self-employed work is legitimate professional experience and recruiters treat it that way, especially when you show concrete results. A bonus: continuous freelancing fills what would otherwise look like an employment gap, so a stretch between salaried jobs reads as active, paid work rather than time off.
