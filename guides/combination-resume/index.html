The combination resume: skills up top, timeline intact

The combination — or hybrid — resume is the format most career changers and non-linear careers should reach for. It leads with your strongest skills, then backs them with a full dated work history. Here's exactly how it's built, a copy-ready template, and when it beats the other two formats.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A combination resume — also called a hybrid resume — is the third and least-understood of the three resume formats. It borrows the best of the other two: it opens like a functional resume, leading with a section of grouped skills and highlights, then continues like a chronological resume, listing your jobs in reverse date order with every title, employer, and date intact.

That structure solves the exact problem the functional format creates. You get to steer the recruiter's eye toward the skills that matter for the role you want — and you keep the timeline that recruiters and applicant tracking systems both need to trust you. How a combination resume is structured

  1. Header — name, one-line title, email, phone, city, and a link (LinkedIn or portfolio).
  2. Summary — two or three lines naming your target and your headline strengths. See resume summary examples.
  3. Core skills / highlights — the hybrid's signature section: two or three themed clusters of your most relevant abilities, each with a couple of achievement bullets. This is what a recruiter reads first.
  4. Work history — the full reverse-chronological list: title, employer, dates, and two to four bullets per role. Nothing is dropped.
  5. Education — degree, institution, year. See how to list education.

The order matters: skills before history is what makes it a combination resume. Flip them and you have a plain chronological resume with a strong skills section — which is fine, just not the same tool.

What the highlights section looks like

Each cluster names a capability and backs it with one or two of your best, most relevant wins. Unlike a functional resume, you don't need to hide where they came from — the dated history right below fills in the rest:

• Ran cross-functional launches of up to 12 people, shipping 9 releases on schedule over two years.
• Cut average delivery time 30% by introducing a lightweight weekly planning cadence.

Data & Analysis
• Built the reporting dashboard the leadership team now uses weekly, replacing three manual spreadsheets.
• Identified a churn driver worth $180K/yr through cohort analysis.

Start every bullet with a verb and put a number in as many as you can. See resume action verbs and quantifying achievements.

The one rule that keeps it working: never let the highlights block replace the dated history — keep both. The whole advantage of the combination format over the functional one is that your timeline is right there. Drop it, and you've quietly turned your hybrid back into a functional resume, red flags and all.

When to use a combination resume

Reach for the hybrid when your value isn't captured by your most recent job title alone:

  • You're changing careers: your old titles don't signal the new role, so you lead with transferable skills — then prove them with the dated history. See career-change resumes.
  • Your history is non-linear or fragmented: freelance, contract, or varied roles read better when grouped by skill up top and listed by date below.
  • You're senior and your edge is a specific mix: a combination of capabilities matters more than any single position — foreground the mix.
  • You want to emphasise skills without hiding anything: the hybrid is the honest way to do exactly that.

If your path is a clean, upward line in one field, you don't need the extra machinery — a straightforward chronological resume is simpler and reads just as well.

Combination vs the other two formats

  • vs chronological: both keep a full timeline; the combination adds a skills-first block on top. Use chronological for a straightforward path, combination when you need to reframe.
  • vs functional: both lead with skills; the functional one drops the dated history and the combination keeps it. That single difference is why the combination is trusted and the functional one usually isn't. See the functional resume guide for why.

Keep the whole thing single-column, with standard headings and real text, and it stays ATS-friendly — the parser finds your skills block and your timeline both.

Frequently asked questions

What is a combination resume?

A combination resume, also called a hybrid resume, blends the two other formats: it opens with a skills or highlights section that groups your strongest, most relevant abilities, then follows with a full reverse-chronological work-history section that keeps every job title, employer, and date intact. You get the "here's what I'm great at" framing of a functional resume up top, and the transparency and ATS-friendliness of a chronological resume below it.

Who should use a combination resume?

It's the strongest choice for career changers, people with non-linear or fragmented histories, and senior candidates whose value is a specific mix of skills rather than one obvious job title. By leading with a skills block you steer the recruiter's eye to what's relevant for the new role, while the dated history reassures them that nothing is being hidden. If your career path is straightforward, a plain chronological resume is simpler and just as effective.

Is a combination resume ATS-friendly?

Yes, as long as you keep the dated work-history section complete. Applicant tracking systems parse job titles, employers, and dates into structured fields — the combination format keeps all of those, so it reads cleanly. The one rule is to use a single-column layout with standard section headings and real text (not a graphic), so the parser can find both your skills block and your timeline.

What is the difference between a combination and a functional resume?

Both lead with skills, but a functional resume drops or buries the dated work history, while a combination resume keeps it in full. That one difference is decisive: a functional resume with no timeline reads as evasive and triggers "what are they hiding?", whereas a combination resume gives the same skills-forward framing without the trust penalty. In almost every case where someone is tempted by a functional resume, a combination resume is the better answer.
