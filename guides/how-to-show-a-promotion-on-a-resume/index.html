How to show a promotion on a resume

A promotion is one of the best things a recruiter can see — proof someone already bet on you. But list it wrong and it reads like two short, unrelated jobs, or worse, like job-hopping. The fix is a formatting choice: stack the titles under one company, or split them into separate entries. Here's when to use each, how to phrase the move, and copy-ready examples.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Showing a promotion isn't about adding a sentence that says "I was promoted." It's about laying out your titles so the upward move is obvious in a two-second scan. Do that and you turn one employer into a visible growth story. The whole decision comes down to one question: should the roles share a company header, or each stand alone?

The decision: stacked vs. separate entries

There are two correct ways to show more than one role at the same employer. Picking the right one takes one look at how different the roles were.

✓ Stack the titles

  • The roles were related (Coordinator → Manager)
  • It was a clear promotion or step up
  • You want the progression to read at a glance
  • You want to save vertical space

✓ Split into two entries

  • The roles were genuinely different functions
  • e.g. moved from Support into Engineering
  • Each needs its own full set of bullets
  • The two jobs had little day-to-day overlap

Both are legitimate — this is a readability call, not a rule. When the move is a straightforward step up in the same lane, stack: it's tighter and the promotion is unmissable. When the second role was a different job that happened to be at the same place, split it so each can tell its own story. Either way, keep the format consistent with the rest of your work experience section.

The stacked format (most promotions)

Write the company name and your total tenure once. Under it, list each title with its own date range, newest first, and put the bullet points beneath the role they belong to. The reader sees one company block with a clear climb inside it.

Stacked — one company, two titles Northwind Logistics — Chicago, IL · 2021–Present
  • Operations Manager · 2023–Present
    • Promoted from Coordinator within 18 months after cutting dispatch errors 40%.
    • Manage a team of 9 and a $2.1M regional budget; hit on-time delivery of 98%.
  • Operations Coordinator · 2021–2023
    • Rebuilt the routing workflow, cutting average dispatch time from 22 to 13 minutes.
    • Trained 6 new coordinators on the updated process.

Notice the company and city appear once, the two titles each carry their own dates, and the newest role sits on top with the strongest bullets. That layout does the talking — you barely need to say "promoted" at all.

How to phrase the promotion itself

In most cases the stacked titles already prove it, so a dedicated sentence is optional. When you do add one, make it earn its place: name the move and the reason for it, in a single line, tied to a result.

Phrasing the move
  • "I was promoted to Manager." — states it, proves nothing
  • "Promoted from Coordinator to Manager within 18 months for consistently exceeding targets."
  • "Selected to lead the team after redesigning the process now used company-wide."

Lead with the accomplishment that earned the promotion, in the pronoun-free action-verb voice the rest of the resume uses. A promotion backed by a reason reads as merit; a bare "was promoted" reads as a formality.

The separate-entry format (very different roles)

When your second role was a different job — not a step up the same ladder — give each its own entry, repeating the company name. This lets each role carry a full, distinct set of bullets without the reader mentally untangling which achievement belongs to which job.

Separate — different functions at one company Software Engineer · Meridian Health · 2023–Present
  • Ship features on the patient-portal team; owned the appointment-booking rewrite.
Customer Support Specialist · Meridian Health · 2021–2023
  • Handled 40+ tickets/day; built the macros that cut average response time in half.

Here the two roles are different enough — Support and Engineering — that stacking them under one header would muddy the picture. Separate entries keep each clean. Still list them newest-first so the progression reads top to bottom, the same as any reverse-chronological resume.

Multiple promotions at one company

Three rising titles in one block is one of the strongest signals a resume can send. Stack them all under a single header, newest on top, each with dates. You don't owe every level a full set of bullets — load the detail onto your two most senior or most relevant roles and reduce earlier ones to a title-and-dates line.

Don't dilute a three-title climb with equal detail everywhere

Analyst → Senior Analyst → Team Lead is impressive on its own. Give Team Lead and Senior Analyst real bullets; the first Analyst line can be just the title and dates. Depth where it counts, brevity where it doesn't.

Lateral moves, reorgs, and title changes

Not every title change is a promotion, and that's fine. A lateral move into a new team, or a title that changed in a reorg, still belongs on the resume — just don't dress it up as a step up. Use the stacked format to show both titles honestly; if the change reflects broader scope, let a bullet say so ("Took on the EMEA region after the two teams merged"). Don't invent a promotion that didn't happen — it's the kind of thing that unravels in an interview. And if the title actually went down in that reorg, don't stack it — see how to explain a demotion on a resume, where the move is to consolidate rather than show the step.

Common mistakes

  • Splitting a real promotion into two disconnected jobs. Repeating the company name for a straight step-up wastes space and hides the progression. Stack it.
  • Stacking two genuinely different roles. If the jobs shared no day-to-day, one header forces the reader to guess which bullet belongs where. Split it.
  • Announcing the promotion without a reason. "Was promoted" is a fact; "promoted after cutting errors 40%" is a selling point. Always attach the why.
  • Repeating the same bullets under both titles. Each role should show what changed — more scope, bigger team, harder problems — not the same line twice.
  • Burying the newest title at the bottom. Always newest-first, so the highest role is the first thing read. See common resume mistakes.
The whole thing in one breath. Stack the titles under one company header when it's a real step up in the same lane — company and city once, each title with its own dates and bullets, newest on top. Split into separate entries only when the roles were genuinely different jobs. Let the layout prove the promotion; if you name it, tie it to the result that earned it — and load the detail onto your most senior roles.

Frequently asked questions

How do I show a promotion within the same company on a resume?

Use one company header with the roles stacked under it — company name and total dates once, then each title with its own dates, newest first. Put each role's bullets beneath it so the reader sees what you did at each level. This keeps the employer as a single block and makes the upward move obvious at a glance.

Should I list two roles at the same company as separate entries or stacked?

Stack them when the roles are related and it was a clear promotion — it reads as progression and saves space. Use two separate entries only when the roles were genuinely different functions (say, Support into Engineering), so each carries its own bullets without confusing the reader. When in doubt, stack: a visible promotion is a selling point you don't want to bury.

How do I phrase a promotion on a resume?

Let the stacked titles and dates do most of the work — the jump from "Coordinator" to "Manager" already signals it. If you want to name it, add one line under the senior role like "Promoted from Coordinator to Manager within 18 months for consistently exceeding targets." Keep it short and back it with the accomplishment that earned it.

How do I show multiple promotions at one company?

Stack all the titles under a single header, newest on top, each with its own dates. You don't need full bullets for every level — concentrate detail on your two most senior or relevant roles and keep earlier ones to a title-and-dates line. Three rising titles in one block is one of the strongest signals on a resume, so make the progression easy to read.
