How to list a startup on a resume

The instinct is to sweat the title and hide the fact that it didn't survive. The move is the opposite: list a startup by what you built and what it produced — even a dead one is real operator experience.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

You founded something — a real product, maybe a real team, maybe real revenue — and now you're staring at your resume unsure how to word it. Do you call yourself CEO? Do you admit it shut down? Do you leave it off so it doesn't look like you failed? The unlock is to stop treating a startup as a delicate thing to be explained and start treating it as what it is on a resume: a job, listed at full weight, judged by its outcome. A startup doesn't earn its place by the title on it or by whether it's still alive — it earns it by what you built from nothing, which is precisely the experience most candidates can't show. Once you list it that way, the title stops mattering, a shutdown stops being a confession, and the whole entry reads as the operator credential it actually is.

The core idea: a startup sits on the axis of outcome and operator experience, not title or survival. You took something from zero to one — shipped a product, chased customers, hired, raised or bootstrapped — and that range is what an employer is buying. So list it in Work Experience like any job, title yourself honestly, and let quantified traction (users, revenue, funding, team, growth) carry the weight. Describe the outcome truthfully whether it succeeded, is ongoing, or folded — a failed startup is still real experience — and preempt the one doubt an employer has about a founder: will this person actually be an employee?

List it like a job, because it is one

A startup you founded goes in your Work Experience section, formatted identically to any other role: company name, your title, dates, and a set of quantified bullets. There's no special "ventures" section, no asterisk, no framing device — the moment you give it its own weird treatment, you signal that even you aren't sure it counts. It counts. You ran a real thing, made real decisions, and produced real results, and the resume's job is to present that as the substantial experience it is. The only thing that makes a startup entry different from a corporate job is that you usually have better numbers to put in it, because you touched everything.

List the outcome, not the title

The title anxiety — "can I really call myself CEO?" — is a distraction, because the title is the least convincing thing on the line. What convinces a reader is the outcome. Founder or Co-Founder is accurate and reads fine; use it. What backfires is stacking a corporate C-suite title onto a tiny venture, because a "Founder & CEO" header on a two-person company with no product, customers, or revenue reads as padding and collapses under the first interview question. Keep the title plain and true, pair it with the functional hat you actually wore when that fits the job better, and let the bullets establish the real scope.

Reads as real

  • Founder — accurate, no inflation
  • Co-Founder & Lead Engineer — founder + the hat you wore
  • Bullets carry the weight: grew to 8k users, $14k MRR, hired 4
  • Dates that show the real arc — 2022–2024

Reads as padding

  • "Founder & CEO" on a venture with no product or users
  • "Chief Executive Officer" of a one-person, pre-revenue idea
  • A stack of C-suite titles (CEO/CTO/CPO) for the same tiny team
  • A title with no numbers underneath it to back the scope

Quantify it like a business, not a job description

This is where a startup entry wins or dies. A corporate bullet often describes a responsibility; a startup bullet should describe a result you produced from nothing, and you almost always have the numbers to do it. Reach for the metrics a founder actually moves — the same ones an investor would ask about. Turn duties into accomplishments and put numbers on them:

Founder metrics that land: users / customers (peak or growth) · revenue, MRR or ARR · funding raised — or that you bootstrapped to profitability · team size hired and managed · growth rate · product shipped (from zero) · key partnerships or channels. Even a modest number beats a vague claim: "Grew a two-sided marketplace to 8,000 users and $14k MRR, bootstrapped, before winding down in 2024" says more than any title.

Handle the outcome honestly — succeeded, ongoing, or folded

Every startup entry is in one of three states, and each has a clean move. The failed case is the one people get most wrong, so it gets the most attention here — but the principle is the same across all three: state the outcome truthfully and let the work speak.

It succeeded or was acquired

Say so, with numbers. "Acquired by [Company] in 2024" or "grew to $2M ARR and a team of 12" is the headline — put the outcome in the first bullet, because a good ending is the strongest signal on the page.

It's still running while you job-hunt

List it as current, then defuse the obvious doubt. A single summary line — "Founder transitioning to a senior team role to build [the role's core work] at scale" — reframes the move as a deliberate choice, not restlessness between ventures.

It folded

List it anyway, with closed dates. The dates ("2022–2024") already say it ended — you don't need "(failed)" or a post-mortem. Fill the bullets with what you built and the traction you reached; a wound-down startup is real operator experience, and hiding it just trades that experience for an unexplained gap.

The failed startup is the point, not the problem

The reflex after a startup dies is to bury it — leave it off, shrink it to one line, hope no one asks. That's exactly backwards. A startup that failed still means you shipped a product, found (or fought to find) customers, hired people, managed a burn rate, and made calls under real uncertainty — the 0-to-1 range that a candidate who's only ever worked inside a big company simply cannot demonstrate. Employers who value operators know this; many actively prefer a founder who's been through the fire. And there's a mechanical reason not to hide it: the time your startup occupied doesn't disappear from your timeline, so leaving the venture off doesn't erase the period — it just turns two years of the hardest work of your life into a blank gap you now have to explain away. The startup is the explanation. Put it on the page.

Preempt the one doubt every employer has about a founder

A hiring manager reading "Founder" has one worry, and it's not whether you can do the work — it's whether you'll actually take the job as an employee or bolt the moment you want to start something again. This is the same doubt a freelancer or business owner has to answer ("will this person really be an employee?"), and you answer it the same way: not by hiding the founder experience, but by framing it as capability the company gains, and by signaling in your summary that you're choosing a team role on purpose. "Proven 0-to-1 operator moving into a senior IC role" reads as a deliberate, valuable choice; a founder title with no such signal reads as a flight risk. Say the quiet part out loud, once, and let the rest of the resume prove you deliver.

The bright line: don't inflate a dabble into a venture

A "startup" needs to have actually existed as one. If the thing had no product, no users, no revenue, and no real activity — an idea you registered an LLC for and a landing page you never launched — then "Founder" on it is padding, and it collapses under one interview question ("tell me about your customers"). That's not a startup on your resume; it's a project, and listing it honestly as one is stronger than a borrowed title on an empty venture. The rule cuts both ways: a real venture, however small or short-lived, is a genuine asset you should list at full weight — and a title with nothing behind it costs more credibility than it buys.

A startup isn't the same question as these

"Working for yourself" covers several different situations, and they don't get listed the same way. Know which one you're actually describing.

The situationWhat it's really about — and the resume move
A startup you foundedA venture you built beyond your own labor — a product or company with traction, judged by outcome. List it in Work Experience, title honestly, quantify like a business, and state the outcome truthfully even if it folded.
Self-employment / a business you ownSelling your own services as the product — consultant, sole proprietor, agency-of-one. A related but different framing; see how to list self-employment.
Freelance or contract workProject-based or fixed-term client work — the question is scope and structure, not traction. See freelance work and contract work.
A side project with no real activityAn idea without a product, users, or revenue — not a job. List it as a project, honestly, rather than titling it "Founder."
An employment gapAn unexplained stretch of time — which the startup, if it ran during that period, is exactly the entry that fills. See employment gaps.

The through-line: a startup is an outcome question, so the resume's job is to show what you built and what it produced — which means listing it at full weight, quantifying it like a business, and telling the truth about how it ended.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode isn't that your startup was small or that it died — it's treating the entry as something to inflate or something to hide. Listed as "here's what I built and what it produced," it's an operator credential; listed as a borrowed title or buried out of embarrassment, it's either padding or a gap.

Reads as an operator

  • Founder + quantified traction (users, revenue, funding, team)
  • A folded startup listed with closed dates and results, no apology
  • Outcome stated plainly — acquired, bootstrapped to $14k MRR, wound down 2024
  • A summary line signaling a deliberate move into a team role

Reads as padding or a gap

  • "Founder & CEO" on an empty, pre-product venture
  • A real startup left off entirely, leaving an unexplained gap
  • A title with no numbers to back the scope
  • "(failed)" or a post-mortem where clean closed dates would do

A startup is listed by what you built and what it produced, not by its title or whether it survived. Put it in Work Experience at full weight, title yourself honestly and let the traction numbers carry it, state the outcome truthfully in all three states, and don't trade a real venture — even a dead one — for a gap. Do that and "I founded a startup" stops being a line you're unsure how to word and becomes the strongest evidence on the page that you can build something from nothing.

Quick reference

  • List it like a job → Work Experience, company name, title, dates, quantified bullets — no special "ventures" section.
  • Outcome over title → Founder is fine; let users, revenue, funding, and team size carry the weight, not a C-suite label.
  • Quantify like a business → users, MRR/ARR, funding, growth, team — the metrics an investor would ask about.
  • Failed? List it anyway → closed dates say it ended; fill bullets with what you built, and never trade it for a gap.
  • Preempt the doubt → a summary line signaling a deliberate move into a team role answers "will this person really be an employee?"
  • Don't inflate a dabble → no product, users, or revenue means it's a project, not a "Founder" title.

Frequently asked questions

How do you list a startup you founded on a resume?

List it in your Work Experience section like any real job, because it is one. Give it the company name, an honest title (Founder or Co-Founder is accurate), the dates, and a set of quantified bullets — but let the numbers, not the title, carry the weight. A startup sits on the axis of outcome and operator experience, so the bullets should read like a business report: users or customers reached, revenue or MRR/ARR, funding raised or the fact that you bootstrapped, team size you hired and managed, and growth over the period. What an employer is buying is your proof that you can take something from zero to one and wear every hat, which is exactly the range a corporate-only candidate can't show. Format it identically to any other role; the only thing that makes a startup different is that you have unusually good numbers to quantify — so use them.

How do you put a failed startup on a resume?

You list it — you do not hide it, apologize for it, or trade it for an unexplained gap. A startup that folded is still real operator experience: you shipped a product, chased customers, hired, and made the calls, and none of that experience evaporates because the company later closed. Give it a company name, your title, and closed dates ("2022–2024"), and frame the ending as a completed venture rather than a confession — the dates already say it ended, so you don't need "(failed)" or a post-mortem on the page. Fill the bullets with what you built and the traction you reached before it wound down (peak users, revenue, funding, team), and if a line helps, frame the shutdown forward: what you learned and how it makes you a stronger operator now. The one thing that turns a failed startup into a liability is leaving it off and letting the time it occupied read as a blank hole in your history; the venture is precisely the thing that fills that period.

What title should I use for my own startup on a resume?

Use the true one, and let the bullets do the convincing rather than the title. "Founder" or "Co-Founder" is accurate and reads fine — you did found it. The trap is stacking a corporate C-suite title onto a tiny venture: "Founder & CEO" of a two-person company with no product, customers, or revenue reads as padding, and it collapses under the first interview question ("tell me about your customers"). If the title would overstate the reality, keep it plain ("Founder") and let quantified bullets — users, revenue, funding, team — establish the scope honestly. You can also pair the founder title with the functional hat you actually wore ("Founder & Lead Engineer", "Co-Founder & Head of Growth") when that maps better to the job you're applying for. The rule is simply that the title has to match what actually happened; a borrowed title on an empty venture costs more credibility than it buys.

Is listing a startup the same as listing self-employment or freelance work?

No — they sit on different axes, even though both are "you worked for yourself." Self-employment and freelancing are about selling your own services or hours as the product: you're a consultant, a contractor, or a sole proprietor, and the resume's job is to make that read as a real role and answer the doubt "will this person actually be an employee?" A startup is about building a product or company beyond your own labor — it has traction, possibly a team, possibly funding, and it lives or dies on outcomes rather than billable hours. That difference changes what you quantify (users, revenue, funding, growth versus clients and projects) and it puts the failed/exited/ongoing dimension at the center, which freelancing doesn't have. If what you actually did was sell your own services, use the self-employment or freelance framing; if you built a venture, use this one. And if the "startup" was really a side project with no product or activity, it's a project, not a job — list it as a project.

How do I show I'm serious about a job when I'm still running my startup?

Preempt the one doubt an employer has about hiring a founder: that you'll take the job as a placeholder and bolt the moment you want to start something again. List the startup as a current role (or with an end date if you're genuinely winding it down), then use a single summary line to signal the move is deliberate — "Founder transitioning to a senior IC/leadership role to build [the role's core work] at scale" reframes it from restlessness to a clear choice. Frame the founder experience itself as capability an employer gains — ownership, range, comfort with ambiguity — rather than a flight risk. Keep the bullets about what you built and delivered, not about your ambitions for the next thing. The goal is to make a hiring manager see a proven operator choosing their team on purpose, not a founder killing time between ventures.
