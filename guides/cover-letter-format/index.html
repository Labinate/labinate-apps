Cover letter format: layout, spacing, and what goes where

The words matter most, but a badly formatted letter gets skimmed and skipped. Here's the correct structure for 2026 — header, greeting, body, sign-off, and the spacing and font that make it look like a professional set with your resume.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A cover letter has a format the same way a business letter does — a predictable order of parts a reader expects to find, so nothing makes them stop and hunt. Get the layout right and the letter reads as effortless; get it wrong — a wall of text, an address block from 2005, double spacing that pushes it to two pages — and the content never gets a fair read.

This is the modern, screen-first format: what each section contains, in what order, and the exact spacing, font and margins to use. If you want the wording itself, read how to write a cover letter and the cover letter examples; this guide is purely about the shape.

The seven parts, in order

  1. Your header — name, email, phone, and city. On an emailed or uploaded letter this is one or two tidy lines at the top; you no longer need a full postal address.
  2. The date — a single line, spelled out (e.g. 5 July 2026), below the header.
  3. The greeting — Dear [First Last] or Dear Ms./Mr. [Last] when you can find the name; Dear Hiring Manager when you truly can't. Never "To Whom It May Concern."
  4. The opening paragraph — the hook: name the role and give one specific reason you're writing. No "I am writing to apply for…".
  5. The body — one or two short paragraphs of proof: achievements from your resume, told as short stories with a number, chosen to match the job.
  6. The closing paragraph — a confident, low-pressure ask for a conversation, plus a thank-you.
  7. The sign-off — Sincerely, or Best regards, then your name.

Spacing, font and margins

  • Length: one page, always. Half a page that makes one sharp point beats a full page that hedges.
  • Spacing: single-spaced within paragraphs, with one blank line between each block — header, date, greeting, each paragraph, sign-off. Do not double-space the body.
  • Font: the same font as your resume, so the two look like a set. A clean 10.5–12 pt serif or sans-serif — Calibri, Georgia, Helvetica, Arial. See the resume font and size guide for specifics.
  • Margins: 1 inch on all sides; 0.75 inch is the tightest you should go if you're squeezing.
  • Alignment: left-aligned body, ragged right. Do not justify — it opens ugly gaps between words.
  • File: export to PDF unless the posting asks for Word, and name it Firstname-Lastname-Cover-Letter.pdf. (Same logic as PDF or Word for your resume.)
Match it to the resume, deliberately. The single fastest way to look polished is to give the letter the same header style, font and margins as your resume. When a recruiter opens both, they should feel like two pages of one document — not two files from two different people.

Email vs. uploaded vs. attached

The format flexes slightly by how you send it:

  • Pasted into the email body: drop the header block and date — the email already carries your name and the timestamp. Start at the greeting, and use the subject line as your headline (Cover letter — Jordan Rivera, Product Designer).
  • Uploaded to an application portal: use the full formatted layout above and export to PDF. This is the most common case in 2026.
  • Attached to an email: full layout in the attachment, plus two or three friendly sentences in the email body so the message isn't blank.

Common formatting mistakes

  • Double spacing. It reads as padding and pushes a short letter to a second page. Single-space with blank lines between blocks.
  • An old postal address block. A full employer mailing address is optional now. Keep your own contact line and the date; drop the rest for screen-read letters.
  • A different font from the resume. Mismatched fonts make the pair look thrown together. Match them.
  • Justified text. It creates rivers of whitespace. Left-align, ragged right.
  • Spilling to two pages. If it doesn't fit one page at 1-inch margins and 11 pt, the letter is too long — cut, don't shrink the font to 9 pt.
  • "To Whom It May Concern." A formatting-era relic. Find a name; fall back to "Dear Hiring Manager."

Frequently asked questions

What is the correct format for a cover letter?

A header with your contact details and the date, a greeting to a named person where possible, three or four short body paragraphs (hook, proof, close), and a simple sign-off with your name. Keep it to one page, single-spaced, in the same font as your resume with 1-inch margins.

Should a cover letter be single or double spaced?

Single-spaced within paragraphs, with a blank line between them. Don't double-space the body — it pushes a short letter onto a second page and reads as padding. A single blank line between the greeting, each paragraph, and the sign-off is all the whitespace you need.

What font and size should a cover letter use?

Use the same font as your resume so the two documents look like a set — a clean 10.5 to 12 point serif or sans-serif such as Calibri, Georgia, Helvetica or Arial. Match the font, size and margins to your resume and the pair reads as one considered application.

Do you need an address block on a cover letter in 2026?

A full old-style employer mailing address is optional now that letters are read on screen. Always include your own name, email and phone (and city) in the header; the employer address block is fine to drop for an emailed or uploaded letter. Keep the date and the greeting.
