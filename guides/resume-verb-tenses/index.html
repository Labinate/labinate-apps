Resume tenses: past or present?

It sounds like a grammar nitpick, but tense is one of the fastest tells of a careless resume. A bullet that flips from "managed" to "managing" to "manage" inside one job makes a recruiter slow down for the wrong reason. The good news: there's a single rule that resolves almost every case, and once you know it, the whole document falls into line.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The confusion comes from treating "resume tense" as one decision when it's really two questions asked of every line: is this thing still happening, or is it finished? Answer that, and the tense is decided for you. Everything below is just that rule applied to the places people trip over it.

The one rule

Say it once and apply it everywhere: present tense for what you're still doing, past tense for what's done. That's the whole system.

✓ Present tense

  • Ongoing duties in your current job
  • Your summary ("Product manager who…")
  • Skills and tools you actively use

Past tense

  • Every previous job, top to bottom
  • Finished projects and results — even in your current role
  • Anything with a completion date

Notice the second column isn't styled as a mistake — past tense is right for most of a resume. Present tense is the exception, reserved for the handful of things you're literally still doing. If you're unsure about a line, ask whether you could stop today and it would still be true. "Reduced churn 20%" stays true forever, so it's past. "Manage the analytics team" stops being true the day you leave, so it's present.

Your current job is the tricky one — and it's supposed to be mixed

The single most common tense question is "what tense should my current job be in?" The answer surprises people: both. A current role naturally splits into two kinds of lines, and each takes its own tense.

  • Ongoing responsibilities → present. The things you do every week and will keep doing until you leave. "Lead a team of five." "Own the release pipeline."
  • Completed accomplishments → past. Specific wins you've already banked, even though the job continues. "Cut deploy time from 30 to 8 minutes." "Shipped the billing rewrite two weeks early."

This mix isn't sloppy — it's exactly right, and it's what separates a strong entry from a weak one. A current role written entirely in present tense reads like a copied job description: here's what the role involves. Adding past-tense accomplishments turns it into evidence: here's what I've actually delivered. The tense difference is doing real work for you.

Current role — mixed tense done right Senior Marketing Manager · Northwind Co · 2023 – Present
  • Manage a 4-person content team and own the paid-search budget. (present — ongoing)
  • Grew organic traffic 3× in 18 months by rebuilding the blog taxonomy. (past — done)
  • Launched the referral program that now drives 15% of signups. (past — done)

Previous jobs: all past, no exceptions

Every role you've left is finished, so every line in it is past tense — duties and accomplishments alike. There's no "but I did that for years" exception; you're not doing it now, so it's past. This is the easy half of the rule, and the place to double-check first when you paste an old bullet into a new resume, because stray present-tense verbs love to hide in roles you copied from an earlier version.

Previous role — everything past tense Marketing Coordinator · Acme Media · 2020 – 2023
  • Ran the monthly newsletter for a 40k-subscriber list.
  • Coordinated six product launches with cross-functional teams.
  • Increased email click-through 25% through subject-line testing.

Consistency beats correctness

If you take one thing from this page, make it this: a reader forgives an odd tense call far faster than a mixed one. The jarring thing isn't past-vs-present — it's flipping between them inside a single entry for no reason. Pick the tense each line deserves, then read the entry top to bottom and make sure nothing wobbles.

The trap: half-updated bullets

You get promoted or change roles, copy your old bullets up, and forget to convert the verbs. Now your current job says "Managed the team" (past) next to "Own the budget" (present) — and it reads like you don't know which job you're in. After any edit, re-scan the entry and align every verb to the still-doing / done test.

Skip the -ing openings

A separate tense trap: starting bullets with a gerund — "Managing a team of six," "Leading the migration." Grammatically it's a present participle, but on a resume it just reads as passive and vague, and it dodges the tense decision entirely. Open with a real action verb instead, and the tense comes along for free.

✕ -ing opening

  • Managing a team of six engineers
  • Responsible for the quarterly budget
  • Working on customer retention

✓ Action verb

  • Manage a team of six engineers (present)
  • Own the quarterly budget (present)
  • Lifted retention 12% (past result)

"Responsible for" deserves its own callout: it's not a tense so much as a non-verb, and it makes every line under it flabby. Swap it for the verb it's hiding — "Responsible for reporting" becomes "Produce weekly reporting" — and you fix the tense and the bullet strength in one move.

What tense is the summary?

Your summary (or headline) is the one section that's present tense regardless of your history, because it describes who you are right now, not a job you held. Write it in an implied first person — present tense, no "I":

Summary — present tense, pronoun-free Product manager with 8 years shipping B2B SaaS. Drives roadmap from research to launch; pairs data with customer interviews to prioritize. Led three 0→1 products to profitability.

Note the last clause slips into past tense ("Led") for a specific finished win — same rule as everywhere else. The default frame is present ("drives," "pairs"), with past reserved for the concrete accomplishment. For more on that voice, see how to describe yourself on a resume.

Quick reference

  • Current job, ongoing duty → present ("Manage," "Lead," "Own").
  • Current job, finished result → past ("Increased," "Launched," "Cut").
  • Any previous job → past, every line.
  • Summary / headline → present, no pronoun.
  • Never → start a bullet with "-ing" or "Responsible for."
  • Always → keep each entry internally consistent; re-scan after every edit.
The whole thing in one breath. If you're still doing it, present tense; if it's done, past tense. That makes your current job a deliberate mix — present-tense duties, past-tense wins — and every previous job entirely past. Write the summary in present tense with no "I," never open a bullet with "-ing" or "Responsible for," and after any edit read each entry through once to be sure the verbs don't wobble. Consistency is what the reader actually notices.

Frequently asked questions

Should a resume be in past or present tense?

Both, depending on the item. Use present tense for responsibilities you still perform in your current job ("Manage a team of six"), and past tense for anything that is finished — every previous job, and any completed accomplishment even within your current role ("Launched the mobile app, cutting churn 20%"). The simple rule: if you're still doing it, present tense; if it's done, past tense.

What tense should your current job be in on a resume?

Mixed, and that's correct. Ongoing duties go in present tense ("Lead weekly planning; own the reporting pipeline"), but specific results you already delivered go in past tense ("Reduced load time 40%; hired and onboarded four engineers"). A current role that's all present tense reads like a job description; adding past-tense accomplishments is what makes it read like a track record.

Should the resume summary be in past or present tense?

Present tense, written in an implied first person with no "I." A summary describes who you are now: "Product manager with 8 years shipping B2B SaaS; drives roadmap from research to launch." Keep the pronoun out and the tense present. It's the one section where present tense is the default regardless of your work history.

Can you start resume bullets with -ing words like "managing"?

Avoid it. Gerund openings ("Managing…", "Leading…") read as passive and weaken the line. Start with a strong action verb instead — present tense for current duties ("Manage…", "Lead…") or past tense for finished work ("Managed…", "Led…"). The verb form carries the tense, so once you pick the right verb, the tense takes care of itself.
