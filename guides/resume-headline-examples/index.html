Resume headline examples

The one line directly under your name is the most-read line on your resume — and the most wasted. Done right, it labels you as a match in a single glance and pulls the recruiter into the rest. Here's what a headline is, how it differs from a summary, 25 copy-ready examples by role, and a formula for writing your own.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A resume headline — sometimes called a resume title — is a short, title-style line placed just under your name and contact details. It states who you are professionally in a few words: usually your role plus one or two qualifiers that set you apart. It is not a sentence. It's a label, like a headline on an article, and its whole job is to make the reader want the next paragraph.

Headline vs. summary vs. objective

These three get confused constantly. They sit in the same top-of-page real estate but do different jobs — and you can use more than one.

What it isLength
HeadlineA title-style label under your nameOne line
SummaryYour strongest proof and direction, in prose2–4 sentences
ObjectiveWhat you're seeking (better for entry-level / career change)1–2 sentences

The strongest resumes often pair a headline with a summary: the headline is the hook, the summary is the follow-through. For the full breakdown of the two paragraph styles, see resume objective vs. summary and resume summary examples.

A formula for your headline

Almost every good headline is built from the same three parts. Fill in the ones that apply and drop the rest:

[Job title] + [specialty or industry] + [proof: years, scale, or a signature result]
Example: Senior Data Analyst + fintech, SQL & Python + 6+ years turning data into decisions.

Separate the segments with a pipe ( | ) or a middot so it scans as distinct chunks, not a run-on. Keep it to one line — roughly 8–14 words. If it wraps to a second line, you've drifted into summary territory.

How to write yours in three steps

Step 1
Start with your target job title

Use the title of the role you're applying for, where it's truthful — "Product Marketing Manager," not a vague internal label. This puts the exact term the recruiter and the ATS are looking for at the very top. Tailor it to each posting.

Step 2
Add one or two qualifiers that separate you

A specialty, an industry, key tools, or a credential — whatever makes you the specific candidate, not a generic one. "Nurse" becomes "Registered Nurse | Critical Care & ER." Pull the qualifiers that matter from the job's keywords.

Step 3
End with proof, if you have room

A number, a span of experience, or a signature win turns a label into a claim: "6+ years," "$40M P&L," "3× pipeline growth." Keep the whole thing to one line. Save the fuller story for your summary.

Frequently asked questions

What is a resume headline?

A resume headline is a single line under your name that states who you are professionally — usually your title plus one or two defining qualifiers, like "Senior Data Analyst | SQL & Python | 6+ Years in Fintech." It's the first thing a recruiter reads and it frames everything below it, so it should instantly signal which role you fit.

What is the difference between a resume headline and a summary?

A headline is one line — a title-style label under your name. A summary is a 2–4 sentence paragraph below it that expands with your strongest proof and direction. The headline grabs attention in a glance; the summary earns the next thirty seconds. Strong resumes often use both.

How long should a resume headline be?

One line — roughly 8 to 14 words, or under about 100 characters, fitting on a single line without wrapping. If it needs a second line, you've written a summary. Use a pipe ( | ) to separate two or three punchy segments rather than a full sentence.

Should I put a headline on my resume?

In most cases, yes. It gives the recruiter an instant frame for who you are and puts your target job title high on the page where the ATS and reader both see it first. It's especially useful when targeting a specific role or changing careers. The main exception is a very entry-level resume with no clear title, where an objective may serve better.

Should a resume headline match the job title in the posting?

Where it's truthful, yes. Mirroring the exact target title helps the recruiter and the applicant tracking system recognize you as a match. Tailor the headline to each application so the first line reflects the role you're actually applying for.
