How to list study abroad on a resume

A semester or year abroad feels like a big line — it was a big part of your life. But on a resume it's a small, precise one, and the mistake almost everyone makes is treating it like a second degree. It isn't. Study abroad is an experience that proves adaptability, cross-cultural fluency, and often a language — and it belongs as a sub-line under your degree, framed around those skills. Here's exactly where it goes and how to write it.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Study abroad sits in an awkward spot on the page. It's part of your education, but it's not a degree. It taught you things worth listing, but not as its own credential. It's tempting to give it a whole entry — a second university, a second line with a foreign name — and that's exactly the wrong move, because it implies a qualification you never earned. The right approach is smaller and sharper: anchor it to the degree it counted toward, then frame it around the transferable skills a hiring manager actually values. We'll cover where it goes, how to write the line, when a program earns more than a sub-line, and the honesty rules that keep it credible.

The core idea: study abroad is an experience, not a second degree. In almost every case it's a single sub-line under your home degree in the education section — host university, city/country, term, and what you studied. Include it for the skills it proves (adaptability, cross-cultural communication, language), not the travel — and cut it when the page is tight and the term is old or irrelevant.

First: it's an experience, not a second degree

The single most common study-abroad mistake is a formatting one that becomes an honesty one. Listed as its own education entry — its own institution line, its own dates — a one-semester exchange starts to look like a degree from a foreign university. It isn't, and an interviewer or background check will notice the gap between what the line implies and what actually happened. Anchor it to the real credential instead:

How it's listedWhat the reader concludes
Sub-line under your degree"They spent a term abroad as part of their degree." Accurate, tidy, and it credits the experience without overstating it.
Its own education entry"They have a qualification from this foreign university." Implies a degree you didn't earn — a checkable overstatement.
Buried in a travel or "interests" line"They went somewhere." Loses all the transferable-skill signal that makes it worth listing at all.

Your degree is the credential; the abroad term is a component of it. So it lives beneath the degree entry, the same way relevant coursework or an honors line does — part of the story of that degree, not a second one.

The anatomy of a clean study-abroad line

Once you know it goes under education, the line itself is short and consistent: name the host university, the place, the term, and what you studied. Skills-first framing does the rest.

The standard sub-line

Study abroad: Universidad de Salamanca, Spain — Spring 2024
Coursework in Spanish literature and economics. Clean, honest, and clearly a component of the degree above it — not a separate qualification.

Frame it around the skill, not the trip

Don't write "spent a semester in Japan." Write what it proves: "completed a semester of coursework in Tokyo, working in a second language and adapting to a new academic culture." The reader who's never traveled should still see why it matters — describe the competence, not the adventure.

Name the language honestly

If the term genuinely moved your language level, say so at the level you can defend in an interview — "conversational Spanish" or "intermediate," not "fluent" for a single conversational semester. An overstated proficiency is the fastest way to get caught.

When there's real work, let it graduate

If the program included a research project, internship, fieldwork, or a capstone, that concrete outcome can move up into experience or projects as a full quantified bullet on its own merits — while the study term stays as a sub-line under education.

The one decision: keep it or cut it?

Study abroad is worth including only when it adds relevant signal — and that changes as your career grows. Match it to where you are:

Keep it

  • You're a student or recent grad with a thin page to fill
  • The role values international, cross-cultural, or client-facing skills
  • It produced a language you can honestly claim
  • It included real work — research, an internship, fieldwork

Cut it

  • You're mid-career or senior with stronger, more recent lines
  • The term is old, brief, and unrelated to the job
  • The page is tight and it's crowding out real experience
  • It only says "I traveled" with no transferable thread

The deciding question is relevance to this job, not how meaningful the experience was to you — the same editorial discipline that governs how far back a resume should go and what earns a spot on the page. An irrelevant semester abroad on a crowded senior resume isn't a lie of omission when you drop it; it's just editing.

The honesty rules

Study abroad is easy to list honestly and easy to overstate, and the overstatements are all checkable. Stay on the right side of each:

Honest and precise

  • Study abroad, one semester, anchored to your home degree
  • Language at the level you can hold up: conversational / intermediate
  • A program project listed as the real work it was

A claim the record contradicts

  • "Degree from [foreign university]" for an exchange term
  • "Fluent" after one conversational semester
  • Stretching dates to imply a full year abroad
  • Listing it as a second institution to pad the page

Stated accurately, study abroad works entirely in your favor. It's real evidence of independence and cross-cultural competence, and framed around those skills it strengthens an early-career page. The only failure mode is dressing a study term up as something bigger than it was — a foreign degree, a fluency you don't have — because every one of those claims is verifiable, and the same honesty logic that governs an unfinished degree governs this: state exactly what's true right now.

Quick reference

  • It's an experience, not a degree — list it as a sub-line under your home degree, never as its own education entry.
  • Standard format → host university, city/country, term, and what you studied. Study abroad: Sciences Po, Paris — Fall 2023, comparative politics.
  • Frame the skill, not the trip → adaptability, cross-cultural communication, language — what a hiring manager who's never traveled still values.
  • Let real work graduate → a research project, internship, or fieldwork can move up into experience or projects as its own bullet.
  • Keep or cut on relevance → worth it for students and cross-cultural roles; cut an old, unrelated term from a crowded senior page.
  • Never overstate → not a foreign degree, not "fluent" from a conversational semester — every overstatement is checkable.

Keep sharpening the resume

With study abroad placed, tighten the rest of the education section and the page around it: list your education and relevant coursework at the right depth, state languages honestly, turn program work into quantified achievements, and — if you're early-career — see how to build a strong resume with little experience. If your time abroad was a full year outside a degree program — travel, working, or volunteering — that's a gap year, listed by its activity rather than as academic credit. Frequently asked questions

Where do you put study abroad on a resume?

In almost every case, study abroad belongs as a single sub-line under your home degree in the education section — not as its own separate education entry. Your degree is the credential; the abroad term is a component of it, so it reads as "B.A. in International Relations, University of Michigan, 2025" with a line beneath it: "Study abroad: Sciences Po, Paris, France — Fall 2023, coursework in comparative politics and French." The exception is when the program produced something concrete and relevant — a research project, an internship, fieldwork, a capstone, or genuine language proficiency. That concrete outcome can move up into your experience, projects, or skills section as a real bullet on its own merits, while the study term itself stays under education. The one placement mistake to avoid is listing the host university as though it were a second degree-granting institution, which implies a qualification you didn't earn.

How do you describe study abroad on a resume?

Describe it by the transferable skills it proves, not by the experience of living somewhere new. A term abroad is evidence of adaptability, independence, cross-cultural communication, and often a working second language — and those are the qualities a hiring manager for a global, client-facing, or collaborative role is actually scanning for. So instead of "Spent a semester in Japan," write what it demonstrates: "Completed a semester of coursework in Tokyo, navigating academic work in a second language and adapting to a new professional culture." If the program included a project, internship, or fieldwork, describe that as a quantified achievement the way you would any other experience: what you did, in what context, and what came of it. The framing test is simple — would this line matter to someone who has never traveled and only cares whether you can do the job? Write it so the answer is yes.

Should you put study abroad on a resume?

Include it when it adds relevant signal and leave it off when it doesn't. It earns its place when you're a student or recent graduate with a thin page, when the role values international or cross-cultural competence, when it produced a language you can honestly claim, or when it involved concrete work like research or an internship. It stops earning its place as you gain years of real experience: on a mid-career or senior resume, a distant, unrelated semester abroad is a line that a stronger, more recent accomplishment should own instead. The deciding question is relevance to this job, not how meaningful the experience was to you — the same editorial discipline that governs how far back a resume should go and what belongs on a crowded page. When in doubt for an early-career resume, keep it as a one-line sub-entry; when the page is tight and it adds nothing the employer cares about, cut it.

Can you list a study abroad program as a degree?

No — and doing so is a misrepresentation that can unravel in a background check. A semester or year abroad is a component of your home degree; you did not earn a separate qualification from the host university, so listing it as "Degree, [Foreign University]" or in a way that implies you graduated from it is dishonest. Cite it as what it was: study abroad, an exchange term, or an international program, always anchored to the home degree it counted toward. The same honesty rule applies to language: a conversational semester abroad does not make you "fluent," and claiming a proficiency level you can't sustain in an interview is a checkable overstatement. State exactly what's true — "study abroad, one semester" and "conversational" or "intermediate" rather than "fluent" — and the credential works entirely in your favor.

How do you list study abroad if it's not related to the job?

If it's genuinely unrelated and the page has other, stronger lines competing for space, the honest answer is often to leave it off — an irrelevant semester abroad is not a lie of omission, it's editing. But before cutting it, look for the transferable thread, because "unrelated" is frequently just "not framed for this job yet." A study term almost always produced something a hiring manager values regardless of field: adaptability to an unfamiliar environment, self-management far from your support network, communication across a language or cultural gap, or a demonstrated ability to perform under new conditions. If one of those maps to the role, keep a single tight sub-line under education framed around that skill. If none does and space is scarce, drop it and give the room to a line that speaks directly to the job. Relevance to the reader is the only test that matters.
