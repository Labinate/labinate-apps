ATS Resume Checklist: 12 rules to pass applicant tracking systems in 2026

Most resumes never fail on merit — they fail because a parser misreads them. Here is a practical, recruiter-tested checklist to make sure a human actually sees your experience.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

An applicant tracking system (ATS) is the software almost every mid-to-large employer uses to collect and search job applications. The myth is that it "auto-rejects" you. The reality is quieter and more fixable: the ATS parses your file into structured fields — name, titles, dates, skills — and if that parse is wrong, your experience becomes unsearchable. A recruiter filtering for "React" or "supply chain" simply never surfaces you.

Everything below is about making your resume machine-readable first, beautiful second — without the gimmicks (invisible keywords, fake white text) that modern parsers ignore and recruiters resent.

The 12-point checklist

  • Use a single-column layout. Many parsers read straight across the page, so a two-column design can splice your sidebar into your job history. One column, top to bottom, is the safest structure.
  • Export a text-based PDF. A PDF from a document editor keeps your layout everywhere and stays fully readable. Avoid image-based PDFs (scans, design-tool exports) — the ATS can't read text off a picture. If a posting asks for .docx, give it .docx.
  • Keep standard section headings. "Work Experience", "Education", "Skills" — not "Where I've Made an Impact". Parsers map your content by recognising conventional labels.
  • Mirror the job description's language. Use the exact nouns and skills from the posting where they're genuinely true of you — usually 8–15 role-specific terms, woven into your bullets and a skills list.
  • Put dates in a consistent MM/YYYY format, aligned with each role. Inconsistent or graphical date ranges are a common parsing failure.
  • Skip headers, footers and text boxes for anything important. Some parsers ignore header/footer regions entirely, so a phone number or title placed there can vanish.
  • Drop tables, columns and graphics for core content. Charts, skill "rating bars" and icons carry no text a parser can index. Say "Advanced Excel", not a four-fifths-filled circle.
  • Use a common, embedded font (Arial, Calibri, Georgia, Helvetica). Exotic fonts can render as unreadable glyphs after parsing.
  • Spell out then abbreviate — "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)" — so you match recruiters searching either term.
  • Lead bullets with strong verbs and quantified results: "Cut onboarding time 40% by automating account setup." Metrics are what a human keeps reading for.
  • Name the file clearly: Firstname-Lastname-Resume.pdf. It's what the recruiter sees in their download folder.
  • Never hide keywords in white-on-white text or tiny fonts. Parsers strip formatting and flag it; recruiters read the plain text and it reads as dishonest.
Quick self-test. Open your PDF, press Ctrl/Cmd+A to select all, copy it, and paste into a plain text editor. If the text comes out clean, in reading order, with your headings intact — an ATS will parse it well. If it's scrambled, empty, or out of order, that's exactly what the parser sees.

Frequently asked questions

What is an ATS and why does it reject resumes?

An ATS is software employers use to collect, parse and search applications. Resumes are rarely rejected automatically — a parser reads your file into structured fields, and if it misreads them (from columns, tables, graphics or an image-based PDF) your experience becomes unsearchable and a recruiter never finds you for the role.

Is a PDF or Word document better for ATS?

A text-based PDF exported from a document editor is safe with every modern ATS and preserves your layout everywhere. Avoid image-based PDFs, which the parser can't read. When a job posting explicitly asks for .docx, follow the instruction.

How many keywords should I add?

Mirror the exact nouns and skills from the job description where they're genuinely true of you — typically 8 to 15 role-specific terms woven naturally into your bullets and a skills section. Never use hidden or white-on-white text.

Do columns and tables break the ATS?

They can. Many parsers read left-to-right across the whole page, so a two-column layout can interleave your sidebar into your job history. A single-column layout with standard headings is the safest choice.
