How to email a resume so it actually gets opened

You've written the resume — now for the last, easily-fumbled step. Here's the subject line that gets found, the three-sentence email body that gets read, and the file to attach so nothing breaks on the other end.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Emailing a resume feels trivial, which is exactly why so many get it wrong: a blank email with a mystery Resume(3).docx attached, no subject line, no name in the file. The email is the envelope, and a sloppy envelope makes a strong resume look careless before anyone opens it. This guide covers the three things that matter — the subject line, the body, and the file — and gives you copy-ready text for each.

1. The subject line

The subject line has one job: make the email findable in a full inbox and instantly clear about what it is. Name the role and yourself.

Subject lines that work

Application: Product Designer — Jordan Rivera

Product Designer (Req 4821) — Jordan Rivera

Referred by Sam Lee — Product Designer application, Jordan Rivera

  • Always include the exact role title as written in the posting — recruiters filter and search by it.
  • Add the job ID or requisition number if there is one; it routes your email to the right pipeline.
  • Lead with a referral if you have one — a name in the subject line is the single biggest boost to an open.
  • Never send a blank subject, just "Resume", or "Hi" — those get lost or ignored.

2. The email body

Short, specific, and never blank. Three or four sentences is all you need — the resume does the heavy lifting.

A resume email that reads well

Dear Ms. Alvarez,

I'm applying for the Product Designer role (Req 4821) I saw on your careers page. I've spent the last four years shipping design systems for B2B SaaS teams, most recently cutting a checkout flow's drop-off by 22%, and I'd love to bring that to Acme.

My resume is attached as a PDF. Thank you for your time, and I'm happy to share work samples or references whenever useful.

Best regards,
Jordan Rivera · (555) 019-4472

  • Greet a named person where you can find one; "Dear Hiring Manager" only when you truly can't.
  • Say the role and where you saw it in the first line.
  • Give one sentence of proof — a single achievement with a number, pulled straight from your resume. (See how to quantify achievements.)
  • Point at the attachment and close with a thank-you, your name, and your phone.
Cover letter — body or attachment, not both. If the posting asks for a cover letter, attach it as a second PDF and keep the email body to three friendly sentences. If it doesn't, the short email body above is your cover note. Don't paste a full letter into the body and attach one — pick one. See how to write a cover letter.

3. The file you attach

  • Format: PDF, unless the posting specifically asks for Word. A PDF holds its layout on every device and can't be accidentally re-flowed. Make sure it's a text-based PDF, not a scan — see PDF or Word for your resume.
  • File name: Firstname-Lastname-Resume.pdf. Never Resume.pdf, Resume-final-v3.docx, or Untitled.pdf — the recruiter is saving it to a folder of hundreds.
  • Size: keep it under ~1 MB. A giant file signals embedded images a scanner may choke on. A clean text resume is tiny.
  • Double-check it opens — email the PDF to yourself first and open it on your phone. What looks right in an editor sometimes breaks in export.

The 30-second pre-send checklist

  1. Subject line names the role and you.
  2. Email body is 3–4 sentences, addressed to a person, ending with your phone.
  3. The right file is attached — and you can see the attachment icon before you send.
  4. The file is a PDF named Firstname-Lastname-Resume.pdf.
  5. The recipient address is correct and there are no leftover placeholders ([Company], Dear Hiring Manager at X) from a previous application.

Frequently asked questions

What should the subject line be when emailing a resume?

Name the role and yourself: "Application: Product Designer — Jordan Rivera" or, if there's a job ID, "Product Designer (Req 4821) — Jordan Rivera". A clear subject makes the email findable and searchable in a busy inbox. Avoid a blank subject, "Resume", or "Hi".

What do you write in the body of an email when sending a resume?

Three or four short sentences: a one-line greeting to a named person, the role you're applying for and where you saw it, one sentence on why you're a fit, and a note that your resume (and cover letter) are attached. Close with a thank-you and your name and phone. Never send a blank email with just an attachment.

Should you send a resume as a PDF or Word document?

Send a PDF unless the posting specifically asks for Word. A PDF preserves your layout on every device and can't be accidentally edited, and a text-based PDF (not a scan or image) is still readable by applicant tracking systems. Name it Firstname-Lastname-Resume.pdf.

Should the cover letter go in the email body or as an attachment?

Either works, but pick one — don't do both. Most people attach the resume and put a short cover note in the email body; if the posting asks for a separate cover letter, attach it as a second PDF and keep the email body to three friendly sentences so the message isn't blank.
