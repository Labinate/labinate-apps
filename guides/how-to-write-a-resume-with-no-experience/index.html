How to write a resume with no experience

Everyone's first resume has the same problem: no jobs to list. The fix isn't inventing experience — it's leading with what you actually have. Here's the section-by-section way to build a strong one-page resume from school, projects and skills.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

A resume is not a work-history form. It's an argument that you can do the job — and that argument can be made from relevant coursework, a class project, a volunteering stint, a club you ran, or a side project you built for fun. Hiring managers for entry-level roles know you're early; they're reading for evidence that you can learn quickly, follow through, and communicate. All of that is provable without a single past job.

The trick is order and framing. On a first resume you move the strongest thing you have — usually education or a project — to the top, and you write each line as something you did and its result, not as a duty.

The sections to use (in this order)

Drop the standard "Work Experience first" template. With no jobs, use this running order and stop when the page is full:

1 · Header

Name, city, email, phone, and one link that shows your work — a portfolio, GitHub, LinkedIn, or a Behance. No full address, no photo, no "Objective" heading.

2 · Summary (2 lines)

Name what you're studying or trained in, one real strength, and the role you want. "Second-year CS student with a shipped weather app and a strong stats background, looking for a summer software internship." Concrete beats "motivated team player" every time.

3 · Education

School, program, expected graduation. Add GPA if it's 3.3+/strong, plus 3–4 relevant courses and any honours. For most students this is the anchor of the resume, so give it room.

4 · Projects

The section that saves a no-experience resume. Class projects, hackathons, a personal build, a research paper — each with 1–2 bullets: what you made, the tools, and the outcome (a grade, users, a demo link).

5 · Experience of any kind

Part-time work, volunteering, tutoring, a campus job, running a club or a fundraiser, a sports team you captained. It all counts as experience of showing up and delivering — write it exactly like a job.

6 · Skills

A tight list of real, checkable skills — software, languages, tools, certifications. Skip "Microsoft Word" and soft-skill adjectives; keep the things a manager would actually screen for.

How to write a bullet when nothing was a "job"

Same shape as any strong resume line — a past-tense verb, what you did, and a result:

  • Built a budgeting web app in a weekend, now used by ~30 classmates to split shared costs.
  • Organised a campus blood drive of 120 donors, coordinating 8 volunteers and the venue.
  • Analysed 2 years of city transit data for a class project, presented to 40 students and the professor.

None of those were paid jobs. All of them prove initiative, follow-through and a measurable outcome — which is the whole point. If you're stuck on verbs, our list of 185 resume action verbs is a good place to pull from.

Don't pad — reframe. An empty-looking resume tempts you to widen margins and inflate the font. Resist it. Add one more project or a short volunteering line instead, keep it to one page, and keep every bullet earning its place. A focused half-page reads better than a stretched full one.

Three mistakes that sink a first resume

  • An "Objective" that states the obvious. "Seeking a role to grow my skills" wastes the top of the page. Use a two-line summary with something concrete instead.
  • Listing duties, not results. "Responsible for the register" says nothing. "Handled ~200 transactions a shift with zero till discrepancies over 6 months" says a lot.
  • Going to two pages. With no work history, one page is non-negotiable. See how long a resume should be for the full rule.

Frequently asked questions

Can I write a resume with no work experience?

Yes. Lead with what you have — education, coursework, projects, volunteering, clubs and skills — instead of a work-history section. Employers hiring entry-level expect this and read for evidence you can learn and deliver, not for a decade of jobs.

What do I put on a resume if I have never had a job?

A short summary, education (GPA if strong, plus relevant coursework), projects or coursework, volunteering or extracurriculars, and a skills list. Anything where you produced a result is legitimate experience.

How long should a first resume be?

One page, always. With no work history there's no reason to spill onto a second page, and a tight single page reads as focused. If it feels empty, add a projects section rather than stretching the margins.

Should a resume with no experience have a summary?

A short one — two lines naming what you study or are trained in, one concrete strength, and the kind of role you want. Skip the vague "hard-working self-starter" version; it says nothing a recruiter can act on.
