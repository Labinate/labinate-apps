How to write a cover letter with no experience

Applying for your first job feels impossible when the cover letter seems to be asking for the very thing you don't have yet. But an entry-level letter isn't a confession of what you lack — it's a case for what you bring. You have more usable material than you think: coursework, projects, volunteering, a part-time job, a club you ran. The job is to reframe it as evidence and never apologise for the gap.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The guide in one line: open with specific interest in this company and role, spend the body proving transferable skills from wherever you have them — coursework, projects, volunteering, part-time work, self-teaching — each tied to a concrete result, then close by asking for the interview. Never write "although I have no experience." Below: the reframe, a full worked example, the phrases to delete, and a fill-in structure.

"No experience" almost never means nothing

Employers hiring for entry-level and internship roles already know you won't arrive with years behind you. What they're actually screening for is different: can you learn fast, will you show up, and can you point to anything where you did real work and got a real result? Almost everyone can. The mistake is thinking only a salaried job counts.

So before you write a word, take stock. The raw material for a strong first-job letter comes from five places most people overlook:

  • Coursework and academic projects — a capstone, a thesis, a group build, a data assignment that mirrors the job's real work.
  • Volunteering and community work — shows reliability, teamwork, and that you do things without being paid to.
  • Part-time, summer, or casual jobs — retail, hospitality, tutoring: proof you can handle customers, cash, deadlines, and a rota.
  • Clubs, societies, and leadership — running an event, treasuring a society, captaining a team = organisation and initiative.
  • Self-taught skills and side projects — a website you built, a language you learned, a small business you tried. Initiative is exactly what a beginner is judged on.

Turn "not real experience" into evidence

The trick is to describe these the way you'd describe a job: with a specific, quantified outcome. "I'm a hard worker" is a claim anyone can make; a concrete result is proof. Here's the same material, before and after the reframe.

✕ What you think doesn't count

  • "I did a group project at uni."
  • "I volunteered at a charity shop."
  • "I had a summer job in a café."
  • "I ran the photography society."
  • "I taught myself to code."

✓ What it actually proves

  • "Led a 4-person team to ship a working app in 6 weeks."
  • "Reliably covered weekly shifts and trained two new volunteers."
  • "Handled 100+ customers a shift and closed the till accurately."
  • "Grew membership 40% and organised a 60-person exhibition."
  • "Built and shipped 3 projects; comfortable with Python and Git."

Pick the two or three items that map most directly to the posting's top requirements. A cover letter isn't a full inventory — it's the highlight reel that proves you fit this job.

The structure of an entry-level cover letter

The shape is the same as any cover letter — it just leans on different evidence. Three or four short paragraphs, about half a page.

  1. Opening (2–4 sentences) — a specific reason you want this company and role, and the exact job title. Genuine interest is your strongest card when you're new; use it. (See how to start a cover letter.)
  2. Body (1–2 paragraphs) — your best two or three pieces of transferable proof, each with a concrete result, chosen to match the posting's requirements.
  3. Fit + eagerness (optional short paragraph) — why this is the environment you want to learn in, and one honest line about what draws you to their work.
  4. Close (2–3 sentences) — ask for the interview with quiet confidence; thank them. (See how to end a cover letter.)

A full worked example

Here's a complete letter for a recent graduate with no full-time experience applying for an entry-level marketing role — built entirely from coursework, a society, and a part-time job.

Entry-level cover letter — no work experience Dear Ms. Okafor,   When your team turned a local bakery's launch into a campaign that got picked up by three regional papers, I saw exactly the kind of scrappy, story-first marketing I want to learn to do — which is why I'm applying for your Marketing Assistant role.   I don't yet have agency experience, but I've spent the last two years doing the work in smaller settings. As social lead for my university's 300-member entrepreneurship society, I grew our Instagram following from 400 to 2,600 in a year by planning a weekly content calendar and testing post formats — the same instinct-then-measure loop your team runs. My final-year project was a real go-to-market plan for a student startup, which their founders used to land their first 50 customers. And two years of weekend shifts at a busy café taught me to stay calm, organised, and friendly when everything's happening at once.   I'm drawn to your studio because you make small brands sound big, and I'd bring genuine energy, fast hands, and a willingness to learn from people who are clearly better at this than I am yet. I'd love to talk about how I could help — thank you for considering my application, and I hope to hear from you.   Best regards,
Jordan Reyes

Notice what it never does: it doesn't apologise, it doesn't pad, and every claim has a result attached. The one honest line about lacking agency experience is immediately followed by proof of the underlying skills — a pivot, not a concession.

The phrases to delete

The single biggest mistake in a no-experience letter is spending your best space drawing attention to the gap. These openers all do it — cut every one.

✕ Never write

  • "Although I have no experience in this field…"
  • "I know I'm only a student, but…"
  • "Even though I lack the qualifications…"
  • "I don't have much to offer, however…"
  • "I'm hoping you'll give me a chance despite…"

✓ Do this instead

  • Lead with what you bring and why you fit
  • State your evidence; let it carry the weight
  • Match your transferable skills to the posting
  • Name a concrete result you're proud of
  • Ask confidently for the interview

The pattern: never apologise for the gap. The reader can already see your experience level from your resume — announcing it in the letter just spends a sentence making yourself smaller. Present your case and let them judge it.

One honest line is fine — grovelling isn't

There's a difference between a single confident acknowledgement ("I don't yet have agency experience, but…") that pivots straight to proof, and a paragraph of apology. The first is self-aware; the second is self-defeating. If you mention the gap at all, do it in half a sentence and immediately follow it with what you do bring. Acknowledge once, then pivot to evidence.

Fill-in structure you can start from

  • Opening: "When [specific thing the company did], I [genuine reaction] — which is why I'm applying for your [exact role]."
  • Proof 1: "As [role in a project / club / job], I [quantified result] — the same [skill the job needs] your team relies on."
  • Proof 2: "My [coursework / volunteering / side project] gave me [specific, relevant outcome]."
  • Fit: "I'm drawn to [company] because [honest, specific reason], and I'd bring [energy / reliability / fast learning]."
  • Close: "I'd love to talk about how I could help. Thank you for considering my application — I hope to hear from you."

  • Prove, don't claim → every point gets a concrete, quantified result.

Frequently asked questions

How do you write a cover letter with no experience?

Open with genuine, specific interest in the company and the role, then spend the body proving transferable skills instead of apologising for missing ones. Draw evidence from wherever you have it — coursework and academic projects, volunteering, part-time or summer jobs, clubs and societies, side projects, or things you've taught yourself — and tie each to a concrete result the way a work bullet would. Close by asking for the interview with quiet confidence. Never write a sentence like "although I have no experience"; lead with what you bring, not with the gap.

What do you put in a cover letter if you have no work experience?

Use transferable evidence: a class project that mirrors the job's real work, a leadership role in a club, a volunteering shift that shows reliability, a part-time job that proves you can handle customers or deadlines, or a self-taught skill you can demonstrate. Pick two or three that map most directly to the posting's top requirements, and describe each with a specific outcome — "organised a 40-person charity event that raised £2,000" says more about competence than "I am a hard worker" ever could.

Should you mention that you have no experience in a cover letter?

No — don't announce the gap or apologise for it. Phrases like "even though I lack experience" or "I know I'm only a student" spend your best space drawing attention to a weakness the reader can already infer from your resume. Instead, present your transferable evidence confidently and let it speak. A single honest half-sentence that pivots straight to proof is fine; a paragraph of apology is not.

How long should an entry-level cover letter be?

Keep it to about half a page — roughly 200 to 350 words across three or four short paragraphs: a specific opening, one or two body paragraphs of transferable proof, and a brief confident close. With no work history to summarise, a beginner's letter runs on the shorter end. Never pad it to a full page to look more substantial; a tight, specific half-page reads far stronger than a page of filler.
