How to list an interim or acting role on a resume

You stepped up — Acting Director, Interim CFO, Acting Head of Engineering — to cover a seat that fell empty, and for a stretch you did the bigger job. It was probably the most senior work of your career, and now you're unsure how to claim it: undersell it because "it wasn't really my title," or drop the qualifier and claim the permanent seat? Neither. Claim the elevated work in full, keep the honest "Interim" or "Acting" label in the title, and show the dated stint.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

An interim or acting role is a temporary elevation: your employer put you into a higher-level seat for a defined period — usually to cover a departure, a leave, or a search — while the role above you sat vacant. You carried the scope, the decisions, and the accountability of that bigger job, but the title came with a built-in asterisk: provisional. That asterisk is the whole resume problem. The work is real and senior, so it deserves prominence; but the title was never formally yours to keep, so it needs an honest label. Get both halves right and an acting stint becomes one of the strongest lines on your resume.

The core idea: an interim role is where honesty and ambition point the same way. The elevated work is genuinely impressive, so claim it fully — real title, real dates, quantified results. And because the title was provisional, keep the "Interim" or "Acting" qualifier visible, so a recruiter reads a person trusted to step up, not someone inflating a seat they never held.

First, what an interim role is — and what it isn't

"Interim" and "acting" get tangled up with promotions, secondments, and temp jobs, but the defining trait is specific: a temporary step up in level at your own employer, into a seat that was empty. Pin that down, because it decides how the entry is labeled.

Not the same as…How an interim role differs
A promotionA promotion is a permanent move up — the elevated title is yours to keep, and you show it as a second title at the same employer. An interim role was provisional from day one and often reverts, so the honest label stays "Interim" or "Acting." (If your interim role was later made permanent, it converts to a promotion — more on that below.)
A secondmentA secondment moves you sideways in location — loaned to another team, client, or division while you stay on your home payroll. An interim role moves you up in level within your own reporting line. Secondment asks "whose name goes on the entry"; an interim role asks "how do I claim a bigger title that was temporary."
A temp jobA temp job is an external fixed-term hire — you were brought in from outside, often through an agency, for a set assignment. An interim role is the internal step-up of an existing employee into a higher seat. One is a job you were hired into; the other is a role you were elevated within.

The two ways people get this wrong

Almost every interim-role mistake is one of two opposite errors. Knowing both keeps you in the honest middle, which also happens to be the strongest position.

Underselling — hiding your best work

  • Leaving the stint off because "it wasn't officially my title."
  • Burying it in one bullet under your regular role, so the elevation disappears.
  • Keeping your old junior title on the whole tenure even for the months you ran the bigger seat.
  • Apologizing for it — "temporarily covered while we searched" — instead of owning the results.

Overselling — inflating the seat

  • Dropping "Interim" and listing the permanent title you never formally held.
  • Stretching the dates to make a two-month cover look like a year in the chair.
  • Claiming decisions or headcount the acting role didn't actually own.
  • Implying you were promoted when the role reverted to someone else.

The fix for both is the same discipline: label it honestly, then sell it hard. The "Interim" tag is what lets you claim the senior scope without a reader wondering whether you're inflating — it's a credibility signal, not a weakness. Once the label is in place, the bullets are free to be as ambitious as the work really was.

How to format it — one tenure, the stint labeled and nested

Keep everything under a single employer, and give the acting stint its own labeled line with its own dates. The higher title carries the "Interim" or "Acting" qualifier right in the title, not hidden in a bullet.

The clean interim format

Northwind Health — one tenure, Jun 2019 – Present

Acting Director of Operations (Interim) · Mar 2024 – Nov 2024
  • Ran the full 30-person ops function during the director search
  • Owned the $4M operating budget and delivered the annual plan on time

Operations Manager · Jun 2019 – Present
  (substantive-role bullets)

The acting stint sits at the top of the tenure with its elevated title and dates; your substantive role follows with its own continuous range. A reader immediately sees the arc: trusted to step up, then back in the regular seat — one employer, no invented departures.

Put the interim entry above your regular role when the elevated work is your strongest selling point for the target job, so it's the first thing read. Keep the qualifier — (Interim), Acting, or covering the director vacancy — visible in the title line itself. And show real dates for the stint; a defined, honest window reads as more credible than a vague "2024."

Lead with what the elevation proves

The bullets under an acting role should argue that you can do the senior job — because you already did. Don't narrate that it was temporary or that you were "helping out"; the label already says it's interim. Lead with the scope you owned and the results you delivered.

Helped cover management duties while the role was vacant.		Acting Engineering Manager · 8 months
  • Led a team of 9 through two releases with zero missed deadlines
  • Owned hiring, sprint planning, and the roadmap for the quarter

Treat the acting stint as real, quantified work — headcount, budget, launches, decisions. The interim label handles the honesty; the numbers handle the persuasion. That combination is exactly why a well-listed acting role often outperforms a permanent title on the same resume.

A few common situations

The interim role became permanent

If you were made permanent after acting, it's now a promotion. Show the interim stretch as the on-ramp — Acting Director (Interim) · Mar–Aug 2024, then Director · Aug 2024 – Present — and drop the qualifier from the date it became official. The interim months become proof you earned the seat, which is a stronger story than a promotion out of nowhere.

You went back to your old title afterward

This is the normal ending for an acting role and it is not a demotion — everyone knows an "Acting" seat is temporary cover. Show it as one continuous tenure with the stint nested inside: regular role, interim stretch, resumption of your role. The read is "stepped up when it counted, handed the seat back," which argues for trust, not decline. If you worry it looks like a step down, a one-line note — returned to substantive role after permanent hire joined — closes the gap.

The acting role was brief or light-touch

Not every "covered while my boss was out" stretch earns its own entry. If it was a few weeks of light cover with nothing quantifiable, fold it into a bullet under your regular role — served as acting lead during a 3-week transition — rather than a standalone title. Reserve the labeled entry for stints with real scope and results.

You acted up but kept your own job too

Sometimes you absorb a vacant senior role on top of your existing duties rather than instead of them. Say so — Acting Head of Product while retaining Senior PM responsibilities — because carrying both is a capacity signal. This is still an internal elevation, not a second job: it's one employer stretching your remit, not outside work held concurrently.

The bright line — label honestly, sell fully. The two failure modes both come from treating "Interim" as a problem. It isn't. Hide the stint and you throw away your most senior work; strip the label and you claim a title a background or reference check will contradict, sinking the whole resume. Keep the honest qualifier and let the scope and results speak — that's the version that reads as both credible and impressive.

Quick reference

  • Include it — it's usually your most senior work → an acting stint shows you ran a bigger job under pressure. Give it prominence.
  • Keep "Interim" or "Acting" in the title → the label is a credibility signal that lets you claim the elevated scope without reading as inflation.
  • One tenure, the stint nested with its own dates → same employer, elevated title on top, substantive role continuing — no invented departures.
  • Lead with quantified scope → budget, headcount, launches, decisions. Drop the apologetic "temporarily covered" framing.
  • Never drop the qualifier to claim the permanent seat → that's fabrication a verification call will expose.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put an interim or acting role on your resume?

Almost always yes — an interim or acting stint is usually the most senior work you've done, and hiding it wastes your strongest evidence. When you stepped up to cover a vacancy, you ran a bigger scope, made higher-stakes calls, and carried responsibility above your normal grade, and that is exactly what a hiring manager wants to see. The catch is not whether to include it but how to label it: keep the word "Interim" or "Acting" in the title so the elevation reads as real but honest. The only time to soften it is if the stint was a few days of light cover with nothing to show — then a bullet under your regular role is enough.

How do you list an acting or interim title on a resume?

Give the stint its own labeled line under the same employer, with the higher title prefixed by "Interim" or "Acting" and its own date range. For example, under one company: "Acting Head of Operations (Interim) · Mar 2024 – Nov 2024" followed by bullets for what you delivered in the seat, then your substantive role above or below it with its own dates. Keep the qualifier visible in the title itself rather than only in a bullet, quantify the elevated results, and if you returned to your prior role afterward, show that return as continuity rather than a step down. The label does the honesty work; the bullets do the selling.

Does an interim role count as a promotion on a resume?

Not quite — the two are different things and the resume treats them differently. A promotion is a permanent move up: the elevated title is yours to keep, and you show it as a second title at the same employer. An interim or acting role is a temporary elevation that was provisional from the start and often reverts, so the honest label stays "Interim" or "Acting." The exception is when an interim role is made permanent — at that point it converts to a promotion, and you can drop the qualifier from the date it became official, showing the interim stretch as the on-ramp that earned the permanent seat.

What if your interim role went back to your old title — does that look like a demotion?

No, as long as you frame the return as planned rather than a fall. An interim role is temporary by definition — everyone reading knows an "Acting" seat is cover for a vacancy, so returning to your substantive role is the expected ending, not a demotion. Show it as one continuous tenure at the employer with the acting stint nested inside: your regular role, then the interim stretch with its own dates and elevated bullets, then the resumption of your role. The story reads as "trusted to step up when it mattered, then handed the seat back" — which argues for reliability, not decline.

Can you drop the word "interim" and just list the senior title?

No — that crosses from framing into fabrication. Listing "Director of Finance" when your actual title was "Interim Director of Finance" claims a permanent seat you never formally held, and a reference check or an employment-verification call will surface the real title, at which point the whole resume loses credibility. The good news is you don't need to inflate: the interim work is genuinely impressive on its own terms. Keep the honest "Interim" or "Acting" label and let the scope and results — the budget you owned, the team you led, the decisions you made — do the persuading. Honest and senior beats inflated and unverifiable every time.
