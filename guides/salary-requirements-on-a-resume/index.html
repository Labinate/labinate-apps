Salary requirements on a resume

It's one of the most common questions job seekers ask, and the honest answer surprises people: your salary requirements almost never belong on the resume itself. Naming a number too early is a negotiation mistake dressed up as being helpful — it can screen you out before anyone reads your qualifications, and it hands the employer the anchor. Here's when (rarely) to state a figure, where it actually belongs, and the exact wording to use if an application forces your hand.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

The one-line version: keep salary requirements off the resume. The resume exists to earn you an interview, not to price you. Discuss compensation later, in conversation, once they want you — and if a posting or form forces a number sooner, give a researched range, never a single figure, and never on the resume itself.

Why the number doesn't belong on the resume

Putting a salary requirement on your resume can only hurt you, in one of two directions:

  • Ask too high and you get filtered out — often automatically — before a human ever sees why you'd be worth it.
  • Ask too low and you've anchored the entire negotiation beneath your value; the offer will land near your number, not near the budget.

Either way you've given away information the employer would normally have to earn, and you've attached a price tag to a document whose whole purpose is to make you look worth interviewing. The person with the most leverage in a salary talk is the one who makes the other side commit to wanting them before money comes up. A resume with a number on it throws that leverage away on page one.

Where salary expectations actually belong

In order of preference:

WhereWhat to do
A live conversationBest. Defer the number until you know the full role and they know they want you. "I'd love to understand the full scope before discussing numbers" is a complete, professional answer.
The cover letterOnly if the posting explicitly asks for salary requirements. One line, a range, then move on. See how to write a cover letter.
An application form fieldIf it's required and won't accept text, enter a range or the market midpoint. Don't leave a required field blank — many systems reject the application.
The resumeNever. There is no version of this that helps you.
The trap to avoid

Basing your number on your current salary instead of the market rate for the role. If you're underpaid now, this locks the underpayment in for another job.

→ Research the market rate for the title, level, and location. Base your range on the job, not on your last paycheck.

How to build a range (when you're forced to give one)

Sometimes a posting demands "salary requirements" or an online form has a required field. When you can't defer, do this:

  1. Research first. Check market data for the exact role, seniority, and city on a salary site or two, and factor in any known range in the posting. Know the real number before you type one.
  2. Give a range, not a point. A window of roughly 10–15% (say $70,000–$80,000) signals a target without boxing you in. A single figure becomes a ceiling.
  3. Aim toward the top of what's supportable. It's far easier to negotiate down from a high anchor than up from a low one.
  4. Add flexibility in words. If free text is allowed, append "negotiable" or "flexible depending on the full compensation package." If only a number fits, use the midpoint of your range.

Wording you can copy

Say this

  • "Based on the role and my experience, I'm targeting a range of $X–$Y, and I'm flexible depending on the overall package."
  • "My expectations are in the $X–$Y range; I'd welcome the chance to discuss the full scope first."
  • In a required field: the midpoint, e.g. 75000.

Not this

  • A single hard number ("$72,000") that becomes your ceiling.
  • "Whatever you offer" — reads as no self-worth and invites a lowball.
  • Your current salary as your ask.
  • A salary line anywhere on the resume.

What to put in that space instead

The resume real estate people are tempted to spend on a number is far better spent on evidence of value. A line that says you "grew revenue 30%," "cut processing time in half," or "managed a team of 8" does more to justify a strong offer than any figure you could quote — because it makes the employer want you before price is even on the table. That's the actual work of a resume:

  • Lead bullets with results, not duties — see responsibilities vs. accomplishments.
  • Attach numbers to your wins — see how to quantify achievements.
  • Open strong bullets with action verbs — see resume action verbs.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put salary requirements on a resume?

No — leave salary requirements off the resume itself in almost every case. The resume's job is to make the case that you're worth interviewing; naming a number too early can screen you out before anyone reads your qualifications. If you ask for more than the range, an algorithm or recruiter may discard you; if you ask for less, you've anchored the offer low and left money on the table. The only time to state a figure is when a job posting or application form explicitly requires it, and even then it belongs in the cover letter or the form field, not on the resume.

Where do salary expectations belong if not on the resume?

Ideally in a live conversation, once you know the full scope of the role and they know they want you — that's when you have the most leverage. When you can't defer it: if the posting demands a number, put a researched range in a single line of your cover letter; if an online application has a required salary field, enter a range or the market midpoint rather than leaving it blank. Never let the number live on the resume, where it becomes a permanent filter attached to your qualifications.

How do you answer salary requirements when an application forces you to?

Give a range rather than a single figure, and base it on real market data for the role, level, and location — not on what you currently earn. A range of roughly 10 to 15 percent wide (for example, $70,000 to $80,000) signals a target without boxing you in. If the field only accepts one number, enter the midpoint of your researched range. If free text is allowed, add 'negotiable' or 'flexible depending on the total compensation package' so you keep room to talk. Aim toward the top of what the market supports; it's easier to come down than to climb up.

What should you write instead of a salary number on a resume?

Write nothing about salary on the resume — use that space for evidence of value instead. Every line that quantifies an achievement (revenue grown, costs cut, time saved, people managed) does more to justify a strong offer than any number you could type. The strongest position in a negotiation is a resume that makes the employer want you before money is ever discussed, so spend the page proving impact, not pricing yourself.
