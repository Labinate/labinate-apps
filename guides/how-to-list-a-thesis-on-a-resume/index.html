How to list a thesis on a resume

You spent a year (or several) on a thesis or dissertation, and it feels like it should be on the page. It should — but a thesis isn't a credential and it isn't a publication; it's one substantial piece of independent research, and on a resume it behaves like a research project. The only real decision is how much room it earns, and that comes down to relevance and stage: promote it to a described entry — method and finding — when the research is your strongest relevant proof, and demote it to a single line under your degree once real experience leads. Here's the exact format for both.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A thesis is easy to handle badly. People bury a genuinely impressive dissertation as a bare title under their degree, list it in a "Publications" section where it doesn't belong, describe it by its topic with no result attached, or stretch the word to cover an ordinary term paper. Done right, a thesis is one of the cleanest signals a resume can carry — proof that you can independently define a hard problem, choose a method, and produce a finding, which is exactly what a research role, an R&D team, or a graduate program is reading for. The craft is knowing when it deserves a full entry and when it deserves a line, and describing it by what you actually did. This guide shows both.

The core idea: A thesis or dissertation is a single major piece of independent, advisor-supervised research that culminates a degree — so it reads like a research project, not a credential. Whether it earns a described entry or a one-line note is governed by relevance × stage. And whichever form it takes, describe it by method and finding, never the title alone.

First, know what this is (and isn't)

A thesis sits near several other academic lines, and it's easy to file it under the wrong one. The distinction that matters: a thesis is a piece of research you produced — not the degree it belonged to, not a peer-reviewed paper, and not a class you took.

The degree (PhD, MS)The degree is the credential — the line that says you earned the qualification. The thesis is the work the credential was awarded for. List the degree in Education; list the thesis as the research it was, either beneath the degree or as its own entry — they're two different claims.
PublicationsA publication is peer-reviewed, citable output in a journal or proceedings. A thesis is an unpublished research document — it may later become a paper, but until it does it isn't one. Don't list a thesis as a citation in Publications; list any papers that came out of it there separately.
ProjectsProjects is the broad category of describable work you can put on a resume. An academic thesis is one specific kind of project — which is why, on an industry resume, a thesis often lives happily in a Projects section, described by what you built and found.
Relevant courseworkCoursework is classes you took — training you received. A thesis is original research you produced — output you created. That's the line that decides whether a piece of academic work is a thesis or just a strong paper: did you independently produce it, or complete an assignment?

Step 1 — Decide how much room it earns (relevance × stage)

This is the decision that settles everything else. A thesis isn't in or out — it's a dial, and where you set it depends on whether the research is still your strongest relevant evidence. Ask two questions: does the topic connect to the job, and is your academic record still carrying the case?

Promote it — describe it fully

  • Current or recent grad student; work history is thin
  • Applying to research, R&D, data, or academic roles
  • The thesis topic maps directly onto the target job
  • The methods you used are the skills the role wants

Demote it — one line, or drop it

  • Several years of relevant work experience now lead
  • Pivoting into a field the thesis has nothing to do with
  • The topic is niche and irrelevant to the role
  • Space is tight and stronger evidence is competing for it

There's no fixed rule tied to the degree level — a well-chosen undergraduate honors thesis can outrank a stale, off-topic PhD dissertation for a given job. What decides it is relevance to the role and what else is on the page. When the research is the best proof you can do the work, give it a described entry. When it's context, a line under the degree keeps it present without spending the space a stronger accomplishment needs.

Step 2 — Format A: the described entry (when it's your headline)

When the thesis is primary evidence, it graduates up out of Education into a Projects or "Research" section, or sits as a described sub-entry under the lab or research role where you did it. Lead with a title and dates, then two to four bullets that state your method and your finding — written exactly like accomplishment bullets on a job.

Title only (a topic)Described (an accomplishment)
RESEARCH
Master's Thesis: "Predictive Modeling of Urban Water Demand"		RESEARCH
M.S. Thesis — Urban Water-Demand Modeling · 2024-2025
· Built a gradient-boosted model on 8 yrs of metered data (1.2M rows)
· Cut forecast error 22% vs. the utility's baseline method
· Presented to a 3-person faculty panel; adopted by the city pilot

The left version names a topic and stops — a reader learns what you studied but nothing about what you can do. The right version reads like real work: a method, a quantified result, and a consequence. That's the whole point of promoting a thesis to an entry — if you're going to spend the space, spend it on evidence, not a title. Name your advisor or lab only if it adds signal (a known lab, a relevant PI); otherwise the bullets carry it.

Step 3 — Format B: the single line (when it's context)

When the thesis is a supporting detail rather than your headline, it belongs as one line under the degree in your Education section — present, verifiable, and cheap on space.

Overbuilt for contextClean one-line note
EDUCATION
M.A. History, State University, 2021
THESIS
· "The Politics of ..." (long topic)
· Advised by Dr. Smith
· 120 pages, defended with distinction		EDUCATION
M.A. History, State University, 2021
Thesis: "The Politics of Regional Trade, 1890-1920" (Adv. Dr. Smith)

If an experienced candidate builds a whole sub-section around a thesis unrelated to the job, it reads as reaching — the same way a college honor does once work carries the story. One line under the degree records that you did substantial research without pulling focus from the experience that's actually selling you. Include the title and, optionally, the advisor; skip the page count and the defense theatrics.

Step 4 — Keep it honest (the word means something)

Two honesty rules do the rest of the work, and both come back to the same idea: a thesis is a specific thing, and the reader can find out what yours was.

Do

  • Use "thesis" only for real independent, supervised research
  • Describe the method and the finding you can defend in an interview
  • Separate the thesis (research) from any papers it produced (publications)
  • Quantify honestly — real sample sizes, real results

Don't

  • Inflate a term paper or group assignment into a "thesis"
  • List the title with no method or result attached
  • Put an unpublished thesis in a "Publications" section as a citation
  • Claim a finding you can't explain when asked

The word "thesis" implies sustained, independent, advisor-supervised research that culminated a degree. Borrowing it for an ordinary class paper is a small inflation that collapses the moment an interviewer asks about your methodology — and anything you write in a bullet is a promise you'll be asked to keep. If the work was a normal course paper, it belongs in relevant coursework, not dressed up as a thesis. If it was real research, describe it plainly and let the result speak.

The bright line — describe the thesis by what you did and found, and only call it a thesis if it was one. A title states a topic; an accomplishment states evidence, and a reader hiring for research wants the evidence. Promote it to a described entry when the research is your strongest relevant proof, demote it to a line under your degree when experience leads, keep it out of Publications until it's actually published — and never stretch the word to cover work that wasn't independent research. Placed and described honestly, a thesis is proof you can do the whole arc of real work: define, execute, deliver.

Quick reference

  • It's research, not a credential → a thesis is the work your degree was awarded for, listed as a project or research entry — not the degree line itself.
  • Relevance × stage sets the size → a described entry when the research is your strongest relevant proof; one line under the degree when experience leads.
  • Describe, don't just name → method + finding + a number, written like accomplishment bullets; the title alone is a topic, not evidence.
  • It isn't a publication → keep an unpublished thesis out of the Publications section; list any papers it produced there separately.
  • Guard the word → "thesis" means independent, supervised research; an ordinary paper goes in relevant coursework instead.
  • The bright line → describe what you did and found, and only claim it if it was real research.

Frequently asked questions

Should I put my thesis on my resume?

It depends on whether the research is doing work for your candidacy. Include it — and give it real space — when your academic record is carrying the case: you're a current or recent grad student, your work history is thin, you're applying to research, R&D, or academic roles, or the topic maps directly onto the job you want. In those situations a thesis is often the single best proof you can independently define a hard problem, run a method, and produce a result. Demote it to one line, or drop it, when experience leads instead: you have several years of relevant work, or you're pivoting into a field your thesis has nothing to do with. The test isn't the degree or the prestige of the topic — it's whether the thesis is still your strongest relevant evidence. When it is, describe it fully; when it isn't, a single line under your degree (or nothing) is right.

How do I format a thesis on a resume?

There are two correct formats, and you pick by how much the research matters. As a described entry (when the research is your headline): put it in a Research or Projects section, or under the job or lab where you did it, led by a title and dates, with two to four bullets that state your method and your finding — "Designed and ran a 240-participant study on X; found Y, reducing Z by 30%; presented results to a faculty panel." As a single line (when it's context): tuck it under the degree in your Education section — "M.S. Chemistry, State University, 2025 — Thesis: [Title] (Advisor: Dr. Name)." In both cases the title alone is not enough for the described version: a title names a topic, but a hiring reader wants the accomplishment. Keep the wording plain and quantified, and match the style of the rest of your resume.

Where does a thesis go — Education, Projects, or Research?

Its home follows its importance. If the thesis is context or a supporting detail, it lives as one line inside your Education section, attached to the degree it culminated — that's where a reader expects to see it and it costs almost no space. If the thesis is a primary piece of evidence, it graduates up out of Education into a dedicated "Research" section (standard for academic and R&D resumes) or a "Projects" section (natural for industry resumes), or it can sit as a described sub-entry under the lab, department, or research role where you did the work. The principle: a thesis that's just proof you finished your degree stays as a line under the degree; a thesis that's proof you can do the target job earns its own described entry above Education.

Is a thesis the same as a publication?

No, and conflating them is a common mistake. A thesis is a single, unpublished (or institutionally-archived) research document you wrote to complete a degree. A publication is peer-reviewed, citable work that appeared in a journal, conference proceedings, or book. A thesis often becomes one or more publications later, but until it does, it isn't one — so don't list your thesis in a "Publications" section formatted as a citation, because a reader who looks it up will find a degree requirement, not a peer-reviewed paper. List the thesis as research or a project; list any papers that came out of it separately in Publications. If your thesis is genuinely published (some are, as a book or a monograph), then it can appear in both places, described as research and cited as a publication — but that's the exception, not the default.

Should I list my undergraduate thesis on my resume?

Yes, if it was a real, independent, advisor-supervised piece of work — an honors thesis, a capstone research project, a senior design project with a genuine deliverable — and especially if your work history is thin or the topic is relevant. For a new graduate it's strong evidence of exactly the skills a first job asks for: defining a problem, doing the work, and producing something. Give it a described entry or a solid line under your degree. What you shouldn't do is call an ordinary term paper or a routine group assignment a "thesis" to borrow the weight of the word — a thesis implies sustained independent research, and an interviewer who asks about your methodology will quickly tell the difference. If it was substantial, list it proudly and describe what you found; if it was a normal class paper, it belongs in relevant coursework, not as a thesis.
