Relocation on a resume

Being in the wrong city rarely costs you a job because you can't do it — it costs you because a recruiter or an applicant-tracking system filters by location before anyone reads your skills. An out-of-area address is a screening problem, not a qualification problem. The fix isn't to hide where you are; it's to control the location signal: manage the line in your header, state relocation intent specifically, put it where it's seen, and quietly answer the two things every employer wonders about a relocating candidate.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

You're qualified, you'd take the job tomorrow, and you'd happily move for it — but your resume says "Cleveland" and the job is in Austin. Somewhere between you and the hiring manager sits a filter that sorts on location, and to that filter you look like someone who can't show up. Most relocation advice stops at "just write 'willing to relocate'." That's the phrase that does the least work of anything you could write. This guide is about the version that actually moves you past the filter — honestly.

The core idea: your location on a resume is a signal an early filter reads, and you get to shape it. The move is to make the signal say the right thing — the right city, a real timeframe, and an answer to "who pays and will they actually come" — instead of leaving it to read "lives far away, probably a stretch."

First, know what this is not

A few situations look like a location question but call for different handling. Mixing them up is how relocation ends up either invisible or oversold.

SituationHow it differs from relocating
Your contact headerThe general rules for what goes in your header — email, phone, links, and the location line. Relocation is one strategic use of that location line; this guide is the whole moving-cities playbook, not the header basics.
Remote workThe opposite situation — a remote role means no move is needed, so "Remote" goes in the location field and location stops mattering. Relocation is about physically changing cities for an on-site or hybrid job.
A gap from movingIf a past move created a break in your work history, that's a gap to frame, not a relocation-intent question. This guide is about a move you're making for the job, not one that already interrupted your work.
Moves that look like hoppingSeveral short stints in different cities can read as job-hopping. There the fix is the career narrative; here it's a forward-looking location signal for the next role.

Step 1 — Control the location line in your header

Your contact header is the first thing scanned and often the field an ATS parses for a location match. You have three honest options; pick by how committed the move is.

Already committed to a specific city → lead with the target

List the destination with a short relocation note: Austin, TX · relocating from Chicago (Aug 2026). This matches the job's location for the filter and tells the truth. Use it when you have a date or a firm plan.

Open to relocating generally → current city + an "open" line

Keep Cleveland, OH as your location and add Open to relocation beside it or in the summary. Honest, and it flags flexibility without pretending you've already moved.

Either way → drop the full street address

City and state is all a modern header needs — a full mailing address adds nothing and can expose an out-of-area ZIP to the filter. See what to put in your header for the rest of the fields.

Step 2 — State intent specifically, not vaguely

This is the difference between a relocation note that works and one that gets ignored. "Willing to relocate" is weak precisely because everyone writes it — it reads as a checkbox, not a plan. Specificity turns it into a commitment the reader believes.

Vague — reads as fillerSpecific — reads as a real plan
Willing to relocateRelocating to Denver in September 2026
Open to relocation anywhereOpen to relocation to the Austin metro at own expense
Flexible on locationSeeking roles in the Seattle area; family relocating Q3 2026

Name the city if you have one, add a timeframe, and — when it's true — signal who pays. A targeted note ("relocating to their city") beats "anywhere" every time, because "anywhere" reads as someone carpet-bombing applications rather than someone who wants this job in this place. If you're aiming at one region, treat the location note like the rest of your tailoring and match it to the posting.

Step 3 — Put it where it gets seen

A relocation line only helps if the screener reaches it. Two placements do the work; a third wastes it.

Where it lands

  • The header location line — read first, parsed by the ATS
  • One line in the summary — "…relocating to Seattle in Q3, seeking…"
  • For an out-of-area role, a sentence in the cover letter as reinforcement

Where it dies

  • Buried at the bottom of the resume, below the fold
  • Only in the cover letter a screener may never open
  • Left to the recruiter to infer from an out-of-state address

If you use a targeted headline or objective, that top-of-page real estate is a natural home for the intent too — "Relocating to Boston · Operations Manager" tells the reader both things at a glance.

Step 4 — Answer the two worries employers actually have

A relocating candidate raises exactly two quiet questions. A good relocation note answers both without being asked.

"Will we have to pay to move them?"

Relocation packages cost money, and for many roles the employer would rather hire local than fund a move. If you're covering your own move, say so — at own expense removes the single biggest reason to skip you. If you'd need relocation support, leave it off the resume and raise it later; don't lead with a cost.

"Will they actually come — and stay?"

Employers have been burned by candidates who interview from afar and then flake, or move and leave in six months. A specific date, a named city, and a reason tied to the area ("family relocating," "returning home to Denver") all read as committed. Vague "open to anywhere" reads as flight risk. The more concrete and rooted the plan sounds, the lower the perceived risk.

A few special cases

Moving for a partner, family, or return home

You don't owe an explanation, but a short rooted reason reduces flight-risk fear: "Relocating to Columbus (family)" signals the move is happening regardless, so you're not a maybe. Keep it to a few words — it's a reassurance, not a story.

International relocation and work authorization

Across borders, location is the smaller issue — work authorization is what the filter and the recruiter care about. If you're already authorized (citizen, PR, existing visa), say so plainly, because it removes the real blocker. If you'd need sponsorship, know that's a separate, larger conversation than relocation and many postings screen on it; be honest rather than surprising them later.

Open to several cities

If you'll genuinely go anywhere for the right role, still avoid the word "anywhere." Either name the two or three target metros ("Open to relocation: Austin, Denver, or Raleigh") or, better, tailor a version of the resume to each posting's city so the location line always matches the job in front of the reader.

The bright line — don't fake a local address. Borrowing a friend's address or inventing a local one to slip past the filter is tempting and a mistake: it unravels when an area code doesn't match, mail bounces, or an employer expects you on-site before you can be there — and it starts the relationship on a lie. The honest version does the same job: list the target city with a relocation note, which clears the location match without the fabrication. If you're relocating on a fixed date and will genuinely have a local address by the interview stage, using it is fair — but never claim a presence you don't have.

Quick reference

  • It's a screening problem → location filters cut you before your skills are read; the goal is to control the signal, not hide it.
  • Header → city + state only; lead with the target city + relocation note if committed, or current city + "Open to relocation" if flexible.
  • Be specific → a named city, a date, and "at own expense" beat a bare "willing to relocate" every time.
  • Placement → header location line and one summary line; don't bury it or leave it only in the cover letter.
  • Answer the worries → who pays (say "own expense" if true) and flight risk (a date + a rooted reason).
  • International → work authorization matters more than location; state it plainly.
  • Bright line → never fake a local address; use a target-city note instead.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put your address on a resume if you're relocating?

Not your full street address — almost no one needs that anymore, and an out-of-area one can get you filtered before a human reads a word. Use just the city and state, and choose which one on purpose. If you're already committed to a move, you can list the target city with a relocation note ("Austin, TX (relocating from Chicago, Aug 2026)"). If you're open to relocating generally, use your current city and state and add an "Open to relocation" line. The goal is to control the location signal a recruiter and the applicant-tracking system read first, not to hide where you are.

How do you say you're willing to relocate on a resume?

Be specific, not vague. "Willing to relocate" is weak because it reads as something every mass-applicant writes; "Relocating to Denver in September 2026" or "Open to relocation to the Austin metro at own expense" reads as a real, committed plan. Name the target city if you have one, add a timeframe, and — if it's true — signal that you'll cover the cost. Specificity is what turns a throwaway phrase into a credible commitment the reader believes.

Where do you put relocation on a resume?

Put it where it gets seen early: the location line of your contact header, and again in your summary. The header is what a recruiter scans first and what many applicant-tracking systems parse for a location match, so a target city or an "open to relocation" note belongs there. A one-line reinforcement in the summary ("Operations manager relocating to Seattle in Q3, seeking...") catches the human reader. Don't bury it at the bottom of the resume or leave it only for the cover letter, where the screener may never reach it.

Should you use a local address if you're planning to move?

Only if it's genuinely yours — a place you'll actually be reachable at. Borrowing a friend's address or inventing a local one to dodge the filter is risky: it unravels the moment an area code doesn't match, mail is returned, or an employer expects you on-site sooner than you can be there, and it starts the relationship on a false note. A cleaner honest move is to list the target city with a relocation note, which gets you past the location match without the fabrication. If you're relocating on a fixed date and will have a real local address by the interview stage, using it can be legitimate — but never claim a presence you don't have.

Does saying "willing to relocate" hurt your resume?

A bare "willing to relocate anywhere" can hurt a little — it reads as unfocused, and it raises the question of who pays and whether you'll really move. It helps when it's specific and tied to a real plan: a named city, a date, and (if true) "at own expense" answer the two things an employer actually worries about — relocation cost and flight risk. Targeted relocation intent is an asset because it tells the reader you're serious about their location; vague, open-ended relocation is the version that reads as noise.
