How to list a scholarship on a resume

A scholarship is money awarded to fund your education — not a job you worked and not, by itself, an achievement. That's why most scholarships don't belong on a resume at all, and the ones that do never go under experience. Two questions decide everything. Was it a competitive selection or a financial fact — and how recent is your degree?

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

People misread a scholarship in one of two ways. Some list every award they ever received — need-based grants, automatic GPA discounts, a $500 local scholarship — as though quantity signals merit. Others leave off a genuinely competitive national award because it "was just for school." Both miss the same point: a scholarship is only worth a resume line when it represents selection against a strong pool, and even then it has a short shelf life. Unlike a fellowship — a funded appointment that can be real work — a scholarship is money for your studies, so you never performed a role to earn it. That single fact rules out the experience section and reframes the whole decision.

The core idea: a scholarship is funding, not work, so it's never experience — it's recognition, at best. Two filters decide whether it earns a line: merit vs. need (a competitive, merit-based award signals selection; a need-based or automatic one is a private financial fact) and shelf life (scholarships are education-adjacent — strong for students and new grads, fading fast once real experience fills the page).

Filter one: was it merit, or was it money?

Before you decide where a scholarship goes, decide whether it goes anywhere. The test is what got you the money. If you were chosen — a competitive application, a named or national award, a discipline-specific scholarship with a real selection process — it's a signal an employer cares about. If the money followed automatically from your enrollment, your GPA, or your financial need, it's a fact about your finances, not an accomplishment.

The instinct to list everything hurts you: a page of tiny, automatic awards dilutes the one competitive scholarship that actually matters. Keep the signal, cut the noise — the same discipline you'd apply to any awards section.

Filter two: how recent is your degree?

Even a real, competitive scholarship has an expiry date. Scholarships live in the orbit of your education, and education-adjacent signals fade as work experience accumulates. Where you are in your career decides how much room a scholarship gets — or whether it stays at all.

Current student / new graduate

Your page is thin on work history, so academic signals carry real weight. A competitive scholarship belongs here — often near the top, alongside GPA, honors, and relevant coursework. This is when scholarships work hardest for you, so list the ones that show selection. If your history is genuinely light, the same logic in writing a resume with no experience applies — lean on what you've been chosen for.

A few years in

Work now leads and education drops below it. A prestigious scholarship can survive as a single line under Education, but the bar rises — if it isn't nationally recognizable or unusually selective, it's probably crowding out something more recent and more relevant.

Mid-career and beyond

Scholarships come off. A decade into a career, your track record is the story; a college scholarship reads as filler no matter how impressive it once was. The rare exception is a scholarship so famous it functions as a lifelong credential — and even then, one clean line, never a section.

When it earns a line: how to format it

A scholarship that passes both filters gets a line, not a paragraph — it wasn't a role, so it doesn't take achievement bullets. Place it under Education as a sub-line beneath the relevant degree, or group several into a dedicated Honors & Awards section.

The standard scholarship line

National Merit Scholarship, 2022 — or Recipient, Chevening Scholarship, UK Government, 2023. Name, awarding body, year. That's the whole line; a recognizable name needs no explanation.

Add selectivity only when it's not obvious

If the name alone doesn't convey the competition, a short accurate qualifier helps: "1 of 50 awarded nationally". State it only when you can support it — overstated selectivity is exactly the kind of claim that unravels in an interview.

Leave the dollar amount off

The money isn't the signal — the selection is. "$40,000 scholarship" reads as either boastful or beside the point. Name the award, not the sum.

Let funded work graduate on its own

If the scholarship paid for concrete work — a research project, study abroad, a thesis — that work can earn its own line in the section where it belongs, separate from the scholarship. The funding is the honor; the output stands on its merits, the way a fellowship's project does.

Honest and proportionate vs. padded

The failure mode here isn't lying — it's padding, and padding is easy to spot. Keep to the left column.

Signal, kept small

  • One or two competitive scholarships, name and year
  • Placed under Education or a tidy Honors section
  • Selectivity stated only when accurate
  • Retired as real experience earns the space

Padding, easy to spot

  • Every small or automatic award listed for bulk
  • A need-based grant dressed up as an honor
  • A dollar figure standing in for selectivity
  • A high-school scholarship still on a mid-career page

A competitive scholarship, placed right, quietly says a selection committee chose you over strong applicants. That's a real signal early in a career — but only if it isn't buried under a pile of automatic awards, and only while it's still your most impressive proof of selection. Once your work does that job, the scholarship's shift is over. The same recency logic that governs how far back a resume should go governs this: the page belongs to what's recent and relevant, and education-era honors make room for it.

Quick reference

  • Never experience → a scholarship is funding, not a role, so it lives under Education or Honors, never work history.
  • Filter one: merit vs. need → competitive, merit-based awards earn a line; need-based, automatic, or tiny ones don't.
  • Filter two: shelf life → strongest for students and new grads; fades fast, and comes off entirely mid-career.
  • Format → Name, awarding body, year — one line, no dollar amount, no achievement bullets.
  • Selectivity → add a qualifier only when it's accurate and the name doesn't already convey it.
  • Funded work graduates → a project, study abroad, or thesis the scholarship paid for earns its own line on its own merits.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put scholarships on a resume?

Only the competitive, merit-based ones, and only while they still signal something. A scholarship you won against a strong pool — a national award, a named or discipline-specific merit scholarship, a full-ride you competed for — is a selection signal worth showing, because it tells an employer someone chose you over other strong candidates. A need-based grant, an automatic award tied to your GPA or enrollment, a renewal you kept by staying enrolled, or a small local scholarship is a private financial fact, not an accomplishment, and it doesn't earn resume space. The second filter is time: scholarships are education-adjacent, so they're strongest for current students and new graduates and fade fast once real work experience fills the page. A decade into a career, even a prestigious scholarship usually comes off.

Where do scholarships go on a resume?

Under Education, or in a dedicated Honors and Awards section — never under experience, because a scholarship is money for your education, not a role you performed. If you have one or two worth listing, put them as a short sub-line beneath the relevant degree in your Education section: the degree, then "Recipient, [Scholarship Name], [year]." If you have several honors — scholarships, awards, dean's-list mentions — group them into their own Honors and Awards section so they read as a cluster of recognition rather than crowding the education entry. New graduates with little work history can let this section sit higher on the page; experienced candidates keep it small and low, if it stays at all.

How do you format a scholarship on a resume?

Name, awarding body, year — one clean line: "National Merit Scholarship, 2022" or "Recipient, Chevening Scholarship, UK Government, 2023." Add a short selectivity qualifier only when it's accurate and not already obvious from the name — "1 of 50 awarded nationally" earns its space when the name alone doesn't convey the competition. Do not list the dollar amount; the money isn't the signal, the selection is, and a figure reads as either boastful or beside the point. Do not describe a scholarship with achievement bullets the way you'd describe a job — it wasn't a role, so it gets a line, not a paragraph. If the scholarship funded concrete work — research, a project, study abroad — that work can graduate into its own section on its own merits, separate from the scholarship line.

What's the difference between listing a scholarship and a fellowship on a resume?

A fellowship is a funded appointment — often a role you performed, with duties and output — so it can go under experience formatted like a job. A scholarship is money to fund your education, and you didn't perform a role to receive it, so it never goes under experience; it lives under Education or Honors as a recognition line. That's the core split: a fellowship raises the question "was this work or recognition," while a scholarship is only ever recognition, and its real question is "is this competitive enough, and recent enough, to earn a line at all." If something called a scholarship actually involved a defined project or job you carried out, treat that work like a fellowship and let it graduate into experience — but the funding itself stays a one-line honor.

Should you list high school scholarships on a resume?

Only very early, and briefly. If you're a current college student or a new graduate with a thin page, a genuinely competitive high school scholarship — a national award, a large merit scholarship — can appear as a single line, usually under Education. But it ages out faster than almost anything else on a resume: once you have college honors, internships, or any real work experience, high school scholarships come off entirely, the same way high school itself drops off once you have a degree. If you're more than a year or two past college, a high school scholarship is taking up space a stronger, more recent line should own. When in doubt, cut it and let your most recent accomplishments carry the page.
