How to list publications on a resume

Most things on a resume get folded into an existing section. Publications are the exception: when they're relevant, they earn a dedicated "Publications" section, each entry written as a full citation in one consistent style, your own name in bold, and the most relevant work first. Here's when to include them, what counts, how to cite co-authored and forthcoming work, and how to tame a list that's grown too long.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The one-line version: put publications in their own section, cite them all the same way, and bold your name. The audience that reads this section — hiring committees, research leads, principal investigators — reads it closely, so consistency and correct citation matter more here than almost anywhere else on the page. Everything below is the detail: whether to include the section at all, what belongs in it, and how to format the tricky entries.

First: should publications even be on your resume?

The section is powerful when it's evidence for the job and dead weight when it isn't. Use this test:

  • Include a full section for academic, research, scientific, medical, and R&D roles — anywhere published work is the qualification. Same for postdoc, PhD, faculty, and lab positions where a hiring committee expects to see it.
  • Include one or two selected items when a specific publication directly proves a skill the role wants — a data scientist citing a well-received methods paper, a technical writer citing a widely-read industry report.
  • Leave it off for most other jobs. A peer-reviewed paper is proof for a research post and noise on a resume for a sales, operations, or general management role. When in doubt, ask whether the reader will care — if not, that space is better spent on work experience.

This is a relevance decision, the same one behind what to put on a resume at all: every section has to earn its space by mattering to this job.

What counts as a publication

Keep the section credible by including only things that were genuinely published or peer-reviewed:

Counts

  • Peer-reviewed journal articles
  • Conference papers & proceedings
  • Books and book chapters
  • Formally published reports / whitepapers
  • Patents (often a separate line or section — a patent is granted IP, not literature)
  • Bylined articles in recognized outlets
  • Preprints — labeled as such

Doesn't (put elsewhere)

  • Internal company documentation
  • Unpublished drafts or class papers
  • Ordinary personal blog posts
  • Slide decks or webinar recordings
  • Anything you can't point a reader to

Work that's real but isn't a formal publication often belongs under projects (things you built or shipped) or a portfolio link instead — that's the line between this section and those. And recognition for your work — a best-paper prize, a fellowship — goes under awards and achievements, not here; publications list what you produced, awards list what you won for it.

How to cite each entry

Pick one citation style — APA, MLA, IEEE, or Chicago — and apply it identically to every line. Which one you choose matters far less than using it consistently; mixed formats read as carelessness to the exact people scrutinizing this section. A clean, resume-friendly pattern:

Authors (Year). Title of the work. Venue, volume(issue), pages. DOI/link

Bold your own name in the author list. Keep co-authors in their real published order — never move yourself up.

→ Example: Rivera, J., Chen, A., & Okafor, B. (2025). Sparse retrieval for long-context models. Proc. NeurIPS, 38, 1120–1134.

Practical rules that keep it tight on a resume:

  • Bold your name so a reader finds your contribution in one glance across a dozen authors.
  • Add a DOI or short link if space allows — it lets the reader verify and read the work.
  • Abbreviate very long author lists if needed (e.g. Rivera, J., Chen, A., et al.), but always keep your own name visible.
  • Don't invent a citation style mid-list, and don't mix numbered IEEE entries with APA parentheticals.

Order and grouping

Two workable orderings — choose based on your goal:

ApproachUse it when
Reverse chronologicalRecency signals an active research trajectory — the default for CVs and most academic applications.
By relevance / importanceOn a short resume, lead with the pieces closest to the target role or your highest-impact venues, regardless of date.
Grouped by typeYou have many entries — split into Journal Articles, Conference Papers, Books & Chapters, each internally reverse-chronological.

If you number entries, some people count backwards (newest = highest number) so the total publication count is visible. That's a CV convention; on a resume, keeping it simple usually reads better.

The long-list problem: "Selected Publications"

A resume is one to two pages — see how long a resume should be — so a 40-item publication list simply won't fit, and dumping it there fights the whole point of a resume, which is to sample, not to catalog. The fix:

  • Title the section "Selected Publications" and include your five to eight strongest or most relevant pieces.
  • End with a link to the complete record: Full list: Google Scholar / ORCID / personal site.
  • Curate for the audience — pick the items closest to the role you're targeting, then the highest-impact ones.
Resume vs CV. This is the same content at two scales. A CV has no length limit and lists every publication, often grouped by type and placed high up, because the complete record is the point. A resume samples: "Selected Publications", a handful of items, a link to the rest. If you're applying in academia or research, you likely need the CV version; for industry, the resume version.

Forthcoming, under review, and co-authored work

The edge cases people get wrong:

  • Accepted but not yet printed → label it (in press) or (forthcoming) in place of the year. It's legitimate to list — it's been accepted.
  • Submitted, not yet accepted → only include if it strengthens your case, and mark it clearly (under review) so no one thinks it's published. Never let a submission look like an accepted paper.
  • Preprints → fine to include, labeled (preprint, arXiv:…), especially in fast-moving fields — but don't disguise a preprint as a peer-reviewed paper.
  • Co-authored work → keep the real author order; bolding your name is how you show your part. If your specific contribution matters, a one-line note ("lead author; designed the experiments") is better than reordering the byline.

The through-line: never overstate status. The people who read this section can check, and an inflated entry costs you more credibility than an honest "under review" ever would — it's the same honesty principle behind avoiding common resume mistakes.

Quick reference

  • Where do publications go? → their own "Publications" section, usually after experience and education.
  • Include the section? → yes for academic/research/scientific roles; only relevant items elsewhere; skip it if it doesn't help.
  • Citation style → pick one (APA/MLA/IEEE/Chicago) and use it identically everywhere.
  • Your name → bold it; keep co-author order as published.
  • Order → reverse chronological, or by relevance on a short resume; group by type if long.
  • Long list → "Selected Publications" (5–8) + a link to Google Scholar / ORCID.
  • Not yet out → label in press / forthcoming / under review — never overstate.
  • Not a real publication? → move it to projects or a portfolio, not here.

Frequently asked questions

How do you list publications on a resume?

Give them a dedicated "Publications" section, usually placed after your experience and education. List each one as a full citation in a single, consistent style — author names, year, title, venue (journal, conference, or publisher) — and bold your own name so a reader can find your contribution instantly. Order them with the most relevant or most recent first. If the list is long, title the section "Selected Publications", include your five to eight strongest, and link to a complete record such as Google Scholar or ORCID.

Should you put publications on a resume?

Include them when they're evidence for the job. For academic, research, scientific, medical, and some technical or thought-leadership roles, publications are core proof of expertise and belong on the resume. For most other jobs they aren't expected, so leave them off unless a specific piece directly demonstrates a skill the role wants — in which case one or two selected items are enough. The test is relevance: a peer-reviewed paper matters for a research position; it's noise on a resume for a sales role.

What counts as a publication on a resume?

Peer-reviewed journal articles, conference papers and proceedings, books and book chapters, and formally published reports or whitepapers all count. Patents, preprints, and substantial bylined articles in recognized outlets can be included, ideally grouped or labeled so their status is clear. What generally doesn't count as a "publication" is internal company documentation, unpublished drafts, class assignments, or ordinary personal blog posts — those may belong under projects or a portfolio, not a publications section.

How do you cite a publication on a resume?

Pick one citation style — APA, MLA, IEEE, or Chicago — and use it identically for every entry. A workable format is: Authors (Year). Title. Venue, volume/pages. Bold your own name in the author list, keep co-authors in their real order, add a DOI or link if space allows, and label anything not yet out as "forthcoming", "in press", or "under review". Consistency matters more than which style you choose; mixing formats looks careless to exactly the audience that reads this section closely.

Where do publications go on a resume versus a CV?

On a one-to-two-page resume, publications go in a compact section after experience and education, usually as "Selected Publications" with only your strongest work. On an academic CV, which has no length limit, publications get a full, exhaustive section — often high up and grouped by type (journal articles, conference papers, books) — because the complete record is the point. Same content, different scale: the resume samples, the CV catalogs.
