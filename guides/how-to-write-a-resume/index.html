How to write a resume, step by step

A resume has one job: get you the interview. This walkthrough covers the seven sections, what goes in each, the ATS rules that decide whether a human ever reads it, and the errors that quietly get resumes cut — plus links to a deeper guide for every step.

You don't need a design degree or a fancy template. You need the right sections, in the right order, filled with specific results a recruiter can scan in six seconds — and a layout the applicant tracking system can read. That's the whole game, and this guide walks it end to end. If you want the build mechanics instead — format, template, export — see how to make a resume; this page is about the words.

The seven steps

Header — make you easy to reach

Name, one professional title, phone, a professional email, your city, and a LinkedIn or portfolio link. No photo, no full home address, no date of birth — those add risk and waste space.

Summary — earn the next six seconds

Two or three lines naming your role, years of experience, and one or two headline results, tailored to the job. Skip an "objective" unless you're a student or career-changer — see objective vs summary for which to use.

Experience — the core of the resume

Reverse-chronological: most recent job first. For each role give title, company, dates, and three to five bullets. Every bullet starts with a strong action verb and carries a result — ideally a number. This is where you win or lose; learn the shape in how to write bullet points. Got gaps? Here's how to handle employment gaps.

Education — short and factual

Degree, institution, graduation year. Recent grads put this above experience; everyone else keeps it below. Details in how to list education.

Skills — concrete and matched

List real hard skills and tools that appear in the job posting, not generic "team player" claims. See which skills to include and how to weave in the right keywords.

Optional sections — only if they help

Certifications, projects, volunteer work, or languages, when they're relevant to the target role. Hobbies rarely earn their space — here's when they do. Keep references off entirely: why.

Format, tailor, proofread

Make it ATS-safe (single column, standard headings, real text, the right file type), keep it to the right length, tailor it to each job, and proofread. Then check it against the common mistakes list.

The ATS reality

Most applications pass through an applicant tracking system before any human sees them. It parses your file into plain text and searches it for the skills in the job description. If your layout uses tables, columns, text boxes, or graphics, that parse can scramble — and a scrambled resume gets filtered out no matter how strong your experience is. Pick a clean single-column format, use standard section headings, and export a text-based PDF. Boring formatting is a feature here.

The whole thing in one line. Seven sections in order — header, summary, experience, education, skills, optional, format — every experience bullet a verb plus a result, one page, single column, tailored to the job, proofread. Do that and you're ahead of most of the pile.

A quick before/after

Weak bullet Responsible for managing social media accounts and posting content.
Strong bullet Grew Instagram from 4k to 27k followers in 8 months by shipping a daily content calendar, lifting referral traffic 3×.

Same job — the second one names a verb, a scope, a result, and a timeframe. That's the difference every bullet should aim for.

Frequently asked questions

What are the main sections of a resume?

Header, professional summary, work experience (reverse-chronological), education, skills, and optional sections like certifications or projects. Experience is the core; the rest supports it.

How long should a resume be?

One page for most people; up to two only with roughly ten or more years of relevant experience. See the full answer.

What order should a resume go in?

Header, summary, experience, education, skills — most recent job first within experience. New graduates can move education above experience.

Do I need a different resume for every job?

Not from scratch, but tailor it: match your summary and top bullets to each posting's language and priorities. Here's how.
