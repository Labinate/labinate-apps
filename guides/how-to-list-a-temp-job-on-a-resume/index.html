How to list a temp job on a resume

Temp work isn't contract work and it isn't job-hopping — it's W-2 employment through a staffing agency. That one fact answers almost every question people have about listing it. The whole game comes down to a single question: who was your employer, the agency or the client company? Get that right, group your assignments correctly, and three short placements in two years read as steady, in-demand work — not a churn of jobs you keep leaving.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

Here's the fear that makes people hide temp work: you did a six-month assignment, then a four-month one, then a three-month one, and now your resume looks like you can't hold a job. So you either leave the temp roles off — and open an unexplained gap — or you list them raw and brace for the "why so many short jobs?" question. Both are avoidable. Temp assignments have a built-in, recruiter-friendly explanation the moment you format them as what they are: agency placements with planned end dates. This guide shows you how to name the employer correctly, group multiple assignments so they read as one continuous job, and make sure a short assignment never gets mistaken for a firing.

The core idea: your employer was the staffing agency; the client was where you worked. Naming both — agency as the anchor, client as the placement — is what makes temp work simultaneously honest to a background check and meaningful to a recruiter. Everything else is grouping and labeling.

First, what "temp" means — and what it isn't

"Temp job" gets used loosely for anything short, which is exactly why it's confusing to list. Pin down which arrangement you actually had, because the label you choose has to be accurate.

Not quite the same as…How temp work differs
Contract workContract work usually means you were an independent contractor (1099), engaged directly or through a firm, often at a higher skill and rate level. Temp work means you were a W-2 employee of a staffing agency that placed you at a client. They're listed the same way, but the word matters: calling temp "contract" overstates your independence, and calling contract "temp" understates your level.
A single short-term jobA short-term job is one brief stint at a normal employer — a keep-or-cut decision about a single anomaly. Temp work is a pattern of intentionally short assignments, so the framing problem is different: you're not explaining one blip, you're making a series of planned placements read as coherent, steady employment.
FreelancingA freelancer finds and bills their own clients. In temp work the agency finds the client and employs you. If you truly ran your own book of business, that's freelance or self-employment, listed under your own name — not temp work.
Job-hoppingThis is the misread you're defending against. Job-hopping is choosing to leave permanent roles quickly. Temp assignments end because they were designed to end — the short duration is the arrangement, not a red flag about you. The temp label is what tells a reader which of the two they're looking at.

The one question that decides the format: agency or client?

When you list a temp role, two names are in play — the staffing agency and the client company where you did the work. People freeze here because both feel like "the employer." The answer is that you name both, in a specific order, and each does a different job.

The agency — your legal employer

  • Paid you and issued your W-2
  • What employment verification confirms
  • The honest answer to "who employed you"
  • → Use it as the anchor / heading

The client — where the work happened

  • The recognizable, relevant name a recruiter cares about
  • Where your actual accomplishments took place
  • Not your legal employer — don't list it as one
  • → Name it as the placement, in parentheses

Put them together and the entry is both truthful and useful:

The clean single-assignment format

Robert Half (staffing agency) — Financial Analyst
Contract placement at Regional Health Network · Mar–Sep 2026

The agency anchors the entry so a background check matches; the client tells the recruiter where and what. "Contract placement" or "Temporary" labels the arrangement so the six-month span reads as planned, not as a job you left.

Listing only the client as your employer is the common mistake — it reads cleaner, but it won't match employment verification, and a mismatch on a background check is a far bigger problem than a temp label ever was. List only the agency, on the other hand, and you throw away the recognizable name that makes the role relevant. Name both.

The move that fixes everything: group your assignments

This is the single most important technique for temp work, and it's the one most people miss. If you did several placements through the same agency, don't list them as separate jobs. Group them under one agency heading, with each client as a sub-entry. Five entries collapse into one continuous line of employment — which is exactly what it was.

Grouping is honest — it was one employer — and it reframes the whole story. Instead of "why do they keep leaving?" the reader sees "this person was kept busy on assignment after assignment because clients wanted them." That's the reputation temp work should build, and grouping is what surfaces it. If your assignments were through different agencies, you can still group by agency, or if each was substantial, list them as normal roles — just keep the temp label on each.

Make each assignment earn its line

A temp placement is only "just a temp job" if you describe it like one. The reader doesn't discount work because it was temporary — they discount it because it's written as a duty list. Treat each assignment like any real role: lead with what you did and delivered, not what you were assigned to do.

Quantify, even on a short assignment

Three months is enough to point to a result. Numbers beat tenure — "processed 200+ invoices/week at 99% accuracy" says more than "responsible for accounts payable" from a two-year role. A short assignment with a real outcome outperforms a long one described as a job description.

Show extensions and conversions — they're gold

If an assignment was extended or you were converted to a permanent role, say so: 6-month assignment extended to 14 months or converted to full-time Analyst. A client choosing to keep a temp is one of the strongest endorsements on a resume — it's proof the work was wanted.

Use consistent, honest dates

Month-and-year dates on every entry. Don't stretch a placement's dates to hide a gap between assignments — small gaps between temp roles are normal and expected. Consistency and honesty read as more stable than padded dates that unravel on verification.

A few common situations

Temp-to-hire that converted to permanent

List it as one continuous role, and note the conversion: Analyst — [Company] (started as a temp placement via [Agency], converted to permanent Jul 2026). You get full credit for total tenure and the strong signal that a client wanted to keep you. This is the best-case temp story — don't bury the conversion.

A temp assignment covering an employment gap

This is temp work's quiet superpower. A three-month placement during a longer job search shows you were working and keeping skills current — often worth listing even if it's not perfectly on-target, precisely because it closes a hole. See the employment-gaps guide for how it fits the larger timeline.

Lots of very short, unrelated placements

You don't have to list every one. Group the relevant assignments under the agency and let the minor, off-target ones fold into the agency date range without their own line, or summarize: plus additional short placements in office administration. Relevance and clarity beat exhaustiveness — the same keep-or-cut judgment applies within a temp history.

A current temp role while you job-search

List it in the present tense with the temp label — it shows you're active and employed. There's nothing awkward about looking for permanent work while on assignment; that's the normal arc of temp employment, and recruiters know it.

The bright line — name your real employer. Listing the client company as if it directly hired you is tempting because the name looks better, but it will not match your W-2 or employment verification, and a discrepancy on a background check reads as dishonesty even when you were just simplifying. Always keep the staffing agency as the employer of record and the client as the placement. The honest version — agency plus client, clearly labeled temporary — is both stronger and safer than a cleaner-looking one that falls apart on the first check.

Quick reference

  • Temp ≠ contract ≠ job-hopping → it's W-2 work through a staffing agency, with planned end dates. Label it "temporary" or "contract placement" so short spans read as designed.
  • Name both, in order → agency as the employer of record (matches verification), client as the placement in parentheses (the relevant name).
  • Group assignments → one agency heading, each client as a sub-entry. Five short "jobs" become one continuous, steady-looking tenure.
  • Make each line earn it → quantify a result even on a three-month stint; surface any extension or conversion, because a client keeping you is a top endorsement.
  • Bright line → never list the client as your direct employer; it won't match your W-2 and a background-check mismatch is worse than any temp label.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put temp jobs on your resume?

Usually yes. Temp work is real, verifiable employment — it fills your timeline, shows you were working, and often demonstrates exactly the skills a target job wants. Leaving relevant temp roles off can also create an unexplained gap that raises more questions than the temp work would. The exception is a very old or completely irrelevant assignment that adds nothing and isn't needed to cover a gap; that you can cut. The real issue is almost never whether to include temp work — it's how to format it so several short assignments read as steady employment instead of a string of jobs you left.

Who do you list as the employer for a temp job — the agency or the company?

The staffing agency is your legal employer — it paid you, issued your W-2, and is what employment verification will confirm — so the agency name is the anchor. But the client company is where you actually did the work and is usually the more recognizable, more relevant name, so you name it too. The clean format is to list the agency as the employer and the client as the placement: "Staffing Agency Name — Business Analyst (contract placement at [Client Company])". That way the entry is both honest to a background check and meaningful to a recruiter who cares about where and what you did.

How do you list multiple temp assignments on a resume?

Group them under one heading for the staffing agency, then list each placement as a sub-entry beneath it. So the agency and your overall temp dates form the top line, and under it sit two or three client placements, each with its own role, dates, and one accomplishment. This turns what looks like five separate jobs in three years into a single continuous line of employment through one agency — which is exactly what it was. Grouping is the single most important move for making temp work read as stable rather than as job-hopping.

Is temp work the same as contract work on a resume?

No, and it helps to keep them distinct. Temp work means you were a W-2 employee of a staffing agency that placed you at a client. Contract work usually means you were an independent contractor (1099) engaged directly or through a firm, often at a higher skill and rate level. They're listed similarly — label the arrangement, name the client, quantify the work — but calling temp work "contract" can overstate your independence, and calling contract work "temp" can understate your level. Use the word that's accurate: "temporary," "temp," or "contract placement" for agency temp roles.

How do you show a temp job ended without looking like you were fired?

Label the role as temporary and it does the work for you. A recruiter who sees "Temporary — 6-month assignment" or "contract placement" understands the end was planned and built into the arrangement, not a firing. You never need to write "assignment ended" as an excuse — the temp label already signals it. If the assignment was extended or converted, that's worth showing, because "extended twice" or "converted to a permanent role" is a strong signal a client wanted to keep you. The mistake is listing a temp job with no temp label, which invites a reader to wonder why you left after four months.
