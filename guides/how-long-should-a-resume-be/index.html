How long should a resume be? One page vs two, settled

The honest answer isn't "one page" — it's "as long as it stays relevant, and no longer." For most people that lands on a single page. Here's the rule by experience level, and exactly how to cut down to size when you're over.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

"How long should a resume be" is really two questions in a trench coat: how many pages is acceptable, and how do I fit everything on them. The page count is the easy part. The hard part — the part that actually gets you the interview — is deciding what earns a spot.

So start with relevance, not length. Every line should answer "does this help prove I can do this job?" Once you cut everything that doesn't, the page count usually sorts itself out.

The rule, by experience level

Student / new graduate · 0–3 years

One page, always. You don't yet have two pages of relevant material, and padding to fill space is obvious. Lead with education, projects, internships and skills. A tight, confident single page reads far better than a stretched one.

Early to mid career · 3–10 years

One page for almost everyone. This is where most people wrongly reach for a second page. You can nearly always fit 3–4 roles at 3–5 bullets each on one page. Drop internships and part-time jobs once you have real full-time experience to show.

Senior / 10+ years

One or two pages — two is fine if earned. With a long track record, a second page is justified. Use it for depth on recent, relevant roles, not a complete history. Older jobs shrink to a title and a line; the 15-year-old role can go entirely.

Executive · academic · some technical

Two pages, occasionally more. Executive resumes, academic CVs, and roles with long publication, patent or project lists legitimately run longer. This is the exception, not a license — even here, relevance still rules every line.

The real rule: a focused two beats a padded one

Page count is a proxy, not the target. A tight two-page resume where every line pulls its weight beats a one-pager crammed to 9-point font with quarter-inch margins. And a padded two-pager — half a page of leftovers spilling onto page two — looks worse than a clean single page.

So the test is never "is this one page?" It's "does every line earn its place?" Fix that and the length follows.

How to cut a resume down to size

Over by a few lines? In rough order of what to cut first:

  1. Ancient history. Roles older than ~15 years, or before they're relevant, come off entirely or shrink to one line.
  2. Weak bullets. Keep the strongest 3–5 per job. A bullet without a result or a real responsibility is the first to go.
  3. Dead lines. Your street address, "References available on request", an objective statement, and a skills list that repeats the bullets — all reclaimable space.
  4. Wordiness. "Was responsible for managing a team of five" becomes "Managed a 5-person team." Tighter and stronger.
  5. Layout. A single-column layout with sensible margins and one clean font recovers more room than shrinking text — and stays readable to both humans and applicant tracking systems.
Don't shrink the font to win. Anything under 10-point body text or margins under half an inch signals you couldn't make the cut. If it only fits by squeezing, it doesn't fit — cut a bullet instead. Readability beats a page count every time.

Frequently asked questions

How long should a resume be?

For most people, one page. Use one page under about 10 years of relevant experience, and up to two when you have more and every line earns its place. Past two pages is rarely justified outside academic CVs and some senior roles.

Is a one-page resume better than two?

Not automatically — a focused two-pager beats a padded one-pager. The rule is relevance, not page count: keep whatever proves you can do this job and cut the rest. One page is simply where most people land once they cut the filler.

When is a two-page resume acceptable?

When you have roughly 10+ years of relevant experience, a long publication or project list, or a senior role where breadth matters. If you use two pages, fill the second — half a page of leftovers looks worse than a tight single page.

How do I make my resume shorter?

Cut roles older than ~15 years, trim each job to its strongest three to five bullets, remove the address and "references available on request", and tighten wording. A single-column layout with sensible margins usually recovers more space than expected.
