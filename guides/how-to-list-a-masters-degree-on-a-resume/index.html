How to list a master's degree on a resume

A master's degree is the strongest line in most Education sections — but only if it's placed and named right. Put it below your bachelor's, spell it "Masters of Science," or bury it under high-school details, and you undersell the one credential that outranks everything else on the page. The fixes are small and the payoff is immediate. Here's exactly how to list it.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Here's the whole thing in one sentence. A master's degree is a vertical level-up, correctly named — a completed graduate credential that ranks above your bachelor's — so you list it first in Education, name it precisely, and then let its rank do the work instead of over-explaining it. The failure modes are all small and all avoidable: ordering it below the bachelor's, misspelling the degree ("Masters of Science"), padding the section with high school and a decade-old GPA, or dressing up an in-progress master's as finished. Get the four basics right and a single education line quietly tells a recruiter you did the advanced work.

1. Put the master's first — it outranks the bachelor's

The Education section is ordered by level and recency, and a master's wins on both counts: it's higher than a bachelor's and it's newer. So it takes the top line, with the bachelor's directly beneath it. This one is rarely done wrong on purpose, but people who list degrees in the order they think of them — or oldest-first — bury their best credential.

Correct order

M.S. in Data Analytics — Northwestern University, 2025

B.A. in Economics — University of Michigan, 2021

Highest and most recent on top. The bachelor's confirms the foundation, briefly.

A separate decision is where the whole Education section sits on the page. If you're a recent graduate and the master's is your headline, keep Education near the top. Once you have a few years of relevant work experience, Experience leads and Education moves toward the bottom — but inside it, the master's still comes first.

2. Name it correctly — this is where most resumes slip

The degree has an exact name, and getting it wrong is a small but visible tell. Spelled out, it's Master of Science or Master of Arts — no apostrophe, and no "of" hanging after a possessive. Abbreviated, it's M.S. (or MS) and M.A. (or MA). Choose one style and use it for every degree in the section.

WrongRight
Masters of Science in NursingMaster of Science in Nursing (or M.S.N.)
Master's of ScienceMaster of Science — "master's degree" is fine as a phrase; the degree name has no apostrophe
Master Degree, MarketingMaster of Arts in Marketing (or M.A., Marketing)
Master's (no field)Always name the field — "Master of Public Health," not just "Master's degree"

The apostrophe rule trips people up because the everyday phrase — "I have a master's degree" — is possessive and correct. But the formal degree title on the resume line is "Master of Science," not "Master's of Science." Small thing; recruiters notice it.

If your field uses a post-nominal credential — an M.S.W., M.P.H., or M.B.A. — you can also place the abbreviation after your name at the top of the resume, the way licensure credentials are shown, when the industry expects it.

3. Include what matters, cut what doesn't

Every master's line needs four things: the degree, the field, the institution, and the graduation year. Everything else is optional and belongs only when it's relevant to the target job.

Add only if relevant

  • A concentration that matches the role ("M.S. in CS, concentration in Machine Learning").
  • A thesis title for research or academic roles.
  • Honors or a strong, recent GPA (≈3.5+) early in your career.

Cut once you hold a master's

  • High school — a graduate degree makes it irrelevant and dates you.
  • A decade-old GPA — mid-career, it looks like reaching.
  • Heavy coursework lists on the bachelor's — trim it to one line.

The principle: the master's earns detail, the bachelor's earns a line. Once the graduate degree is on the page, the undergraduate one usually needs only degree, field, school, and year — enough to confirm the foundation, not enough to compete for attention.

4. In progress? Mark it honestly

A master's earned while you work is common and reads as ambition — but only if you label it as unfinished. Never give an in-progress degree a clean past-tense graduation year it hasn't earned; an employer who verifies enrollment will catch the gap, and there's nothing to gain by hiding it.

Honest and clear

  • "M.S. in Finance — expected May 2027."
  • "M.A. in History (in progress)" + a line of relevant completed coursework.
  • "Coursework complete, thesis in progress" when that's the real status.

Reads as inflated

  • A finished-looking year on a degree not yet earned.
  • "M.S., 2027" with no "expected" — ambiguous at best, false at worst.
  • Listing it as complete because the last class is "almost" done.

If you're only partway through and unsure how much to show, the in-progress degree guide has the exact phrasing for every stage — including how to handle a master's you started but paused.

The one bright line. A master's is a completed graduate level-up, so let its rank do the work. List it above the bachelor's, name it precisely, trim what sits below it, and mark an unfinished one "expected." A correctly-placed, correctly-named degree needs no explanation — the reader grants it authority on sight. The only ways to lose that are to misplace it, misspell it, or dress up an in-progress one as done.

What this is not

"Master's degree" is a family, and a few close relatives sit differently on a resume. Getting the category right sets both where the item goes and how much authority it carries.

The bottom line. Your master's is the highest, most recent credential on the page, so treat it that way: put it first in Education, write the degree name exactly, give it its field and year, trim the bachelor's and cut high school, and if it's still underway, say "expected" and mean it. Four small moves, and the one line that most changes how a recruiter reads you does its job in a single glance.

Quick reference

  • Master's first → it outranks and postdates the bachelor's; top line of Education.
  • Name it exactly → "Master of Science in X" or "M.S., X" — never "Masters of Science."
  • Four essentials → degree · field · institution · graduation year.
  • Trim below it → cut high school; drop a stale GPA; shrink the bachelor's to one line.
  • Add only if relevant → concentration, thesis, or strong recent honors.
  • In progress → "expected [date]" → never a finished-looking year on an unearned degree.

Frequently asked questions

Does the master's degree go before or after the bachelor's on a resume?

Before it. The Education section is ordered by level and recency, and a master's degree both outranks your bachelor's and is more recent, so it goes on the top line. List the master's first with its institution and graduation year, then the bachelor's below it. A separate question is where the whole Education section sits on the page: if you are a recent graduate and the degree is your strongest selling point, Education goes near the top, above or just under a short summary; once you have a few years of relevant work, Experience leads and Education drops toward the bottom, where the master's still sits above the bachelor's. Either way, within the section the graduate degree comes first.

How do I correctly write a master's degree on a resume?

Spell it out as "Master of Science" or "Master of Arts" — no apostrophe and no "of" after a possessive — or abbreviate it consistently as M.S./MS or M.A./MA. So "Master of Science in Data Analytics" or "M.S., Data Analytics" are both correct; "Masters of Science," "Master's of Science," and "Master Degree" are all wrong. Pick the spelled-out or abbreviated style and use it consistently across every degree in the section. Always name the field ("Master of Science in Nursing"), because a degree line that just says "Master's degree" with no subject forces the reader to guess. If your field uses a post-nominal credential — for example an M.S.W., M.P.H., or M.B.A. — you can also place the abbreviation after your name at the top of the resume when it signals licensure or is expected in the industry.

Should I still list my bachelor's degree and GPA once I have a master's?

Keep the bachelor's — always list it below the master's, with institution and graduation year — because it shows the foundation the graduate degree was built on and confirms an unbroken academic record. But trim it: once you hold a master's, the bachelor's usually needs only the degree, field, school, and year, without coursework, activities, or honors unless one is genuinely notable. Drop your GPA unless it is recent and strong (roughly 3.5 or above) and you are early in your career; a mid-career candidate listing a decade-old GPA looks like they are reaching. And drop high school entirely — a master's makes it irrelevant, and keeping it dates you. The goal is a clean two-line education section that leads with the highest, most recent credential and gives everything below it just enough to be credible.

How do I list a master's degree that is still in progress?

List it as in progress and never in a way that implies it is finished. The standard formats are "Master of Science in Finance — expected May 2027" or "M.A. in History (in progress)." Include the institution and, if it helps, a line noting relevant completed coursework, so an in-progress degree still demonstrates current, advancing knowledge. If your coursework is done but your thesis or capstone is not, say exactly that ("coursework complete, thesis in progress") rather than leaving it ambiguous. The one thing you must not do is write the degree with a clean past-tense graduation year it has not earned yet — an employer who verifies enrollment will catch the gap, and an in-progress master's honestly labeled is a strength, not something to disguise. A master's underway while you work is common and reads as ambition.

Is a master's degree the same as an MBA or a graduate certificate?

No. An MBA is a specific kind of master's — a Master of Business Administration — with its own naming and placement conventions, so if that is your degree, follow the dedicated MBA guide; this guide covers the broader family of academic and professional master's degrees such as the M.S., M.A., M.Ed., M.P.H., and M.S.W. A graduate certificate is something different entirely: it is a short, focused cluster of graduate-level courses, not a full degree, so it does not belong on a degree line in the Education section — list it under certifications or professional development, where it reads accurately as a credential rather than a degree. And a PhD is a level above a master's: a doctorate earned through original research and a dissertation, with its own conventions. Getting the category right matters because it sets both where the item goes on the page and how much authority the reader grants it.
