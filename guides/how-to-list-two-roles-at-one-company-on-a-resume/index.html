How to list two roles at one company on a resume

You wore two hats at one employer — held two positions at once. List them as two separate entries and it reads as a data-entry error; blur them into one bullet list and neither role gets credit. Keep it one employer block, both titles legible, results grouped by hat, framed as range and trust — not overload. Lead with the hat that matches the job.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Here's the situation almost every startup and small-business employee eventually has to put on a page: for a stretch, you did two jobs at once at one company. Officially or not, you were the Office Manager and the Marketing Lead; you ran Operations and Customer Success; you were the engineer who was also, in practice, the product manager. It's one of the most common shapes a real career takes on a lean team — and one of the easiest to wreck on a resume. There are two ways people ruin it. They either split it into two separate entries under the same employer, so a reader sees the company name twice with overlapping dates and reads a duplicate, a typo, or job-hopping-at-one-company — or they mash both roles into a single undifferentiated list, where the marketing wins and the operations wins dissolve into a "jack of all trades" blur that reads as scattered instead of skilled. Handled right, two concurrent roles is a genuinely strong signal: the company trusted one person with the scope of two, and you delivered on both. The whole task is to make that legible.

The core idea: one employer block, both titles legible, results grouped by hat. Don't split it into two top-level entries that read as a duplicate — keep a single continuous tenure with both titles stacked or combined. Separate the two functions so each role's scope is scannable on its own, frame it as range and trust rather than being understaffed, and lead with the hat that matches the target job, compressing the other by relevance. Combined scope, cleanly attributed, is the case for the reader.

One employer block — not two duplicate entries

The instinct is to give each role the full treatment: two separate headers, each with the company name, each with its own date range. Resist it. When both titles sat at the same employer over the same period, two stand-alone entries produce a duplicated company name and overlapping dates — which a reader parses as a mistake, a copy-paste slip, or two jobs you left. It also shatters what should read as one continuous, trusted tenure into fragments.

Keep it a single Work Experience block. You have two clean formats:

Combined header — when the two roles are tightly linked

Put both titles on one line: "Office Manager & Marketing Lead — Acme Co. · 2022–2024." Best when the functions overlapped heavily or the second was a natural extension of the first. One title line, one date range, one set of bullets that can still be grouped by area underneath.

Nested sub-roles — when each function is substantial

Name the employer once at the top, then two sub-headers beneath it, each with its own bullets: Acme Co. (2022–2024) → Operations Manager [bullets], Customer Success Lead [bullets]. This gives each role visible, distinct scope while making it unmistakable it was one tenure — the same structure you'd use to nest roles in a rotational program, just held at once instead of in sequence.

Either way, use one continuous date range for the tenure so the reader never sees two "departures." Get the dates right and the whole block reads as one job that happened to have two titles.

Separate the two hats so neither blurs

The second failure mode is quieter but just as costly: pile every accomplishment from both roles into one long bullet list, and the reader can't tell where operations ends and marketing begins. The wins bleed together into an impression of "did a bit of everything," which reads as unfocused — the opposite of the impression you want. The fix is to keep each function's results in its own visible cluster.

Grouped by function — scannable

  • Operations: cut fulfillment time 30%, onboarded 3 vendors
  • Marketing: grew email list 4× to 18K, ran launch that drove 1,200 signups
  • Each role has visible, distinct scope
  • Reader sees two jobs done well

One undifferentiated pile

  • Nine mixed bullets, ops and marketing interleaved
  • No boundary between the two functions
  • Reads as "jack of all trades" — scattered
  • Neither role gets clear credit

Whether you use a combined header with grouped bullets or nested sub-roles, the principle is the same: a reader should be able to see each hat's scope in one glance. And each bullet should still be a result, not a duty — the same accomplishments-over-responsibilities rule applies to both hats. Two roles, each quantified, beats two roles smeared into one.

Frame it as range and trust — never as overload

How you talk about the arrangement decides whether it reads as a strength or a liability. The exact same facts can land either way.

Reads as baggage

  • "Covered two roles because we were short-staffed"
  • Implies you were stretched thin — did you do either well?
  • Sounds like a complaint about the workload

Reads as capability

  • "Took on marketing alongside operations as the team scaled"
  • Implies the company trusted you with more
  • Backed by results in each area, not just claimed

Two roles held at once is, at its core, evidence of capacity, range, and trust — the company handed one person the scope of two, and the results in both areas prove it worked. Present it that way. Let the quantified outcomes carry the breadth, and never editorialize about being understaffed; that turns your strongest signal into a red flag about whether the work was any good.

Tailor — lead with the hat that matches the job

You don't owe both roles equal airtime. Which hat leads, and how much of the second you keep, is a tailoring decision driven by the job you're applying for.

Applying for a marketing role

Put the marketing title first, give it the fuller set of bullets, and compress the operations role to a line or two that still shows the breadth. The reader sees a focused marketing fit up front, with "also ran operations" as evidence of range — not a competing story.

Applying for an operations role

Flip it: operations leads with the depth, marketing shrinks to a supporting line. Same tenure, same honesty — you're only choosing how much of the off-target function to keep. The fact that you held both stays visible either way.

Leading with the relevant hat does double duty: it makes the entry read as a focused fit and quietly turns the second role into a range signal rather than a distraction. State your strongest, most-relevant title first in your summary, too, so the whole page points at the job.

The bright line

Present two concurrent roles as one employer entry with both titles legible and results cleanly attributed to each. Claim the combined scope as capability — the company trusted you with two jobs and you delivered on both. Never split it into duplicate top-level entries with overlapping dates that read as an error; never let the two roles blur into one undifferentiated list where neither gets credit; never frame it as being overloaded or understaffed. And keep it honest: if the "second role" was really one extra project or a handful of stray tasks, don't inflate it into a co-equal title — reserve the two-hat treatment for a genuine second function you actually owned. Two real roles, cleanly separated and quantified, is one of the strongest capacity signals a resume can carry.

Two roles at one company vs. the situations it's mistaken for

The dual-role shape sits next to four others that look similar and are handled differently. Getting the category right decides the format.

The situationWhat it really is — and how it differs
Job shareOne role split between two people — the mirror image. In a job share, a single full-time position is staffed by two people who each work part of the week; here, one person holds two full roles at once. The test: how many roles and how many people? Two roles / one person is a dual role; one role / two people is a job share.
MoonlightingA second, separate job at a different employer on the side. Moonlighting is two employers with overlapping dates that you list as two entries; a dual role is two titles at the same employer that belong in one block. The test: same company or two different ones?
Promotion / stacked titlesSequential title changes within one continuous role — you held one title, then moved up to another, and you stack them chronologically with the newer on top. A dual role is concurrent: both titles ran over the same dates, neither replaced the other. The test: did the first title end when the second began (promotion) or run alongside it (dual role)?
Rotational programMoving through functions one at a time, in sequence, over a fixed program. A rotational program is nested sub-roles held consecutively; a dual role is two functions held simultaneously. Same nesting format, opposite timing.

Quick reference

  • One employer block → never two duplicate top-level entries with overlapping dates; combined title line or nested sub-roles, one continuous date range.
  • Separate the hats → group results by function so each role's scope is scannable; don't mash both into one pile.
  • Frame as range and trust → "took on marketing alongside operations," never "we were short-staffed."
  • Tailor → lead with the hat that matches the job, compress the other by relevance; both stay visible.
  • Each hat is quantified → results, not duties, in both clusters — achievement math applies twice.
  • Keep it honest → a genuine second function you owned earns a title; a few stray tasks don't.
  • Know the category → dual role ≠ job share ≠ moonlighting ≠ promotion.

Keep sharpening the resume

With the two roles cleanly attributed, tune the rest: make every bullet a result with achievement math, and read responsibilities vs. accomplishments to keep both hats outcome-first. Frequently asked questions

How do you list two roles at the same company on a resume?

Keep it as one employer block, not two separate top-level entries. List the company once, show both titles — either combined on one line ("Office Manager & Marketing Lead") or as two sub-headers nested under the single employer name — and give one continuous date range for the whole tenure. Underneath, group the bullets by function so a reader can see each role's scope on its own: the operations wins in one cluster, the marketing wins in another. Two separate entries with the same employer name and overlapping dates read as a data-entry error or a duplicate the reader deletes; one clean block with both hats legible reads as exactly what it was — one person the company trusted with two jobs.

Doesn't holding two roles at once look like you were overworked or the company was understaffed?

Only if you frame it that way. "Covered two roles because we were short-staffed" reads as baggage and invites the reader to wonder whether you did either job well. The same facts framed as capability read as a strength: the company trusted one person with the scope of two, and you delivered on both. Present it as range and trust — "took on the marketing function alongside operations as the team scaled" — and quantify the results in each area so the breadth is backed by evidence, not just claimed. Two roles handled well is one of the clearest signals of capacity and initiative a resume can carry; the trick is to let the outcomes, not a complaint about the workload, do the talking.

Should you split two concurrent roles into separate entries?

No — not as two separate top-level jobs. When both roles were at the same employer over the same period, two stand-alone entries with a duplicated company name and overlapping dates look like a mistake, and they fragment your tenure so a reader can't see it was one continuous stretch. Keep a single employer block. If the two functions are substantial and distinct enough that they'd blur together, use two sub-headers inside that one block — the employer named once at the top, then each title with its own cluster of bullets underneath. That gives each role visible, scannable scope without manufacturing the appearance of two separate jobs or two departures.

How do you decide which role to emphasize?

By relevance to the job you're applying for. You do not have to give both hats equal airtime — lead with the function that matches the target role and compress the other. Applying for a marketing job? Put the marketing title first, give it the fuller set of bullets, and reduce the operations role to a line or two that still shows the breadth. Applying for an operations role? Flip it. The tenure and the fact that you held both stays honest and visible either way; you're just deciding how much of the off-target function to keep, the same tailoring judgment you'd apply to any role. Leading with the relevant hat also keeps the entry from reading as scattered — the reader sees a focused fit first, with the second role as evidence of range.

Is holding two roles at one company the same as a promotion?

No, and the difference decides the format. A promotion is sequential: you held one title, then moved up to another within the same continuous role, and you show it by stacking the titles in chronological order under one employer with the newer role on top. Two concurrent roles are held at the same time — both titles run together over the same dates, neither replaced the other. You show that by presenting both titles for one shared date range with results grouped by function, not as a chronological ladder. The quick test: did the first title end when the second began? If yes, it's a promotion or a title change; if the two ran side by side, it's a dual role. It's also different from a job share, which is one role split between two people — the mirror image of two roles held by one person.
