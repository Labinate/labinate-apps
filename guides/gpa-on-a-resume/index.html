Should you put your GPA on a resume?

A GPA can be a quiet credential or dead weight — and which one it is comes down to a single number and how long ago you graduated. The short version: include it if it's 3.5 or higher and you're a recent grad, and drop it once you have real experience. Here's the full rule, the edge cases, and exactly how to format it.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The GPA question trips up almost every new graduate, because both answers feel risky. Leave it off and you worry the recruiter assumes the worst; put it on and you worry a middling number counts against you. The good news is there's a clear rule, and it takes one look at your number and your experience to apply it.

The rule: the 3.5 threshold

Recruiters and career offices converge on the same cutoff. If your GPA is 3.5 or higher, list it. If it's below 3.5, leave it off. A 3.5+ reads as a genuine strength; a 3.1 reads as a number you had to report, and a blank space says nothing at all — which, when the number isn't helping you, is exactly what you want. Nobody is rejected for omitting a GPA.

✓ Include your GPA

  • It's 3.5 or higher (on a 4.0 scale)
  • You graduated within the last 2–3 years
  • You're a student or brand-new grad
  • The job posting explicitly asks for it

✕ Leave it off

  • It's below ~3.5
  • You have 3+ years of experience
  • Your work history is the stronger story
  • You're padding the education section to fill space

Treat 3.5 as a guideline, not a law — in a very competitive field a 3.7+ is the real bar, while for some roles a 3.3 in a hard major is worth showing. But if you're unsure, the safe default is simple: 3.5 and up goes on, below stays off. The same restraint applies to the rest of your education section.

The experience override: seniority beats grades

The threshold only matters while your education is your strongest evidence. The moment you have a real job or two to point to, the GPA stops earning its line — a recruiter reading three years of shipped work does not care about your sophomore-year transcript. As a rough marker, drop the GPA once you're about three years past graduation, or as soon as you have full-time roles worth describing.

Rule of thumb

New grad with a 3.8 and one internship → keep the GPA; it's your best number. Same person four years and two jobs later → cut it, and give that space to a bullet about what you built. Experience always outranks a GPA once you have it.

The exception is fields that formally require it. Some investment banks, consulting firms, and competitive graduate or fellowship programs ask for a GPA on every application regardless of experience. When an employer asks, you answer — include it until they stop asking, even if you'd otherwise have retired it.

How to format a GPA on a resume

A GPA is a detail under your degree, not a section of its own. Put it on the same line as the degree in your education section, and always include the scale so a 3.8 isn't quietly measured against someone's 5.0 system.

Correct — GPA on the degree line, with scale B.S. in Mechanical Engineering · University of Michigan · 2025
  • GPA: 3.8/4.0

Write it as GPA: 3.8/4.0 — number, slash, scale. Round to one decimal place; "3.80" and "3.847" both look fussy. Don't invent precision, don't bold it larger than the degree, and never give it its own heading.

Major GPA and weighted GPA

If your overall GPA is under 3.5 but your major GPA clears it, list the major GPA and label it plainly: Major GPA: 3.7/4.0. This is honest — you're not hiding the overall number, you're highlighting the one that reflects the work you'll actually do. Do not list a weighted or "on a 5.0 scale" figure without saying so; an unlabeled 4.2 looks like an error, not an achievement.

Latin honors are stronger than the number

If you graduated cum laude, magna cum laude, or summa cum laude, lead with that — it's a recognized distinction that carries more weight than a raw figure, and you can list it instead of or alongside the GPA.

Honors — stronger than a bare number B.A. in Economics, magna cum laude · Boston College · 2025
  • GPA: 3.9/4.0 · Dean's List (6 semesters)

Honors, Dean's List, and scholarships are all worth a line in the education section while you're early-career — the same show-the-evidence instinct that governs the rest of the resume.

Common mistakes

  • Listing a GPA under 3.5 out of a sense of duty. No employer expects one. If it doesn't help you, the blank space helps you more.
  • Dropping the scale. "GPA: 3.8" is ambiguous; "GPA: 3.8/4.0" is not. Always name the scale.
  • Keeping a GPA years into your career. A GPA on a resume with five years of experience signals you couldn't find something better to say. Cut it and add a result instead.
  • Reporting a weighted or 5.0-scale number as if it were a 4.0. It reads as a typo. Label any non-standard scale explicitly.
  • Giving the GPA its own section or bold heading. It's one line inside education — see common resume mistakes for more over-formatting traps.
The whole thing in one breath. Include your GPA if it's 3.5 or higher and you graduated in the last couple of years, or if the employer asks. Put it on the degree line as "GPA: 3.8/4.0", scale included, one decimal. Use your major GPA if it's the stronger number, and lead with Latin honors when you have them. Once you've got a few years of experience, cut the GPA and let your work do the talking.

Frequently asked questions

Should I put my GPA on my resume?

Include it if it's 3.5 or higher and you graduated within the last two to three years, or if the employer explicitly asks. Leave it off once you have a few years of real experience — at that point your work history is the stronger signal and a GPA just takes up space. A GPA below about 3.5 is usually better left off, since a modest number can hurt more than a blank space.

Is a 3.5 GPA good enough to put on a resume?

Yes — 3.5 is the common cutoff. At or above it, listing your GPA adds a credential; below it, most people leave it off. If your overall GPA is under 3.5 but your major GPA clears it, list the major GPA and label it, e.g. "Major GPA: 3.7". Show the number that helps you, not every number you have.

How do I format a GPA on a resume?

Put it on the same line as your degree in the education section, written as "GPA: 3.8/4.0". Always include the scale so a 3.8 isn't misread against a 5.0 system. If you earned Latin honors, list those instead of or alongside the number, e.g. "B.A. in Economics, magna cum laude". Keep it to one line — the GPA is a detail under the degree, not its own section.

When should I remove my GPA from my resume?

Remove it once you have roughly three or more years of professional experience, or as soon as you have a full-time role or two worth writing about. After that, recruiters care about what you've shipped, not your transcript. The exception is fields that formally require a GPA — some finance, consulting, and graduate programs ask for it regardless of experience, so keep it there until they stop asking.
