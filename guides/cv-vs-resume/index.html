CV vs resume: what's the difference, and which to send

A resume is a short, tailored pitch — one or two pages of your most relevant experience. A CV is the complete archive — every role, publication, and credential, growing over a career. But the words also mean different things in different countries, and that's where most confusion starts.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

"Do they want a CV or a resume?" is a fair question with a two-part answer: the documents genuinely differ in length and purpose, and the same word means different things depending on where the job is. Get both parts and you'll always know what to send.

A resume answers "why you, for this role?" — so you cut everything that doesn't serve that answer and tailor it to the posting. A CV answers "what is the complete record of your scholarly work?" — so you leave nothing out and it only grows.

The geography twist

Here's the part that trips people up. The words don't mean the same thing everywhere:

United States & Canada

"Resume" and "CV" are two different documents. Use a resume for nearly all jobs; a CV only for academic, research, and medical roles.

UK, Ireland, Europe, Asia, Africa, Oceania

"CV" is just the everyday word for what Americans call a resume — a short, one-to-two page job application document. If a posting in London asks for your "CV," send a resume.

So when a listing says "CV," check where it's from. Outside North America, it almost always means the short document — not a multi-page academic record.

When you genuinely need a full CV

Reach for the long-form academic CV when applying for:

  • University faculty and lecturer positions
  • PhD programs, postdocs, and research roles
  • Grants and fellowships
  • Most medical and scientific positions

These readers expect the complete picture — your publications, conference talks, teaching history, and funding. For everything else, a resume is what wins.

Quick decision. Academic, research, or medical role in the US? Full CV. Any other job in the US? Resume. Job outside North America asking for a "CV"? They mean a resume — send one or two tailored pages.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a CV and a resume?

A resume is a short, tailored one-to-two page summary of relevant experience, adjusted per job. A CV is a long, comprehensive record of your entire academic and professional history that grows over time. A resume is a highlight reel; a CV is the full archive.

Is a CV the same as a resume?

It depends where you are. In the US and Canada they're different documents. In the UK, Europe, Asia, and most of the world, "CV" is simply the word for a resume — a short application document.

When should I use a CV instead?

Use a full academic CV for faculty roles, research, PhD/postdoc programs, grants, and most medical and scientific jobs. For nearly everything else in the US, use a resume. If a posting abroad says "CV," they mean a resume.

How long should each be?

A resume is one page early on, up to two with more experience. An academic CV has no limit and can run many pages. A "CV" requested outside academia should be treated as a resume — one or two pages.
