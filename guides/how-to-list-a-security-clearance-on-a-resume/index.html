How to list a security clearance on a resume

A clearance isn't a certificate you earned and keep — it's an active, government-held status that lapses on its own once you leave a role that needs it. To a cleared-jobs recruiter, one word on the line matters most. Is it still live — active, current, or reinstatable — and are you stating the status without spilling the classified specifics?

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Most resume lines describe something you did or something you have. A security clearance is neither, exactly — it's a permission the government granted, tied to a job, that it can and does let expire. That makes it behave nothing like a certification: you didn't pass a test to keep it forever, you don't own it, and its value to an employer is almost entirely about how live it still is. A recruiter filling a cleared position isn't impressed that you were once trusted — they're calculating whether they can put you to work without paying for a months-long, five-figure background investigation. The status word answers that question, which is why it's the most important token on the line.

The core idea: a clearance line is level + status, and status carries the value. Active or current means an employer can use it right away; inactive but reinstatable still saves them a full investigation; long-expired is barely an asset. State the level and status plainly — they're verifiable through the government's own systems — but never disclose the classified specifics: no program names, no compartments, no investigation details.

Filter one: how live is the clearance?

Before you decide how to phrase anything, be honest about status — because status is the whole reason the line has value. The government tracks the state of every clearance in its own personnel-security systems, and recruiters read the words below as precise terms, not marketing. Use the one that's true.

StatusWhat it means, and what it's worth
ActiveYou're currently in a position that uses the clearance — it's in daily force. Highest value: an employer can move you onto cleared work immediately.
CurrentGranted and still within its investigation scope and periodicity, ready to transfer to a new sponsor. Nearly as valuable — no new investigation needed.
Inactive (reinstatable)You left the cleared role, but you're still within the reinstatement window and it can be reactivated without a full new investigation. Worth listing — say so explicitly.
Expired / lapsedOutside the reinstatement window; a fresh investigation would be required. Little live value, though "previously cleared" can still signal you're investigable.

The word you choose is a claim, and it's an easily checked one. Calling a lapsed clearance "active" doesn't survive first contact with the employer's security office, so it reads as the exact kind of overstatement that ends candidacies — the same discipline that governs every honest resume claim applies with extra force here, because this one is verified against a federal record.

Filter two: level, and the line that's safe to write

Once status is settled, the level completes the line. The three baseline levels are Confidential, Secret, and Top Secret, and higher access is layered on top of Top Secret — most commonly SCI (sensitive compartmented information) access, sometimes with a counterintelligence or full-scope polygraph. Those additions raise the value of the line, so they're worth naming as designations: TS/SCI with CI polygraph. What you name is the label; what you must never name is what sits behind it.

Safe to state — and expected

The level, any higher-access designation, and the status: "Active Secret clearance", "Top Secret/SCI, current", "Public Trust (current)". These are standard, verifiable, and exactly what a cleared-role posting is screening for.

Never write — it's the thing the clearance protects

No program, project, or mission names. No SCI compartment names or descriptions of the access. No details of your investigation, adjudication, or associated dates and identifiers. Spelling these out isn't a stronger resume — it's a security violation, and it's exactly what a security officer will flag.

Describe the work you did under the clearance the way you'd describe any sensitive role — in unclassified, generalized terms: the skills, the scale, the kind of environment, never the classified subject matter. If you're translating a uniformed background, the same instinct carries over from listing military experience on a resume: convey the weight of the work without naming what can't be named.

How to format it, and where it goes

A clearance is a top-tier qualifier for the jobs that need it, so it goes high — not buried at the bottom. Give it a line, not a paragraph; the value is the level and status, and there's nothing safe to add.

The standard clearance line

Active Top Secret/SCI clearance — or Secret clearance (current, transferable). Level, then status, then any polygraph. That's the whole line.

Put it in the summary or a labeled section — near the top

For cleared roles, place it in or just under your summary as a single bold line, or in a clearly labeled Security Clearance or Clearances & Certifications block near the top. Both a recruiter and the keyword filter should hit it in the first few seconds — see what belongs in the top block.

Note citizenship when it helps

U.S. citizenship is a prerequisite for a clearance, and many cleared postings state it as a hard requirement, so some candidates add "U.S. Citizen" beside the clearance line. It's optional, but it preempts a question the posting is already asking.

Match the term to the posting

Write the level exactly as the job asks for it — "Top Secret," "TS/SCI," "Secret." Filters match on the literal phrase, so a paraphrase can drop you before a human reads a word. When the job doesn't require a clearance, move the line down or cut it — there it's just noise.

Useful and safe vs. oversharing

The failure mode here isn't padding — it's saying too much. One column gets you hired; the other gets you flagged.

Precise and safe

  • Level + accurate status on one line
  • Placed high — summary or a labeled block
  • Polygraph named as a designation only
  • Cleared work described in unclassified terms

Oversharing or overstating

  • A lapsed clearance called "active"
  • Program or compartment names spelled out
  • Investigation dates and details listed
  • Classified subject matter in the bullets

A clearance line, done right, says one thing an employer badly wants to hear: you're ready to work without a wait or a bill. That's a genuine, sometimes decisive advantage in the defense and intelligence market — but only when the status is true, the placement is high, and the specifics stay behind the label. State what you're cleared to; never state what you were cleared for.

Quick reference

  • It's a status, not a credential → a clearance is government-held and role-tied, and it lapses on its own — treat it accordingly.
  • Status is the value → active > current > inactive-but-reinstatable > expired; use the accurate word, it's verified.
  • Line = level + status → "Active TS/SCI clearance" — plus any polygraph designation, nothing more.
  • Place it high → summary or a labeled Clearances block near the top, term written exactly as the posting asks.
  • Never the specifics → no program or compartment names, no investigation details — that's a security violation, not a stronger resume.
  • Irrelevant off cleared work → outside the cleared market, the line is usually just noise; drop it.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put a security clearance on your resume?

If you hold an active or current clearance and you're applying to roles that require one, yes — put it high on the page, because it's often the single most valuable line on your resume. A recruiter filling a cleared position is looking for exactly this: a candidate they can put to work without paying for a months-long, five-figure investigation. State the level and the status plainly. If your clearance has lapsed, list it only when it's still within the reinstatement window — say "inactive, eligible for reinstatement" — because a clearance that can be reactivated without a full new investigation still saves the employer time and money. If it's long expired and can't be reinstated, it's no longer a live asset, though noting that you were "previously cleared" can still signal you're investigable. And if you're applying outside the cleared world entirely, a clearance is usually irrelevant and can come off.

How do you write a security clearance on a resume?

Level plus status, on one line: "Active Top Secret/SCI clearance" or "Secret clearance (current, transferable)." The level is Confidential, Secret, or Top Secret, with any higher access — SCI, or a CI or full-scope polygraph — appended: "TS/SCI with CI polygraph." The status word is the part recruiters scan for, so make it explicit: active means you're in a role that currently uses it; current means it's granted and in scope; inactive means it lapsed but may be reinstatable. Put the line in your summary, in a dedicated Security Clearance line near the top, or in a Clearances and Certifications section. Because U.S. citizenship is a prerequisite for a clearance, some candidates note it alongside. Keep it to the level and status — nothing more.

What should you never include about a clearance on a resume?

Never the classified specifics. Do not list the names of programs, projects, or missions you supported; do not name SCI compartments or describe the access they granted; do not include details of your background investigation, your adjudication, or dates and identifiers tied to it. The level and status are expected and verifiable through the government's own personnel-security systems, so stating them is standard — but the specifics of what you did under that clearance are exactly what the clearance exists to protect, and spelling them out is a security violation, not a stronger resume. Describe your cleared work in unclassified, generalized terms: the skills, the scale, the type of environment, never the classified subject matter.

What's the difference between an active, current, and inactive clearance?

These words describe how live your clearance is, and the difference is money and time to an employer. Active usually means you're presently in a position that uses the clearance — it's in daily force. Current means the clearance was granted and is still within its investigation scope and periodicity, so it's ready to transfer to a new sponsor. Inactive means you've left the cleared role and the clearance is dormant; it can often be reinstated without a full new investigation if you're still within the reinstatement window, which is why "inactive but eligible for reinstatement" is worth stating. Once that window closes, the clearance is effectively expired and you'd need a fresh investigation. Use the most accurate word — recruiters and the government record know the difference, and overstating it is caught fast.

Where should a security clearance go on a resume?

High, where both a human recruiter and the applicant-tracking system will catch it in the first few seconds. For cleared roles, the clearance is a top-tier qualifier, so it belongs in or just under your summary — a single bold line like "Active TS/SCI clearance" — or at the very least in a clearly labeled Security Clearance or Clearances and Certifications section near the top, not buried at the bottom next to hobbies. Many defense and intelligence job postings are filtered on the clearance keyword before a human ever reads the resume, so the exact term ("Top Secret," "TS/SCI," "Secret") needs to appear as written. If a specific job doesn't require a clearance, move it down or drop it — there it's just noise.
