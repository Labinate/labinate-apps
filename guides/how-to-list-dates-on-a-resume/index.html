How to list dates on a resume

Dates are the smallest thing on a resume and the easiest to get subtly wrong. A recruiter reads the date column before they read a single bullet — it's how they judge tenure, spot gaps, and check whether you're being straight with them. The rules are simple, but consistency is everything: one format, everywhere, aligned in a clean column. Here's exactly how to format them.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

The one-line version: use Month Year – Month Year, pick one month style, and apply it to every entry without exception. The dates themselves aren't where you win or lose the job — but an inconsistent, ambiguous, or evasive-looking date column is a needless way to plant doubt before the reader reaches your accomplishments.

The standard format

For work experience, use the month and the year on both ends of each role:

March 2022 – June 2024  or  Mar 2022 – Jun 2024
Full months or three-letter abbreviations — either is fine. Just never mix the two in the same resume.

Three small mechanics carry most of the polish:

  • Use a dash with spaces around it — an en dash (–) or a hyphen (-) between the two dates, e.g. "2022 – 2024," not "2022-2024" jammed together.
  • Right-align the dates in a column. Job title and company on the left, dates on the right, lined up down the page. The reader scans tenure in one vertical glance.
  • Keep the style identical everywhere. If one job says "Mar 2022," every job says "Mon YYYY." One "2021" hiding among month-level dates is the tell recruiters look for.

Should you include months?

For jobs, yes — include the month. Bare years invite suspicion:

FormatHow it reads
Mar 2022 – PresentClear and honest. Shows true tenure; ATS parses it cleanly.
2022 – PresentAmbiguous. Did you start in January or December 2022? Years-only reads as rounding to hide a short stint or gap.
03/2022 – PresentAvoid. Numeric dates are ambiguous across countries and look less finished.

The exception is education, where the graduation year alone is completely normal — "B.S. Marketing, 2021." You don't need a start month for a degree. See how to list education on a resume for the full format.

There's one legitimate reason to lean on years-only: if you're deep enough into your career that a graduation year would broadcast your age, dropping it on old education is fine. That's about education, not your recent jobs — those should still carry months.

How to write your current job

For the role you're in right now, write the start date and then Present:

Current role

Product Manager — Northwind     Jan 2024 – Present

→ "Present" is the standard end-word. "Current" works too — just pick one and use it for every ongoing role.

  • Don't leave the end blank ("Jan 2024 –"), which looks unfinished.
  • Don't put today's date as the end — that reads as though the job ended today.
  • Match the tense. A "Present" job should use present-tense verbs in its bullets ("Manage…"); past roles use past tense. More on that in resume verb tenses.

Tricky cases

Short stints and multiple roles at one company

If you were promoted, stack the roles under one company header and date each one — the reader sees growth, not job-hopping:

Acme Corp
Senior Analyst    Feb 2023 – Present
Analyst    Jun 2021 – Feb 2023

See how to show a promotion on a resume for the full layout.

Gaps between jobs

Honest month-level dates will expose a gap — that's fine, and better than hiding it with fuzzy years. The dates aren't the place to fight a gap; the framing is. Read how to explain employment gaps for how to handle it without apologizing.

How far back to date

You don't need to date your entire life. Most resumes cover the last 10–15 years and let older roles drop off. The how far back should a resume go guide covers where to draw the line.

Quick reference

  • Jobs → Month Year – Month Year (e.g. "Mar 2022 – Jun 2024").
  • Current role → start date, then "Present."
  • Education → graduation year alone is fine.
  • One style everywhere → don't mix full and abbreviated months, or years and months.
  • Alignment → right-aligned in a single column so tenure scans in one glance.
  • Never numeric → "03/2022" is ambiguous; spell or abbreviate the month.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put months on a resume, or just years?

Include months for your work experience — Month Year to Month Year (e.g. "Mar 2022 – Present"). Months are the standard for jobs because they show the true length of each role and reassure a recruiter you're not using bare years to hide a short gap. Years-only ("2022 – 2024") looks like you're rounding to cover something, and applicant tracking systems parse month-and-year cleanly. The one place years-only is fine is education, where the graduation year alone is normal. Whatever you choose, be consistent: don't mix "Mar 2022" on one job and "2021" on another.

What is the correct date format for a resume?

The safest format is Month Year – Month Year, using the same month style throughout: either full months ("March 2022 – June 2024") or three-letter abbreviations ("Mar 2022 – Jun 2024"), but never both. Use an en dash or hyphen with a space on each side. Avoid numeric formats like 03/2022 – 06/2024, which are ambiguous across countries (is 03/06 March or June?) and read as less polished. Right-align the dates in a consistent column so the eye can scan tenure down the page.

How do you write your current job's dates on a resume?

Write the start date, then "Present" for the end: "Jan 2024 – Present". "Present" is the standard; "Current" and "Now" also work but are less common — pick one and use it for every ongoing role. Don't leave the end date blank and don't put today's date, which looks like the job ended. Capitalize it to match your other months, and make sure the tense in that job's bullets is present tense ("Manage…") while past roles use past tense.

Do you put dates on education on a resume?

List the graduation year (or expected graduation), and that's usually enough — "B.S. Computer Science, 2021" or "Expected May 2026". You don't need the start year for a degree. If you graduated long ago and would rather not signal age, it's acceptable to drop the graduation year entirely once you have a decade or more of experience. Keep the education date style consistent with itself, even though it can differ from the month-level dates you use for jobs.
