Returning to a former employer on a resume

You worked somewhere, left, and later came back — and now you're staring at two blocks with the same company name, unsure whether to merge them or keep them apart. The instinct is often to combine them into one clean, continuous stretch. Resist it. A return is a boomerang, and its whole value is that it's visible: two separate stints, spelled identically, with the gap in between telling the reader something a single block never could — they wanted you back.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Going back to a former employer is far more common — and far more of an asset — than people assume when they're formatting it. A company that rehires you is vouching for you twice: once by keeping you the first time, again by choosing to bring you back. Yet the entry candidates most often mishandle is exactly this one, usually by trying to smooth it into a single continuous role so the departure disappears. That instinct throws away the best part. This guide shows how to list two stints so the return reads as the strength it is.

The core idea: a return is two distinct tenures with a real gap between them, and you list it as two entries, not one. Merging them hides the departure, pads your tenure, and won't survive verification. Kept separate — same employer name both times, the return on top — the two entries say plainly that you left and they brought you back, which is a reference built into your history. Make the return legible, not invisible.

First, know what this is not

A return to a former employer looks like a few neighboring situations, and treating it as one of them is how you list it wrong. The distinction that matters: here you actually left the company and later came back — two separate spans with a genuine gap between them.

SituationHow it differs from returning to a former employer
A promotion within one jobThat's continuous upward movement inside one unbroken tenure — you never left, so you stack the titles under a single employer block. A return means you departed and came back, which is two separate blocks with a gap between them, not stacked titles in one span.
A seasonal job you were rehired forThat's a cyclical rehire into the same short recurring role — holiday retail every winter, the same summer camp — which you consolidate into one entry that shows the pattern. A boomerang is two permanent stints kept separate, not a recurring seasonal loop.
A company that changed namesThere the organization was renamed while you stayed employed continuously — one tenure, one entry, a "formerly OldCo" note. In a return, you left and came back; the company didn't change, your relationship to it did.
A general employment gapThat's the framing for any stretch out of work. A return uses the gap — the time between your two stints — but the story isn't the gap, it's the rehire. Frame the gap normally; let the second entry at the same employer carry the meaning.

Step 1 — Two entries, in the timeline, most recent on top

List each stint as its own Work Experience entry, in ordinary reverse-chronological order. The return — your most recent role there — sits at the top of the section (or wherever its dates place it), and the earlier stint appears lower down in its correct chronological spot, possibly with other jobs in between. Each entry gets its own title, its own dates, and its own bullets, exactly as if they were two different companies.

Why not one block? Because the merged version claims a continuous tenure you didn't have. It hides the departure, overstates how long you were actually there, and the moment a background check verifies each employment span separately, your merged dates won't match the record — a small honesty problem you created for no reason.

Step 2 — Make the return legible: spell the employer identically

The single move that makes a boomerang read correctly is trivial: write the company's name exactly the same way in both entries. Same name, same formatting. A reader scanning your history sees the same employer twice, clocks the gap between, and reads "returned" without you saying a word. Inconsistent naming — "Acme Co." in one, "Acme Corporation" in the other — hides the connection and makes two entries look like a coincidence.

Hides the return

  • Different name formats for the same company
  • Merging the stints so the gap disappears
  • A vague "(various dates)" span
  • Burying the earlier stint off the timeline

Makes the return obvious

  • Identical employer name in both entries
  • Two clean entries, real dates, gap visible
  • A progression in titles across the two stints
  • One optional bullet: "Recruited back to…"

You don't need a "(rehired)" tag on the entry — the repeated name and the dates do the work. If it genuinely helps the reader, a single bullet in the return entry can name it plainly: "Recruited back to lead the platform team I'd previously helped build." Keep it to results, not backstory.

Step 3 — Show the growth you brought back

The most compelling version of a boomerang isn't "I went back" — it's "I left, grew, and returned with more." If you departed as one thing and came back more senior, in a bigger role, or in a different function, that arc is the whole selling point. Let the two titles show it, and use the return entry's bullets to demonstrate what the time away added.

Let the titles carry the progression

If you left as "Analyst" and returned as "Senior Manager," the two entries already tell a growth story — you don't need to editorialize it. The reader connects the dots: this person was good enough to keep, went and leveled up elsewhere, and was valuable enough to bring back at a higher level.

Use the return bullets to show what you brought

If the time away gave you a skill, a certification, or exposure the first stint didn't, let the return entry's quantified bullets reflect it — the new process you introduced, the results you delivered with what you learned. That turns the gap from dead space into the reason you came back stronger.

Step 4 — Save the "why" for the cover letter

A resume never states a reason for leaving any job, and it doesn't owe one for a return either. But a boomerang often has a genuinely good story behind it — you were recruited back, the grass wasn't greener, you tried something and returned to work you're great at, they had a specific mandate only you could run. When the story is a real strength, one forward-looking sentence in the cover letter is the place for it.

Frame it as growth, not regret: "I left Acme to lead a larger team at a startup, and returned to bring that scale-up experience back to the company that trained me." Keep it to what you gained and what you did with it — never an apology for having left. On the resume, the two clean entries do the talking.

The bright line — never merge two stints into one continuous entry. The one tempting shortcut backfires. Combining the tenures to make the timeline look unbroken hides the departure, pads how long you were really there, and creates a verification mismatch the moment each employment span is checked separately — a self-inflicted honesty problem. And it discards the asset: two honest entries at the same employer, with a visible gap between them, say "they wanted you back" in a way no single block can. Tell the truth in the layout — separate entries, identical name, the return on top — and the rehire becomes exactly the signal you want.

Quick reference

  • Two entries, not one → each stint gets its own title, dates, and bullets; reverse-chronological, return on top.
  • Spell the employer identically → same name both times so the reader instantly sees you returned.
  • Never merge to hide the gap → a padded continuous span fails verification and throws away the boomerang signal.
  • Let the titles show growth → left as one thing, came back more senior — that arc is the selling point.
  • The return is a strength → a rehire is a reference built into your history; surface it, don't smooth it over.
  • Save the "why" for the cover letter → one forward-looking line framed as growth; the resume owes no reason.

Frequently asked questions

Should I list two separate stints at the same company, or combine them?

List them separately. They're two distinct tenures — different titles, different dates, and a real gap in between where you worked somewhere else or were away — so each gets its own entry in reverse-chronological order, with the return at the top and the earlier stint lower in the timeline where it belongs. Combining them into one continuous block misrepresents your history: it erases the departure, inflates how long you were there, and the merged dates won't match what a background check finds when it verifies each employment span independently. Separate entries are both the honest version and the stronger one, because two entries at the same employer with a gap between them tell a story a single block can't — that you left and they brought you back.

How do I make it obvious that I returned rather than looking like a coincidence?

Spell the employer's name identically in both entries — same company name, same formatting — so a reader scanning the page instantly sees the same name twice and reads "returned," not two unrelated jobs that happen to share a name. If the roles were meaningfully different, let the titles show the progression (you left as one thing and came back as something more senior or in a different function). You don't need a label like "(rehired)" on the entry; the repeated employer name plus the dates do the work. If it helps the reader, a single bullet in the return entry can name the return plainly — "Recruited back to lead the team I'd previously been part of" — but keep it to results, not backstory.

Is going back to a former employer a red flag on a resume?

The opposite — it's usually a strength, and you should let it show rather than smooth it over. A company rehiring someone is a strong signal: they already know your work, they'd manage you again, and they were willing to bring you back, which is effectively a glowing reference built into your history. The only thing that turns it into a question is trying to hide it — merging the stints, fudging dates, or being cagey about the gap. Presented cleanly as two honest entries, a boomerang reads as "valued enough to be wanted back," which is exactly the impression you want a new employer to have.

Do I have to explain why I left the first time and then came back?

Not on the resume. A resume never states a reason for leaving any job, and it doesn't owe one here either — the page shows roles, dates, and results, not the story behind each move. If the return is genuinely a strength worth telling (you left for a bigger title, grew, and brought that back; you tried something that didn't fit and returned to work you were great at; they recruited you back for a specific mandate), that belongs in one forward-looking sentence in the cover letter, framed as growth rather than regret. Keep it to what you gained and what you did with it. On the resume, let the two clean entries and the progression between them speak for themselves.

What if the two stints were nearly back-to-back with only a short gap?

Still list them as two entries, but you can make the format do more work. If the roles and dates genuinely differed, keep two entries and let the short gap sit visibly between them — a few months away is not something you need to explain, and it reads as a brief detour, not instability. If the second stint was essentially a continuation of the same role at the same level with only a trivial break, you can note it as one entry with a clear line acknowledging the brief interruption, but never collapse two substantively different tenures into one span just because the gap was short. The test is honesty of the dates: if a verifier pulls each employment record, what you wrote should match exactly.
