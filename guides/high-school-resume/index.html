High school student resume

A high school resume isn't a shrunken adult resume — it's a proof-of-potential document. You don't have a career to summarize, and that's fine, because nobody hiring a sixteen-year-old expects one. The entire game is building the page from what you do have — school, activities, volunteering, babysitting, a driver's license, a good attendance record — instead of apologizing for the job history you haven't started yet.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Most resume advice is written for people with jobs, and it makes writing your first one feel impossible: it assumes a work-experience section you can't fill, a professional summary you can't write, and accomplishments you haven't had the chance to earn. So the first thing to unlearn is that a blank experience section means you have nothing to say. A manager hiring for a first part-time or summer job isn't buying a résumé of past employers — they're buying three things: will this person show up, will they take the work seriously, and can they learn. Your whole page is just evidence for those three questions, and you have far more of it than you think.

The core idea: build from what you have, don't apologize for what you don't. School is your headline, not a footnote. Unpaid and informal work — babysitting, tutoring, yard work, a serious volunteer role — is real experience the moment you describe it like real work. You lead with an objective, not a summary, because there's no track record to summarize yet. And the whole thing fits on one clean, honest page — often less, and that's completely normal.

What actually goes on it

A first resume has fewer sections than an adult one, and it puts them in a different order. Because your strongest asset is school, not work, education climbs near the top. Here's the full set — you won't have every one, and you don't need every one.

1 · Contact information

Name, a phone number you actually answer, and a professional email address — jordan.lee@email.com, not a childhood gamertag. City and state are enough; never put your full street address. This is the one section that has to be perfect, because a typo here means the job offer can't reach you. (More on getting it right: contact information.)

2 · Objective

One or two sentences naming the job you want and why you're a safe bet. This replaces the professional summary an adult would write — see the section below on why.

3 · Education — your headline

Your school, your expected graduation year, and, if it helps, your GPA, relevant courses, honors, or a strong attendance record. For a high schooler this is the biggest, most credible section on the page, so give it room. (How to lay it out: listing education.)

4 · Experience — broadly defined

Any part-time or summer job, but also babysitting, tutoring, yard work, a family business, or a big volunteer commitment. Described with a real verb and a result, all of it counts (there's a whole section on this below).

5 · Skills

Concrete, verifiable things: a spoken language, software you actually use, a certification (CPR, food handler, lifeguard), a driver's license and reliable transportation. Skip vague filler like "hard worker" — prove that in your bullets instead. (What's worth listing: skills to put on a resume.)

6 · Activities & awards

Clubs, sports, band, student government, a robotics team, a part in the play — especially anything where you led, organized, or stuck with something for a while. A team captain or a two-year club member is showing exactly the commitment an employer wants. (See hobbies and interests and awards and achievements.)

Lead with an objective, not a summary

Adult resumes open with a professional summary — a few lines condensing years of wins. As a high schooler you don't have those years yet, so a summary comes out empty or padded. The fix is to use the format's older cousin, the objective, which points forward instead of backward: it names the job you want and one or two reasons you're a good bet for it.

Empty summary

  • "Motivated and hardworking individual with a passion for excellence and a strong work ethic."
  • Says nothing specific, could belong to anyone, names no job

Forward-looking objective

  • "Reliable high school junior seeking a part-time cashier role, bringing two summers of babysitting experience and a 96% attendance record."
  • Names the job, names the proof, reads as a safe hire

Notice the good version isn't a boast — it's a promise the rest of the page keeps. Tailor it to each posting: if you're applying to a café, mention customer service; if it's a stockroom, mention reliability and being able to lift and move things. Two sentences, aimed at the job in front of you. (Plenty of patterns to copy: resume objective examples.)

The experience you didn't know counted

This is the section that separates a weak first resume from a strong one, and it comes down to a single habit: treat informal work as real work. "Just babysitting" becomes a genuine experience entry the moment you describe it with an action verb and a concrete detail — because caring for two kids for fifteen hours a week is responsibility, reliability, and trust, which is precisely what a first employer is buying.

Weak — dismissedStrong — described like work
"Babysitting sometimes"Cared for two children up to 15 hrs/week for two years — trusted with meals, homework, and bedtime routines by three regular families
"Did yard work for neighbors"Built and managed a lawn-care route of 6 regular clients over two summers, handling scheduling and payment on my own
"In the science club"Organized a 20-student robotics team's weekly meetings and led our entry to the regional competition
"Volunteer at the shelter"Volunteered 80+ hours over a year at the animal shelter — handled feeding, intake paperwork, and weekend adoption events

The same move works for tutoring, dog-walking, helping in a family business, coaching a younger team, running a school fundraiser, or selling something you made. Start each line with a real verb — managed, organized, built, led, cared for, sold, coached — and add one specific number or detail. (A whole list to pull from: resume action verbs, and how to sharpen the details: quantify your achievements.) Volunteering especially deserves full billing here — it's some of the most credible evidence a teenager can show; see volunteer work on a resume.

Format: one clean page, honest length

Keep it to one page — and usually well under one, which is completely normal and expected for a first resume. Half a page of true, specific content beats a full page padded with filler, and a manager hiring for a first job reads padding as a red flag, not as effort. So don't stretch the margins, blow up the font, or invent entries to fill space. (The reasoning behind length: how long a resume should be.)

  • One simple font, 10–12pt, with clear section headings and real white space. Clean beats decorated.
  • Reverse chronological within each section — most recent first.
  • Save and send it as a PDF so your layout doesn't shift on someone else's computer, unless the application asks for something else.
  • Proofread twice, then have a parent or teacher read it once. A typo on a first resume is the easiest thing to fix and the easiest to overlook.

If you want the whole start-to-finish walkthrough, not just the high-school angle, read how to make a resume — and if you're a bit further along, the general no-experience resume guide covers new grads and career-starters too.

The bright line — never invent, never inflate. A first resume's entire value is that it's believable, so the one unforgivable move is making something up: a job you didn't hold, a title you didn't have, hours you didn't work, a grade you didn't earn. It's also unnecessary — you have real material, and honest small experience always beats impressive fiction. This runs the other way too: you're never obligated to volunteer a weakness. A low GPA, a job that ended badly, a hobby that doesn't help — those can simply be left off. Include what makes your case, omit what doesn't, and never write a word you couldn't calmly back up in the interview.

Quick reference

  • It's proof of potential → employers want reliability, initiative, and learning ability; make every line evidence of one.
  • Education is the headline → put it near the top with GPA (if 3.0+), courses, honors, or attendance.
  • Objective, not summary → point forward to the job you want; you have no track record to summarize yet.
  • Informal work counts → babysitting, yard work, tutoring, volunteering — a verb + a number turns any of it into real experience.
  • One honest page → often less; never pad, never invent, and leave off what doesn't help.

Keep building the resume

Once the structure's down, sharpen the pieces: write an objective that fits the posting, lay out your education and decide on GPA, give your volunteering and activities full billing, and start every entry with a strong action verb. Frequently asked questions

What do you put on a resume with no work experience in high school?

You build it from what you do have. The core sections are: contact information at the top; a one-line objective naming the job you want; an education section (your school, expected graduation year, GPA if it helps, and relevant courses or honors) — which is your headline, not a footnote; an experience section that counts unpaid and informal work like babysitting, tutoring, yard work, a family business, or a big volunteer role, each described with an action verb and a result; a skills list of concrete, verifiable things (languages, software, a certification, a driver's license); and activities and awards — clubs, sports, a leadership role, a competition. An employer hiring a teenager isn't looking for a career; they're looking for reliability, initiative, and the ability to show up and learn. Everything on the page should prove one of those.

Should a high school resume have an objective or a summary?

An objective. A summary condenses a track record — the wins and years you've already accumulated — and as a high schooler you don't have one yet, so a summary reads as empty or padded. An objective points forward instead: it names the specific job or type of work you're after and one or two things you bring to it. One or two sentences, tailored to the posting — for example, "Reliable, punctual high school junior seeking a part-time cashier role, bringing customer-service experience from two summers of babysitting and a 95% attendance record." It tells the manager exactly what you want and why you're a safe bet, which is the whole job of the top of a first resume.

How long should a high school student's resume be?

One page — and usually much less than a full page, which is completely fine. A first resume that fills half a page with real, honest content beats one padded to a full page with filler. Don't stretch margins, inflate the font, or invent entries to reach some imagined length; a manager hiring for a first job expects a short resume and reads a padded one as a red flag. Use clean formatting, leave white space, and let the page be as long as your genuine experience makes it. Half a page of true, specific detail is a strong high school resume.

Do you put your GPA on a high school resume?

Include your GPA if it's roughly 3.0 or higher, because for a student with little work history a strong GPA is real evidence of the reliability and follow-through an employer is buying. If it's below that, leave it off — a resume never has to volunteer a weakness — and let your activities, a relevant course, an award, or your attendance record carry the education section instead. The same logic applies to test scores and honors: include the ones that help your case and quietly omit the ones that don't. Nothing on a resume is mandatory just because it exists.

Can babysitting, yard work, or volunteering go on a resume?

Yes — and for most high schoolers this is the whole experience section. Babysitting, tutoring, dog-walking, yard work, helping in a family business, or a serious volunteer commitment are all real work that demonstrates responsibility, reliability, and initiative, which is exactly what a first employer is looking for. List them like any job: a title ("Babysitter," "Volunteer Tutor"), the context and rough dates, and a bullet or two describing what you did and any result — "Cared for two children up to 15 hours a week for two years; trusted with meals, homework, and bedtime routines." The key is to describe it with a real action verb and a concrete detail, not to dismiss it as "just babysitting." Treating informal work as real work is the single biggest difference between a weak first resume and a strong one.
