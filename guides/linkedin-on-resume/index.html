How to add LinkedIn to your resume

A LinkedIn link is now a standard part of a resume header — recruiters expect it and most will click it. But the default profile URL is an ugly string of numbers, and a link to a half-finished profile can cost you the interview. Here's whether to include it, where it goes, how to format a clean custom URL in two minutes, and the other links worth adding.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

Your resume answers "can this person do the job?" Your LinkedIn answers "who is this person, and does the story hold up?" A recruiter who likes your resume will almost always cross-check the profile — so a good link adds credibility, and a missing or messy one raises a small question mark. This guide covers the decision to include it, exactly where and how to place the link, how to claim a short custom URL, and when a portfolio or GitHub link belongs next to it.

Should you include LinkedIn at all?

For almost every professional role, yes — with one condition: the profile has to be worth clicking.

Include it → when your profile backs up your resume

A complete profile — clear headline, a photo, a summary, roles that match your resume dates — turns a link into a second point of proof. This is the default for most office, tech, sales, and management jobs.

Fix first → when the profile is bare or contradicts your resume

A profile with no photo, no summary, and dates that don't line up with your resume actively hurts you. If yours isn't ready, spend twenty minutes tightening it before you add the link — or leave it off until it is.

Optional → for some trades and creative fields

In fields where a portfolio or physical work matters more than a network, LinkedIn is nice-to-have rather than essential. Lead with the link that best proves you can do the work.

Where the link goes

The LinkedIn URL belongs in your contact header at the very top, next to your email and phone — not in the body, and not in its own section. Everything a recruiter needs to reach or verify you sits in one place.

If you also have a portfolio or GitHub worth showing, add them on the same line or just below, formatted identically. Keep the whole header to two lines at most — see what to put in the contact section for the full list.

How to format the URL

Three small fixes make the difference between a link that looks deliberate and one that looks like a copy-paste accident:

  • Use your custom URL, not the default. LinkedIn hands out URLs like linkedin.com/in/jordan-rivera-8a4b91276 — claim the clean version instead (steps below).
  • Drop the https://www. so it reads as linkedin.com/in/jordanrivera. Shorter, cleaner, and still obviously a URL.
  • Make it a live hyperlink in your PDF, so it's one click for the recruiter. A PDF preserves clickable links; plain text should still read cleanly if the link doesn't render.

Claim your custom LinkedIn URL (2 minutes)

  1. On LinkedIn, open your profile and click Edit public profile & URL (top-right on desktop).
  2. Under Edit your custom URL, click the pencil icon next to your current URL.
  3. Type a clean handle — ideally firstname-lastname. If it's taken, add a middle initial or your profession, not a random number.
  4. Save. Your profile is now reachable at linkedin.com/in/your-handle — use that everywhere.

Portfolio, GitHub and other links

LinkedIn is the baseline; a link that shows your actual work can matter more. Add them when they prove something for this role:

Add these when relevant
  • ✓ Portfolio — near-mandatory for designers, writers, and front-end developers. Link to the work, not a login page.
  • ✓ GitHub — helps for most engineering roles if the profile has real, presentable projects pinned. See how to put GitHub on your resume.
  • ✓ Personal site — when it collects your work, writing, or projects in one credible place.
Leave these off
  • ✗ An empty or stale GitHub — a link to nothing reads worse than no link.
  • ✗ Personal social media — Twitter/X, Instagram, and Facebook rarely belong on a resume.
  • ✗ A portfolio behind a password — if a recruiter can't open it, it isn't helping.

Format every link the same clean way, and only include pages you'd be glad to see a hiring manager open. For where these fit among everything else, see what to put on a resume.

Common mistakes

  • Pasting the default number-string URL. It looks careless; the two-minute custom URL fixes it.
  • Linking a profile that contradicts your resume. Mismatched dates or titles create doubt. Reconcile them — or see other common resume mistakes.
  • Burying the link in the body. It belongs in the contact header, not next to a job entry.
  • A dead or non-clickable link. Test it. In a PDF it should be a live hyperlink; make sure it actually resolves.
  • Padding with irrelevant links. Every URL invites a click — don't spend that on a personal account that doesn't help your case.
The whole thing in one breath. Include LinkedIn when your profile is complete and consistent with your resume. Put the link in your contact header with your email and phone, use a clean custom URL (firstname-lastname, no https://www.), and make it clickable in the PDF. Add a portfolio or GitHub when they prove your work — and only ever link to pages you'd want a hiring manager to open.

Frequently asked questions

Should I put LinkedIn on my resume?

Yes, if your profile is complete and consistent with your resume — it's expected for most professional roles and recruiters routinely check it. The exception is a bare or outdated profile: a link to a half-finished page with gaps that contradict your resume does more harm than leaving it off. Fix the profile first, then link to it.

Where does the LinkedIn link go on a resume?

In your contact header at the top, with your email and phone, alongside any portfolio or GitHub link. Never bury it in the body or in its own section — it belongs where a recruiter looks first.

How do I format my LinkedIn URL on a resume?

Use your custom (vanity) URL, not the default one full of numbers, and drop the "https://www." so it reads as linkedin.com/in/yourname. In a PDF, make it a live hyperlink. Claim the custom URL under "Edit public profile & URL" — it takes about two minutes.

Should I include a portfolio or GitHub link too?

Yes, when they add evidence for the role. A portfolio is close to mandatory for designers, writers, and front-end developers; GitHub helps for engineering roles if the profile has real, presentable work. Format them the same clean way, and only link to pages you'd be happy for a hiring manager to open.
