How to list languages on a resume

A second language can be the detail that moves your resume to the "yes" pile — but only if it's placed where it counts and labeled with a proficiency the reader can trust. The two mistakes are burying a job-relevant language at the very bottom, and rounding "I studied it in school" up to "Fluent." Here's exactly where languages go, how to state your level, and which scale to use.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

Languages are one of the few resume skills that are genuinely hard to fake — an interviewer can switch mid-sentence and find out in ten seconds. That's what makes an honest, well-placed languages line credible and a padded one risky. This guide covers where the section goes, how to describe proficiency in plain terms (and in formal scales like CEFR and ILR when a job asks for them), how to format each entry, and which languages are worth the space.

Where languages go

Placement comes down to one question: how much does this language matter to the job you're applying for?

Required or a real edge → near the top

If the posting asks for a language, or being bilingual is a clear advantage (customer support for a specific market, an international role, a translation job), put it in your summary or high in the skills section. A recruiter should see it in the first few seconds, not on a second scroll.

Nice-to-have → a dedicated "Languages" section

When a language is a bonus rather than a requirement, give it its own short "Languages" section near the end, after experience and education — present and credible, without crowding your main story.

Central to the role → in the summary itself

For a translator, interpreter, or a job whose entire point is the language, work it into your summary line ("Bilingual English–Mandarin support specialist…"). Here the language is the qualification, so it leads.

How to describe your proficiency

Never list a language without a level next to it — "Spanish" alone forces the reader to guess. Pair each language with a clear label. For most resumes, plain self-rated terms are enough and instantly understood:

LabelWhat it means in practice
Native / BilingualA first language, or one you speak like a first language — no limits in any setting.
FluentComfortable in almost any situation, including work, with only rare gaps.
Professional working proficiencyCan work effectively in the language — meetings, email, calls — with occasional help.
Conversational / IntermediateHandles everyday conversation and simple work tasks, not complex or technical ones.
Basic / ElementarySimple phrases, basic reading — enough to be useful, not to hold a real discussion.

"Professional working proficiency" and "Native / Bilingual" come from the widely-recognized terminology that many recruiters and LinkedIn already use, so they read as precise rather than vague.

When to use a formal scale

Some roles and regions expect a standardized level. Use one when the job names it or when you hold an official test result:

  • CEFR (A1–C2) — the European standard. A1/A2 = basic, B1/B2 = independent, C1/C2 = proficient. Common on European resumes and for any role tied to a language certificate (e.g. "German — B2").
  • ILR (0–5) — used by U.S. government agencies and some multinationals. 0 = none, 3 = professional working, 5 = native. Often written "ILR Level 3."

If you have a specific test score (IELTS, TOEFL, DELE, JLPT, HSK), you can add it in parentheses when it's relevant — but a plain proficiency label is fine for most jobs. Match whatever the posting uses.

The format of a single entry

Keep every entry to the language plus a proficiency label, formatted consistently down the list. That's it — no flags, no progress bars, no rating dots.

Dedicated section Languages
  • English — Native
  • Spanish — Professional working proficiency
  • French — Conversational (CEFR B1)
Inline in a skills line Languages: English (native), Mandarin (fluent), German (basic)

Avoid the visual gimmicks — five-star ratings, filled circles, or little flag icons. They mean different things to different readers, and an applicant tracking system can't read a graphic. A word like "Fluent" is both clearer to a human and machine-readable. See the ATS checklist.

Which languages are worth listing

The test mirrors the rest of your resume: does it help you get this job?

List these
  • ✓ Any language the posting asks for — even at an honest intermediate level.
  • ✓ Languages that fit the role or market — a customer base, an office region, an international team.
  • ✓ A genuine second (or third) language at conversational level or above, when space allows.
Think twice about these
  • ✗ Your native language when it's the job's default and adds nothing.
  • ✗ A language you've mostly forgotten — don't claim a level you can't hold in an interview.
  • ✗ Irrelevant + basic — a few words of a language the job doesn't need is filler.

Common mistakes

  • Inflating your level. The single most exposing resume claim. If you wrote "Fluent," expect part of the interview in that language. When unsure, round down — see other common resume mistakes.
  • Listing a language with no level. "Italian" tells the reader nothing; "Italian — Conversational" tells them exactly what to expect.
  • Using rating graphics. Dots and bars aren't standardized and don't survive a parser. Use words.
  • Padding with your default language. "English (native)" on an English-language job in an English-speaking country is a wasted line.
The whole section in one breath. Put a job-relevant language near the top; give bonus languages their own short section near the end. Pair every entry with an honest proficiency label — plain terms (Native, Fluent, Professional working, Conversational, Basic) for most jobs, or a formal scale (CEFR, ILR) when the role asks. Skip rating graphics and your default language, and never round a level up.

Frequently asked questions

Where do languages go on a resume?

If a language is required or a real advantage, put it near the top — in your summary or high in the skills section — so a recruiter sees it fast. If it's a nice-to-have, a short "Languages" section near the end, after experience and education, is the right place. When the language is central to the role (translator, bilingual support), it can lead in the summary itself.

How do I describe my language proficiency on a resume?

Pair each language with a clear label. Plain self-rated terms work for most resumes: Native, Fluent, Professional working proficiency, Conversational, or Basic. For roles that test language formally, use a recognized scale — CEFR (A1–C2) in Europe, or the ILR scale (0–5) for U.S. government and some multinationals. State the level you could hold up in a five-minute conversation.

Should I list my native language on a resume?

Only if it isn't obvious from the job's default language or adds real value — for a bilingual, international, or differently-languaged posting. Listing your first language on a same-language job in your own country adds nothing and can read as filler.

Should I include a language I only know a little of?

Only if it's relevant and you label it honestly as "Basic" or "Elementary." A little of a valued language can still help — but never inflate it, since an interviewer may switch to it on the spot. If it's both weak and irrelevant, leave it off.
