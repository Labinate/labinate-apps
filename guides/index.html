How to List a Name Change on a Resume (current name only, bridge the rest off-page)

Married, divorced, changed your name legally or in transition — and now the references, diplomas, and old employer records all carry a name your resume doesn't? The fix isn't to document it on the page. How to List a Gap Year on a Resume (list the activity, not the absence)

Staring at twelve months that don't fit a tidy box? Stop treating a gap year as an absence from your resume — it's a presence on it. A year off your life is never a year off your resume, because you always did something with it, and that something goes on the page. First ask: was the year structured or self-directed? A structured year — a working holiday, a volunteer or service program, teaching abroad, an internship — is real experience: give it a full work-style entry (title, program, place, dates, accomplishment bullets), often your lead on a light early-career resume. A self-directed year — travel, self-study, a project — gets one honest, forward line that names what it developed: a language, logistics managed across countries, a shipped project, freelance work. Don't manufacture wins and don't recite an itinerary — credit real transferable skills only. Put it in the timeline, not an "Interests" footnote, and keep the tone confident: a well-used gap year is an asset.

How to Put Work Authorization on a Resume (usually nothing — unless it clears a filter)

Wondering where the visa line goes? The move is to stop asking what is my status and ask does this posting need the answer to proceed. Work authorization sits on the axis of legal eligibility — the I-9 checkbox verified at the end — and a resume sells fit, so the default is to say nothing.
