How to put work authorization on a resume

The right answer isn't "state your status" — it's answer the question the posting actually asked. Most of the time that's nothing at all; sometimes it's one clean line that clears a filter.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

You're staring at your resume wondering where to put your visa status, or whether to write "authorized to work" at all, or how to handle the fact that you'll eventually need sponsorship. The move that unlocks all of it is to stop asking "what is my status?" and start asking "does this employer need the answer to move forward?" Work authorization isn't a credential you display or a personal fact you owe — it's a legal-eligibility checkbox the employer must eventually verify, and a resume exists to earn the interview, not to fill out that checkbox early. Once you see it that way, the whole thing collapses into a couple of narrow cases where a single line helps, and a large default where the right amount to say is zero.

The core idea: work authorization sits on the axis of legal eligibility to be hired — the right-to-work question every employer verifies on an I-9 at the end. A resume sells fit, not paperwork, so the default is to say nothing. You add a line only when it answers a question the posting raised — a role that requires authorization without sponsorship, or a status that lets you clear that requirement and remove friction. Where you'll need sponsorship, you don't advertise it on the resume; you disclose it truthfully on the application form, where the employer actually asks.

The default: your resume isn't the place for it

For most applicants and most jobs, work authorization simply doesn't belong on the resume. The document's one job is to make a hiring manager want to talk to you, and status has nothing to do with whether you can do the work. Volunteering it up front does only harm: it hands a screener a fact they aren't permitted to weigh but are human enough to notice, and it spends header space on paperwork instead of on fit. Employers are legally required to verify eligibility — but at the end, on the I-9, after they've decided they want you. Front-loading it on the resume just moves an end-of-process checkbox to the top of the funnel, where it can only cost you. So the baseline is quiet: no visa line, no status note, nothing.

Case 1 — the posting requires it and you clear it

Some postings put eligibility in the requirements: "US Citizen required," "must be authorized to work in the US without sponsorship," or the same demand implied by federal, defense, or cleared roles. When you meet that bar, silence works against you — a recruiter scanning for the requirement can't confirm it, and an ATS filtering for the keyword may drop you. Here one clean line removes the doubt and gets you through.

Clears the filter

  • Authorized to work in the US for any employer
  • US Citizen — when a posting states it as a requirement
  • Permanent Resident (Green Card)
  • One line in or just under the contact header, or in the summary

Over-shares or clutters

  • Visa number, A-number, or category codes — I-9 detail, not resume content
  • A whole "Immigration Status" section for one line
  • Your status story or how long you've been in the country
  • The line at all when the posting never asked for it

Case 2 — you're authorized without sponsorship and want to remove friction

If you already hold authorization that doesn't require the employer to sponsor you — a green card, OPT or STEM OPT with an active window, a TN, or a spouse EAD — you're in a strong spot for many roles, and a brief line can turn a worry into a non-issue. The message an employer cares about is simple: I can start now and you don't have to file anything. State exactly that, and match the specificity to the truth.

Clean phrasings: Authorized to work in the US — no sponsorship required. · Authorized to work in the US (STEM OPT, valid through 2027). · US work authorization; no visa sponsorship needed. Give a through-date only when it's firm and reassuring; the line signals "I can work now," not your long-term immigration plan.

Case 3 — you'll need sponsorship: disclose it, don't advertise it

This is the case people get wrong in both directions. Writing "requires H-1B sponsorship" on the resume gets you auto-filtered before a human reads your qualifications — you've answered a gatekeeping question before anyone was invested in you. But hiding a real sponsorship need by lying on the application is worse, because it surfaces at the I-9 and can cost you the offer. The resolution is that these are two different surfaces. The resume earns the interview and stays silent on status. The application form is where the employer explicitly asks — "Are you legally authorized to work?" and "Will you now or in the future require sponsorship?" — and there you answer truthfully. That's not evasion; it's putting each fact where it belongs.

The resume is a fit document, not an eligibility form

It exists to make them want you. Leaving sponsorship off the resume isn't dishonest — the resume has no field for it, and the honest disclosure happens where the employer actually asks.

Answer the application's sponsorship question truthfully

The yes/no questions are the legal disclosure point. Answer them straight — a false "no" unravels at the I-9 and E-Verify, and a rescinded offer is far costlier than an early filter.

Target roles that fit your reality

Some employers sponsor and say so; many don't. Spending your effort where sponsorship is on the table beats trying to disguise the need on a resume that was never the right place to raise it.

The rule under all three cases: eligibility, never immigration detail

Whatever you write, keep it to your eligibility to be hired and stop there. "Authorized to work in the US" is a resume line; a visa number, an A-number, your immigration category, or the story of how you got here are I-9 and interview matters, not resume content. And never overstate — don't write "authorized for any employer" if you'd need sponsorship, and don't inflate a lapsing status into an open-ended one. The reason is mechanical, not moral: every hire runs through Form I-9, and many through E-Verify, so a status claim is checked against the federal record before you start. A clean, true, minimal line clears a filter; anything more either clutters the page or invites a problem.

Work authorization isn't the same question as these

Three neighbors get tangled with work authorization because they all sound like "things about me an employer needs to know" — but each sits on a different axis, with a different move. Know which one the posting is actually asking about.

The situationWhat it's really about — and the resume move
Work authorizationLegal eligibility to be hired — the right-to-work checkbox verified on the I-9. Say nothing by default; add one clean eligibility line only when the posting requires it or when it removes a sponsorship worry.
A security clearanceGovernment-held access status for a cleared role — a level-and-status question, not an eligibility one. See how to list a security clearance.
RelocationWhere you'll physically work and your willingness to move — geography, not legal right to work. See relocation on a resume.
Contact informationThe standard header fields a recruiter needs to reach you — work authorization isn't one of them, and joins the header only as a deliberate one-line exception. See resume contact information.

The through-line: work authorization is an eligibility question, and eligibility is verified at the end, so the resume's job is to get you to the end — which usually means saying nothing, and occasionally means one line that clears a filter.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode isn't your status — it's treating the resume as the place to declare it. Answered as "does the posting need this?", it's either a clean one-liner or a non-event; answered as "let me get my paperwork out of the way," it filters you before your fit is read.

Clears the path

  • No status line at all when the posting never asked
  • One declarative line — Authorized to work in the US — when a role requires it
  • Sponsorship need disclosed truthfully on the application, not the resume
  • Eligibility only, no visa numbers or backstory

Filters you out

  • "Requires H-1B sponsorship" on the resume, read before your fit
  • A false "no" to the application's sponsorship question
  • Visa numbers, A-number, status history a resume doesn't trade in
  • "Authorized for any employer" when you'd actually need sponsorship

Work authorization is an eligibility question, and the resume's job is to get you to the point where eligibility is verified — which usually means saying nothing. Stay silent by default; add one clean line when a posting requires it or when it removes a sponsorship worry; disclose a real sponsorship need truthfully on the application, not the resume; and keep every mention to eligibility, never immigration detail. Do that and status stops being a thing you have to explain and becomes what it should be — a checkbox handled at the right time.

Quick reference

  • Default is silence → no visa line; a resume sells fit, and eligibility is verified at the end on the I-9.
  • Posting requires it and you clear it → one line, "Authorized to work in the US for any employer" or "US Citizen".
  • Authorized without sponsorship → optional friction-remover, "Authorized to work in the US — no sponsorship required."
  • You'll need sponsorship → don't advertise it on the resume; answer the application's sponsorship question truthfully.
  • Eligibility only → no visa numbers, A-number, or status story; never overstate — the I-9 and E-Verify check it.
  • Know your axis → work authorization (eligibility) ≠ security clearance (access status) ≠ relocation (geography) ≠ contact information (header fields).

Frequently asked questions

Should you put work authorization on a resume?

Usually no — only when the posting raises the question. A resume's job is to sell your fit, not to volunteer paperwork, and a bare mention of visa status invites a bias a screener isn't allowed to act on but is human enough to feel. It earns a line in exactly two situations: when a posting explicitly requires authorization without sponsorship (federal, defense, cleared, or "must be authorized to work in the US without sponsorship" roles) and you can clear it — then one clean line like "Authorized to work in the US for any employer" or "US Citizen" removes the doubt; and when you already hold authorization that needs no employer sponsorship (green card, OPT/STEM OPT, TN, spouse EAD) and want to remove friction — "Authorized to work in the US, no sponsorship required." In every other case, leave it off the resume.

Should I mention that I need visa sponsorship on my resume?

No — you disclose that you need sponsorship truthfully, but not on the resume. Leading a resume with "requires H-1B sponsorship" hands a screener a reason to filter you out before your fit is ever read, and the resume's only job is to earn the interview. The honest place to answer is where the employer actually asks: the standard yes/no application questions, "Are you legally authorized to work in the country?" and "Will you now or in the future require sponsorship?" Answer both truthfully there. That keeps you honest without volunteering, on the resume itself, a fact that filters you before your qualifications are seen. Never hide a sponsorship need by answering the application question falsely — it surfaces at the I-9 and can cost you the offer.

How do you write a work authorization statement on a resume?

Keep it to eligibility, in one short line, placed in or just under your contact header or in a brief summary — not as its own section. State what an employer needs to know to proceed and nothing more: "Authorized to work in the US for any employer," "US Citizen," "Permanent Resident (Green Card)," or, when your status is time-bound, "Authorized to work in the US — no sponsorship required (STEM OPT, valid through 2027)." Do not include a visa number, an A-number, your specific immigration history, or a personal status story — those are I-9 details, not resume content. The line exists to clear a filter, so make it declarative and clean; if the posting doesn't demand it and you don't need it to remove friction, don't write it at all.

Is work authorization the same as a security clearance?

No — they sit on different axes and get confused only because cleared federal jobs often demand both. Work authorization is about your legal eligibility to be hired at all — the right-to-work question every employer must verify on an I-9. A security clearance is a government-held access status tied to a cleared role, a level-and-status question (Confidential, Secret, Top Secret, active vs. current vs. inactive). You can be fully authorized to work and hold no clearance, and the two lines, when both apply, say different things. A posting that says "US Citizen required" is usually testing eligibility (and clearance-eligibility); a posting that says "active Secret clearance" is testing access. Answer each with the line it asks for.

What if I'm on OPT or a visa that's about to expire?

State the eligibility, not the anxiety. If your OPT or STEM OPT currently authorizes you to work without employer sponsorship, that's a genuine near-term asset for many roles, and a brief line — "Authorized to work in the US (STEM OPT, valid through 2027)" — removes friction for an employer worried about paperwork, because it tells them you can start now with no sponsorship on their side. Match the specificity to the truth: give the through-date if it's firm and reassuring, and if the role would eventually need sponsorship you can't provide yourself, don't paper over it — answer the application's sponsorship question honestly. The resume line signals "I can work now"; it is not the place to explain your long-term immigration plan, which belongs in conversation once you're a candidate they want.
