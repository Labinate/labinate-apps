How to address a criminal record on a resume

You have a felony or a conviction in your past and you're staring at a blank resume wondering where it goes. The honest, strategic answer surprises most people: it doesn't go on the resume at all. A resume sells your qualifications, and a conviction isn't one — you disclose it later, in a conversation, only if and when you're actually asked. What the resume does have to handle is the gap the record may have left, and that you handle honestly.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

This is one of the most stressful resume questions there is, and a lot of the stress comes from a wrong premise — that a criminal record is a thing you have to place somewhere on the page, like a job or a degree. It isn't. A resume is a marketing document: its entire purpose is to argue that you can do the job. A conviction is not evidence you can do the job, so it has no home on the resume, and leaving it off is not concealment — it's using the document correctly. The record is real and you will address it, but you address it in the right place, at the right time, in a human conversation. Below is exactly how to keep the page clean, handle the one thing that does land on it — the employment gap — and answer well when you're finally asked.

The core idea: A criminal record is disclosed, not listed. Keep the resume focused on your qualifications; deal with the conviction separately, only when directly asked, and only then. The single resume-level task the record creates is an honest, neutral treatment of any employment gap from incarceration — never a fabrication to hide it.

Why the conviction stays off the page

Three reasons, and they reinforce each other:

  • A resume markets qualifications. Everything on it should answer "why hire me for this role." A conviction answers a different question that nobody has asked yet, and putting it there hands the reader a reason to stop before they've seen your case.
  • The law increasingly agrees. In much of the US, ban-the-box and fair-chance laws prohibit employers from asking about convictions on the initial application, and many delay any such question until after an interview or a conditional offer. A clean resume isn't dodging the rules — it's exactly what the modern hiring process is built to expect.
  • Timing is leverage. Once an employer has seen your skills and met you, a disclosure lands on a person they already want. Front-loading it throws that advantage away.

So the resume's job is unchanged: make the strongest possible case for the work. The record is handled elsewhere.

The one thing that does land on the resume: the gap

If a conviction led to incarceration, it likely left an employment gap — and unlike the conviction, a gap is visible on the page. You don't explain the reason for it on the resume; you handle it with the same neutral, honest techniques you'd use for any gap:

Use years, not months

Warehouse Associate · 2016 – 2019 reads cleaner than March 2016 – November 2019, and quietly narrows how sharply a gap stands out — without misstating a single date. This is standard practice for everyone, not a trick.

Fill the time with what you actually did

The years weren't empty. List the legitimate, resume-worthy things you did: a GED or diploma earned while incarcerated, a vocational certificate or trade training, a prison-industry or work-release job (by the work performed, with a neutral employer name), an apprenticeship or reentry program, or volunteer work. These show a person who kept building.

Consider a skills-forward structure

A functional or hybrid layout that leads with a skills summary can put your capabilities first and let the timeline follow — useful when the chronology is what you'd rather not open with. Use it because it genuinely presents you better, not purely to obscure dates; a pure skills-only resume with no dates at all can read as evasive.

The bright line: never fabricate. Do not invent an employer, stretch a real job's dates across the gap, or claim work you didn't do. A background check reconciles employment history, and being caught in a lie is a far bigger disqualifier than the record itself — it turns "past mistake, moved on" into "can't be trusted now." An honest gap with constructive content beats a fabricated timeline every time.

When you are asked — how to answer

At some point you may be asked directly: a conviction question on an application (where the law still allows it), or the background-check stage after they've decided they want you. That's the moment to disclose — briefly, honestly, and forward-looking. A simple, confident structure:

  1. Name it plainly. Don't euphemize or over-explain. "Yes, I have a felony conviction from 2019."
  2. Take accountability in one sentence. Own it without a long defense or a list of excuses. Blaming circumstances reads worse than a clean acknowledgment.
  3. Pivot to what changed. This is most of your answer: the program you completed, the years since, the skills you've built, the references who'll vouch for the person you are now.

Do

  • Wait until you're actually asked.
  • Be brief, calm, and specific.
  • Emphasize growth, accountability, and what you've done since.
  • Line up references who can speak to your reliability now.
  • Know your record's exact status before you talk about it.

Don't

  • Volunteer it on the resume or in the first line of a cover letter.
  • Over-explain, argue innocence, or blame others.
  • Lie or minimize if a truthful "yes" is required.
  • Disclose a sealed or expunged record you're not obligated to.
  • Let it dominate — pivot back to the job.

Know your leverage: expungement and fair-chance laws

Two facts change the calculus in your favor, and both are worth confirming for your own situation:

  • Expunged or sealed records generally don't have to be disclosed and typically won't appear on a standard background check — in most jurisdictions the law treats the conviction as if it didn't occur for most employment purposes. Some sensitive fields (law enforcement, certain government, healthcare, finance, roles with vulnerable people) can reach further, so read each question precisely. If you're eligible for expungement or sealing and haven't pursued it, it's often the single highest-leverage move you can make.
  • Ban-the-box / fair-chance laws limit when — and sometimes whether — an employer can ask about convictions, frequently barring the question until after an interview or a conditional offer. That's precisely why a clean resume is the norm: the process is designed to judge your qualifications first.

Because the specifics vary by state and by offense, confirm your own record's status and disclosure obligations rather than guessing. The baseline, though, is encouraging: the system increasingly gives you room to be judged on your qualifications first.

What this is not the same as

A criminal-record gap gets confused with a few adjacent resume situations. Keeping them separate clarifies exactly what you're dealing with.

Not the same as…How the criminal-record case differs
A general employment gapA record gap is a sensitive subset of the general gap problem. The neutral-framing mechanics (years not months, fill the time honestly) are identical — the one added rule is that you never name the reason on the page, and you disclose the conviction itself only when directly asked.
A gap yearA gap year is a chosen, positive break — travel, study, caregiving — that you can frame as deliberate and even name outright. An incarceration gap is involuntary and sensitive; the strategy is quiet, honest neutrality, not a proud explanation.
A legal name changeA name change is about making a background check reconcile — ensuring the verifier can match your records under the right name. It's adjacent because a record surfaces at the background-check stage, but that guide is about identity matching, not about disclosing a conviction.
The bottom line. Don't put the conviction on your resume — a resume sells qualifications, and disclosure belongs to a later conversation, if you're asked at all. Handle any incarceration gap honestly and neutrally: years not months, fill the time with real training and work, and never fabricate. When you're directly asked, be brief, accountable, and forward-looking. Know your leverage — expungement and fair-chance laws exist to let you be judged on who you are now. Spend your energy making the strongest case for the job; that's the fight worth having.

Quick reference

  • The conviction stays off the resume → it's disclosed later, if asked, never listed on the page.
  • Handle the gap, not the reason → use years not months and fill the time with real education, certificates, and work.
  • Never fabricate → a made-up employer or stretched dates fail the background check, and dishonesty disqualifies harder than the record.
  • Disclose only when asked → then be brief, accountable, and forward-looking, and pivot back to the job.
  • Know your leverage → expunged/sealed records usually don't need disclosing; fair-chance laws limit when employers can even ask.

Frequently asked questions

Do you have to put a criminal record on a resume?

No. A resume is a marketing document for your qualifications, and a conviction is not a qualification, so it does not belong on the page at all. There is no resume section, line, or note where a criminal record goes — leaving it off is not hiding anything, it's using the document for what it's for. Disclosure, if it happens at all, belongs to a later stage: an application form question (only if the employer legally asks), the interview, or the background-check step. In many US states and cities, ban-the-box and fair-chance laws actually prohibit an employer from asking about convictions on the initial application, so a clean resume is both the right strategy and what the law increasingly expects. Put your energy into making the resume argue for the job; deal with the record separately, when and if you're asked.

How do I explain an employment gap from incarceration on a resume?

You handle it like any employment gap — honestly and neutrally, without naming the reason on the page. First, use years instead of months for your dates (2019 – 2022 rather than March 2019 – August 2022), which quietly narrows how visible a gap is without misstating anything. Second, fill the time with the legitimate things you actually did: a GED or diploma earned while incarcerated, a vocational certificate or trade training, a prison-industry or work-release job (list it by the work performed and a neutral employer name), an apprenticeship or reentry program, or volunteer work. These are real, resume-worthy activities that show you kept building skills. What you must not do is invent an employer, stretch a real job's dates to cover the gap, or fabricate anything — a background check will catch it, and being caught in a lie is a far bigger disqualifier than the gap itself. A gap with honest, constructive content reads as a person who kept moving forward.

When should I disclose a felony conviction to an employer?

Only when you're directly asked, and never on the resume. The trigger is usually one of two moments: a conviction question on an application form (which many jurisdictions now bar until after an interview or conditional offer under fair-chance laws), or the background-check stage after they've decided they want you. When that moment comes, be brief, honest, and forward-looking: name the conviction plainly, take accountability in a single sentence without long excuses, and pivot immediately to what has changed — the program you completed, the years since, the skills you've built, the references who'll vouch for you. Volunteering it earlier than asked rarely helps and often shifts the conversation away from your qualifications; waiting past the point where you're directly asked reads as concealment. Answer the question you're actually asked, when you're asked it, and keep the focus on the person you are now.

Do I have to disclose an expunged or sealed record?

Generally, no — that's much of the point of getting a record expunged or sealed. In most jurisdictions an expunged or sealed conviction is treated as if it did not occur for the purposes of most employment applications, meaning you can lawfully answer "no" to a general question about convictions and it typically will not surface on a standard background check. There are exceptions worth knowing: certain sensitive fields (law enforcement, some government, healthcare, finance, roles involving vulnerable people) may run deeper checks or ask questions that reach sealed records, so read each application's question precisely. Because the rules vary by state and by the specific offense, it's worth confirming your own record's status and what you're required to disclose — but as a baseline, an expunged or sealed record is exactly the tool that lets you move on, and you're usually under no obligation to raise it.

Should I explain the conviction in my cover letter?

Usually not. Like the resume, the cover letter is a marketing document, and leading with a conviction hands the reader a reason to stop before they've seen your case for the job. The letter's job is to connect your skills to their needs, so keep it doing that. There's one narrow exception: if you're applying through a fair-chance or reentry program, to an employer who has explicitly stated they hire people with records, or to a role where a values-forward organization has invited that context, a short, confident, forward-looking line can work in your favor — "I'm rebuilding my career after a past mistake and have spent the last three years doing X." Even then, keep it brief and pivot straight back to what you offer. For the vast majority of applications, leave the explanation out of the letter and handle disclosure when you're directly asked.
