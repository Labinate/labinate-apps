How to list a secondment on a resume

Your employer loaned you out — to a client, a partner, a government body, another division — for a fixed stretch, and the whole time you stayed on your own company's payroll. Now you're wondering whose name goes on the entry. List the host as a separate job and you invent a departure that never happened and make a claim a background check can't confirm; bury it and you hide the most impressive placement of your career. The fix: one tenure under your home employer, the secondment nested beneath it, the host named.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A secondment is one of the better things that can happen to a career and one of the more confusing things to put on a resume. You didn't change jobs — your employer lent you to someone else for a while and took you back afterward. So the entry sits in an odd place: the work happened at the host, but the employment stayed at home. Get the format wrong and you either erase a trust marker most people never earn, or you claim an employer that will have no record of you. This guide shows the structure that keeps it honest and makes it land.

The core idea: a secondment is one continuous tenure at your home employer with a temporary assignment inside it — not a job at the host. So your home company stays the employer of record, and the secondment is nested beneath it as a sub-role that names the host. Spinning the host off as its own employer entry misrepresents who paid you and fails verification; hiding the secondment throws away the placement that most impresses a reader.

First, know what this is not

A secondment looks like several neighboring situations, and mistaking it for one of them is how you format it wrong. The distinction that matters: here you stayed employed and paid by your home company the whole time, and were placed temporarily at a host that never put you on its payroll.

A rotational programA rotation moves you through different functions inside one company — finance, then operations, then strategy, all internal. A secondment sends you to a different organization entirely — a client, a partner, a public body. Both nest under your home employer rather than splitting into separate jobs, but a rotation shows internal breadth while a secondment shows an external placement and the trust it took to send you.
Returning to a former employerA boomerang is two separate stints with a real gap — you actually left and came back. A secondment is one unbroken tenure; you never left your home employer at all. The assignment happens inside your continuous employment, so it's nested, not listed as a return.
Contract work at a clientAs a contractor you're either self-employed or on an agency's books, and the client is who you serve, not who employs you long-term. A secondment is the reverse setup where it matters: you have a permanent home employer who keeps you and takes you back — the host is a temporary stop, not the shape of your working life.
A fellowshipA fellowship is a funded post you were awarded in your own name and hold yourself. A secondment is your employer's decision: they're on the payroll, they chose to send you, and the placement belongs to the employment relationship, not to you personally.
A promotionA promotion is permanent and vertical — a new, higher role you keep. A secondment is temporary and lateral — a fixed-term placement you return from. You stack a promotion on top of your history; you nest a secondment inside one tenure with a clear end date.

Step 1 — Home employer stays the employer; nest the secondment

Give your home company one Work Experience entry with the full, continuous dates of your employment there. The secondment goes underneath as a sub-role, not as a second employer. This is the whole game: it keeps the record accurate (you were paid by your home company throughout) and it keeps your tenure honest (you never left).

Instead ofWrite
Delivery Lead, UK Cabinet Office · 2023–24
Senior Consultant, Meridian · 2021–23, 2024–now		Meridian Consulting · 2021–Present
 • Senior Consultant (2021–2023, 2024–Present)
 • Seconded to the UK Cabinet Office — Delivery Lead (2023–2024)

The left version reads as a job at the Cabinet Office wedged between two runs at Meridian — a departure and a return that never happened, and an employer the Cabinet Office has no payroll record of. The right version reads as what actually occurred: one continuous tenure at Meridian, during which you were trusted to lead a delivery at a government client, then returned. Same facts, opposite impression — and only one of them survives a verification call.

Step 2 — Use the word "seconded," and name the host

Two small choices carry most of the weight. First, the word "seconded" (or "on secondment") — it tells a reader in one word that you stayed employed by your own firm, were placed at the host temporarily, and returned, which is exactly why the assignment has a clean end date that reads as the placement ending rather than you being let go. Second, name the host — it's usually the most impressive thing in the line.

Signals that land

  • "Seconded to a FTSE 100 client to lead a finance transformation"
  • "On secondment to the Ministry of Justice, 2023–24"
  • Naming a host bigger or better-known than your own firm
  • One line on why they sent you — the scope you owned

What undercuts it

  • Listing the host as a stand-alone employer
  • Dropping the word "seconded," so it reads as a job change
  • Hiding the host behind a vague "external assignment"
  • Leaving the secondment off entirely

Don't be shy about the host outranking your employer — that contrast is the pitch: a smaller firm trusted you enough to send you into a marquee organization. The only reason to describe the host generically ("seconded to a global investment bank") is a genuine confidentiality obligation — never omission for its own sake.

Step 3 — Quantify what you delivered at the host

A secondment usually hands you scope you wouldn't get in your day job — a bigger budget, a client's whole function, a stakeholder table you'd never otherwise sit at. That's the second reason it belongs on the page: not just that you were chosen, but what you did once you got there. Treat the sub-role like any role and give it quantified bullets.

Lead with the scope the secondment gave you

The value of a secondment is often the level you operated at, not just the badge. "Led a 14-person client delivery team and a £2.4M workstream" says more than "supported a project" — the numbers show you carried real weight at the host, which is the whole reason the placement is worth listing.

Keep the title honest to what you actually owned

Use the title you genuinely held at the host, not an inflated version. If you were the delivery lead, say "Delivery Lead" — borrowing a grander title the host would never confirm turns a real, verifiable strength into a claim that collapses on one reference call. The placement itself is impressive enough without padding the title.

Step 4 — Show the return without making it a footnote

Because you came back, your home role brackets the secondment — and that return is a quiet asset, not an afterthought. It tells a reader the secondment was successful enough that your employer took you back, often into more responsibility. Let the dates carry it: the home role resumes after the secondment's end date, and if you returned to something bigger, let that title show it.

Read top-down, the entry becomes: home employer, one continuous span → your substantive role → the secondment nested inside it → your role resuming after. That sequence says: you were a valued employee, trusted enough to be sent to represent the firm somewhere that mattered, and welcomed back — the exact story a secondment is supposed to tell, told without a single misrepresented employer.
The bright line — never list the host as a stand-alone employer, and never bury the secondment. The host never employed or paid you, so a separate host entry is a claim a background check will contradict the moment it calls them; nesting keeps it truthful. And leaving the secondment off hides your most impressive placement and can leave an unexplained change of focus inside your home tenure that a reader notices anyway. One tenure, host named, "seconded," honest title — that's the format.

Quick reference

  • Home employer is the employer of record → they paid you throughout; the secondment nests under their one continuous entry.
  • Nest, don't split → the host gets a sub-role, never its own stand-alone employer block.
  • Use "seconded" and name the host → the word signals a chosen, time-boxed placement; the host is usually the flex.
  • Quantify the host-side work → the scope you were given is the point; honest title, real numbers.
  • Let the return show → your home role resumes after the end date, often into more responsibility.
  • The bright line → never list the host as a separate employer; it fails verification and invents a departure that never happened.

Frequently asked questions

Who do I list as the employer for a secondment — my company or the host?

Your home employer. Through the whole secondment you were employed, paid, and carried on the books by your own company; the host is where you physically worked, not who employed you. So the employer of record on your resume is your home company, and the secondment lives underneath it as a sub-role that names the host. Listing the host as a separate employer entry misrepresents who employed you and fails verification the instant a background check contacts the host and finds no record of you on their payroll — because you were never on it. Keep one continuous tenure under your home employer and nest the assignment beneath it, exactly the way you'd nest a rotation or an internal role change.

How do I format a secondment entry?

Keep the home employer as the single entry with the full continuous dates, and nest the secondment as a sub-role beneath it. For example: "Meridian Consulting (2021–Present)" on top, then under it "Seconded to the UK Cabinet Office — Delivery Lead (2023–2024)" with its own quantified bullets, and your substantive home role above or below it. The word "Seconded to" does the heavy lifting: it tells a reader in two words that you stayed employed by Meridian, were placed at the Cabinet Office temporarily, and returned — so the clean end date reads as the assignment ending, not you being let go. Name the host plainly, because the host is usually the flex.

Should I name the host organization?

Yes, almost always — the host is frequently the most impressive part of the line. A secondment means your employer trusted you enough to send you to represent them somewhere, and that somewhere is often a marquee client, a government body, a regulator, or a partner bigger than your own firm. "Seconded to a FTSE 100 client to lead their finance transformation" says two things at once: your employer chose you, and you operated at a level your day job might not show. Name the host the way you'd name any employer, and don't shy away from the host being more prestigious than your own company — that contrast is the point. The only exception is a confidentiality obligation, in which case describe the host generically ("seconded to a global investment bank") rather than omitting it.

How is a secondment different from just changing jobs?

A job change means you left one employer for another — two separate tenures, two payrolls, a real break. A secondment means you never left: your home employer kept you on their payroll and loaned you to a host for a fixed period, after which you returned. That's why the two are formatted oppositely. A job change gets its own stand-alone employer entry; a secondment gets nested under your home employer as one continuous tenure. Formatting a secondment as a separate job invents a departure that never happened, manufactures apparent job-hopping, and — because the host never employed you — leaves a claim a background check can't confirm. The word "seconded" is precisely what keeps it legible as a temporary placement rather than a move.

Should I mention a secondment at all if it was short?

Usually yes, even if it was short, because a secondment tends to carry more signal per month than ordinary work — it's a trust marker and often a stretch assignment. Hiding it does two kinds of damage: it throws away your most impressive placement, and it can leave an unexplained sub-gap or an odd change of focus inside your home tenure that a reader notices anyway. A three-month secondment to a major client to stand up a new function is worth a nested sub-role with one or two quantified bullets. The only time to leave it off is if it was genuinely incidental and adds nothing — a two-week cover assignment — in which case it can fold into your main role's bullets rather than getting its own sub-line.
