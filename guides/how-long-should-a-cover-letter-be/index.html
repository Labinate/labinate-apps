How long should a cover letter be?

Short answer: half a page to one full page — about 250 to 400 words in three or four tight paragraphs, and never more than a single page. The longer answer is where those words should go, why shorter usually wins, and how to trim a letter that's run away from you.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

A cover letter isn't an essay and it isn't your resume in sentences. It's a short, specific case for why you and this job fit — and its job is to earn a few minutes of attention for your resume, not to replace it. That's why length matters: past a certain point, every extra sentence lowers the odds the whole thing gets read.

The numbers, and what they actually mean

Three ways of measuring the same thing, so you can check your letter against whichever is easiest:

  • Word count: 250–400. Roughly 300 is the comfortable middle. Under ~150 and you probably haven't made a real case; over ~400 and you're almost certainly padding or repeating the resume.
  • Paragraphs: 3–4. An opening, one or two body paragraphs, and a close. That's the natural shape and it's hard to improve on.
  • Page: half to one, never more. One page is the ceiling, not the target. A sharp half-page beats a padded full page. If your letter reaches a second page, it isn't finished — it's overwritten.
One page is a maximum, not a quota

You do not have to fill the page. If you make your case cleanly in 250 words, stop — a reader would rather finish a tight half-page than wade through 500 words of throat-clearing. Fill the page only when you genuinely have a page of specific, relevant things to say.

Where the words go: a paragraph budget

Length problems are almost always distribution problems — one paragraph swells while another goes missing. Here's roughly how a ~300-word letter divides up:

  • 40–60 wordsOpeningThe role you want and the single strongest reason you're a fit. Name the job and hook fast — skip "I am writing to apply for…"
  • 140–200 wordsBody (1–2 paragraphs)Your proof: two or three specific, quantified wins that map to what this job needs. This is the part that earns the read — give it the most room.
  • 40–60 wordsClosingA line on why this company specifically, then a clear ask for the interview and a thank-you. Short and confident.

If your letter is too long, one of these is over budget — usually the body has three examples where two would land harder. If it's too short, you're probably missing the proof paragraph entirely and jumping straight from hello to goodbye.

Why shorter usually wins

Recruiters and hiring managers read a stack of these under time pressure, often on the first skim deciding only whether to open the resume at all. A tight letter respects that: it makes its point before attention runs out. A long one asks for patience you haven't earned yet, and it quietly signals the opposite of what you want — that you can't tell the important from the incidental. Concision is the audition for "can this person communicate."

There's also a simple trap in going long: the more you write, the more you end up restating your resume in paragraph form. The reader already has the resume. The letter's whole value is the context and judgment the resume can't carry — and that fits comfortably in 300 words.

Email cover letters run shorter

Length depends a little on where the letter lives:

In the email body

  • ~150–250 words
  • 2–3 short paragraphs
  • Front-load the ask — people read email on phones and skim hard
  • No formal address block; open with the greeting

Attached / uploaded document

  • ~250–400 words
  • 3–4 paragraphs
  • Full letter format with heading block and date
  • The standard one-page ceiling applies

When the cover letter is the email, treat it like the most important message in a crowded inbox: shorter, punchier, and impossible to misread on a small screen. When it's a separate attachment or an upload field, you have a bit more room for the full three-to-four-paragraph shape.

How to cut a letter that's too long

If you're over a page — or just want it tighter — cut in this order, and you'll lose length without losing the case:

  1. Delete the throat-clearing. "I am writing to express my interest in the position of…" is a sentence that says nothing. Start with the hook.
  2. Cut the third example. Two strong, specific proof points beat three where one is filler. Keep the ones that map most directly to the job posting.
  3. Remove anything the resume already says plainly. If a sentence just narrates a resume bullet, it's redundant — the letter should add context, not echo.
  4. Kill adverbs and hedges. "I truly believe I would be a really strong fit" → "I'm a strong fit." Confidence is shorter than qualification.
  5. One idea per paragraph. If a paragraph is doing two jobs, split it or drop the weaker half.

Quick reference

  • Sweet spot → 250–400 words, ~300 is comfortable.
  • Structure → 3–4 paragraphs: opening, body (proof), closing.
  • Page limit → one page, hard ceiling; half a page is fine.
  • Email version → shorter, ~150–250 words, 2–3 paragraphs.
  • Too long? → cut throat-clearing, a third example, and anything the resume already says.
  • Never → a second page, or padding to "fill" the first.
The whole thing in one breath. Write half a page to one page — about 250 to 400 words in three or four short paragraphs: a quick opening, a proof-packed body, and a brief ask for the interview. Keep an email version tighter still. If it spills past one page, it's not longer, it's unedited — cut the opening filler, the third example, and anything your resume already covers.

Frequently asked questions

How long should a cover letter be?

Half a page to one full page — about 250 to 400 words across three or four short paragraphs. That's long enough to make a specific case and short enough that a busy reader will actually finish it. The hard ceiling is one page: a cover letter that spills onto a second page almost never gets the second page read, and it signals you couldn't edit yourself.

How many words should a cover letter be?

Aim for 250 to 400 words, with roughly 300 as the comfortable middle. Under about 150 words usually means you haven't made a real case; over 400 means you're padding or repeating your resume. Word count matters less than whether every sentence earns its place — but if you're outside that range, it's a signal to add substance or cut fluff.

How many paragraphs should a cover letter have?

Three or four. A short opening that says what role you want and why you're a fit, one or two body paragraphs with specific proof, and a brief closing that asks for the interview. More than four paragraphs and each one tends to shrink into a single line; fewer than three and you're usually skipping either the proof or the close.

Should an email cover letter be shorter?

Yes. When the cover letter is the body of an email rather than an attached document, keep it tighter — roughly 150 to 250 words, or two to three short paragraphs. People read email on phones and skim hard, so lead with the role and your single strongest reason to talk, and cut anything that isn't pulling weight.

Is a one-page cover letter too long?

No — one page is the maximum, not a target you have to hit. A strong half-page letter beats a padded full page every time. Fill the page only if you genuinely have a page of relevant, specific things to say; if you make your case in 250 words, stop there. What you never do is go past one page.
