15 common resume mistakes — and the quick fix for each

Most resumes aren't rejected on merit. They're rejected on avoidable, mechanical mistakes — a layout a parser can't read, a bullet that lists a duty, a typo in line two. Here are the 15 that come up most, each with a one-line fix.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

A recruiter spends seconds on a first pass and an applicant tracking system spends milliseconds. Both are looking for a reason to say no, because saying no clears the pile faster. The good news: nearly every reason on that list is fixable in one edit. Work down the 15 below and you'll clear the bar that most applicants trip on before anyone even reads your experience.

Mistakes that break the parser

1 · Tables, columns and text boxes

They look tidy but ATS software reads them out of order or drops them entirely.

Fix: single-column layout, top to bottom, no boxes.

2 · Contact info in the header/footer

Many parsers ignore the header and footer region, so your email and phone vanish.

Fix: put contact details in the body, at the very top.

3 · Photos, logos and icons

Images carry no readable text and can confuse or bloat the parse.

Fix: remove them; text-only. Save visuals for a portfolio link.

4 · Creative section headings

"Where I've Made Magic" won't be recognised as your experience section.

Fix: standard headings — Experience, Education, Skills.

5 · Wrong file format

An image-based PDF or an exotic format can arrive as a blank to the parser.

Fix: a text-based PDF (or .docx if asked). Confirm text is selectable.

Mistakes in what the bullets say

6 · Listing duties instead of results

"Responsible for social media" describes the job, not your impact.

Fix: verb + what + number — "Grew Instagram from 2k to 11k in 6 months."

7 · No numbers anywhere

Unquantified claims are easy to skim past and easy to disbelieve.

Fix: add a metric to at least half your bullets — %, $, count, time.

8 · Weak openers

"Helped with", "Worked on", "Responsible for" bury your contribution.

Fix: start with a strong verb (see our action verbs list).

9 · Empty adjectives

"Hard-working, detail-oriented team player" is what everyone claims and no one proves.

Fix: cut them; prove the trait with a bullet instead.

10 · A vague objective

"Seeking a role to grow my skills" wastes the most valuable inch of the page.

Fix: a two-line summary — level, specialty, one proof (examples).

Mistakes in the details

11 · Typos and grammar slips

The one document you had unlimited time to perfect — errors read as carelessness.

Fix: read aloud, check the next day, have one person proofread.

12 · Too long

Two pages when one would do reads as padding, not seniority.

Fix: one page for most (the full rule).

13 · One resume for every job

A generic resume matches no posting's keywords well.

Fix: mirror the exact terms from each job description you apply to.

14 · Unprofessional or missing links

An old email handle, or no link to the work that proves your claims.

Fix: a name-based email, plus one portfolio/GitHub/LinkedIn link.

15 · Irrelevant filler

A 2014 retail job or a hobbies section crowding out what matters now.

Fix: keep the last ~10 years and only what supports this role.

The 60-second audit. Read only the first two words of every bullet — any that start with "Responsible", "Helped" or "Worked" get rewritten. Then scan for numbers; if a bullet has none, add one or cut it. Then check the layout is one plain column. That pass alone clears most of the 15 above.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most common resume mistake?

Listing duties instead of results. Most bullets describe what a role involved rather than what you achieved. Recruiters skim for impact, so a resume of duties reads as a job description, not a case for hiring you.

Do typos really get a resume rejected?

Often, yes. A typo signals carelessness on the one document you had unlimited time to perfect, and many managers use it as an easy first cut. Read it aloud, check it the next day, and have someone proofread before sending.

Why does my resume get auto-rejected?

Usually the layout, not your background. Tables, text boxes, columns, headers/footers and images confuse the applicant tracking system, so your experience arrives garbled. A single-column, standard-heading layout parses cleanly and reaches a human.

Should I use a photo or fancy template on my resume?

In most markets, no. Photos, colour blocks and sidebars break ATS parsing and add nothing a recruiter scores on. A clean, single-column, text-based layout is safer and reads faster. Save the visuals for a portfolio.
