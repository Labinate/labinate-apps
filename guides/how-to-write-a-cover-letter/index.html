How to write a cover letter that gets read

Most cover letters are skimmed in seconds or skipped entirely — usually because they restate the resume in worse words. A good one does the opposite: it connects your experience to this specific job in a way the resume can’t. Here’s the structure that works.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Do you still need a cover letter?

Sometimes. Write one when the application asks for it, when there’s a text box or upload field for it, or when you have something a resume can’t carry — a career change, an unusual path, a genuine reason you want this job. Skip it only when the posting explicitly says not to include one. A tailored letter rarely hurts and often tips a close decision; a generic one is worse than none.

The structure that works

Four short paragraphs, under one page. Each has one job.

1. The header — match your resume

Same name, font, and contact block as your resume, so the two read as one application. Then address a person if you can find their name; “Dear Hiring Manager” is a fine fallback. Skip “To Whom It May Concern.”

2. The hook — why you’re writing

Name the role and open with something specific: a result you’re proud of, a real reason this company interests you. Not “I am writing to apply for…” — the reader knows why you’re writing.

3. The proof — why you

One or two paragraphs connecting your experience to what the posting asks for. Don’t list your resume; pick the two or three requirements that matter most and give a concrete example of each — the same quantify-it discipline as your resume bullets.

4. The close — why them, and next step

A line on why this company specifically (shows you did homework), a confident thanks, and a simple sign-off. No begging, no “I hope to hear from you.”

How long should it be?

Half a page to one page — roughly 250 to 400 words. If it’s spilling onto a second page, you’re restating the resume. The reader is giving you thirty seconds; respect that.

Tailor it, or don’t bother

The single thing that separates a letter that works from one that’s ignored is whether it was written for this posting. Pull the two or three priorities the job description repeats and answer them by name — the same read behind tailoring your resume. A recruiter can spot a mail-merge letter instantly, and it reads as “I sent this to fifty companies,” which is exactly the impression you don’t want.

Mistakes that get a letter ignored

  • Restating the resume. If a paragraph could be lifted straight from your experience section, cut it. The letter is for what the resume can’t say.
  • All about you, nothing about them. “This role would help my growth” is the wrong direction. Lead with what you bring.
  • Generic openings. “I am a hard-working team player” says nothing. Replace every claim with a specific example.
  • Typos and the wrong company name. The fastest way to the reject pile. Read it aloud, and check you didn’t leave last week’s company name in.

Frequently asked questions

Do I still need a cover letter in 2026?

When the application asks for one, provides a field for it, or when you have context a resume can’t carry — a career change, an unusual path, a real reason you want this specific job — yes. Skip it only when the posting explicitly says not to include one. A tailored letter often tips a close decision; a generic one is worse than sending nothing.

How long should a cover letter be?

Half a page to one full page, roughly 250 to 400 words in four short paragraphs. If it’s running onto a second page, you’re almost certainly restating your resume — cut it back. Assume the reader is giving you about thirty seconds.

What should a cover letter include?

Four parts: a header matching your resume and a named greeting; a hook that names the role and opens with something specific; one or two paragraphs of proof connecting your experience to the posting’s top priorities with concrete examples; and a close on why this company, with a simple sign-off. Keep it under one page and tailored to the job.

What is the biggest cover letter mistake?

Restating the resume in weaker words, and sending the same generic letter everywhere. The letter exists to say what the resume can’t and to prove you wrote it for this specific posting. Pull the two or three priorities the job description emphasises and answer them by name, with real examples.
