How to list contract work on a resume

The one problem with contract work on a resume is that, left unlabeled, a string of six- and nine-month roles looks exactly like job-hopping. The fix is small and it's decisive: mark the role as a contract. The moment a recruiter sees "(Contract)," a short tenure stops being a red flag and becomes what it always was — a job that was supposed to end. Here's how to label, group, and quantify contract, temp, and consulting work so a fragmented history reads as a clean, deliberate career.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Contractors, consultants, and temp workers routinely undersell themselves on paper. The work is usually in-demand and adaptable — companies bring in contractors precisely because they need a specific skill fast — but the resume tells the opposite story: lots of employers, none for very long, no explanation. The reader's brain fills the gap with the worst assumption. Your entire job is to close that gap with one word and a bit of structure.

Step 1: label every contract as a contract

This is the whole game. Add "(Contract)" — or "(Contract via [Agency])," "(Consultant)," "(Temp)" — right after the job title or the company name. It costs you nothing and it reframes the entire entry.

Reads as job-hopping

  • Software Engineer, Northwind — 2023–2024
  • Software Engineer, Contoso — 2022–2023
  • Software Engineer, Fabrikam — 2021–2022

Reads as deliberate

  • Software Engineer (Contract), Northwind — 2023–2024
  • Software Engineer (Contract), Contoso — 2022–2023
  • Software Engineer (Contract), Fabrikam — 2021–2022

Same three jobs, same dates — but the right-hand column carries no whiff of instability, because contracts are expected to end on a fixed date. A recruiter reads it in half a second and moves on. Never make them guess; an unlabeled short tenure is the single most common own-goal on a contractor's resume.

Step 2: group placements under one staffing agency

If several of your contracts came through the same staffing or consulting firm, don't list them as separate jobs — that re-creates the fragmented look you're trying to avoid. Instead, make the agency the top-level "employer" and nest each client engagement beneath it. A run of six-month placements becomes one continuous, multi-year relationship.

Acme Staffing — Contract Consultant · Remote · 2021 – 2024

Client: Northwind Traders (2023–2024) — Rebuilt the checkout service handling 40k daily orders; cut latency 35%.

Client: Contoso Ltd. (2022–2023) — Led a 3-person team migrating billing to a new provider on a fixed 9-month timeline.

Client: Fabrikam Inc. (2021–2022) — Shipped an internal reporting tool adopted by 120 staff.

→ One employer heading, three engagements. Three years of "job-hopping" becomes one stable relationship with a clear thread.

If your placements came through different agencies, keep listing the client company as the employer — it's the recognizable name — and note the agency in a short parenthetical: "Northwind Traders (via Acme Staffing)." The client is what the recruiter recognizes and what the ATS matches against; the agency is footnote-level detail.

Step 3: don't confuse contract with freelance

These get mixed up constantly, and mislabeling one as the other misrepresents how you actually worked. They are different employment types and they get listed differently.

Contract / tempFreelance / self-employed
Fixed-term role, usually W-2 (via an agency) or a direct contract with one companyYou run your own business, typically 1099 / invoiced
One client at a time; you slot into their teamSeveral clients at once; you're external to all of them
The client sets the work, hours, and toolsYou set your own terms, scope, and schedule
List the client (or agency) as employer + "(Contract)"List yourself as "Self-Employed" or your business name

If you were placed at a company through a staffing firm and worked their hours on their team, that's contract work — call it that. If you invoiced multiple clients from your own shingle, that's freelance, and it has its own rules in how to list freelance work on a resume. Plenty of careers include both; just label each entry for what it actually was.

Step 4: quantify what you shipped — length doesn't

A short engagement is not a weak one. Contractors are hired to deliver something specific, fast — so lead every bullet with the accomplishment, not the duty, and let the result carry the entry. A six-month contract that shipped a product beats a two-year role that "assisted with" things.

Duties (weak)

  • "Contracted to help with the data migration project"
  • "Assisted the marketing team on a temporary basis"
  • "Provided engineering support as needed"

Delivered (strong)

  • "Migrated 2.4M records to the new platform in a 4-month contract, zero data loss"
  • "Launched 3 campaigns during a 6-month engagement, lifting signups 22%"
  • "Cleared a 60-ticket backlog and cut average resolution time 40%"

Good numbers for contract work: what you shipped, on what timeline, at what scale, with what measurable result. Because contracts are outcome-driven, they're often easier to quantify than open-ended permanent roles — use that. Start each bullet with a strong action verb.

Contract-to-hire and back-to-back contracts

  • Converted to permanent? List it as one entry across the full span, and note the transition: "Data Analyst (Contract-to-Hire), Jan 2023 – Present." Don't split it into two jobs — one continuous tenure that converted is a positive signal that the company wanted to keep you.
  • Two contracts at the same client, back to back? Merge them into a single entry with the combined dates; the renewal is itself evidence you did good work.
  • A gap between contracts? A month or two between engagements is normal for contractors and needs no explanation — the "(Contract)" label already tells the reader why the timeline looks the way it does. For a longer gap, the same rules as any employment gap apply.
  • A remote contract? Note the arrangement in the location field as you would any remote role — "Remote" or "Remote (Contract)."

Where contract roles go in the timeline

Contract work lives in your main work-experience section, in the same reverse-chronological order as everything else — it is real experience, not a footnote. Two cases worth a second thought:

  • Mostly contract history: a combination resume or a strong summary up top lets you lead with skills and headline results, so the reader sees the through-line before the individual short tenures.
  • A current long-term contract plus older permanent roles: straight reverse-chronological is perfect — just keep the "(Contract)" labels consistent so the two kinds of role are never ambiguous.

Quick reference

  • Label → "(Contract)," "(Temp)," or "(Consultant)" after every fixed-term title. Non-negotiable.
  • Same agency, many placements → group under the agency, nest clients beneath.
  • Different agencies → list the client as employer, agency in parentheses.
  • Contract ≠ freelance → client sets the work vs. you set the terms; label accurately.
  • Bullets → quantified deliverables, not duties. Length doesn't sell; results do.
  • Contract-to-hire → one entry, note the conversion.
  • Gaps between contracts → the label explains short gaps; no note needed.
  • Keywords → mirror the target posting so the ATS matches.

Frequently asked questions

How do you list contract work on a resume?

List each contract like a normal job — title, employer, location, dates — but add the word "(Contract)" after the title or the company so a recruiter instantly understands the short tenure was by design, not a firing or a failure. Write accomplishment bullets that quantify what you delivered during the engagement. If several contracts came through the same staffing or consulting firm, group them under that firm as one heading with each client nested beneath it, so a run of six-month roles reads as one continuous relationship instead of six separate jobs.

Does contract work look bad on a resume?

Only if you hide that it was contract work. An unlabeled string of six- and nine-month jobs reads as job-hopping and raises a red flag. The instant you mark each one "(Contract)," the same history reads as normal — contracts are supposed to end, and recruiters know it. Labeled contract work is completely respectable and often signals in-demand, adaptable skills; it's the missing label, not the contracts, that hurts you.

What is the difference between contract work and freelance on a resume?

Contract work is a fixed-term role, usually as a W-2 employee of a staffing agency or directly for one company, working one client at a time on terms the client sets — you slot into their team for a defined period. Freelance work is self-employed (typically 1099), where you run your own business, serve several clients at once, and set your own terms. On a resume, contract roles list the client (or the agency) as the employer with "(Contract)" after the title; freelance work lists you as self-employed or your business name. Label each one accurately — calling agency contract work "freelance" misrepresents how you worked.

How do you list contract-to-hire on a resume?

If a contract converted into a permanent role at the same company, list it as a single entry with the full span of dates and note the transition — for example, "Data Analyst (Contract-to-Hire), Jan 2023 – Present." There's no need to split it into two jobs; showing one continuous tenure that converted is a positive signal that the company valued your work enough to bring you on permanently.

Should you put the staffing agency or the client on your resume?

List the client company as the primary employer, because that's the recognizable name and the work a recruiter cares about, and note the staffing agency in a short parenthetical if it helps — "Client Co. (via Acme Staffing)." The exception is when you had many back-to-back placements through one agency: then make the agency the top-level heading and nest each client engagement beneath it, which turns a fragmented list into one continuous, easy-to-read relationship.
