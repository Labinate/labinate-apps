How to write a letter of interest

There's a company you'd love to work for, but they aren't advertising a role that fits you — or aren't advertising at all. So you write to them anyway. That's a letter of interest (also called a letter of inquiry or prospecting letter), and the mistake almost everyone makes is treating it like a cover letter. It isn't. A cover letter answers a posting; a letter of interest answers a question nobody asked. There's no job description to mirror and no one is waiting to hear from you — which changes what belongs on the page.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A cover letter has it easy: the job exists, the requirements are printed, and the reader is actively looking for someone who fits them. A letter of interest has none of that. You're interrupting a busy person who has no open role and no obligation to reply, and you have to create your own reason to matter. Do it well and you get on the radar of a place before a seat opens — the kind of edge you never get as one of three hundred applicants to a public posting. This guide is built around the one thing that makes that happen: your research, standing in for the job description you don't have.

Step 1: understand what actually changes

A letter of interest isn't a lightly-edited cover letter — three things flip, and each changes what belongs on the page.

What changesCover letter → Letter of interest
The triggerA posted job with listed requirements → no posting; you're reaching out on your own initiative.
What you tailor toThe job description → the company — its direction, a recent move, a need you can see.
The reader's stateActively evaluating candidates for a slot → not hiring, no obligation to reply; you're an interruption.
The ask"Please consider me for this role" → "I'd love a conversation / to be considered when something opens."
The riskBlending into the applicant pool → reading as spam, or as all-about-you with no reason for them.

This is a different letter from a standard cover letter, which is entirely driven by the job description. Here there is no job description — so the work you'd normally spend decoding the posting, you spend researching the company instead.

Step 2: let your research replace the job description

A cover letter gets its shape from the posting: mirror these requirements, hit these keywords. Strip the posting away and something has to fill that role, or the letter is just a list of your qualities floating in space. That something is what you know about the company.

  • Find a specific, current hook. A recent product launch, a funding round, an expansion into a new market, a stated goal, a public problem. One concrete detail proves you're reaching out to them, not blasting fifty companies.
  • Identify a need you could meet. "You're expanding into Europe; I've built localized support teams" is a reason to keep reading. It reframes your letter from a favor you're asking into value you're offering.
  • Skip it and the letter dies. With no posting and no company-specific hook, there's nothing anchoring your letter to reality — it reads as a template, and templates get deleted.
The hook does the job the posting can't: "I saw you just opened a second warehouse in Austin — scaling fulfillment is exactly the problem I spent three years solving at [Company], where I cut order-to-ship time by 35%." That's not "please hire me." It's "here's a need I can see, and here's proof I can meet it."

Step 3: connect your value to a direction, not a requirement

Because there's no list of requirements to answer, don't recite qualifications into a void. Tie your track record to where the company is going — the letter should read as if you'd already started thinking about how you'd help.

  • Lead with a relevant, quantified win. Pick the one accomplishment that maps to the need you identified in Step 2, not your whole career.
  • Say what you'd contribute, concretely. "I'd love to help your team do X" beats "I'm a hard worker looking for opportunities." Name the contribution.
  • Keep it about the fit, not the favor. The reader should finish the paragraph thinking "this person could actually help us," not "this person needs a job."

Turn duties into outcomes the same way you would anywhere — see responsibilities vs. accomplishments and how to quantify achievements. The prospecting twist is only this: choose the win that speaks to the company's direction, and let the resume carry the rest.

Step 4: keep the ask soft — you're not applying for a job

There's no role to apply for, so demanding an interview reads as confused. The whole letter builds to a light, low-friction ask that's easy to say yes to.

Too hard — skip it

  • "I am applying for the position of…" (there is no position)
  • "I look forward to interviewing next week."
  • Demanding a decision from someone with nothing to decide

Right weight — do this

  • "I'd welcome a brief conversation about where I could contribute."
  • "I'd love to be considered if a relevant role opens."
  • Offer to send more, propose a short call, give your availability

A soft ask isn't a weak one — it's the correct one. You're opening a door, not walking through it. The payoff of a good letter of interest is usually a conversation, a referral, or being the name someone remembers when a seat opens — so make that first small step feel effortless.

Step 5: get it to the right human

A cover letter has an applicant tracking system routing it to the right desk. A letter of interest has nothing — so who you send it to is entirely on you, and it decides whether the letter is read at all.

  • Find the actual decision-maker. The team lead, department head, or hiring manager who would own the kind of role you want — usually locatable on LinkedIn or a company team page.
  • Address them by name. "Dear Ms. Okafor" says you did the work; "To Whom It May Concern" says you didn't. See how to address a cover letter for finding and formatting the name.
  • Avoid the generic inbox. A letter to careers@ or a contact form usually dies because no one owns it. If you truly can't find a name, a specific department beats a company-wide address.
  • Plan a light follow-up. One polite nudge a week or two later is fair — you're not chasing a decision on a posting, just keeping a low flame lit.

Step 6: the structure — short and company-first

Keep it to half a page. You're the one asking for attention, so earn it fast and get out. A clean three-part shape:

  • Open: the company-specific hook from Step 2 — one sentence that proves you're here on purpose. See how to start a cover letter, but skip the generic "I'm writing to express my interest."
  • Middle: the need you see plus the quantified win that meets it, and a line on what you'd contribute. This is the core.
  • Close: the soft ask, a note that your resume is attached, and a confident sign-off. See how to end a cover letter.

On length, treat the shorter end of how long a cover letter should be as your ceiling — an unsolicited letter should never overstay its welcome.

Don't undo it — the two failure modes

There's a ditch on each side of this road. Steer between them.

A cover letter by mistake

  • "I am applying for the position of…" when there's no position
  • Mirroring requirements that don't exist
  • Demanding an interview for a job that isn't posted

All about you, nothing about them

  • A generic "please keep me in mind" with no company hook
  • Listing your qualities with no need to attach them to
  • A template you clearly sent to fifty other companies

The center line is a letter that's specific about them and useful to them: here's what I noticed about where you're headed, here's a proven result that fits it, and here's a low-friction way to talk. That's neither a misfired application nor a cold self-advertisement — it's an invitation worth opening.

Quick reference

  • It's not a cover letter → there's no posting; don't write "I'm applying for the position of…".
  • Research replaces the job description → open with a specific, current hook about the company.
  • Tie value to a need → connect one quantified win to where the company is going.
  • Keep the ask soft → a conversation or future consideration, not an interview demand.
  • Find the right human → a named decision-maker, never a generic inbox; address them by name.
  • Keep it short → half a page, company-first, resume attached.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a letter of interest and a cover letter?

A cover letter responds to a specific job posting: the role exists, the requirements are listed, and your job is to argue you fit them. A letter of interest — also called a letter of inquiry or prospecting letter — reaches out to a company that has no advertised opening. That single difference changes everything. You have no job description to mirror, so your research into the company replaces it. The reader isn't actively evaluating candidates for a slot, so your letter is an interruption you have to justify rather than an application they're expecting. And there's no specific role to ask for, so the ask is softer — a conversation, or to be considered when something opens, not an interview for a posted job. Writing a standard cover letter when there's no posting is the most common mistake, and it reads as confused.

How do you start a letter of interest?

Open with something specific about the company, not about yourself. Because there's no job description to react to, your hook has to prove you're reaching out to them on purpose — name a recent product launch, an expansion, a mission you share, or a problem you can see they're working on. That opening does the job the job description would: it establishes relevance and shows this isn't a mass-blasted template. A letter that starts "I am writing to express my interest in opportunities at your company" with no company-specific reason is indistinguishable from spam and gets deleted. Earn the reader's attention in the first sentence by showing you did the homework.

Who should you send a letter of interest to?

A real person, not a generic inbox. Because there's no posting, no applicant tracking system is routing your letter to the right desk — the human routing is entirely on you, and it's what makes or breaks a prospecting letter. Find the actual person who would manage the kind of role you want: the team lead, department head, or hiring manager, usually locatable on LinkedIn or the company's team page. Address them by name. A letter of interest sent to "To Whom It May Concern" or a careers@ address almost always dies, because no one owns it. If you truly can't find a name, a specific department beats a generic company address.

How long should a letter of interest be?

Short — half a page, three tight paragraphs at most. You're interrupting someone who wasn't expecting to hear from you and has no open role, so respect for their time is part of the pitch. Lead with the company-specific hook, connect your track record to a direction or need you can identify, keep the ask light, and stop. A long letter reads as if you don't understand that you're the one asking for a favor here. Attach your resume so the detail lives there, and use the letter itself to earn the click — make the next step, a brief conversation, feel easy and low-commitment.

When should you send a letter of interest instead of applying to a job?

Send one when there's a company you genuinely want to work for but no posting matches you right now — or no posting exists at all. It's the tool for getting on the radar of a place before a role opens, for reaching a small company that hires through its network rather than job boards, or for pitching yourself for work you can see needs doing even if no one has written the req yet. It won't always land a job immediately, and it shouldn't try to; its real payoff is a conversation, a referral, or being the name someone remembers when a seat opens. Pair it with genuine research and the right human recipient, and it opens doors that public postings — where you're one of hundreds — rarely do.
