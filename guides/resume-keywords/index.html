Resume keywords: find them, place them, don't stuff them

Before a human reads your resume, software often ranks it — on how well it matches the job's keywords. The good news: the honest way to win that match is also what convinces the recruiter. Here's how to do both at once.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Most large employers run applications through an applicant tracking system (ATS) that scores each resume against the job posting. Score too low on the terms that matter and a person may never see your file. But keyword-matching isn't a trick to game — it's a signal of genuine fit, and the tactics that fool a machine also insult the human who reads next. This guide is about the version that works on both.

What resume keywords actually are

Keywords are the specific skills, tools, certifications, methods, and job-title terms an employer is hunting for — and they're sitting right there in the job description. They come in a few flavours:

Hard skills & tools

Salesforce, Python, QuickBooks, AutoCAD, Google Analytics, GAAP, Kubernetes. The concrete, checkable ones.

Job titles & qualifications

"Project Manager," "PMP certification," "RN license," "5+ years." Systems often weight the exact title heavily.

Methods & domain terms

Agile, A/B testing, financial modelling, supply-chain, onboarding. The verbs and nouns of the work itself.

How to find them: read the posting as a checklist

You don't need a tool for this — you need the job description. Paste it somewhere and highlight every specific skill, tool, qualification, and the exact job title. Pay attention to repetition: anything the posting mentions two or three times is a priority the employer is telling you about out loud. Those repeated, specific terms are your keyword list. Then compare it against your resume and note what's missing but true of you — those are the gaps to close. (This is the same read behind our tailoring guide.)

Mirror the employer's exact wording

An ATS doesn't always know that "CRM software" and "Salesforce" are the same thing, and a busy recruiter scanning for "Salesforce" won't pause to translate. Use the employer's word.

Experienced with customer relationship management platforms
Managed the sales pipeline in Salesforce for a 200-account territory

Same truth, but now it matches the posting and proves the skill in one line.

Where to place keywords

Spread them across the parts a recruiter actually reads — not a hidden block at the bottom:

  • Summary: work in your title and one or two headline skills. See summary examples.
  • Skills section: the natural home for hard-skill keywords. See which skills to list.
  • Experience bullets: the strongest placement — a keyword sitting right next to proof you used it. A skill demonstrated beats a skill merely listed.

Why stuffing fails

Every "beat the ATS" hack you've seen — white text keywords, a hidden list, repeating a term a dozen times — is both easy for modern systems to detect and instantly disqualifying to any human who opens the file. Recruiters read the resume the parser passed along; a page that's obviously gamed goes straight in the bin. The durable move is simpler: use each keyword where it's genuinely true and backed by evidence. A resume that reads naturally to a person is the same one that scores well.

The keyword pass. Open the job posting and your resume side by side. Highlight the specific skills, tools, and the job title in the posting; circle anything repeated. Mark which of those are true of you but missing from your resume, and work each one — in the employer's exact wording — into your summary, skills, or (best) an experience bullet where you can prove it. Read the result aloud: if it sounds like a real person describing real work, you've done it right.

Frequently asked questions

What are resume keywords?

They're the specific skills, tools, qualifications, and job-title terms an employer is looking for — the words in the job description. Both applicant tracking systems and recruiters scan for them, so a resume that mirrors the posting's language reads as a stronger fit.

How do I find the right keywords for my resume?

Read the posting as a checklist. Highlight the hard skills, tools, certifications, methods, and exact job title, and note anything mentioned more than once — repetition signals priority. Mirror the employer's wording: if they say "Salesforce," write Salesforce, not "CRM software."

Where should keywords go on a resume?

Spread them across your summary, skills section, and — most importantly — your experience bullets, where a keyword sits next to proof you used it. A skill demonstrated is far more convincing than the same word in a hidden block.

Does keyword stuffing work on ATS?

No, and it backfires. White text and repeated terms are easy for modern systems to flag, and any human who opens the file rejects it on sight. Use each keyword where it's genuinely true and backed by evidence — the natural-reading resume is also the one that scores well.
