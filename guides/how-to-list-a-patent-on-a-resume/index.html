How to list a patent on a resume

A patent is one of the strongest lines a technical resume can carry — proof you invented something novel enough to survive a patent examiner. But it's also the line people format wrong, because they treat it like a publication or an award. It's neither. A patent is a legal grant of intellectual property, and it has its own citation format and its own honesty rule. Here's exactly how to list it.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Most resume items are self-explanatory: a job, a degree, a skill. A patent isn't — it lives in the small space between your publications, your honors, and your projects, and it doesn't behave like any of them. It's a government-issued legal document with a number, a status, and a list of named inventors, and the reader who values it — a hiring manager in R&D, an IP-literate interviewer — can look it up in a public database in seconds. So the format has to be precise. We'll settle what a patent is first, then the one placement decision, then the anatomy of a clean entry, and finally the granted-versus-pending line you can never cross.

The core idea: a patent is granted IP, not published literature and not a prize. List it with its title, number, status, and issuing office — and decide up front whether it earns a dedicated Patents section (engineering, hardware, pharma, biotech, research) or a single supporting bullet under the job (everyone else). The credential is the same; the placement is the strategy.

First: a patent is not a publication (or an award)

The most common mistake is filing a patent under the wrong mental category, which produces the wrong format. A patent shares a shelf with three other credentials but behaves like none of them:

CredentialWhat it actually is — and how it's cited
PatentA government grant of intellectual property. Cited by number + status + issuing office. Proves novel, non-obvious, protectable invention.
PublicationPeer-reviewed or editorial literature. Cited by authors, title, venue, year. Proves scholarly contribution — a different thing entirely.
AwardA competitive honor someone chose to give you. Belongs in awards. A patent isn't won; it's granted after examination.
CertificationA professional credential you earned by exam or training. Lives with certifications. A patent certifies nothing about you — it protects an invention.

If you have both patents and papers, keep them in separate sections. A reader scanning for IP wants a patents list; a reader scanning for scholarship wants publications — merging the two blurs what each is proving, and a patent listed like a paper (no number, no status) reads as if you don't fully understand your own credential.

The one decision: dedicated section or supporting bullet?

Everything about placement comes down to a single question — is inventing central to the role you're applying for, or is the patent a strong side-proof? Get that right and the patent lands with exactly the weight it should.

Dedicated "Patents" section

  • Engineering, hardware, semiconductors, pharma, biotech, materials, industrial or academic R&D
  • Roles where a hiring manager expects to scan an IP list
  • Placed after experience, near publications; each patent its own line
  • → The section itself signals inventing is part of your identity

A supporting bullet under the job

  • Business, product, consulting, marketing, generalist, or leadership roles
  • One or two patents that prove impact, not a core skill
  • Folded into the job where the invention happened, framed as an outcome
  • → e.g. "Co-inventor on a granted US patent for the core fraud-detection model"

Don't build a whole section for a single patent when applying to a non-technical role — it reads as academic overkill. And don't bury three patents in a bullet when applying to an R&D lab that wants to see the list. Match the shape to the reader, exactly as you would when you tailor the resume to the job description.

The anatomy of a clean patent entry

Once you know where it goes, the line itself is consistent. Name the invention, cite the number, state the status, and — where it matters — your position among the inventors.

A granted patent

US Patent 11,123,456 — Low-Power Sensor Array for Wearable Devices, granted 2024 (co-inventor)
Title, number, status, year, and your role. This is the complete, verifiable form — an IP-literate reader can confirm every word in a public database.

Your position in the inventor list

Patents don't rank inventors the way papers rank authors, but naming your role is honest and useful: sole inventor, co-inventor, or "one of five named inventors." Don't imply you were the sole inventor of a team patent — the front page of the patent lists everyone, and it's public.

The number — include it when it's verifiable value

For technical roles, the number is the proof; keep it. For business roles, "co-inventor on a granted US patent for [what it does]" is enough. Either way, a real number quietly proves the claim — see the FAQ for when to drop it. Never invent or misstate a number; it's checkable in seconds.

Multiple patents — summarize, then list

With many patents, lead the section with a one-line count — "8 granted US patents, 3 pending, in sensor and RF design" — then list the most relevant three to five in full. A long patent list, like a long resume, earns its length only in the fields where completeness is the point.

The bright line: granted vs. pending

This is the one distinction you can never blur. A patent that has been examined and issued is granted (or "issued") and has a real patent number. An application that's been filed but not yet issued is patent pending, and once it publishes — usually about eighteen months after filing — it has a published-application number, which is not the same as a patent number. Both are legitimate to list; only one is a patent.

Honest and precise

  • US Patent 11,123,456 — [Title], granted 2024
  • Patent pending — [Title] (US Application 17/987,654, filed 2023)
  • Co-inventor, US patent application for [what it does] (pending)

A claim the record contradicts

  • "Issued patent" for an application that's still pending
  • Dropping the word "pending" to make a filing sound granted
  • Citing an application number as a patent number
  • Claiming sole inventorship on a multi-inventor patent

Pending is respectable — claim it accurately and it works for you. A pending application shows current, active invention, and in many roles that's more impressive than a patent granted years ago. The only failure mode is overstating status: a filed application dressed up as an issued patent is a misrepresentation, and because every number resolves to a public record, it's a checkable one. The same honesty logic that governs an in-progress certification or a degree you haven't finished governs a patent: state exactly what's true right now.

Quick reference

  • A patent is granted IP — not a publication, award, or certification. Cite it by number + status + issuing office, and keep patents in their own section, separate from papers.
  • Placement is the strategy → dedicated "Patents" section for engineering, pharma, biotech, and research; a single supporting bullet under the job for business and generalist roles.
  • Entry format → title, patent (or application) number, status, year, and your inventor role. US Patent 11,123,456 — [Title], granted 2024 (co-inventor).
  • The number → include it for technical readers who value or verify it; drop it for business readers who need only the fact of a named-inventor patent.
  • Granted vs. pending is the bright line → never call a filed application an issued patent, never present an application number as a patent number.
  • Inventorship is public → name your real role (sole/co-inventor); never imply you were the only inventor of a team patent.

Keep sharpening the resume

With patents placed, tighten the rest of the technical page: list publications and honors at the right depth, turn invention work into quantified achievements, describe the projects the patents came from, and load the page with the right keywords so an ATS surfaces it. Frequently asked questions

How do you list a patent on a resume?

List it as a legal document, not a publication: give the patent title, the patent number (or application number if it hasn't issued), the status, the issuing office, and the year, and where it matters your position in the inventor list. A clean entry reads "US Patent 11,123,456 — Low-Power Sensor Array for Wearable Devices, granted 2024 (co-inventor)." If the patent is still pending, write it as "US Patent Application 17/987,654 — [Title], patent pending, filed 2023" so the status is unambiguous. Where the entry lives depends on the role: for engineering, hardware, pharma, biotech, and research positions where inventing is central, gather patents under a dedicated "Patents" section; for business, product, or generalist roles, fold a single patent into a supporting bullet under the job where you did the work — "Co-inventor on a granted US patent for the core recommendation algorithm." The one rule that never bends is that the number and status you write must match the public record, because any patent number can be looked up in seconds in a patent database.

What's the difference between listing a patent and listing a publication?

They look similar on the page but they're different kinds of credential, and conflating them is the most common mistake. A publication is peer-reviewed or editorially published literature — a journal article, a conference paper, a book chapter — and it's cited with authors, title, venue, and year. A patent is a legal grant of intellectual property issued by a government office, and it's cited with the patent number, status, and issuing office; there's no journal and no peer review, just examination and grant. The practical consequences are real: a patent proves you invented something novel and non-obvious enough to survive a patent examiner, which signals applied, commercial, protectable innovation, whereas a publication signals scholarly contribution to a field. Keep them in separate sections if you have both — a "Patents" section and a "Publications" section — because a reader scanning for one is looking for a specific kind of evidence, and merging them blurs what each is proving. A patent listed as if it were a paper (no number, no status) reads as if you don't understand your own credential.

Should you include the patent number on a resume?

Include it when the reader is technical and will value or verify it, and you can usually drop it when the reader is not. For engineering, R&D, hardware, pharmaceutical, biotech, and research roles, the patent number is the credential's proof of existence — an IP-literate interviewer or hiring manager can and sometimes will look it up in a public patent database, and a real, verifiable number is far stronger than a vague "holds several patents." For business, product, marketing, or executive roles where the point is simply that you were a named inventor on protected IP, the number adds clutter without adding signal, and "co-inventor on a granted US patent for [what it does]" communicates everything the reader needs. When in doubt, include it: a correct number costs one short string and quietly proves the claim is real. The only wrong move is inventing or misstating a number, or citing an application number as though it were an issued patent — both are checkable and both are disqualifying if caught.

How do you list a patent that's still pending?

List it honestly as pending, never as granted. A patent application that has been filed but not yet issued is "patent pending," and once the application publishes (typically about eighteen months after filing) it has a published-application number you can cite. Write it as "Patent pending — [Title] (US Application 17/987,654, filed 2023)" or simply "Co-inventor, US patent application for [what it does] (pending)," which is entirely legitimate and often impressive because it shows current, active invention. What you must never do is call a pending application an "issued" or "granted" patent, drop the word "pending," or present an application number as though it were a granted patent number — the distinction between a filed application and an issued patent is fundamental, publicly checkable, and something any IP-literate reader will notice. Pending is a real, respectable status; claim it accurately and it works entirely in your favor.

Where should patents go on a resume?

It depends on how central inventing is to the role you want. For patent-heavy fields — engineering, hardware, semiconductors, pharma, biotech, and industrial or academic research — create a dedicated "Patents" section, usually placed after experience and near publications, and list each patent as its own line; here the section itself is a signal that IP generation is part of your professional identity. For everyone else — business, product, consulting, generalist, or roles where the patent is a nice proof point rather than the core skill — don't build a whole section for one or two patents; instead fold each into a supporting bullet under the job where the invention happened, framed as an outcome: "Co-inventor on a granted US patent covering the fraud-detection model, now core to the product." The test is the reader: if a hiring manager in this field expects to scan a patents list, give them one; if they'd read a patents section as academic overkill for the job, let a single strong bullet carry the credential.
