The best resume font and size — settled

Typography won’t get you hired, but the wrong choice can quietly cost you: a font that looks cramped, dated, or that an applicant tracking system struggles to read. The good news is the safe zone is narrow and easy to hit.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

There is no single “best” resume font — but there is a short list of safe ones and a shorter list of point sizes that work. Get inside those ranges and your resume reads clean on screen, prints sharp, and parses correctly. Stray outside them and you invite the two problems that matter: a recruiter’s eye slides off the page, or the parser mangles your text before a human ever sees it.

Which fonts are safe

Stick to widely installed, highly legible typefaces. They render the same everywhere and every parser knows them. Pick one and use it for the whole document.

Sans-serif (modern, clean on screen)

Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, Verdana, Lato, Source Sans, Tahoma. A safe default for almost any field.

Serif (traditional, sharp in print)

Georgia, Cambria, Garamond, Book Antiqua. A good fit for law, academia, finance, and other formal fields.

Avoid

Comic Sans, Papyrus, Impact, and script or “handwriting” fonts — unprofessional or hard to read. Skip the Times New Roman cliché too: it’s legible but reads as the default nobody chose. And never use a font your reader may not have installed, or it silently swaps to something else.

The right sizes

  • Body text: 10–12 pt. Eleven is the sweet spot for most fonts. Drop to 10 only if you’re fighting for space; never go below.
  • Your name: 16–24 pt. It should be the biggest thing on the page — the anchor a recruiter’s eye lands on first.
  • Section headings: 12–14 pt, bold. Enough to break the page into scannable blocks without shouting.

If your resume runs slightly long, resist shrinking the font to 8 pt to force it onto one page. That’s a readability tax the reader pays. Cut words instead — see how long a resume should be.

Spacing and margins

Whitespace does as much work as the font. Line spacing of 1.0 to 1.15 keeps lines from touching without floating apart. Margins between 0.5″ and 1″ on all sides give the text room to breathe — tighten toward 0.5″ only if you need the space, and keep all four sides equal. A page that’s wall-to-wall text signals “too much” before a word is read.

Why the ATS cares about your font

Applicant tracking systems don’t just skim — they extract your text into a database. Standard fonts extract cleanly. Decorative or non-embedded fonts can come out as garbled characters, and text placed inside images or unusual layout boxes may not come out at all. This is the same reason a parser-safe layout matters as much as the font: see the ATS checklist and resume format guide.

The 30-second rule. One font, used everywhere. Body at 11 pt, name around 20, headings around 13 and bold. Line spacing ~1.1, margins ~0.75″ and equal on all sides. If it fits those numbers and reads easily at arm’s length, your typography is done — spend the rest of your energy on the words.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best font for a resume?

There isn’t one single best font, but a safe short list: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, Lato or Verdana if you want sans-serif; Georgia, Cambria or Garamond if you prefer serif. All are widely installed, highly legible, and parse cleanly through applicant tracking systems. Pick one and use it throughout the whole document.

What font size should a resume be?

Body text should be 10 to 12 point — 11 is the sweet spot for most fonts. Your name should be the largest element at roughly 16 to 24 point, and section headings 12 to 14 point in bold. Don’t shrink body text below 10 point to squeeze onto one page; cut words instead.

Is Times New Roman a good resume font?

It’s legible and perfectly acceptable, but it reads as the default font nobody actively chose, so it can look a little dated. A cleaner serif like Georgia or Garamond, or a modern sans-serif like Calibri, gives the same safe legibility with a more considered look.

Do fonts affect ATS parsing?

Yes. Applicant tracking systems extract your text into a database, and standard fonts extract cleanly while decorative or non-embedded fonts can turn into garbled characters. Text placed inside an image won’t be read at all. Sticking to common system fonts — and keeping text as real text, not pictures — keeps your resume machine-readable.
