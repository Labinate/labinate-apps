How far back should a resume go?

The standard answer is 10 to 15 years — enough to show a real track record, not so much that your best recent work gets buried under a first job from another era. Here's the rule, the exceptions that override it, and how to keep a long career tight.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

A resume isn't an autobiography. Its job is to make the case that you're right for this role, and that case is built almost entirely from your recent, relevant experience. Older roles compete for the same limited space and usually lose. So how far back should you go? Start with the rule, then apply the exceptions.

The rule: 10 to 15 years

For the large majority of job seekers, a resume should cover the last 10 to 15 years of work. That window is long enough to demonstrate progression and depth, and short enough that everything on the page still reflects who you are as a professional today. In most careers that works out to three to six roles — plenty to tell your story.

Anything older than that goes in one of two places: a brief "Earlier experience" line, or nowhere at all.

Why not include everything?

Space is your scarcest resource

Every early-career line is space stolen from a recent achievement a recruiter actually cares about. See how long a resume should be.

Old roles date you — and can invite bias

A job from 1998, tools no longer used, a graduation year far in the past: none of it helps, and it can quietly trigger age bias before anyone reads your recent work.

Relevance beats completeness

Nobody is impressed that you listed all 11 jobs. They're impressed by the three that match the role. Curate.

The exceptions that override the rule

Keep an older role — even one past the 15-year line — when it genuinely strengthens your case:

  • It's directly relevant. Same industry, same function, or a skill the job specifically names. Relevance always beats recency.
  • It's a recognizable name. A well-known employer or a marquee project can be worth a single line even if it's old.
  • You're in a field that values tenure. Academia, executive roles, and some trades expect a fuller history — norms vary, so match your field.
  • It fills a gap that would otherwise raise questions. Sometimes an older role is what keeps your timeline coherent. (See explaining employment gaps.)

When you keep an old role for one of these reasons, keep it short: title, employer, and a single line — no need for dates-heavy detail or a stack of bullets.

How to compress a long career

If you've got 25 years of experience and a rule that says show 15, here's how to make it fit without losing what matters:

  1. Full detail for recent roles. Give your last two or three positions real bullet points with quantified achievements.
  2. Trim the middle. Older-but-still-relevant roles get one or two lines each — just the highlight.
  3. Summarize the rest. Roll everything beyond 15 years into a compact "Earlier experience" block: "Earlier roles at [Company], [Company] in [field] (1999–2008)." One line, done.
  4. Cut repetition. If three roles list the same duty, keep it once. Duties repeat; achievements don't.
The quick test. Look at your oldest listed role and ask: "If I deleted this, would a recruiter be less likely to call me?" If the honest answer is no, cut it or shrink it to a line. Repeat until every remaining entry earns its space.

Frequently asked questions

How far back should a resume go?

For most people, 10 to 15 years — enough to show a track record without burying recent work under early-career roles. Anything older can be summarized in a line or left off, unless it's directly relevant to the job.

How many jobs should I list?

No fixed number — usually three to six relevant roles from the last 10 to 15 years. Give recent, relevant positions the most space and compress or drop older ones. Relevance matters more than completeness.

Should I include jobs from 20 years ago?

Usually not — they rarely help and can invite age bias. The exception is a directly relevant older role (same industry, notable employer, or a skill the job wants), which you'd keep as a short single line.

How do I fit a long career on one or two pages?

Full bullets for recent roles, a line or two for older ones, and a brief "Earlier experience" summary for anything past 15 years. Cut duties that repeat and keep only achievements.
