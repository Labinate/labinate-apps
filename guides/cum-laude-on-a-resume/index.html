How to list cum laude on a resume

You graduated with Latin honors — cum laude, magna cum laude, or summa cum laude — and now you're staring at your education section wondering where it goes, whether to capitalize it, and if it belongs next to your GPA or instead of it. The short answer: put it lowercase and italicized right on the degree line, lead with it over the raw number, and claim exactly the tier your diploma says.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

Latin honors are a graduation-wide distinction: at the end of your degree, your institution looks at your cumulative GPA (or your rank against the graduating class) and confers one of three honors — cum laude, magna cum laude, or summa cum laude — which then appears on your official transcript and diploma. That's what makes it such a clean resume signal: it summarizes four years of grades into two Latin words, it's tied to a real threshold, and it's verifiable. The only things people get wrong are cosmetic — capitalizing it, doubling it up with a GPA it already implies, or confusing it with an honors program they enrolled in. Get the small stuff right and it's one of the strongest lines a recent graduate can show.

The core idea: Latin honors are a summary, not a decoration. Because the honor already encodes a high GPA, the move is to lead with the honor and drop the number — written the way the convention expects (lowercase, italic, on the degree line), so it reads as a natural part of the credential rather than something you dressed up.

The three tiers — and what they mean

Latin honors come in three levels, awarded in ascending order of GPA or class rank. The exact thresholds vary by school (some use fixed GPA cutoffs like 3.5 / 3.7 / 3.9; others award to the top 10% / 5% / 1% of the class), so use whichever tier your institution actually conferred — you don't get to pick.

HonorLiterally meansRoughly
cum laude"with praise / with honor"The entry tier — a strong GPA, often ~3.5+ or the top ~10–15%.
magna cum laude"with great praise"The middle tier — often ~3.7+ or the top ~5%.
summa cum laude"with highest praise"The top tier — often ~3.9+ or the top ~1%, sometimes just a handful per class.

You don't translate these on a resume and you don't explain them — the Latin is the recognized credential, and every recruiter and ATS knows what they mean. Just make sure you're claiming the right one: summa is the highest, not the lowest, and mixing them up on your own resume is an unforced error.

First, what Latin honors are — and what they aren't

"Honors" is a crowded word on a resume, and Latin honors get tangled up with GPAs, Dean's Lists, awards, and honors programs. The defining trait is specific: a degree-wide distinction conferred at graduation on your cumulative record. Pin that down, because it decides both where the line goes and what you should not file under it.

Not the same as…How Latin honors differ
A GPAYour GPA is the raw number; Latin honors are the distinction that number earned you. Because the honor already implies a high GPA, it's usually the stronger and cleaner thing to show — often you list the honor instead of the figure, not alongside it.
The Dean's ListThe Dean's List is a per-semester recognition — you make it term by term for that term's grades. Latin honors are a single, final, graduation-wide classification on your whole degree. You can list both, but they answer different questions.
An award or scholarshipAn award recognizes a specific accomplishment or a competitive selection. Latin honors aren't won against other people for a project — they're conferred automatically once your cumulative GPA crosses the threshold. Keep them on the degree line, not in an awards list.
An honors college / thesis honorsAn honors college or program is a track you were admitted to; "honors in the major" or thesis honors is a research track you completed. Neither is a Latin honor. List those separately by their real names — don't blur an honors-program enrollment into "graduated with honors."

How to format it — lowercase, italic, on the degree line

Latin honors live in the education section, attached to the degree they belong to. Write the phrase in lowercase and italics, right after the degree and major.

The clean format

B.A. in Economics, magna cum laude
Boston College · 2025

The honor sits inline with the degree, italicized as the Latin phrase it is, no GPA needed beside it. A reader takes it in as part of the credential in a single glance.

Two small rules carry most of the weight. First, lowercase: it's magna cum laude, not Magna Cum Laude and never MAGNA CUM LAUDE — it isn't a proper noun, and title-casing it makes it read as a manufactured award. Second, italicize it, the standard convention for a foreign-language phrase. If your resume format strips italics (some plain-text and ATS exports do), lowercase alone is fine and still correct.

If you have several academic distinctions — Latin honors plus, say, a departmental honor and a scholarship — you can group them under a short "Honors" sub-line beneath the degree rather than crowding the degree line. But for the honor alone, inline is cleaner.

Lead with the honor, not the number

The most common question is whether to show your GPA and the honor. Usually: just the honor. Latin honors are awarded on a GPA threshold, so magna cum laude already tells a recruiter your GPA was high — repeating GPA: 3.8/4.0 next to it is redundant, and stacking both can read as padding.

Weak — redundant, tries too hardStrong — the honor carries it
B.S. in Biology
GPA: 3.9/4.0, Summa Cum Laude
Honors: graduated with highest honors		B.S. in Biology, summa cum laude

Show the number too only when there's a reason: an application field explicitly asks for GPA (some finance, consulting, and graduate programs require it), or your GPA is exceptional and you want the exact figure visible next to a lower honors tier. Otherwise the honor is the better single signal — treat your GPA and your Latin honors as two ways of saying one thing, and pick the stronger one.

A few common situations

You were in the honors college but didn't earn Latin honors

These are different things and it matters. An honors college or university honors program is a track you were admitted to; Latin honors are a GPA distinction conferred at graduation. If you completed the program but didn't hit the Latin-honors threshold, list it by its real name — University Honors Program — not as "graduated with honors," which implies the Latin distinction. Blurring the two is the one honesty trap here.

You earned departmental or thesis honors

"Honors in Economics" or a graduated-with-thesis-honors track is a real, separate credential earned through advanced coursework or a thesis — not the same as Latin honors. Name it precisely: Honors in Economics (thesis). If you have both, you can show them together: B.A. in Economics, magna cum laude; Honors in the Major.

Your school uses English or a different system

Some institutions confer "with distinction," "with high distinction," or "with honors" instead of the Latin, and many countries use their own system — UK first-class or upper-second-class honours, for instance. Use the exact form your institution conferred; don't convert "first-class honours" into a Latin tier or vice versa. Write what's on your diploma.

You're not sure you earned it

Latin honors are printed on your official transcript and diploma — if you're unsure, check there before you list anything. Because the distinction is on your permanent record, an employment or education verification can confirm it in one call, so claim exactly the tier that was conferred and no higher. There's no upside to guessing.

The bright line — claim the exact tier, format it plainly. Latin honors are one of the cleanest signals a graduate can show, and they need no dressing up. Write the tier your diploma actually says, lowercase and italic, on the degree line; lead with it over the raw GPA; and don't inflate it into a capitalized "award" or borrow the label from an honors program you enrolled in. Honest and understated reads as more confident than loud and padded — and it's the version a transcript check confirms rather than contradicts.

Quick reference

  • Lowercase and italic → cum laude, magna cum laude, summa cum laude — never title-cased or all-caps.
  • On the degree line → right after the degree and major, in the education section, not in an awards list.
  • Lead with the honor over the GPA → the honor already implies the number; show both only if a field asks or the GPA is exceptional.
  • Claim the exact tier → summa is highest; list what your diploma conferred, not the one you nearly made.
  • Keep it a few years, then let it fade → a headline as a new grad, a quiet education-line detail once your work carries the resume.

Frequently asked questions

How do you write cum laude on a resume?

Put it right on the degree line in your education section, lowercase and italicized: "B.A. in Economics, magna cum laude — Boston College, 2025". It's a Latin phrase, so the convention is to italicize it and leave it in lowercase — it's not a proper noun and doesn't get title-cased. Place it immediately after the degree and major, before or in place of the GPA, and don't give it its own "Honors" heading unless you have several distinctions to group. The whole point is that it reads as a natural part of the credential, not a decoration bolted on.

Should you capitalize magna cum laude?

No. Latin honors are written in lowercase — cum laude, magna cum laude, summa cum laude — and italicized as a foreign-language phrase. Capitalizing it into "Magna Cum Laude" or, worse, "MAGNA CUM LAUDE" makes it read as a manufactured award rather than the standard degree classification it is, and to anyone who knows the convention it signals you're straining to inflate it. Lowercase italic is both the correct form and the more confident one. The one exception is the start of a sentence or a bullet, where normal capitalization rules apply to the first word.

Do you list both your GPA and Latin honors on a resume?

Usually just the honors. Latin honors are awarded on a cumulative-GPA threshold, so "magna cum laude" already tells a recruiter your GPA was high — repeating the number is redundant and can even look like you're padding. Lead with the honor and drop the figure. The times to show both are narrow: when an employer or application field explicitly asks for a GPA (some finance, consulting, and graduate programs do), or when your GPA is exceptional and you want the exact number visible alongside a lower honors tier. Otherwise the honor is the cleaner, stronger signal on its own.

How long should you keep cum laude on your resume?

Longer than a GPA, but not forever. Because Latin honors are a fixed, verifiable distinction rather than a number that can be misread, you can reasonably keep them for the first several years of your career — through your first job or two. As your experience becomes the story, the honor fades to a quiet detail on the education line rather than a headline, and after roughly five to seven years most people let it sit at the bottom of the resume or drop it entirely. It never becomes wrong to include; it just stops earning its space once your work speaks louder than your transcript.

Is cum laude worth putting on a resume?

Yes, especially early in your career. Latin honors are one of the cleanest academic signals you can show: they're conferred on the whole degree, tied to a real GPA threshold or class percentile, and printed on your diploma, so they're both meaningful and verifiable. For a new graduate or someone with a few years of experience, cum laude and above are worth a place on the education line — they mark you as top-band without a single line of explanation. The only caution is honesty: list exactly the tier your institution conferred, because it sits on your official transcript and is trivially checked.
