The six steps to build one

Pick a format

For almost everyone the answer is a reverse-chronological, single-column layout — most recent job first, one column top to bottom. It's what recruiters expect and what parsers read most reliably. Skills-based and hybrid layouts exist for specific cases; the format guide covers when to reach for one.

Start from a clean template

Begin with a simple structure, not a decorative one. Two-column designs with sidebars, icons, and graphics look impressive but frequently scramble when software parses them — and a scrambled resume gets filtered out. Here's why most templates backfire and what to use instead.

Fill the core sections, in order

Header, then a short summary, then work experience, then education, then skills. Most recent job first. Every experience line is a strong verb plus a measurable result — that's the shape of a good bullet point. Not sure what belongs where? See what to put on a resume.

Keep it ATS-safe

Single column, standard fonts, real text — no tables, text boxes, or images — standard section headings, and one page for most people. Run it against the ATS checklist before you go further.

Tailor it to the job

A generic resume loses to a tailored one every time. Match your summary and top bullets to the wording and priorities of the specific posting — the method is in how to tailor your resume, and pulling the right keywords from the listing is half of it.

Export the right file

Export a text-based PDF unless the posting explicitly asks for Word. Name the file with your name and the role, proofread once more against the common-mistakes list, and you're ready to send — see how to email it.

Builder or blank word processor?

You can make a resume in any word processor, but two things trip people up there: the layout drifts out of ATS-safe territory the moment you add columns or text boxes, and the formatting slowly goes inconsistent as you edit. A purpose-built resume maker removes both — it holds the structure for you, so your attention goes entirely to the words. Whichever route you take, the output rule is the same: single column, standard headings, text-based PDF.

The whole build in one line. Reverse-chronological single-column format → clean template → header, summary, experience, education, skills in order → each experience line a verb plus a result → one page, ATS-safe → tailored to the job → exported as a text PDF. That's a finished resume.

Frequently asked questions

How do I make a resume for free?

What is the best program to make a resume?

A purpose-built builder beats a blank word processor for most people because it handles the ATS-safe layout for you. Avoid heavily designed templates with columns and graphics — they look nice but can scramble in a parser.

How long does it take to make a resume?

About an hour for a solid first draft if you know your job history. Most of that hour goes into writing strong bullet points, not formatting — a good builder handles the layout automatically.

Should I make my resume in Word or a builder?

Either works, but a builder removes the two things people get wrong in Word: an ATS-unsafe layout and inconsistent formatting. Whichever you use, export a text-based PDF and keep it single-column.
