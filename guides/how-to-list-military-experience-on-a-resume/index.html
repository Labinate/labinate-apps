How to list military experience on a resume

The core skill is translation. A civilian recruiter — and the applicant tracking system reading first — doesn't know what a Staff Sergeant, an 11B, or a "battalion S-4" does. Your service was full of leadership, budgets, logistics, and technical skill; the job is to say all of that in plain civilian language a hiring manager instantly recognizes. Here's how to translate ranks and MOS codes, turn responsibility into quantified wins, list a security clearance, decode your awards, and decide what to cut.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

Veterans usually have stronger raw material than most civilian applicants — real leadership under pressure, six-figure budgets, teams they trained, equipment they were accountable for. The problem is almost never the experience; it's that it's written in a language the reader doesn't speak. Every acronym, rank, and MOS code the recruiter has to decode is a moment they might give up. So the whole task reduces to one rule: if a civilian who never served can't read it and see the value, rewrite it.

Step 1: give each assignment a civilian job title

Lead with what the role was, in civilian terms — not your rank. A recruiter scanning your experience needs a title they can map to their own org chart. Put the branch as the employer and, optionally, note your rank once for context.

Operations & Logistics Manager · U.S. Army (Sergeant First Class) · Fort Bragg, NC · 2018 – 2024

Then accomplishment bullets in plain civilian language — the scope you led, the budget you owned, the results you produced.

→ Title first, rank as context. The reader knows what this job is before they read a word of detail.

Not sure what your MOS or rate maps to? Free public tools translate them for you — the O*NET military crosswalk and the branch "skills translators" take your code and suggest civilian job titles and matching occupations. Use them as a starting point, then pick the title that best matches the job you're targeting.

Step 2: translate the rank into leadership scope

"Staff Sergeant" tells a civilian nothing about whether you led three people or thirty. Rank is a pay grade, not a job description — so convert it into the concrete scope of responsibility it represented:

Military phrasingCivilian translation
Served as Squad Leader, Staff SergeantSupervised and trained a 9-person team; accountable for their performance, safety, and development
Battalion S-4 NCOManaged supply and logistics for a 600-person organization, including a $12M equipment inventory
Platoon SergeantSecond-in-command of a 40-person department; ran daily operations and personnel management

The pattern: people led, budget owned, equipment value, scope of the mission. Those are the numbers civilian hiring managers use to size a candidate, and the military gives you unusually strong ones.

Step 3: turn duties into quantified accomplishments

This is the same discipline every strong resume needs — accomplishments, not responsibilities — and service records are full of quantifiable wins if you mine them. Start bullets with a civilian action verb and attach a number.

Jargon & duties

  • "Responsible for PMCS on assigned vehicles"
  • "Conducted MOUT training for the platoon"
  • "Maintained 100% accountability of sensitive items"
  • "Served as unit RTO during deployment"

Translated & quantified

  • "Managed maintenance for a 20-vehicle fleet, sustaining 98% operational readiness"
  • "Designed and led urban-operations training for 40 personnel, improving readiness scores 25%"
  • "Maintained flawless accountability of $2M in sensitive equipment across a 2-year period"
  • "Operated and maintained tactical communications for a 150-person operation"

Good sources of numbers: headcount led or trained, dollar value of budgets and equipment, readiness or accuracy percentages, hours of training delivered, missions or operations completed, and zero-incident safety records. If you quantify the achievement, the reader doesn't need to understand the military context to be impressed.

Step 4: kill the acronyms and jargon

Acronyms are the single biggest reason a strong military resume gets skipped — every one the reader can't decode is friction. The fixes are simple:

  • Spell it out or cut it. "NCOIC" becomes "supervisor / person in charge"; "OIC" becomes "officer in charge / manager." If the plain phrase carries the meaning, use it and drop the acronym entirely.
  • Translate ranks, units, and codes. "Company" → "department of ~120," "battalion" → "organization of ~600," MOS/rate codes → the civilian job title from Step 1. Never leave a raw code on the page.
  • Keep only the acronyms a civilian employer actually knows. A recognized certification or a clearance level (Secret, TS/SCI) can stay. Internal service jargon cannot.
  • Match the target job's words. Mirror the keywords from the posting so the ATS and the recruiter both see the match — "logistics," "project management," "team leadership," whatever the civilian role calls it.

Security clearance: a real asset — list it

An active or recently-active clearance is expensive and time-consuming to obtain, and many employers filter for it — so it belongs on the page. But keep it to the level and status only.

Do

  • State the level and status: "Active Secret clearance" / "TS/SCI, current"
  • Put it in your summary or a short "Clearances & Certifications" section
  • Note if it's active — an active clearance is worth far more than a lapsed one

Don't

  • Name classified programs or projects
  • Describe what the clearance gave you access to
  • Include any sensitive or operational detail

The level and status are all a recruiter needs to know you're a match. Everything beyond that is off-limits — and volunteering it signals poor judgment, the opposite of what a cleared role wants.

Decode your awards and medals — don't just name them

A civilian reading "Army Commendation Medal" learns nothing; a civilian reading what you did to earn it learns everything. Translate the achievement, not the ribbon.

As listedAs translated
Awarded Army Commendation MedalRecognized among top 5% of unit for leadership and mission performance (Army Commendation Medal)
Received Meritorious Service MedalAwarded top service honor for sustained results managing a 200-person operation over 3 years

Naming the medal in parentheses is fine as a credibility marker — but the value lives in the plain-English accomplishment in front of it. A short "Awards & Recognition" line works, or fold the biggest ones into your accomplishment bullets.

Which format works best?

Most transitioning veterans are best served by a combination resume or a strong summary at the top. Here's why the choice matters:

  • Open with a civilian-facing summary. Three or four lines that state your target civilian role, your leadership scope, your clearance, and your top skills — in civilian terms. This reframes everything below it before the reader hits the first military title.
  • A combination layout lets you lead with a translated skills section (leadership, logistics, project management, technical skills) and then back it with your service timeline — useful when your MOS doesn't map cleanly to your target job.
  • Straight reverse-chronological is fine when you're targeting a role that closely matches your military work; just make sure each entry is fully translated.

Whatever the format, one page for under ~10 years of service, two if you led at scale — and how far back to go follows the same rules as any resume.

What to leave off

  • Unexplained acronyms and MOS/rate codes — translate or cut every one.
  • Classified or sensitive operational details — never, under any circumstances.
  • Most medal and award names on their own — translate the achievement behind them instead.
  • Heavy combat specifics — unless directly relevant to the job, keep it about leadership, skills, and results, not operations.
  • Internal jargon and unit nicknames — anything that only makes sense inside the service.
  • Personal data from your service record — no service number, no full DoD ID; the resume follows the same contact-info norms as any other.

Quick reference

  • Job title → civilian, not rank. "Operations Manager," not "Sergeant First Class."
  • Rank → translate into leadership scope: people led, budget, equipment value.
  • Duties → quantified accomplishments in plain language.
  • Acronyms & MOS codes → spell out or delete; use the O*NET crosswalk for titles.
  • Security clearance → list level + status only. No classified detail.
  • Awards → decode the achievement; name the medal in parentheses at most.
  • Format → civilian summary up top; combination if your MOS doesn't map cleanly.
  • Keywords → mirror the target job posting so the ATS sees the match.

Frequently asked questions

How do you put military experience on a civilian resume?

Translate it. List each assignment like a civilian job — a plain-English title (e.g. "Operations Manager" instead of your rank), the branch as the employer, location, and dates — then write accomplishment bullets that replace military jargon, acronyms, and MOS codes with words a civilian recruiter and the applicant tracking system understand. Quantify the scope you actually held: people led, budget and equipment value managed, missions completed. The goal is that a hiring manager who has never served can read your resume and immediately see the civilian value of what you did.

Should you put your military rank on a resume?

Don't lead with the rank alone — a civilian recruiter usually can't tell whether "Staff Sergeant" means you led three people or thirty. Instead, translate the rank into its civilian leadership equivalent in your job title and bullets: "Supervised a 12-person team," "Managed a $4M equipment inventory." You can note the rank once for context, but the scope of responsibility is what wins the interview, not the insignia.

How do you translate military experience for a resume?

Go line by line and swap military language for civilian equivalents: rank becomes leadership scope, MOS/rate becomes a civilian job title, unit becomes "team" or "department," and acronyms are spelled out in plain terms or removed. Turn responsibilities into quantified results — number of people led, dollar value of assets, percentage improvements, missions completed on time. Then mirror the keywords from the civilian job posting you're targeting so both the recruiter and the ATS recognize the match. Free online tools like the O*NET military crosswalk can suggest civilian titles for your MOS.

Should you list a security clearance on your resume?

Yes, if you hold an active or recently-active one — it's a genuine, expensive asset that many employers actively search for. List the level and status (e.g. "Active Secret clearance" or "TS/SCI, current"), usually in a summary line or a short "Clearances & Certifications" section. Never include classified details, program names, or specifics of what the clearance gave you access to; the level and status are all a recruiter needs, and everything beyond that is off-limits.

What should you leave off a military resume?

Leave off unexplained acronyms and MOS/rate codes, classified or sensitive operational details, most medal and award names (translate the achievement behind them instead), heavy combat specifics unless directly relevant to the job, and internal jargon a civilian won't understand. Keep what demonstrates transferable value — leadership, budgets, logistics, training, technical skills, security clearance — and cut or translate everything that only makes sense inside the service.
