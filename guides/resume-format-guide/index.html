The best resume format, settled

There are only three real resume formats, and for most people the right choice takes about ten seconds. Here's what each one is, the one you should almost always use, and the two situations where an alternative genuinely helps.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

"What format should my resume be?" hides two different questions. One is about structure — how you order your experience — and there really are only three answers. The other is about file and layout — the mechanics that decide whether software can read it at all. Get both right and you're done. We'll take them in order.

The three formats, in one glance

1 · Reverse-chronological — use this

A short summary, a skills line, then your jobs newest-first with dates and results. It's what recruiters expect and what parsers read best. If you're not sure, this is your answer.

2 · Combination (hybrid) — for career changes

Leads with a strong summary and a grouped skills section, then still lists a real dated work history. You get to front-load transferable strengths without hiding the timeline.

3 · Functional — almost never

Groups everything by skill and pushes dates and employers out of sight. Recruiters read it as "what are they hiding," and many parsers choke on it. Skip it unless you have a very specific reason — and even then, a combination format usually does the job better. See the full breakdown in the functional resume guide.

Why reverse-chronological wins for almost everyone

Two audiences read your resume: a piece of software first, then a human. Reverse-chronological is the one format both are trained on. The parser knows where to find your last title, employer and dates because they sit exactly where it expects. The recruiter can answer their first question — "what have they done lately?" — in three seconds. Any format that makes either of them work harder costs you, and reverse-chronological makes neither work harder.

When a combination format is the smarter play

There's one honest case for not leading with your last job title: when your last job title isn't the story you want to tell. A career changer, or someone whose recent role undersells their target, can open with a summary and a skills section that reframe them for the new field — and then still show the dated history underneath. That's a combination resume, and it threads the needle: it leads with strengths without triggering the distrust a fully functional, date-hiding resume creates. The functional format, and why to avoid it

A purely functional resume — all skills, no timeline — is usually a mistake, even when your instinct says otherwise. If you're reaching for it to hide an employment gap, don't: a well-presented gap reads fine in a normal timeline, and we cover exactly how in the employment-gaps guide. Hiding the dates draws more suspicion than the gap ever would.

The mechanics that matter more than the format

Whichever structure you choose, the layout has to survive parsing. This is where most resumes actually fail:

  • One column, always. Two-column layouts and sidebars scramble in most parsers — text jumps out of order.
  • Standard headings. "Experience", "Skills", "Education" — not clever labels a parser won't recognize.
  • No tables, text boxes or images. They're the single most common reason a resume comes out blank on the other side.
  • Real dates, consistently formatted. "Mar 2023 – Present", the same way every time.
  • Export a PDF (unless they ask for .docx). A PDF holds its layout; name it for the role.

The 10-second decision. Default to reverse-chronological. Changing careers or reframing a recent role? Use a combination format that still shows the dates. Never use a purely functional, date-hiding layout. Then, whatever you picked, keep it to one clean column with standard headings and export a PDF.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best resume format in 2026?

For almost everyone, reverse-chronological: work history newest-first, under a short summary and a skills line. Recruiters expect it and applicant tracking systems parse it most reliably. Only switch if you have a specific reason, like a career change or a first resume with no history.

Is a functional resume a bad idea?

Usually. It groups skills and hides dates and employers, and both recruiters and parsers distrust it because it reads as if you're covering something up. If your reason is a gap or a career change, a combination format that keeps a short dated history is the safer way to lead with skills.

What resume format do applicant tracking systems prefer?

A single-column reverse-chronological layout with standard headings — Summary, Experience, Skills, Education — plain text, real dates, and no tables, text boxes or images. The mechanics matter more than the structure: keep it to one clean column a parser can read top to bottom.

Which format is best for a career change?

A combination format. Open with a summary and skills that frame you for the new field, then still list your real work history in reverse-chronological order. You lead with transferable strengths without triggering the distrust a date-hiding functional resume creates.
