A phased return to work on a resume

You're easing back into your job after leave — three days a week, then four, then five — and you're wondering how to put that on your resume. Here's the short, freeing answer: you don't. A phased return is an arrangement about hours, and a resume doesn't itemize hours. List the job like any other, keep your dates continuous, and leave the ramp-up where it belongs — off the page.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Here's the whole thing in one sentence. A phased (or graduated) return is a schedule you negotiate with one employer — the same category of thing as flexible hours or a compressed week — so it's not a label, a bullet, or a section anywhere on your resume. The resume represents you by the role you hold and the results you produce, both of which are fully intact while you ramp your hours back up. The instinct to explain the arrangement is understandable, but it's backwards: putting "phased return" on the page raises a question a recruiter would never otherwise have thought to ask.

The core idea: a phased return is schedule mechanics, not resume content. List the role with continuous, honest dates; don't annotate your hour count; explain any leave-gap briefly if it shows; and keep the ramp-up — and any health reason behind it — private. You're back and you're capable, and that's the only thing the page needs to say.

1. It's a schedule, not a section

Start by naming what a phased return actually is: an arrangement about hours. You return to your role and increase your weekly hours gradually — often agreed with HR or occupational health after medical, parental, or long-term leave. It sits in the same bucket as "works flexible Fridays" or "compressed four-day week": a scheduling detail between you and your employer.

And resumes don't record scheduling details. A full-time employee doesn't annotate their entry "(37.5 hours/week, flexible Fridays)", and you shouldn't annotate yours "(part-time, phased return)" either. There is simply no place on a resume for an hour count — which means there's no place for a phased return.

What people think they need to write

Marketing Manager (returning part-time on a phased basis, 3 days/week rising to full-time) · 2022–Present

What actually belongs on the page

Marketing Manager · 2022–Present

Same role, same dates, no schedule annotation. The arrangement is between you and your employer.

2. List the role normally — continuous dates, no hour count

If you're still employed through a phased return, the resume shows the job as ongoing: "2022–Present." A temporary reduced-hours ramp doesn't change your title, doesn't end the role, and doesn't make the position "part-time" in the sense that matters to a reader. The value of a job on a resume is the responsibility you hold and what you deliver — and neither shrinks because you're at three days a week on your way back to five.

Over-explainingJust showing the job
Tagging the role "(part-time)" or "(phased return)"Title, employer, and continuous dates — nothing else

If a role is contractually and permanently part-time — not a temporary ramp, but the actual shape of the job going forward — that's a different situation, and you'd handle it the way you'd list any part-time job. A phased return isn't that. It's a full-scope role you happen to be easing back into. And if the role is permanently split between two people rather than ramped by you alone, that's a job share — a different arrangement again.

3. If a gap precedes the return, explain the leave — not the phasing

Here's the one thing a recruiter can actually see: any gap before you came back. The phased ramp-up is invisible to them; a stretch of time away is not. So if there's a gap, handle the leave, and let the phasing stay behind the scenes.

Usually that's a brief, neutral note — or nothing at all. A short gap of a few months often needs no explanation. A longer one can take a single line:

A leave, noted neutrally — the phasing never appears

Parental leave · 2023–2024

Medical leave · 2023

One line for the time away. The mechanics of easing back in (three days rising to five) stay off the page entirely.

Then let your return speak for itself. A role that resumes and continues is proof enough that you came back and you're performing. If the leave was parental, the maternity-leave guide covers that gap directly; if it was a longer stretch away from work, the career-break and employment-gaps guides handle the framing. In every case, the phasing itself is not part of the story you tell.

4. Put availability in the cover letter — never the accommodation

There's one legitimate reason hours might come up: you're applying to a different employer while still ramping, and your current availability is genuinely limited. Even then, the resume isn't the place — a forward-looking availability line is.

Say this, if you must

  • "Available for full-time work from March 2026."
  • A short availability line in the cover letter or header.
  • An honest answer in interview about when you can start.
  • Nothing at all, if your availability isn't actually limited.

Never this

  • "Currently on a phased return after medical leave."
  • Any mention of a health condition, disability, or accommodation.
  • A backstory explaining why your hours are reduced.
  • "(Part-time while I recover)" on the role itself.

You are under no obligation to disclose a health-related reason to be hired, and a resume is the worst possible place to do it: it invites bias you can't see or correct, and it does nothing to strengthen your case. The cover letter can carry a clean availability statement if one is genuinely needed. The reason behind the schedule stays yours.

The one bright line. A phased return is a schedule you arrange with an employer, not a status you announce on a resume. Represent yourself by the job and the results, keep employment continuous, explain any leave-gap briefly if it shows, and keep the ramp-up mechanics — and any health reason behind them — off the page.

What this is not

A phased return sits near several other situations, and it's easy to reach for the wrong playbook. Each of these carries a different concern — getting the category right tells you what to explain and what to leave alone.

Not the same as…How a phased return differs
A career breakA career break is a completed past period of not working that you're accounting for. A phased return is the mechanics of the comeback itself — you're already back. The break may need a line on the page; the phasing never does.
Maternity / parental leaveA specific kind of leave that may sit before a phased return. The leave — the gap — is the part that might appear on your resume. The gradual ramp-up back to full hours is not, and that guide owns the gap while this one owns "don't itemize the return."
A part-time jobA part-time job is a role that's genuinely part-time by nature, and you label it as such because that is the job. A phased return is a temporary reduced-hours ramp inside a full-scope role — don't call it part-time, and don't tag the hours.
A stay-at-home-parent resumeThat frames a whole re-entry after caregiving. Yours overlaps only if the leave was caregiving — but that guide is about rebuilding the whole resume after time out, whereas this one is narrowly about not putting a transitional schedule on the page.
A plain employment gapThe broad home for accounting for time away. The leave preceding a phased return is one instance of a gap and is handled there. The phasing itself, though, isn't a gap and isn't resume content at all.
The bottom line. The question "how do I put my phased return on my resume?" has a liberating answer: you don't. It's a schedule, and schedules aren't resume content. List your role with continuous dates and results-led bullets, note any leave-gap in one neutral line if it's visible, keep availability to a forward-looking cover-letter line if it's genuinely limited, and keep the accommodation private. You're back and you're doing the work — let the page say exactly that and nothing more.

Quick reference

  • Don't list the phasing → no "(phased return)" or "(part-time)" tag; it's a schedule, not a section.
  • Keep the role continuous → "2022–Present," no hour count, no split before/after leave.
  • Explain the leave, not the ramp → one neutral line for a visible gap ("Medical leave, 2023"); the return itself stays invisible.
  • Availability goes in the cover letter → "Available full-time from [month]," only if genuinely limited.
  • Never disclose the health reason → no condition, disability, or accommodation on the page.
  • Contractually part-time is different → if the role is permanently part-time, list it as a part-time job.

Frequently asked questions

Do I put a phased return to work on my resume?

No — in almost every case a phased return does not belong on your resume at all. A phased return is an arrangement about hours: you go back to your job and ramp your weekly hours up gradually over a few weeks or months, usually agreed with HR or occupational health after medical, parental, or long-term leave. Resumes list roles, titles, dates, and results — they don't itemize how many hours you work in a given week, any more than a full-time employee annotates their entry "(37.5 hours, flexible Fridays)". So there is no "phased return" label, bullet, or section to add. List the role the way you'd list any job: title, employer, continuous dates, and what you accomplished. The schedule is a private matter between you and the employer, disclosed if ever in conversation during onboarding, not advertised on the page. The instinct to explain the arrangement is understandable but backwards — putting it on the resume raises a question a recruiter would never otherwise have asked.

How do I show reduced hours during a phased return on my resume?

You don't show them — and that's the correct move, not a dodge. A temporary reduced-hours ramp inside a full-scope role doesn't change your title and doesn't make the job "part-time" in the sense that matters to a resume. If you're still employed while phasing back in, show the role as ongoing — "Marketing Manager, 2022–Present" — with no hour count and no "(part-time)" tag. The value of a role on a resume is the responsibility you hold and the results you produce, and those don't shrink because you're temporarily at three days a week on your way back to five. The only situation where hours become relevant is if you're applying to a different employer and genuinely need them to know your current availability — and even then it belongs in a forward-looking availability line ("available full-time from [month]"), not as a description of your accommodation. If a role is contractually and permanently part-time, that's a different thing: see how to list a part-time job.

Should I disclose that my phased return was for medical or health reasons?

No. You are under no obligation to disclose a health condition, a disability, or a medical accommodation to get a job, and a resume is exactly the wrong place to do it. Disclosing a medical reason invites bias you can't see and can't correct, and it does nothing to strengthen your candidacy — the resume's job is to show you can do the work, and a phased return after leave is evidence that you can. If there's a gap before your return because you were on leave, you can note the leave itself neutrally and briefly ("Medical leave, 2023") or leave a short gap unexplained entirely, but the reason behind a phased schedule stays private. Keep the framing forward-looking: you're back, you're capable, and you're building toward full capacity. Nothing about a private accommodation needs to travel onto the page to make that case.

How do I explain the gap before a phased return?

Explain the leave, not the phasing — because the gap, not the gradual ramp-up, is the only part a recruiter can actually see. If you were away for a stretch before returning, treat it like any period of leave: a brief, neutral note is usually enough ("Parental leave, 2023–2024" or "Family medical leave, 2023"), and a short gap of a few months often needs no explanation at all. Then let your return speak for itself — a role that resumes and continues is proof you came back and are performing. The mechanics of how you eased back in (three days a week rising to five, say) are behind the scenes and irrelevant to the reader. If the leave was parental, the maternity-leave guide covers the gap directly; if it was a longer stretch away from work entirely, the career-break and employment-gaps guides handle that framing. In every case the phasing itself stays off the page.

I'm job-hunting during a phased return — what do I tell a new employer?

Keep it forward-looking and only as detailed as the new employer needs. On the resume, list your current role normally with continuous dates and no mention of the phased schedule. The one thing a prospective employer legitimately cares about is when you can start and at what capacity — so if your availability is genuinely limited right now, put a short availability line in your cover letter or resume header ("Available for full-time work from [month]") and leave it at that. You don't owe them the reason: not that you were on leave, not that you're phasing back, not any health detail. If asked directly in an interview whether you can meet the role's hours, answer honestly about your availability going forward without narrating the medical or personal history behind it. The goal is to present a capable professional who is ready — or nearly ready — for the role, and to keep a private arrangement with your current employer exactly that: private.
