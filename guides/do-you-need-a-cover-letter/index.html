Do you need a cover letter?

The honest answer is "almost always yes" — not because a cover letter is magic, but because it's one of the few levers you still control after your resume is done, and skipping it rarely helps you. This guide is the decision, not the writing: when a cover letter clearly moves the needle, the handful of times you can safely skip it, what "optional" really means, and whether anyone still reads them.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

The guide in one line: default to sending a cover letter, and only skip it when the posting tells you to, the form gives you no way to attach one, or a recruiter has said resume-only. A tailored letter can only help when it's read; a missing one can only hurt when it's expected — so the safe bet is to write it.

The short answer: send one by default

Frame it as a bet, not a rule. Writing a short, specific cover letter costs you maybe twenty minutes. If the reader skims past it, you've lost twenty minutes. If the reader actually reads it — and you can't tell in advance who will — a good letter can be the thing that separates you from three other candidates with near-identical resumes. That's an asymmetric payoff: small, bounded downside, meaningful upside. When the downside is capped and the upside isn't, you take the bet.

So flip the usual question. Don't ask "does this job require a cover letter?" Ask "is there a specific reason to skip it?" If there isn't a clear one — and there usually isn't — you write the letter.

When a cover letter clearly helps

In some situations a letter isn't just a nice-to-have; it's doing real work your resume can't. Send one for sure when:

  • You're changing careers. Your resume shows where you've been; the letter is where you connect those dots to where you're going. See the career-change resume guide for the resume half.
  • You have an employment gap or an unusual path. A sentence of context beats leaving the reader to guess. (More in handling employment gaps.)
  • The role is competitive or senior. The more the resumes look alike, the more the letter becomes the tiebreaker.
  • You're a strong fit but your resume doesn't scream it. Transferable skills, a non-obvious match, a relevant side project — the letter is where you spell out the connection.
  • It's a smaller company or a specific team. Hiring managers there are far more likely to read every word than a high-volume corporate recruiter is.
  • The posting asks for one. Obvious, but worth stating: if it's requested, a missing letter can get you filtered out before anyone reads your resume.

When you can safely skip it

There are a few genuine exceptions. Skipping is fine — not lazy — when:

  • The posting explicitly says not to send one. "No cover letters, please" is an instruction; ignoring it reads as not following directions.
  • There's nowhere to put it. If the application form has no upload field and no message box, you're not expected to force one in.
  • A recruiter has told you resume-only. If someone running the process says they just need your CV, believe them.
  • It's a one-click quick-apply. On some job boards, "apply" fires off your resume with no cover-letter step at all — that's the platform's design, not a mark against you.

Notice what's not on that list: "I don't feel like it," "I'm applying to a lot of jobs," or "I don't think they read them anyway." Those are reasons it's tempting to skip, not reasons it's smart to.

"Optional" is a soft test — take it

The trickiest case is the application that marks the cover letter optional. Read that word carefully: optional means you won't be rejected for leaving it out — it does not mean leaving it out helps you.

What "optional" really sorts

Two candidates with the same resume apply. One attaches a sharp, tailored letter; one skips it. To a hiring manager, the second candidate is now indistinguishable from everyone who couldn't be bothered — and the first has quietly signalled effort and genuine interest. "Optional" is where motivated applicants separate themselves for free. If you want the job, write the letter.

Do employers actually read cover letters?

Honestly? It's a mixed picture, and pretending otherwise doesn't help you. High-volume recruiters filling dozens of similar roles often skim or skip them. Hiring managers — especially at smaller companies, and for competitive or senior positions — frequently read them closely and use them to break ties. Some applicant systems surface the letter prominently; others bury it.

The point that actually matters: you don't know which reader you'll get. You're not deciding "will this specific person read my letter?" — you can't know that. You're deciding whether to hold a ticket in a lottery that costs twenty minutes and occasionally pays out an interview. Across a job search of many applications, holding those tickets wins more often than not.

If you're going to send one, don't phone it in

One caveat flips the whole calculation: a generic cover letter — the "Dear Sir/Madam, I am a hardworking team player writing to apply for the position" kind — can actively hurt you. It signals low effort and wastes the reader's goodwill. A letter is worth sending only if it's specific to this job and this company.

The good news is that "specific" doesn't mean "long." A strong letter is three or four short paragraphs — 250–400 words — that name the role, open with a real hook, give a piece or two of concrete evidence, and close with a clear ask. If you're going to spend the twenty minutes, spend them making it un-copy-pasteable rather than making it long.

How to decide, in one pass

✓ Send a cover letter

  • The posting asks for one
  • It's marked "optional"
  • Career change, gap, or unusual path
  • Competitive or senior role
  • Smaller company / specific team
  • You're a strong-but-non-obvious fit
  • No reason not to (the default)

✕ Fine to skip

  • Posting says "no cover letters"
  • No field or box to attach one
  • Recruiter said resume-only
  • One-click quick-apply, no step for it

Quick reference

  • Default → send one unless there's a specific reason not to.
  • "Required" → always send; a missing one can filter you out.
  • "Optional" → send; it's where motivated candidates separate themselves.
  • "No cover letter" / no field / recruiter said no → safe to skip.
  • Read or not? → you can't know, so play the odds — the bet is cheap and sometimes decisive.
  • The one rule → a specific, tailored letter helps; a generic one hurts. Never send the generic one.
The whole thing in one breath. Default to sending a cover letter. Skip it only when the posting tells you to, there's nowhere to attach one, or a recruiter said resume-only. "Optional" is not a free pass — it's where you quietly out-effort the people who skipped it. You can't know whether any given reader will read your letter, so treat it as a cheap ticket in a lottery that occasionally pays an interview — and make sure the ticket is tailored, because a generic letter is worse than none.

Frequently asked questions

Do you really need a cover letter?

For most applications, yes — or at least you should default to sending one. A tailored cover letter is one of the few things still under your control once your resume is written: it lets you explain fit, address a career change or an employment gap, and show you actually understand the role. Unless the posting explicitly says "no cover letter required" or provides no way to attach one, writing a short, specific letter can only help. The safe rule is: send one by default, and only skip it when there's a clear reason to.

Do employers still read cover letters in 2026?

Some do, some don't — and you rarely know in advance which kind of employer you're dealing with. Many recruiters skim or skip cover letters, especially for high-volume roles; others, particularly hiring managers at smaller companies and for competitive or senior positions, read them closely and use them to break ties between similar candidates. Because you can't tell which reader you'll get, the expected value of a good letter is positive: it costs you twenty minutes and, when it is read, it can be the thing that moves you from "maybe" to "interview."

Should you send a cover letter if it says optional?

Yes. When an application marks the cover letter "optional," treat that as a soft test rather than a genuine free pass. The candidates who attach a sharp, tailored letter signal extra effort and interest; the ones who skip it look identical to everyone who couldn't be bothered. "Optional" means you won't be rejected for leaving it out, not that leaving it out helps you. If you want the job, write the letter.

When can you skip a cover letter?

You can safely skip it when the posting explicitly says not to send one, when the application form gives you no field to attach or paste it, or when a recruiter has told you directly that they only need your resume. It's also reasonable to skip on quick-apply job boards that submit your resume in one click with no cover-letter step. Outside those cases — and never as a substitute for writing one when you simply don't feel like it — a cover letter is worth including.
