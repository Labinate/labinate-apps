How to list continuing education on a resume

CEUs, CPE, CLE, CME, PDH — the recurring hours you complete to keep a license or certification alive. The instinct is to list them to prove you stay current. The stronger move is almost the opposite: you rarely itemize the hours at all, because an active credential already tells the reader you're meeting the requirement behind it. Here's when continuing education is worth naming, when it's just padding, and exactly where it goes.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

The one-line version: let the active credential do the work, and only name a specific CE course when its topic is directly relevant to the job. Continuing education is a maintenance obligation that every holder of your license meets — so a running tally of hours proves nothing. What earns space on the page is a course whose subject makes a hiring manager for this role more confident in you.

Why you almost never itemize the hours

Continuing education exists to keep a credential from lapsing. A board sets a number of hours per renewal cycle, you complete them, and your license stays active. That's the whole transaction — and it's the reason a list of CE hours reads as filler:

  • Everyone with the credential does it. If you hold an active RN license, CPA, or bar admission, meeting your CEU/CPE/CLE requirement is the price of keeping it. Listing it says only that you did the minimum required to keep working — which the credential itself already proves.
  • Hour counts signal nothing. "40 CEUs completed 2024" is not an achievement; it's a compliance receipt. It occupies a line a reviewer skims past, and it pushes your real accomplishments — the results that actually differentiate you — further down the page.

The fix is quiet and effective: show the credential is current and in good standing, and let it imply the continuing education behind it. A line like RN License, State of Ohio — Active carries the whole CE story without a single hour on the page.

When a specific course is worth naming

There's one case where continuing education earns real estate: when a specific course is directly relevant to the job you're targeting and sets you apart from others holding the same license. Not the hours — the topic.

The test: would naming this exact course make a hiring manager for this specific role more confident you can do the work? A nurse who completed a specialized wound-care CE and is applying to a wound-care unit; a CPA who took forensic-accounting CPE and is moving into forensic work; an engineer whose PDH went into a code or safety standard the role centers on — each has targeted evidence worth one clean line. Generic required hours never pass this test.

When a course passes, treat it like any relevant credential: name the subject, the provider, and the year — not the CEU count.

Name it when

  • The topic maps directly onto the target role.
  • It's specialized — not everyone with your license took it.
  • It fills a gap the job posting emphasizes.

Leave it off when

  • It's generic required hours to renew.
  • You're just listing a total ("30 CEUs").
  • The topic has nothing to do with the role.

Where it goes on the page

Placement follows the same volume-and-relevance logic as the rest of the resume:

  1. Default — fold it into the credential. Show the license or certification is active (see how to list a license and certifications on a resume). No separate CE line needed; the active status carries it.
  2. Two or three standout courses — a compact subsection. If you have relevant, name-worthy courses, add a short "Professional Development" or "Continuing Education" block near your certifications. One line each: subject, provider, year.
  3. Never a wall of hours. No running CEU tally, no list of every required course. That's the padding to avoid — it reads as filler and costs you the reader's attention.
The trap

Turning mandatory continuing education into a "professional development" highlight reel — listing every renewal course and a total hour count as if the volume itself is impressive. It reads as padding, and to anyone in your field it signals you don't realize CE is table stakes, not a differentiator.

→ Show the credential is active, name only the one or two courses genuinely relevant to the role, and spend the reclaimed space on quantified results.

Formatting a course line, when you do include one

Keep it to a single line, subject-first, and skip the hour count:

Instead ofWrite
"Completed 24 CEUs, 2024"Wound Care Management (advanced) — American Nurses Credentialing Center, 2024
"40 hours CPE, various topics"Forensic Accounting & Fraud Examination — AICPA, 2024
"CLE requirement met"Data Privacy & Cybersecurity Law — State Bar CLE, 2025

The right column works because it names a skill a reader can picture you applying. The left column just confirms you renewed something.

A note on honesty

Continuing education is board-auditable. Licensing bodies keep records of what you actually completed, and reference or credential checks can surface a course you claimed but didn't take. Only list CE you genuinely completed — the point of surfacing a course is that it's true and relevant, not that it fills a line. As with every part of the resume, a credential you can't back up collapses on verification.

Frequently asked questions

Should you list continuing education on a resume?

Usually not as itemized hours. Continuing education — CEUs, CPE, CLE, CME, PDH — is the coursework you complete to keep a license or certification active, and it is a maintenance obligation that everyone with the credential meets, not an achievement that sets you apart. Listing a wall of hours ("30 CEUs completed in 2024") tells a hiring manager nothing except that you did the minimum required to keep working. The far stronger move is to show the credential itself is current and in good standing, which already implies you are meeting the continuing-education requirement behind it. Only surface a specific CE course when its topic is directly relevant to the job you are targeting.

When is it worth naming a specific continuing-education course?

When the course topic is directly relevant to the target role and differentiates you from other candidates who hold the same license. A nurse who completed a specialized wound-care CE course and is applying to a wound-care unit, or a CPA who took forensic-accounting CPE and is moving into forensic work, has real, targeted evidence worth one line. In that case, name the course and the subject — not the hour count — and treat it like any other relevant credential. The test is simple: would this specific course make a hiring manager for this specific job more confident you can do it? If yes, name it. If it is just generic required hours, leave it off.

Where does continuing education go on a resume?

Fold it into the license or certification entry it maintains — showing the credential is active and current (for example, "RN License, State of Ohio — Active") already tells the reader you meet the continuing-education requirement, so no separate CE line is needed. If you have two or three standout, role-relevant courses worth naming, place them in a compact "Professional Development" or "Continuing Education" subsection near your certifications, formatted as one clean line each: course or subject, provider, and year. Never create a long list of every required course or a running tally of hours — that reads as padding and pushes your actual accomplishments down the page.

What is the difference between continuing education and certifications on a resume?

A certification is a credential you earned — you passed an exam or met a standard and an issuing authority awarded it. Continuing education is what you do afterward to keep that credential alive: the recurring CEU, CPE, CLE, CME, or PDH hours a licensing board requires so the certification or license does not lapse. They sit on opposite ends of the same credential's life. Certifications are worth listing by name because earning them is the achievement; continuing education is usually implied by the credential still being active, and is only worth naming when a specific course is directly relevant to the job.

Is continuing education the same as an online course on a resume?

No. Online courses are typically self-directed, elective learning — a Coursera, edX, or Udemy course you chose to take to build a skill. Continuing education is required, recurring coursework tied to maintaining a professional license or certification, governed by a board with a set number of hours per cycle. An online course is worth listing when it demonstrates a relevant, self-taught skill; continuing education is usually best left implied by the active credential, and surfaced only when a specific required course happens to be directly relevant to the target role.
