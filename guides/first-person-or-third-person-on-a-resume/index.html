First or third person on a resume?

The honest answer is neither — not literally. A resume isn't written in full first person ("I led a team") and it's definitely not written in third person ("John led a team"). It uses a third voice, unique to resumes: implied first person, where you keep your own perspective but drop the pronoun — "Led a team." This guide shows you the rule, the two traps that break it, how to apply it across every section, and the specific documents where writing "I" is actually the correct move.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

This question comes up because a resume feels like it should be about you, so people reach for the two voices they know from writing class: first person ("I did this") or third person ("She did this"). Both are wrong on a resume, for different reasons — one is redundant, the other is oddly distant. The convention every recruiter expects is the one nobody teaches in school: start with the verb, let the "I" be understood. Once you see it, you can't unsee it.

The one rule: the resume body — every bullet and your summary — is implied first person: drop "I", "me", "my", and "we", and lead with the verb. The moment you switch to a document that reads as prose in your own voice — a cover letter, your LinkedIn "About", a bio — you switch back to explicit first person with "I". The one voice that's never right for talking about yourself, anywhere, is third person.

First, know what this is not

"What person is a resume in" gets tangled up with a few neighboring questions about wording. They sound related but are different choices — and mixing them up is how a bullet ends up correct on one axis and wrong on another.

QuestionHow it differs
Verb tense (past vs. present)Tense is about time: past for finished roles ("Led"), present for your current one ("Lead"). Person is about the pronoun: implied first person drops the "I". You set both on every bullet independently — a past-role line is implied-first-person and past tense.
Words to avoidThat's a list of empty buzzwords and clichés ("results-driven", "team player") to cut. This is about grammatical voice, not word choice — you can write a jargon-free bullet that still wrongly starts with "I".
How to describe yourselfThat covers what to say about yourself — which strengths and framing. This covers the grammatical form that description takes on the page. Same content, and this is the wrapper it goes in.
Action verbsWhich verb to pick ("spearheaded" vs. "helped"). Implied first person is why the verb comes first at all — dropping the pronoun is what puts the action in the lead position.

The three voices, side by side

Take one accomplishment and write it three ways. Only the third belongs on a resume.

VoiceHow the bullet reads
Explicit first person (too wordy)"I managed a team of six and I cut delivery time by 30%."
Third person (sounds like a biography)"John managed a team of six and reduced delivery time by 30%."
Implied first person (correct)"Managed a team of six, cutting delivery time 30%."

The third version keeps your voice — it's still you doing the managing — but the subject is understood, so the line opens on the action and the number. That's the entire trick: same perspective as "I", just with the "I" deleted.

The two traps

There's a ditch on each side of implied first person. Most resumes fall into one of them.

The "I" trap

  • "I was responsible for the monthly close"
  • "In my role, I managed vendor contracts"
  • "I have strong communication skills"
  • Every bullet opening with an identical "I"

The third-person trap

  • "Sarah is a dedicated professional who..."
  • "He led the migration to the cloud"
  • "Her responsibilities included reporting"
  • Writing about yourself like a press release

Why the "I" trap hurts: it's not just convention. Padding each line with "I" pushes your verbs to the right, makes the first word of twenty bullets identical and information-free, and slows the six-second skim a recruiter does down the left edge of the page. It also eats space you can't spare on a one-page resume.

Why the third-person trap hurts: writing "John managed…" reads as though someone else wrote the document about you, which is both odd and untrue — everyone knows you wrote your own resume. It wastes space repeating your name or "he/she", and it puts a strange distance between you and your own wins. The one exception people cite — an executive bio or a speaker profile — is a different document, not a resume.

How to apply it, section by section

Experience bullets → implied first person, verb-first

Delete the subject and open with the action verb: not "I built the dashboard", just "Built the dashboard used by 40 sales reps". This is where the rule does the most work — see how to write resume bullet points.

Summary / profile → still no "I"

The summary is the one place the "I" temptation is strongest, because it reads like prose. Resist it: "Product manager with 8 years in B2B SaaS; drives roadmap from research to launch" — not "I am a product manager…". Present tense, no pronoun. See resume summary examples.

Skills, education, everything else → fragments, no pronoun

These are lists, so the pronoun question barely arises — just keep them as clean fragments. The only place a stray "I" tends to sneak back in is a wordy objective line; keep that pronoun-free too, or drop the objective entirely.

Where "I" is actually correct

The pronoun-free rule is specific to the resume. The moment you move to a document meant to sound like you talking, the rule flips and explicit first person becomes right — dropping the pronoun there would read as clipped and cold.

DocumentVoice to use
The cover letterExplicit first person. "I'm drawn to this role because…" It's a letter — full sentences, "I" throughout. See how to start a cover letter.
LinkedIn "About" sectionExplicit first person. It's a narrative summary, not a resume — "I help B2B teams…" reads warm and human. (Your LinkedIn headline, like a resume, stays pronoun-free.)
Personal bio / portfolio "About"First person for a personal site; third person only for a formal speaker or company bio, which is a different genre.
Networking notes & letters of interestExplicit first person. Anything written as a message from you uses "I".

So the clean split: resume and headline → implied first person (no pronoun); anything that reads as prose in your voice → explicit "I". Third person is never the right choice for describing yourself in a job search.

Quick reference

  • Resume voice = implied first person → drop "I", "me", "my", "we"; lead with the verb. "Managed a team," not "I managed."
  • Never third person → "John managed…" reads like a biography and wastes space; nobody writes their resume about themselves.
  • Why no "I" → it's redundant on every line, slows the scan, and eats space you need for content.
  • Person ≠ tense → set the pronoun (drop it) and the tense (past for old roles, present for current) separately on each bullet.
  • The rule flips → cover letters, LinkedIn "About", and bios use explicit "I"; the resume and headline don't.

Frequently asked questions

Should you use "I" on a resume?

No — a resume is written in implied first person, which means first person with the pronoun dropped. You write "Led a team of five to cut costs 20%," not "I led a team of five." It's still your voice, but the "I" is understood, not typed. Two reasons: convention (recruiters expect the pronoun-free, verb-first style and a stray "I" reads as amateur), and economy (every bullet would start with the same "I", wasting the most valuable space on the line and breaking the fast top-left scan a recruiter uses). Drop "I", "me", "my", and "we" throughout the resume body and start each bullet with the verb instead.

Is a resume written in first person or third person?

Neither, taken literally — a resume is written in implied first person, a voice unique to resumes and LinkedIn headlines. Explicit first person ("I managed a team") is too wordy and repetitive for a resume. Third person ("John managed a team" or "He managed a team") is a real and common mistake: it reads as though a biographer or a recruiter wrote the document about you, it wastes space repeating your name or "he/she", and it creates an odd distance from your own accomplishments. The correct voice sits between them: keep the first-person perspective but delete the subject, so "I managed a team" and "John managed a team" both become "Managed a team."

When is it correct to use first person with "I" in a job search?

The rule flips in the documents that are meant to sound like you speaking directly. A cover letter is written in full first person — "I'm drawn to this role because..." — because it's a letter, and dropping the pronoun there would read as clipped and strange. Your LinkedIn "About" section is also first person with "I" (it's a narrative summary, not a resume). A personal bio, a portfolio "About" page, a networking message, and a personal statement all use explicit first person too. The dividing line is simple: the resume itself and its bullet-style summary use implied first person (no pronoun); anything that reads as prose written in your own voice uses explicit first person with "I". Never write about yourself in the third person in any of these.

Why can't a resume use full sentences with "I"?

Because a resume isn't prose — it's a scannable list of evidence, and the pronoun-dropped style is what makes it scannable. When every bullet begins with a strong verb ("Led", "Built", "Cut", "Launched"), a recruiter's eye catches the action instantly down the left edge of the page. Put "I" in front of each one and the verbs move right, the first word of every line becomes an identical, information-free "I", and the six-second skim gets slower. The implied-first-person style also saves real space: on a one-page resume, cutting a repeated "I" from twenty bullets recovers room for content that actually helps. It's the same reason resumes drop articles and use fragments — density and speed of reading beat grammatical completeness here.

How is grammatical person different from verb tense on a resume?

They're two separate choices you make on every bullet, and it's easy to conflate them. Person is about the pronoun: implied first person means you drop "I". Tense is about time: past tense for jobs you've left ("Managed", "Built"), present tense for your current role ("Manage", "Build") and for a present-state summary. You apply both at once — a past-role bullet is implied first person AND past tense ("Led a team of six"), while a current-role bullet is implied first person AND present tense ("Lead a team of six"). Getting the person right (no "I") and the tense consistent within each role are independent habits; a bullet can be correct on one and wrong on the other.
