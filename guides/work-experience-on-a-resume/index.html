How to list work experience on a resume

Your work experience section is the part a recruiter actually reads to decide. Everything else supports it. Here's the exact format for each job entry, the order to list them, and — the part most people get wrong — how to turn a list of duties into proof that you're worth hiring.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Most resumes fail here in the same way: they describe what the job was instead of what the person did with it. "Responsible for managing the team" tells a recruiter nothing — every person who held that title was responsible for it. This guide fixes that. First the mechanical format so every entry is clean and ATS-safe, then the writing part that separates a resume that gets read from one that gets skimmed and closed.

The format of a single entry

Every job entry has the same four required parts. Get these consistent across all your roles and the section reads as one clean document.

  • Job title — your real title, or a clearer equivalent if yours was internal jargon (keep it honest).
  • Company name — and location, if you want it.
  • Dates — month and year, start to end (or "Present"). Be consistent: don't mix "2023" with "Jan 2023" across entries.
  • 3–6 bullet points — what you achieved, most impressive first.
Standard entry Customer Success Manager · Northwind Software — Austin, TX
Mar 2023 – Present
  • Grew net revenue retention from 92% to 108% across a 60-account book in 12 months.
  • Cut average onboarding time from 6 weeks to 3 by rebuilding the kickoff playbook.
  • Flagged and saved 8 at-risk accounts (~$240K ARR) through a new quarterly review.

The order: reverse-chronological

List your most recent job first, then work backward. This is what recruiters expect and what applicant tracking systems parse most reliably — it's the backbone of the standard chronological resume. Two rules keep it tight:

Weight detail toward the top

Your current and last role get the most bullet points. A job from eight years ago might get one line or none — see how far back a resume should go.

Where the whole section sits

For experienced professionals, experience goes right below the summary — it's your strongest card. For students and career-starters, education or a skills block may lead instead. See writing a resume with no experience.

Duties → results: the part that matters

A duty describes the job. A result describes you. Recruiters are hiring the second thing. The reliable formula for every bullet: action verb + what you did + the outcome (with a number when you can).

Rewrite in one move
  • ✗ Responsible for managing social media accounts.
  • ✓ Grew Instagram following 3,200 → 14,000 in a year, lifting referral traffic 28%.

Two levers do most of the work:

  • Start with a strong verb. "Led," "built," "cut," "launched," "negotiated" — not "responsible for" or "duties included." A full list is in resume action verbs.
  • Add a number. Even when you think you don't have one, you do: volume handled, time saved, money touched, people trained, percentage improved. How to quantify achievements shows how to find numbers you actually have.

For the mechanics of tight, scannable bullets — length, tense, and structure — see how to write resume bullet points.

Tricky situations

Short stints

A job you held for a few months isn't automatically a red flag, but you can soften it: use year-only dates ("2024" rather than "Jan–Apr 2024"), and keep it only if it's relevant or needed to avoid an unexplained gap. Contract and seasonal roles can be labeled as such — "(Contract)" after the title tells the real story.

Employment gaps

Don't paper over a gap with stretched dates — it surfaces in a background check and costs you the offer. A short, honestly-presented gap is far safer, and there are clean framings (year-only dates, a brief "Career Break" entry) covered in how to explain employment gaps.

Same company, multiple roles

List the company once, then stack the titles beneath it with their own date ranges. It shows promotion and continuity in one glance, and keeps the section from looking like job-hopping.

Little or no formal experience

Internships, freelance work, volunteering, and significant projects all count as experience — label the section "Experience" rather than "Work Experience" and they belong. The no-experience guide walks through building a full section from these.

Formatting details that trip people up

  • Keep it single-column, real text — no tables, columns, or icons an ATS will scramble. See the ATS checklist.
  • Match the job posting's language. If they say "stakeholder management," and you did it, use their words — that's how resume keywords get matched. Then tailor the section to the specific job.
  • Stay consistent in tense (past jobs in past tense, current job in present), date style, and punctuation across every entry.
  • Cut the filler. "Responsible for," "duties included," "various tasks" — delete them and start with the verb.

Frequently asked questions

What should each work experience entry include?

Four things, always: your job title, the company name, the dates (month and year), and three to six bullet points describing what you achieved. Location is optional. The bullets are where the entry earns its space — show results, not just duties.

In what order should I list my jobs?

Reverse-chronological — most recent first, working backward. It's what recruiters and ATS expect. Give recent, relevant roles the most detail and trim jobs older than about 10–15 years.

How do I describe work experience if the duties were routine?

Turn duties into outcomes. Instead of "responsible for answering customer emails," write "resolved 40+ customer emails a day, keeping response time under 2 hours." Start with an action verb and add a number wherever you honestly can.

Should I include short jobs or gaps?

Include short jobs if they're relevant or fill a timeline gap; use year-only dates to de-emphasize a brief stint. Never hide a gap with faked dates — it surfaces in a background check. An honest gap is far safer.
