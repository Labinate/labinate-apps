Should you put pronouns on a resume?

You've seen she/her, he/him, and they/them in email signatures and LinkedIn profiles, and now you're wondering whether they belong on your resume — and if so, where. The short answer: it's an optional line in your contact header, not a rule. Include it by choice when being addressed correctly matters to you; leave it off just as freely — both read as a complete, professional resume.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Unlike most resume questions, this one isn't really about how to format something — it's a disclosure decision. Gender pronouns are a small piece of personal identity, and putting them on a resume simply makes that identity visible from the first contact. So the only real question is whether to include them at all, and there's no single right answer: it depends on what matters to you, who you're applying to, and whether you're out in a professional context. What we can settle cleanly is the mechanics — where they go, how to keep them ATS-safe, and how to weigh the trade-off — so that whichever way you decide, the resume looks intentional rather than uncertain.

The core idea: Pronouns on a resume are an optional identity line in your contact header — a single, factual detail you add by choice, never an obligation. Including them signals you'd like to be addressed correctly (and, for many people, that you value an inclusive workplace); omitting them costs nothing and is a perfectly normal, sometimes protective, choice. Decide once, keep it small, and move on.

Where they go — one line in the header

If you do include pronouns, they belong in the contact header alongside your name and details, formatted as a plain, understated line. There are two clean placements:

Inline, right after your name

Jordan Lee (they/them)
jordan.lee@email.com · (555) 019-2245 · Austin, TX

Compact and unobtrusive — the pronouns read as a natural part of the name line.

On its own labeled line

Priya Nair
Pronouns: she/her · priya.nair@email.com · Seattle, WA

Slightly more explicit, useful if you want the label to be unmistakable.

Either works. Keep the pronouns the same size and weight as your email and phone number — they're a contact detail, not a headline. Don't build a separate "Pronouns" section, don't set them in a colored badge or icon, and don't inflate them. One quiet line does the entire job.

It's a choice — here's how to weigh it

Because there's no universal rule, the useful thing is a clear-eyed read of both sides. Neither column is the "right" answer; they're the considerations that should drive your call.

Reasons to include

  • You want to be addressed correctly from the very first email or call.
  • Your pronouns may not be obvious from your name, and you'd rather set them than be guessed.
  • You want to signal that you value an inclusive workplace — and filter toward employers who respond well.
  • You already list them on LinkedIn and in your email signature, and want the resume to match.
  • You're applying to a role, team, or organization where inclusion is clearly part of the culture.

Reasons to leave them off

  • You're not out in a professional context and would rather not disclose on paper.
  • You're targeting an employer or region where you judge it could invite bias.
  • You prefer to keep the resume neutral and raise it once you've met people.
  • You simply don't feel the need — omitting is not "hiding," it's your prerogative.
  • Space is tight and you'd rather use the header line for something else.

A resume with no pronouns is not incomplete, and a resume with them is not unprofessional. The bias risk is real enough that leaving them off is a legitimate, self-protective choice — and the inclusion signal is real enough that many people add them deliberately. Decide based on your situation, not on a fear of getting it "wrong."

Keep it plain text so the ATS can read it

The one genuine mechanical rule: plain text. An applicant tracking system reads the words in your header the same way it reads your email address, so Jordan Lee (they/them) parses without trouble. The only ATS trap is a formatting one that has nothing to do with pronouns: if your header is a designed graphic, logo, or image, the parser can't read any of it — your name and contact details included. So keep the whole header as selectable text, don't bury pronouns inside an icon or image, and skip the colored "badge" treatment. Plain, small, and factual is both the safest and the most confident look.

A few common situations

You're nonbinary or trans and want to be addressed correctly

This is the strongest case for including them. Adding they/them (or your pronouns) in the header means the very first email won't misgender you, and it lets you sense early how an employer responds. Many people find that useful as a filter — the workplaces that read it warmly are the ones worth pursuing. If disclosure feels risky for a particular role, though, you're equally entitled to leave it off and raise it later; the choice is always yours.

You're cisgender and want to signal an inclusive stance

Listing she/her or he/him even when your pronouns are "obvious" is a small, recognized way to normalize the practice and signal that you're comfortable in inclusive environments. It's optional and low-stakes — include it if it reflects you, skip it if it feels unnecessary. Either reads fine.

You're worried about bias

That worry is valid, and it's exactly why omitting pronouns is a legitimate choice rather than a failure of nerve. Making your gender identity visible before anyone meets you can invite bias in a workplace or region that isn't genuinely inclusive. You get to decide when and to whom you disclose — keeping the resume neutral and having the conversation in person is a completely reasonable strategy.

You list them everywhere else

If your LinkedIn and email signature already show your pronouns, adding them to the resume keeps your presentation consistent — a recruiter cross-referencing your profile sees the same thing. Consistency is a mild plus, not a requirement; if you'd rather keep the resume itself minimal, the mismatch is harmless.

What this is not the same as

"Pronouns" and "resume" collide with a few unrelated questions. Keeping them separate stops you from over-thinking a small header line.

Not the same as…How the pronouns line differs
Required contact fieldsYour name, email, phone, and location are the expected header fields every resume needs. A pronoun line is an optional addition to that same header — you're deciding whether to add one more small detail, not filling a required slot.
A resume photoA photo is the other classic optional identity element, with a similar include-or-omit decision that swings on geography and bias risk. The difference: in most Western markets a photo is advised against, while a pronoun line is neutral-to-positive. Same decision family, different default.
Grammatical pronouns (I, me, my)A totally different meaning of "pronoun." The style rule about dropping first-person I and me from your bullet points is about writing voice, not identity. Your first-person-vs-third-person choice has nothing to do with whether you list she/her in the header.
How you describe yourselfThat's about tone, framing, and the words you choose in a summary. The pronoun line is a single factual identity detail, not a description — it makes no claim about your skills or fit and needs no justification.
The bottom line — it's your call, and both answers are fine. If you include pronouns, put them in the contact header as one small plain-text line, keep them the same weight as your email, and let them sit quietly. If you leave them off, your resume is no less complete or professional. Don't turn a personal, optional detail into a source of stress — decide once based on what matters to you and the context you're applying into, then spend your energy on the parts of the resume that argue for the job.

Quick reference

  • Optional, not required → including or omitting pronouns both read as a normal, professional resume.
  • Header only, one small line → Jordan Lee (they/them) or a Pronouns: she/her line, same size as your contact details.
  • Plain text, never a graphic or badge → so the ATS reads it and it doesn't draw disproportionate attention.
  • Weigh the trade-off honestly → include to be addressed correctly and signal values; omit if you'd rather not disclose or judge bias a real risk — omitting isn't hiding.
  • Match your other profiles if you like → consistency with LinkedIn and your email signature is a mild plus, not an obligation.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put your pronouns on a resume?

It's optional, and both choices are normal. Adding your pronouns — she/her, he/him, they/them — is a small, personal signal: it tells anyone reading your application how to address you from the first contact, and for many candidates it also signals that they value an inclusive workplace. It's never required, and leaving them off costs you nothing. Include them when being addressed correctly matters to you or when you want to signal your values; consider leaving them off if you're not out at work, if you'd rather not disclose, or if you're targeting an employer or region where you judge it could invite bias. There's no universally "correct" answer — it's your call, and a resume reads as complete either way.

Where do you put pronouns on a resume?

In the contact header, as one short plain-text line — either in parentheses right after your name, "Jordan Lee (they/them)", or on its own labeled line beneath it, "Pronouns: she/her". Keep it small and factual, the same size and weight as your email and phone number; it's a contact detail, not a headline. Don't give it its own section, don't turn it into a colored badge or icon, and don't bury it inside a graphic or image-based header where an applicant tracking system can't read it. One quiet line in the header does the whole job.

Do pronouns on a resume cause bias?

They can, and that's an honest part of the decision. Disclosing pronouns — especially they/them, or a set that doesn't match assumptions about your name — makes your gender identity visible before anyone meets you, and in a workplace or region that isn't genuinely inclusive that visibility can trigger conscious or unconscious bias. That risk is exactly why omitting them is a legitimate, self-protective choice, not "hiding" — you get to decide when and to whom you disclose. Many people include pronouns precisely to filter toward employers who respond well and away from those who don't; others prefer to keep the resume neutral and raise it later. Both are valid reads of your own situation.

Are pronouns in a resume header ATS-safe?

Yes, as long as they're plain text. An applicant tracking system parses the words in your header the same way it parses your email address, so "Jordan Lee (they/them)" or a "Pronouns: she/her" line reads cleanly and won't break anything. The only ATS trap is a formatting one, not a pronoun one: if your entire header is a graphic, logo, or image, the parser can't read any of it — name, contact details, or pronouns. Keep the whole header as selectable text and you're fine.

Is putting pronouns on a resume unprofessional?

No. Pronouns in an email signature, a LinkedIn profile, and a resume header have become an ordinary, widely recognized convention, and a recruiter reading "she/her" next to your name simply registers it and moves on — the same way they register your city or your phone number. It isn't a political statement or a distraction unless it's formatted to draw disproportionate attention, like a large colored badge. Kept as one small factual line, it reads as normal and considerate. If you'd still rather not include it, that's equally fine — the choice, not the label, is what matters.
