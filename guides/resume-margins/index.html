Resume margins: the right size

Margins are the frame around everything on your resume, and they do more work than they get credit for. Set right, they make the page look composed and give a recruiter room to breathe while scanning. Set wrong — too thin, uneven, or wall-to-wall with text — they make even strong content feel cramped and amateur. The good news: there's a simple standard, a safe minimum, and one rule that solves most one-page problems. Here's exactly what to set.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

The one-line version: use 1 inch margins on all four sides, and if you need more room, shrink evenly down to 0.5 inch — never below. Margins are the first thing to adjust when a resume is spilling onto a second page, because you gain space without shrinking your font or deleting a single accomplishment.

The standard: one inch

One inch (2.54 cm) on the top, bottom, left, and right is the default in both Word and Google Docs, and it's the safest choice for a reason: it produces a clean, well-proportioned page that reads as professional at a glance. If you do nothing else, leaving the default one-inch margins in place will never count against you.

You have room to move, though. The workable range is:

Margin sizeWhen to use it
1 inchStandard. The default; safest, most spacious, always acceptable.
0.75 inchComfortable middle. Buys noticeable space, still looks generous. A great first step for one-page fit.
0.5 inchAcceptable floor. Use when you genuinely need the room; the page starts to feel full.
Below 0.5 inchAvoid. Reads as crammed, and printers may clip text near the paper edge.

Keep all four sides balanced

Whatever size you pick, apply it evenly. Uneven margins — tight on the left, loose on the right, or a big gap up top with text crowding the bottom — are one of those subtle flaws a reader feels without being able to name. The page just looks off-balance.

The common mistake

Nudging text or indents by hand to make something fit, which quietly leaves one side wider than the others.

→ Set all four margins in the page-setup dialog instead of dragging text. Equal on all sides is the goal; a slightly larger top and bottom than the sides is also fine.

Margins are your first move for one page

If your resume is running a few lines onto a second page, resist the urge to drop the font to 9pt or hack out a good bullet. Adjust in this order:

  1. Shrink the margins first — 1 inch → 0.75 inch → 0.5 inch. This is free space that costs you nothing in readability.
  2. Then tighten line spacing and the gaps between sections slightly.
  3. Then trim content — cut the weakest bullet, merge two lines, remove filler. See how to write tighter bullet points.
  4. Font size last. Only after the above, nudge the body from 11pt toward 10.5 or 10pt — never below 10. Details in resume font and size.

Most one-page problems are solved by step 1 alone. For how much your resume should hold in the first place, see how long a resume should be.

Whitespace, readability, and ATS

It's tempting to think a fuller page shows more value. It usually shows less, because a wall of edge-to-edge text is harder to scan — and a recruiter gives your resume seconds, not minutes. The white space that margins and section gaps create is what lets someone find your name, your last title, and your top result quickly.

On the machine side, margins don't break applicant tracking systems — an ATS reads the text, not the whitespace, so a 0.5-inch or 1-inch margin parses identically. The reason to keep sane margins is the human who reads after the software, not the software itself. Trim your content before you trim whitespace to zero.

Quick reference

  • Standard → 1 inch (2.54 cm) all four sides.
  • Range → 0.5 to 1 inch; 0.75 inch is a comfortable middle.
  • Floor → never below 0.5 inch (print clipping + crammed look).
  • Balance → keep all four sides equal.
  • One-page fix → shrink margins before line spacing, content, or font.
  • ATS → margins don't affect parsing; they affect the human reader.

Frequently asked questions

What size should resume margins be?

One inch (2.54 cm) on all four sides is the standard and the safest choice — it matches the default in Word and Google Docs and gives the page a clean, professional frame. You can go narrower to fit more content, but stay at or above 0.5 inch (1.27 cm); anything tighter makes the page feel crammed and risks text being clipped when it prints. So the working range is 0.5 to 1 inch on every side, with 0.75 inch being a common, comfortable middle ground.

Can resume margins be smaller than one inch?

Yes. Shrinking margins is the smartest first move when you're trying to fit everything on one page, because it buys space without touching your font size or cutting content. Drop from 1 inch to 0.75 inch, and to 0.5 inch if you still need room. Just don't go below 0.5 inch: printers can't always render text right up to the paper edge, so ultra-thin margins can get clipped, and a wall-to-wall page reads as desperate rather than efficient.

Do resume margins need to be the same on all sides?

Yes — keep the top, bottom, left, and right margins equal (or the top and bottom can be slightly larger than the sides, which is also fine). Uneven margins are one of the most common subtle formatting flaws: a page that's tight on the left but loose on the right looks unbalanced even to a reader who can't say why. If you use a template or builder, this is handled for you; if you're formatting by hand, set all four in the page-setup dialog rather than nudging text around.

Do margins affect ATS or resume readability?

Margins don't break applicant tracking systems directly — ATS reads the text, not the whitespace. But margins shape readability, and readability is what wins with the human who reads after the ATS. Adequate margins plus white space between sections let a recruiter scan in the six seconds they actually spend. Cramming the page to zero margins to squeeze in more usually backfires: it's harder to read, so less of it gets read. Trim content before you trim whitespace to nothing.
