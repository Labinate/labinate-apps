How to describe yourself on a resume

"Hardworking, passionate team player" tells a recruiter nothing — everyone writes it, and none of it can be checked. Describing yourself well isn't about finding better adjectives; it's about swapping adjectives for evidence. Here are the words that work, the clichés to cut, and weak-to-strong rewrites you can copy.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Every resume is a self-description, but the worst way to describe yourself is to describe yourself directly. "Excellent communicator" is a claim a recruiter has read a thousand times and believed zero times. The fix is a single principle that runs through this whole guide: show the trait through what you did, and let the reader name it. Below, the words worth using, the ones to delete, the voice to write in, and side-by-side rewrites.

The one rule: show, don't tell

An adjective is a claim; an accomplishment is proof. "Detail-oriented" asks the reader to trust you. "Reconciled a 12,000-line ledger with zero discrepancies" makes them think it. Whenever you're tempted to describe a trait, describe the moment that trait produced instead.

Tell → Show
  • "Strong leader" → Led a team of six through a platform migration, on time and under budget
  • "Great communicator" → Presented quarterly results to a 40-person leadership team
  • "Results-driven" → Cut new-hire onboarding time 30% by rebuilding the training flow

The right column never uses the trait word — and lands it anyway. That's the whole technique. When you do this across the resume, your professional summary can stay short, because the bullet points below are already doing the describing. Words that work vs. words that waste space

Not all self-describing words are equal. The good ones are either verifiable traits (backed by a fact somewhere on the page) or concrete qualifications (a certification, language, or tool). The wasteful ones are subjective and unprovable — the exact words every other applicant also used.

✓ Use — provable

  • Led · Built · Launched
  • Reduced · Grew · Automated
  • Bilingual · Certified · Licensed
  • Analytical · Methodical (if shown)
  • Cross-functional · Client-facing

✗ Cut — empty

  • Hardworking · Motivated
  • Passionate · Dynamic
  • Team player · People person
  • Detail-oriented (unless shown)
  • Go-getter · Self-starter · Guru

A word on the right isn't banned — it's just useless on its own. "Detail-oriented" is fine the moment a nearby line proves it. Standing alone in a summary, it's filler. Write in the "first person implied" voice

Resumes have their own grammar. You describe yourself without ever saying "I", "my", "he", or "she" — you drop the subject and start with the verb. This "first person implied" style is the professional standard: tighter, faster to read, and focused on the action rather than on you as a subject.

Voice
  • "I managed a team of six and I improved retention."
  • "Maria is a dedicated manager who oversees six people."
  • "Managed a team of six; improved retention 18% in one year."

The third line reads as confident and professional precisely because it removes the grammatical "you" and puts the accomplishment first. Keep every line in that voice.

The professional summary: describe yourself in 2–3 lines

Your headline self-description lives in the professional summary at the top. It answers "who are you, for this role?" in a few lines — a title, your strongest proof point, and what you're aiming at. It is not a place for a personality inventory.

Weak summary — all adjectives Hardworking and passionate professional with excellent communication skills and a strong work ethic, seeking a challenging role to grow and add value.
Strong summary — evidence Operations analyst with 5 years in SaaS. Automated reporting that saved 15 hours a week and cut close time from 8 days to 3. Looking to bring the same rigor to a revenue-ops team.

The strong version never says "hardworking" — but you'd bet the writer is. Match it to the posting: the traits you lead with should be the ones the job actually asks for, which is the heart of tailoring your resume to the job description.

Match the description to the role

"How would you describe yourself" has a different right answer for every job. The same person is "meticulous and audit-ready" on an accounting resume and "fast-moving and scrappy" on a startup one — both true, each chosen for its reader. Read the posting, note the traits it names, and describe yourself in those terms wherever your record honestly supports it.

Pull your describing words from the job post

If the listing says "cross-functional collaboration" and "ownership", those are the traits to demonstrate — through a bullet where you actually did them, not by pasting the phrase into a skills list.

Common mistakes

  • Stacking adjectives with no proof. "Dynamic, driven, dedicated" is three claims and zero facts. Replace with one accomplishment.
  • Describing yourself in the third person. "John is a reliable worker" reads as a reference letter, not a resume. Drop the name and the pronoun.
  • Using the exact same self-description for every job. Generic reads as effort you didn't make. Re-aim the traits per posting.
  • Confusing tone with content. Fancier words don't help — "spearheaded a paradigm shift" is worse than "led the redesign". See common resume mistakes.
  • Describing personality where you should describe results. Save personality for the interview; the resume's job is evidence. Quantify it — how to quantify achievements.
The whole thing in one breath. Don't describe yourself — describe what you did, and let the reader draw the conclusion. Trade unprovable adjectives ("hardworking", "passionate") for verifiable ones ("led", "reduced", "certified"), write in the pronoun-free implied first person, keep your summary to two or three evidence-backed lines, and re-aim the traits at each specific job.

Frequently asked questions

What words should I use to describe myself on a resume?

Concrete, role-specific words a fact can back up — "led", "built", "reduced", "analytical", "bilingual", "certified" — not vague self-praise like "hardworking", "passionate", or "team player". If you can't point to a line that proves the word, it's decoration. Describe what you did, and let the reader conclude what you're like.

How do I describe myself professionally without sounding arrogant or generic?

Show instead of tell. Instead of "excellent communicator", write "presented quarterly results to a 40-person leadership team". Instead of "results-driven", write "cut onboarding time 30%". Specifics read as confident and true; adjectives read as unproven claims. Numbers, named tools, and real outcomes carry the description for you.

Should I describe myself in the first or third person?

Neither literally — use the implied first person with the pronouns removed. Drop "I" and "my", and never use "he" or "she". Instead of "I managed a team of six", write "Managed a team of six". This is the resume standard: concise, professional, and focused on the action.

Where on the resume do I describe myself?

Your main self-description is the professional summary at the top — two or three lines framing who you are for this role. But the strongest description is spread across the whole resume: every work-experience bullet shows a trait through an accomplishment. Lead with a tight summary, then let the evidence prove each claim.
