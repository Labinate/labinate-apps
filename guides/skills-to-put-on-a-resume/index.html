Skills to put on a resume

The skills section is the easiest part of a resume to pad and the easiest for a recruiter to see through. Done right, it's a fast, credible match to the job. Here's what to list, what to cut, and where it belongs.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A skills section works when a recruiter can glance at it and think "yes, this person fits." It fails when it's a wall of generic words — hardworking, team player, Microsoft Word — that every applicant lists and none of them prove. The fix isn't more skills. It's the right skills: specific, relevant to this job, and backed up by the rest of your resume.

Hard skills vs soft skills

The single most useful distinction. Hard skills are specific and testable — a tool, a language, a certification, a method. Soft skills are how you work — communication, leadership, adaptability. They belong in different places.

Hard skills → the skills section

SQL, Python, Adobe Illustrator, financial modelling, Spanish (fluent), Google Analytics, CPR certification, welding, bookkeeping. Concrete things a manager could verify.

Soft skills → your experience bullets

Don't list "leadership" — show it. "Led a team of 6 through a system migration with zero downtime" proves leadership, communication, and reliability in one line, and it's believable because it's specific.

Rule of thumb: if you can't picture a test for it, it's a soft skill and it belongs in a bullet, not the list.

How to choose the right skills (read the posting)

Stop guessing at "good skills for my field" and read the job description instead. It's a checklist the employer wrote for you. Pull out the specific tools, methods, and qualifications they name, then list the ones you honestly have — using their wording. If they say "CRM (Salesforce)," write Salesforce, not "customer database software." This is also how you get past the resume filter; our resume keywords guide covers finding and placing those terms without stuffing.

Skills: hardworking, motivated, good communicator, Microsoft Office
Skills: Salesforce · HubSpot · SQL · Google Analytics · A/B testing · Spanish (fluent)

The second version is shorter, and it tells a hiring manager far more.

What to cut

  • Skills that are assumed. "Email," "internet," "Microsoft Word" for an office role. Listing the baseline reads as if you're padding.
  • Skill ratings and progress bars. "Python ★★★☆☆" means nothing consistent, and the graphics break in applicant tracking systems. Use plain text.
  • Anything you can't defend. If "advanced Excel" would collapse under one interview question, downgrade it or drop it.
  • Bare soft skills. Move them into bullets where they're backed by an example.

Where the skills section goes

Placement depends on how much your skills carry the pitch:

Experienced candidate → below experience

Your track record is the stronger sell. Lead with work history; let skills be a quick confirming scan underneath.

Early-career, career-changer, or technical role → near the top

When the skills are the match — a first job, a career change, or a role defined by its toolset — put them high so the fit is visible in the first few seconds.

The 10-minute skills pass. Open the job posting and your resume side by side. Highlight every concrete tool, method, and qualification in the posting. List the ones you truly have, in their words, 8–12 items max. Delete every rating bar and every skill that's simply assumed. Then check: is each hard skill echoed somewhere in your experience? If not, work it into a bullet so the claim has proof.

Frequently asked questions

How many skills should I put on a resume?

Around 8 to 12 is plenty. The section should be a scannable, credible list — not everything you've ever touched. Prioritise the skills the posting names that you can back up elsewhere on the page, and cut anything generic enough to be assumed.

What is the difference between hard skills and soft skills?

Hard skills are specific and testable — a tool, a language, a certification, a method. Soft skills are how you work with people and problems. Hard skills belong in the skills list; soft skills are more convincing shown through an accomplishment than claimed as a word.

Should I list soft skills on my resume?

A couple, sparingly, and only the ones the role clearly depends on. "Communication" as a bare word proves nothing. It lands far harder as evidence — "presented quarterly results to a 40-person team." Let your experience bullets carry the soft skills.

Where should the skills section go?

For experienced candidates, below work experience — your track record leads. If you're early-career, changing fields, or the role is defined by its toolset, move it up near the top so the match is visible immediately.
