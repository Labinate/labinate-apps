Cover letter examples: 8 that worked, plus a fill-in template

A good cover letter isn't a second resume — it's a short, specific reason you're worth an interview. Here are eight copy-ready examples for common situations, plus a fill-in template you can adapt in five minutes.

Updated July 2026 · ~8 min read · by Labinate

A cover letter is the three or four short paragraphs that sit alongside your resume and answer one question a hiring manager is quietly asking: why you, for this job, at this company? The resume proves you can do the work; the letter shows you understand the role and actually want it.

Below are eight examples for the situations people search for most — career change, no experience, a referral, a cold application, and standard mid-career roles — followed by a reusable template. Adapt the wording to sound like you; don't paste any of it verbatim.

The structure every good letter follows

Almost every strong cover letter is four moves:

  1. The hook — one sentence that names the role and gives a specific reason you're writing (a result, a shared value, a genuine draw to the company). No "I am writing to apply for…".
  2. The proof — one or two achievements from your resume, told as short stories with a number, chosen because they match what this job needs.
  3. The fit — a line or two showing you understand the company or team and where you'd add value next.
  4. The close — a confident, low-pressure ask for a conversation.

Keep the whole thing to 200–350 words. If it fills a page, cut it.

Examples by situation

Standard mid-career application

Dear Hiring Manager,

When your posting mentioned rebuilding the onboarding flow, I recognised the exact problem I spent last year solving: at Northwind I redesigned first-run onboarding and lifted 30-day activation from 41% to 58%. I'd like to bring that same instinct for reducing friction to your product team.

Over six years in product design I've shipped work across web and mobile, most recently owning the design system that cut our build time by a third. What draws me to your team is the bet you're making on a genuinely simple SMB tool in a category that has forgotten what simple means — that's the work I want to be doing.

I'd welcome the chance to walk you through the onboarding rebuild and hear where you're taking the product next. Thank you for your time.

Career change

Dear Ms. Alvarez,

I'm a high-school teacher moving into instructional design, and the through-line is stronger than it looks: for eight years my job has been taking complex material and making it land for people who don't yet care about it. Last year I rebuilt our department's curriculum around measurable outcomes and raised pass rates 22%.

That's the same work your posting describes — turning subject-matter chaos into clear, self-serve learning. I've spent the last six months formalising it, completing a UX-writing course and rebuilding two training modules in Articulate on my own time.

I know I'm coming from outside the field, and I'd value the chance to show you why that's an advantage, not a gap. Thank you for considering me.

No experience / recent graduate

Dear Hiring Manager,

I'm applying for your junior data analyst role, and while I'm early in my career, I'm not starting from zero. For my capstone I built a dashboard that flagged a 15% seasonal revenue dip a local retailer had never quantified; they now use it to plan inventory. It taught me that the value of analysis is the decision it changes, not the chart.

I'm fluent in SQL and Python, comfortable with messy real-world data, and I read your engineering blog closely — your post on treating data quality as a product resonated with how I already work.

I'd love the chance to bring that curiosity to your team. Thank you for your consideration.

Referral (someone recommended you)

Dear Mr. Okafor,

Priya Nair suggested I reach out about your open backend role — we worked together at Loomis, where I owned the payments service that handled our 4× growth without an incident. She thought the reliability work you're scaling now would be a strong match, and from the posting I think she's right.

In five years I've specialised in the unglamorous parts of backend engineering: idempotent APIs, observability, and the boring rigor that keeps money moving. I'd bring the same to your platform team.

Happy to talk whenever suits. Thank you.

Cold application (no posting)

Dear Ms. Chen,

I know you may not have an open marketing role, but I've admired what your team has built and wanted to introduce myself in case that changes. At Beacon I grew organic traffic 3× in eighteen months and built an email program that now drives 30% of monthly revenue — the kind of compounding, low-spend growth I think fits an early-stage company like yours.

If there's ever a conversation worth having about lifecycle or content, I'd be glad to be part of it. Either way, thank you for the work you put out; it's a genuinely good read.

Short version (when the form gives you one small box)

Hi — I'm applying for the customer-success role. In my current job I cut churn 18% by rebuilding the first-90-days playbook and personally onboarding the top 20 accounts. I'm drawn to your team because you treat success as a product function, not a fire brigade. I'd love a short call to talk about how I'd approach your book of business. Thank you!

Returning to work after a gap

Dear Hiring Manager,

After two years as a full-time caregiver I'm returning to project management, and I'm returning sharp — I kept current with a PMP renewal and a fractional consulting engagement that shipped a warehouse migration on time. Before the break I ran cross-functional programs of up to 40 people at Vero.

I'm not easing back in; I'm looking for exactly the kind of complex, multi-team delivery your posting describes. I'd welcome the chance to talk it through. Thank you.

Internal transfer / promotion

Hi Dana,

I'd like to formally put myself forward for the team-lead role. Over the last two years on the platform team I've quietly done a lot of the job already — unblocking the two new hires, owning our on-call rotation redesign, and running the migration that retired our legacy queue. I'd like to do it with the title and the mandate.

Happy to talk through how I'd handle the parts I haven't formally owned yet, like headcount planning. Thanks for considering me.

Write yours in one pass. Draft the four moves as four ugly fragments — hook, proof, fit, ask — then join and trim to under 350 words. Read it aloud; if a sentence sounds like a form letter ("I am writing to express my interest…"), delete it and say the real thing instead.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Restating the resume. If a paragraph just lists what's already on your resume, cut it. The letter's job is the why, not the what.
  • The generic opener. "I am writing to apply for the position of…" wastes your best line. Lead with a specific reason or result.
  • Making it about you. "This role would be a great opportunity for me" is about your needs. Frame it around what you'd bring to them.
  • One letter for every job. A recruiter can spot a mail-merge in a second. Swap the company name, the role, and the one achievement that matches each posting.
  • Length. Over one page and it won't get read. Half a page that makes one sharp point beats a full page that hedges.
  • Typos in the greeting. Get the hiring manager's name and the company name exactly right — it's the fastest trust you can build or lose.

Frequently asked questions

How long should a cover letter be?

Half a page to one page — three or four short paragraphs, 200 to 350 words. A hiring manager skims it in seconds, so a tight letter that makes one clear point beats a full page that repeats your resume.

Do I still need a cover letter in 2026?

When the application offers a field for one, yes — a short, specific letter is a low-effort way to stand out, especially for a career change, a referral, or a role you're a slightly unusual fit for. If a posting explicitly says none is needed, skip it and put the energy into a tailored resume.

Should the cover letter repeat my resume?

No. The resume lists what you did; the letter explains why it matters for this specific job and shows you understand the company. Pick one or two achievements and tell the short story behind them, tuned to the role.

How do I write a cover letter with no experience?

Lead with motivation and transferable proof — coursework, projects, volunteering, a part-time job — and name the specific thing about the company that drew you. Enthusiasm plus one concrete example of relevant work carries a no-experience letter. The same logic applies to your no-experience resume.
