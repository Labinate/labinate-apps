How to address a cover letter

The greeting is one line, and it's the first line a hiring manager reads. Get it right and you sound like someone who did their homework; default to "To Whom It May Concern" and you sound like a form letter. The whole decision comes down to two questions: can you find a name, and if not, what do you write instead?

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

Here's the guide in one line: address it to the hiring manager by name — Dear Jordan Lee, or Dear Ms. Lee,. Can't find a name after a genuine look? Write Dear Hiring Manager, — never "To Whom It May Concern." Everything below is how to find the name, exactly what to write when you can't, and how to punctuate it.

The order of preference

Not all greetings are equal. Work down this list and use the first one you can actually pull off — the higher up you land, the more personal your letter reads before the reader has finished the first line.

  1. 1. Dear Jordan Lee, The named hiring manager, best of all. Full name is the safe form when you're unsure how they identify.
  2. 2. Dear Ms. Lee, A courtesy title plus last name — traditional and correct when you're confident of the person's title.
  3. 3. Dear Marketing Hiring Team, No individual name, but you know the team or department. Specific and warm without guessing.
  4. 4. Dear Hiring Manager, The clean, universal fallback when you have nothing else. Never wrong, never memorable.

Notice what's not on the list: "To Whom It May Concern," "Dear Sir or Madam," and "Hi there." Those are covered — and buried — further down.

How to find the hiring manager's name

Most people skip this because they assume it's hidden. It usually isn't — five minutes of looking finds a name more often than not. Try these in order:

  • Read the job posting closely. The name is sometimes right there — "reports to," a contact line, or the recruiter who posted it. Check the "meet the team" or "about the role" sections too.
  • Search LinkedIn. Look up the company, filter people by the relevant department, and find whoever holds the title this role would report to — a "Marketing Manager" for a marketing coordinator opening, an "Engineering Lead" for a developer role. The person hiring is usually one level up from the job.
  • Check the company's site. Small and mid-size companies often list their team, with names and titles, on an About or Team page.
  • Look at who emailed you. If a recruiter or coordinator has already reached out, their signature is a name — and often a clue to the manager's name format (first.last@company.com tells you the pattern).
  • Ask. If there's an application email or a recruiter contact, a one-line "Who will be reviewing applications for this role?" is completely normal and marks you as thorough.
Get the name right, not just any name

A named greeting only helps if it's the correct name. Double-check the spelling and that the person is actually connected to this role — addressing your letter to someone who left the company last year is worse than a generic greeting. When you're not fully sure, "Dear Hiring Manager," beats a confident guess.

When you genuinely can't find a name

Sometimes the posting is anonymous and the company is large enough that LinkedIn is a coin flip. That's fine — there's a clean fallback, and it isn't the one your parents used.

The modern default Dear Hiring Manager,

It's neutral, professional, and — crucially — it doesn't guess at a gender, a title, or a name you're unsure of. If you know the department, make it one notch more specific:

Better, when you know the team Dear Marketing Hiring Team,
Dear Engineering Recruiting Team,

Both read as if you're writing to real people rather than a void, which is exactly the impression you want.

Salutations to avoid

A few greetings actively work against you. Each one sends a small wrong signal on the very first line.

✕ Don't write

  • To Whom It May Concern,
  • Dear Sir or Madam,
  • Dear Sirs,
  • Hey! / Hi there,
  • Dear Jordan, (first name, stranger)
  • Dear Acme Corp,
  • Dear Recruiter,

✓ Write instead

  • Dear Hiring Manager,
  • Dear Hiring Manager,
  • Dear Hiring Manager,
  • Dear Ms. Lee,
  • Dear Jordan Lee,
  • Dear Marketing Hiring Team,
  • Dear Hiring Manager,

The two worth calling out: "To Whom It May Concern" was the standard a generation ago and now reads as stiff and impersonal — it tells the reader you didn't try. "Dear Sir or Madam" is worse: it assumes a binary you have no reason to assume and lands as dated on top of it. And a bare first name — "Dear Jordan," — is too familiar for a first contact with someone you've never met; use the full name or a title.

Mr., Ms., and the comma-vs-colon

Two small mechanics trip people up. Neither is worth agonizing over.

Titles

If you use a courtesy title, "Ms." is the safe default for a woman — it doesn't assume marital status the way "Mrs." does. "Mx." is a gender-neutral option. But the cleanest move when you're unsure how someone identifies is to skip the title entirely and use the full name: Dear Jordan Lee, sidesteps the guess completely and is never incorrect. If the person holds a doctorate or is a physician, "Dear Dr. Lee," is appropriate and expected.

Punctuation

A comma after the greeting is standard, friendly, and always safe: Dear Ms. Lee,. A colon is slightly more formal and common in US business letters: Dear Ms. Lee:. Both are correct — pick the one that matches the tone of the rest of your letter and stay consistent. What you don't do is skip the punctuation or add an exclamation mark.

The block above the greeting

In a formal letter format, the salutation sits under a small heading block — your contact details, the date, and the company's name and address. In an email or an online application box, you skip most of that and open straight with the greeting. Either way the salutation itself doesn't change; only what sits above it does. Quick reference

  • Best → the hiring manager by name: Dear Jordan Lee, or Dear Ms. Lee,.
  • No name, known team → Dear Marketing Hiring Team,.
  • No name at all → Dear Hiring Manager,.
  • Unsure how they identify → full name, no title.
  • Punctuation → comma (friendly) or colon (formal); both fine.
  • Never → "To Whom It May Concern," "Dear Sir or Madam," a bare first name, or the company name.
The whole thing in one breath. Spend five minutes finding the hiring manager's name — in the posting, on LinkedIn, on the company's team page — and write Dear [Full Name],. If you honestly can't find it, write Dear Hiring Manager, (or "Dear [Team] Hiring Team," if you know the team). Use "Ms." not "Mrs.," skip the title when you're unsure, punctuate with a comma or colon, and never fall back on "To Whom It May Concern."

Frequently asked questions

Who do you address a cover letter to?

Address it to the specific person who will read it — the hiring manager for the role, not a recruiter or a generic department. Write Dear Jordan Lee, or, if you prefer a title, Dear Ms. Lee,. A named greeting shows you did five minutes of homework, which is exactly the signal a cover letter exists to send. Only fall back to a generic greeting when you genuinely can't find the name.

How do you address a cover letter if you don't know the name?

Use Dear Hiring Manager,. It's neutral, professional, and doesn't guess at a gender or title. If you know the team, Dear Marketing Hiring Team, is even better because it's specific. Avoid "To Whom It May Concern," and "Dear Sir or Madam," — both read as dated and impersonal, and the second assumes a binary you have no reason to assume.

Is "To Whom It May Concern" still acceptable on a cover letter?

Skip it. It was the default a generation ago, but today it signals that you either couldn't be bothered to find a name or didn't know you should try. "Dear Hiring Manager," does the same job — a greeting when you have no name — without the stiff, form-letter tone. If you can find an actual name, use it; if you can't, "Dear Hiring Manager," is the safe modern fallback.

Should I use Mr. or Ms., and a comma or a colon?

When you know the person's name but not how they identify, use their full name with no title — Dear Jordan Lee, — which sidesteps the guess entirely. If you use a title, "Ms." is the safe default for women (not "Mrs."), and "Mx." is a gender-neutral option. For punctuation, a comma is standard and friendly (Dear Ms. Lee,); a colon is a touch more formal (Dear Ms. Lee:) and common in the US for business letters. Either is correct — just match the tone of the rest of your letter.
