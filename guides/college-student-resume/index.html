The college student resume

The instinct as a student is to treat your resume as a near-empty page you have to apologize for — "I've never had a real job." That's the wrong frame, and it costs you. A college student resume is not a no-experience resume: you almost always have raw material — coursework, campus roles, projects, part-time and summer work. The job isn't to fill emptiness. It's to recognize what counts, order it right, and describe it like the work it is.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The one-line version: while you're still enrolled, lead with education, then mine every experience you've overlooked and write it in quantified bullets. Your degree in progress is your strongest signal for internships and new-grad roles, and a class project or a shift-lead job is real experience once you describe it with specifics. The page isn't thin because you're a student — it's thin because most students under-count what they've already done.

Why it isn't a no-experience resume

There's a real guide for people with genuinely zero raw material — see how to write a resume with no experience. But most college students aren't actually in that position, and copying that playbook sells you short. You've likely got some or all of this already:

  • Part-time and summer jobs — retail, food service, campus jobs, tutoring, a warehouse shift. "Not related to my field" doesn't mean "doesn't count." Reliability, customer handling, and measurable output translate everywhere.
  • Campus leadership and clubs — treasurer, event lead, team captain, RA, orientation guide. A budget you managed or an event you ran is a bullet with a number in it.
  • Class and capstone projects — a semester-long build, a research paper, a group deliverable. If it produced a result, it's experience.
  • Research, volunteering, and freelance — lab work, a nonprofit role, a few paid gigs. All legitimate.

The task isn't to manufacture content from nothing. It's selection and framing: recognizing that these count, and describing each like the work it actually was.

Lead with education — the order you don't expect

Standard resume advice puts work experience first. As a current student, you flip it: your education section goes at the top, right under your contact header. It's your most relevant credential, and recruiters for intern and new-grad roles look for it there first.

What goes in the education block: degree and major (in progress is fine), school, and expected graduation month/year. Add GPA if it's ~3.5+, a short line of relevant coursework when you're light on work history, and honors or scholarships if you have them. Keep it tight — it's a signal block, not a transcript.

The flip-back rule: once you've graduated and hold a semester or more of relevant internship or full-time experience, move experience to the top and let education sink to the bottom. Until then, education leads.

Turn "just a student job" into real bullets

This is where most student resumes leak value. A job described as a duty ("Responsible for cashier tasks") says nothing; the same job described as an outcome says you can do work. The move is the same one every strong bullet point makes: start with an action, add a number, name the result.

Weak (duty)Strong (quantified outcome)
"Worked as a barista"Served 120+ customers per shift and trained 3 new hires on the espresso line, cutting order errors during peak hours
"Club treasurer"Managed a $6k annual club budget across 14 events; introduced a tracking sheet that eliminated end-of-year shortfalls
"Did a group project"Led a 4-person team building a class inventory app in Python; delivered on deadline and presented to a 30-person section

Don't have exact numbers? Estimate honestly — "~40 hours/week," "roughly 200 attendees," "a team of 5." A reasonable approximation is fine; inventing a precise-looking figure you can't defend is not. Pull the verbs from a real summary of what you did, not a job description you copied.

What order the sections go in

A clean, one-page student resume, top to bottom:

  1. Contact header — name, email, phone, city, and a LinkedIn or portfolio link.
  2. Education — degree in progress, school, expected grad date, GPA if strong, relevant coursework if you're light on experience.
  3. Experience — jobs, internships, and substantial campus roles, most recent first, in quantified bullets. Non-"job" experience counts here.
  4. Projects — a short section for class, capstone, or personal projects, especially in technical fields. Often does more work than a thin job history.
  5. Skills — tools, languages, and software, honestly rated. Salt in the keywords from the posting so you clear the ATS.
  6. Extras — leadership, volunteering, honors, languages, if they earn their space.
The trap

Padding the page to hide a short history — inflating margins, stretching the font, or adding a vague "Objective" that says you're a "hardworking student seeking opportunities." A reviewer reads padding as a lack of judgment about what matters, which is exactly the doubt a student resume needs to overcome.

→ Keep it to one honest page. Fill it by recognizing overlooked experience and writing specific bullets — not by enlarging what little is there.

Where GPA and coursework belong

Two student-specific calls that trip people up:

Do

  • List GPA on the education line if it's ~3.5+ (or a strong major GPA, clearly labeled).
  • Add 4–6 relevant courses when your work history is thin and the courses map to the role.
  • Show "Expected: May 2027" — an in-progress degree is a plus, not a gap.

Don't

  • List a GPA below ~3.5 — no GPA is neutral; a low one hurts.
  • Dump your whole transcript or every course you took.
  • List coursework once you have real, relevant experience to show instead.

The college student resume vs. its neighbors

Three student-adjacent situations get conflated — each takes a slightly different move:

SituationWhat it isResume move
College student resumeYou're currently enrolled with some raw material (jobs, campus roles, projects).Lead with education; frame overlooked experience as quantified bullets.
Internship resumeYou're applying to a specific internship and tailoring to it.Same education-first base, tuned hard to the one posting's keywords and skills.
No-experience resumeYou have essentially zero jobs, roles, or projects to draw on.Lean on skills, education, and potential to fill a genuine gap.

Quick reference

  • Is it a no-experience resume? → No. You almost always have raw material; the job is selection and framing.
  • Education or experience first? → Education first while enrolled; flip once you have real post-grad experience.
  • GPA? → On the education line if ~3.5+; leave off if lower.
  • Coursework? → 4–6 relevant courses when work history is thin; drop once you have experience.
  • How long? → One page. Fill it with evidence, never with padding.
  • The bright line → describe a student job or project as a quantified outcome, not a duty.

Frequently asked questions

What should a college student put on a resume?

More than you think. A college student resume should include your education (degree in progress, school, expected graduation), and then whatever raw material you actually have: part-time and summer jobs, campus leadership and club roles, class and capstone projects, research, volunteering, and relevant coursework. The mistake is assuming that because you have never held a "real" job, you have nothing to list. You do — you just have to recognize a shift lead, a team project, or a treasurer role as genuine experience and describe it with specific, quantified bullet points. The goal is not to fill a page; it is to show a hiring manager you can do work, using the evidence you have.

Should a college student's resume lead with education or experience?

While you are still enrolled, lead with education — the reverse of the standard advice for experienced professionals. Your degree in progress is your strongest and most relevant signal for internships and new-grad roles, and recruiters for those roles expect to see it at the top: degree, major, school, and expected graduation date. Once you have graduated and hold a semester or more of relevant full-time or internship experience, you flip the order and let work experience lead, moving education to the bottom. The rule is simple: put your strongest, most relevant credential first, and while you are a student that is almost always your education.

Does GPA go on a college student resume?

Include your GPA while you are a student or a recent graduate if it is roughly 3.5 or higher; place it on the education line next to your degree. If it is below about 3.5, leave it off — a resume with no GPA is unremarkable, while a low GPA actively works against you. You can also list a strong major GPA instead of a lower overall GPA if the major number is more impressive and clearly labeled. Once you have a few years of work experience, drop GPA entirely regardless of the number; at that point your track record speaks and the GPA no longer earns its space.

How long should a college student resume be?

One page. A college student has neither the volume of experience nor the reason to run longer, and a padded two-page student resume signals poor judgment about what matters. If you are struggling to fill a single page, the answer is not larger margins or filler — it is mining experiences you have overlooked (class projects, campus roles, part-time work, volunteering) and writing each one with two or three specific bullet points. If you are struggling to fit onto one page, cut the least relevant items rather than shrinking the font below readable size.

What is the difference between a college student resume and a no-experience resume?

A no-experience resume is written for someone with essentially no raw material to draw on and leans heavily on skills, education, and potential to fill the gap. A college student resume assumes the opposite: most students actually have material — coursework, campus involvement, projects, part-time jobs, volunteering — and the real task is selecting and framing it well, not manufacturing content from nothing. Treating your student resume as a no-experience resume sells you short; the stronger approach is to recognize the experience you already have and describe it like the work it is.
