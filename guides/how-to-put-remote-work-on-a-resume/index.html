How to put remote work on a resume

The whole answer fits in one field: put the word "Remote" where a city and state would normally go — on the same line as your title, company, and dates. Remote isn't a special kind of job or a section of its own; it's a work arrangement, so it lives in the location slot. No "(Remote)" bolted onto your title, no separate "Remote Work" heading. Here are the exact formats for fully remote, hybrid, and remote-with-a-home-base — plus how to signal you want remote work going forward.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The one-line version: "Remote" goes in the location field of the job, next to the dates. That's the location a recruiter's eye and an applicant tracking system both look for, and it's the honest, unambiguous way to say where the work happened. Everything below is just the details — the formats, the edge cases, and how to turn "I worked remotely" into evidence that you're good at it.

Why remote goes in the location field

Every job entry on a resume has the same four anchors: title, company, location, dates. "Remote" answers the location question — it says where the work was done, which is exactly what that field is for. Treating it as anything else creates problems:

  • It's not a job type. "Remote" isn't like "Full-time," "Contract," or "Internship" — those describe the arrangement of employment. Remote describes where you sat. It belongs with the location, not the title.
  • It keeps your timeline intact. Dropping "Remote" into the location field means your jobs stay in one clean reverse-chronological list — the order recruiters scan and ATS software parses.
  • It resolves a real question. If you live in Denver but the company is headquartered in New York, "Remote" instantly explains the mismatch instead of leaving a reader to wonder how you commuted.

The exact formats

Pick the one that matches your situation. All of them keep "Remote" in the location slot:

Put together, a full job header looks like this:

Senior Marketing Analyst — Acme Corp · Remote · Mar 2022 – Present

Location reads "Remote." Title stays clean. Dates unbroken. That's the whole move.

→ Same line, same format as every other job. Remote is just what fills the location.

What not to do

Avoid

  • A separate "Remote Work" section that breaks date order.
  • "(Remote)" jammed onto the job title — Senior Analyst (Remote).
  • Listing the company's HQ city as if you worked there when you didn't.
  • A "Remote" badge, icon, or colored tag that trips up ATS parsing.
  • Relabeling on-site jobs as "Remote" because you'd prefer remote now.

Do

  • One work-experience section, reverse-chronological, "Remote" in each location field.
  • Plain text — the word "Remote," no graphics.
  • State the true arrangement: remote, hybrid, or on-site.
  • Show your home base if it helps with time-zone or eligibility questions.
  • Prove you're good at remote through your bullet points, not a label.

Turn "remote" into evidence you're good at it

Anyone can type "Remote" in a location field. What actually reassures a hiring manager is proof you deliver without someone watching over your shoulder. Weave that into your accomplishment bullets rather than dumping a list of remote buzzwords:

  • Self-direction and ownership — "Ran a 6-person content pipeline end to end with no daily supervision, shipping 40+ articles/quarter."
  • Async and written communication — "Wrote the team's async status format that cut standup meetings by half across three time zones."
  • Cross-time-zone coordination — "Coordinated a distributed team spanning US, UK, and India to launch on schedule."
  • Remote tooling, in context — name Slack, Zoom, Asana, Notion, Git inside a result, not as a floating skills dump.

Lead these with strong action verbs and attach numbers where you can — see how to quantify achievements. That's what makes a remote history read as a strength.

Hybrid, contract, and freelance remote

Two things people mix up with "remote," because they can overlap:

  • Hybrid is still a location answer — write Hybrid — City, ST so it's clear you split time between an office and home. Don't call a hybrid role "Remote"; it invites a correction in the interview.
  • Contract / freelance is an employment type, which is a different field from location. A job can be both remote and freelance — you'd mark the location "Remote" and note the arrangement separately. If most of your remote work was independent, see how to list freelance work on a resume; and if it was fixed-term agency or contract work, see how to list contract work on a resume — those guides own the employment-type side, while this one owns where the work happened.

How to signal you want a remote job

Wanting remote work next is different from describing remote work you already did — and it's handled in a different place. Never invent "Remote" labels on jobs that were on-site; the experience section describes what was, and your preference is stated up top and in the cover letter:

  • Near your contact information, add a short line: Open to remote · US Eastern or Remote / hybrid — GMT+1.
  • Echo it in your professional summary or headline if remote is central to your search.
  • Say it plainly in the cover letter — one sentence on why you're seeking a remote role and why you thrive in one.
  • Mirror the posting's language: if the listing says "fully remote" or "distributed team," use the same words so keyword matching lines up. See tailoring to the job description and resume keywords.

Quick reference

  • Where does remote go? → the location field, next to the dates.
  • Fully remote → Remote (add (City, ST) to show your base).
  • Hybrid → Hybrid — City, ST, not "Remote."
  • Separate "Remote Work" section? → No. One dated work-experience list.
  • "(Remote)" on the job title? → No. Keep the title clean.
  • Remote + freelance → location "Remote," employment type noted separately.
  • Want remote next? → state it up top / in the cover letter; don't relabel past jobs.
  • Prove you're good at it → self-direction, async comms, cross-time-zone wins — in your bullets.

Frequently asked questions

How do you put remote work on a resume?

Put the word "Remote" in the location field of the job entry — the same slot where a city and state would normally go, on the header line with your title, company, and dates. Remote is a work arrangement, not a separate kind of job, so it belongs where you'd otherwise write a location. For example: "Senior Analyst — Acme Corp · Remote · Mar 2022 – Present." You don't need a special "Remote Work" section, and you don't tack "(Remote)" onto your job title.

Should you have a separate 'Remote Work' section on a resume?

No. Grouping your remote jobs into their own section breaks the reverse-chronological order recruiters and applicant tracking systems expect, and it makes remote sound like an unusual category rather than a normal way to work. Keep every job in one work-experience section, in date order, and mark each one "Remote" in its location field. If most or all of your recent roles were remote, that pattern speaks for itself without a heading announcing it.

Do you need to say you worked remotely on a resume?

It's optional, but usually worth it. Labeling a role "Remote" explains why your job's location may not match where you live, signals that you can be productive and self-managed without an office, and helps you match remote and hybrid job filters. The main reason to include it is honesty and clarity: if the role was remote, saying so is more accurate than listing the company's headquarters city as if you commuted there. The one time to reconsider is if you're targeting on-site roles and worry a fully remote history raises questions — even then, the fix is to prove impact, not to hide the arrangement.

How do you show you want a remote job on your resume?

Signal it forward, not by relabeling past jobs. Put "Open to remote" or "Remote / [your time zone]" near your contact information or in your headline, and mention remote-friendly strengths — async communication, self-direction, running projects across time zones — inside your accomplishment bullets. A cover letter is the natural place to say plainly that you're seeking a remote role and why you thrive in one. What you don't do is invent "Remote" labels on jobs that were actually on-site; the resume describes what was, and your stated preference handles what you want next.
