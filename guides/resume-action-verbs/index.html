185 resume action verbs to start your bullet points (and the ones to stop using)

"Responsible for" is where a bullet point goes to die. Swap it for a verb that names what you actually did — then back it with a number. Here's a categorized list to pull from, grouped by the kind of impact you had.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A recruiter skims each resume in a handful of seconds, reading mostly the first two words of every bullet. If those words are "Responsible for…" or "Helped with…", you've told them about a job description, not about you. A strong action verb does the opposite: it leads with the accomplishment and pulls the reader into the result.

Below are 185 verbs grouped by the kind of thing you did — leading, building, growing, saving, improving, analysing and communicating — plus the weak words to retire. Skim the group that fits your bullet, pick a verb, and pair it with a number.

The rule: verb + what + number

Every strong bullet is the same shape — a past-tense verb, the thing you did, and the measurable result:

  • Led a 5-person team through a billing migration, cutting failed payments 27%.
  • Built an internal dashboard now used daily by 40 people across two departments.
  • Reduced average support response time from 9 hours to under 2.

The verb does the heavy lifting, but the number is what makes a recruiter believe it. A verb without a result is only half a bullet.

The words to retire

These aren't wrong, they're just weak — they describe a role instead of an achievement, so they waste the most valuable line on your resume.

  • Responsible for / Duties included / Tasks involved — names the job, not the result. Start with what you did instead.
  • Helped / Assisted / Worked on / Involved in — hides your actual contribution. If you helped, say what part you owned.
  • Hard-working · detail-oriented · team player · self-starter · go-getter · results-driven — empty adjectives a recruiter has read a thousand times. Prove them with a bullet instead of claiming them.
  • Utilised — a longer word for "used". Just say "used", or better, name the outcome.
One-pass fix. Open your resume and read only the first word of every bullet. Any that start with "Responsible", "Helped", "Worked" or "Duties" get rewritten with a verb from the list above — then check each one has a number. That single pass lifts most resumes more than a full rewrite.

Frequently asked questions

What are resume action verbs?

Strong, specific verbs that begin a bullet point and name exactly what you did — led, built, grew, reduced, launched. They replace vague filler like "responsible for" or "helped with", which describe a role rather than an accomplishment.

Should every bullet point start with an action verb?

Yes. Starting each bullet with a past-tense action verb keeps your experience section punchy, scannable and consistent, and forces you to frame each line as an accomplishment rather than a duty. Use present tense only for your current role.

What words should I avoid on a resume?

Weak openers ("responsible for", "duties included", "helped", "worked on") and empty adjectives ("hard-working", "detail-oriented", "team player"). They take up space without proving anything — replace them with an action verb plus a measurable result.

How do I use action verbs with numbers?

Pair the verb with a quantified outcome: "Reduced support tickets 30% by rewriting the onboarding flow." The verb names the action, the number proves the impact. A strong verb without a result is only half a bullet.
