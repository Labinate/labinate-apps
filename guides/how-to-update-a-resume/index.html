How to update a resume

You don't need to start over — you need a focused refresh. Updating an old resume is three moves: add what's new, delete what's dated, and modernize the format so it still parses and reads like it was written this year. Here's exactly what to change, in order, so a stale file is ready to send today.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Most people open an old resume, add their latest job to the top, and send it. That's the mistake. A resume that's been sitting for a few years is almost always carrying dead weight — an objective statement no one writes anymore, jobs from three roles ago, a format that breaks in applicant tracking systems. The update is as much about what you remove as what you add.

When to update your resume

Don't wait until you need it. The best resume is one you keep current in small doses, so you're never rebuilding a year of wins from memory the night before an application. Update it when:

  • You start job hunting — the obvious one. Refresh fully before your first batch of applications, then lightly tailor per role.
  • You change roles or get promoted — capture the new title, dates and scope while they're fresh. See how to show a promotion.
  • You land a real result — a number worth quoting fades fast. Add it the week it happens.
  • You earn a certification or finish a course — add it to your certifications section immediately.
  • Nothing has changed in 6–12 months — do a light review anyway. Formats and expectations drift; so does your sense of what matters.

The quiet-refresh habit. Keep one master copy and drop new accomplishments in as they happen — a single line is enough. When you actually need a resume, you're editing a full record down, not staring at a blank page trying to remember what you did in Q2.

The refresh checklist

Work through these in order. The first pass is content; the second is format.

  1. Add your newest role at the top. New title, employer, dates, and 3–5 bullets. Keep the format reverse-chronological — most recent first.
  2. Refresh the metrics. Update numbers that have grown, and add results you never captured. Lead with the outcome — see how to quantify achievements.
  3. Trim or drop the oldest jobs. As new experience goes on, old roles shrink. Cover roughly the last 10–15 years and let the rest go — details in how far back a resume should go.
  4. Replace the objective with a summary. "Seeking a challenging role where I can grow" is dead. Swap it for a 2–3 line professional summary of what you offer — see objective vs summary.
  5. Update skills and keywords. Cut tools you no longer use, add the ones that matter now, and match the language of today's job titles. See resume keywords.
  6. Fix your contact block. Current email (a professional one), phone, city — and your LinkedIn. Drop the full street address.
  7. Reread every bullet. Start each with a strong action verb, keep the tense right (present for the current job, past for the rest), and cut filler.
  8. Then fix the format. Single column, standard font, clean PDF. More on this below.

Modernize the format

A resume from five years ago usually needs a format refresh as much as a content one. The current standard is deliberately plain, because plain is what parses:

Modern & ATS-safe

  • Single-column layout
  • Standard font, 10–12pt
  • Short professional summary up top
  • Action-verb bullets with numbers
  • Clear section headings
  • Clean, selectable-text PDF

Dated & risky

  • Two columns / sidebars
  • Photo, logos, colored tables
  • "Objective:" statement
  • Vague duty lists, no results
  • Decorative fonts & icons
  • Image-based or scanned PDF

If your old file is built on a template full of text boxes and columns, patching it is often slower than rebuilding. Dropping your content into a clean modern template gives you a format that parses correctly and reads current in one move — see the best resume format and font and size.

Update vs tailor — they're different

Updating brings your resume current: newest role, fresh metrics, modern format. That gives you a strong, general-purpose master copy. Tailoring is the next step — bending that master copy toward one specific job by mirroring its keywords and reordering to lead with the most relevant experience. You update once, then tailor per application. Do the update first; a clean, current base makes every tailored version faster. Walk through it in how to tailor your resume to a job description.

Quick reference

  • Add → newest role, fresh numbers, new skills/certs, current contact + LinkedIn.
  • Delete → objective, "references on request", street address, old email, jobs >10–15 yrs, obvious skills, photo/columns.
  • Modernize → single column, standard font, summary not objective, action-verb bullets, clean PDF.
  • How often → a light refresh 1–2× a year, a bigger one whenever something changes worth recording.
  • Then → tailor the updated master copy to each specific job.

Frequently asked questions

How often should you update your resume?

Do a small refresh at least once or twice a year even when you're not job hunting, plus a bigger update whenever something changes worth recording — a new role, a promotion, a certification, or a standout result. Keeping it current in small doses is far easier than reconstructing a year of accomplishments from memory the night before you need it. If you're actively applying, update it before every batch of applications and lightly tailor it to each role.

What should you remove from an old resume?

Cut anything that dates you or wastes space: the objective statement, "References available upon request", your full street address, an outdated email address, jobs from more than about 10–15 years ago, obvious skills like "Microsoft Word" or "email", high-school details once you have a degree, and any dated formatting like a photo, colored tables or two columns that break in applicant tracking systems. The goal is a lean, current page — not a complete history.

How do you modernize a dated resume?

Switch to a clean single-column layout in a standard font, replace the objective with a short professional summary, lead each bullet with a strong action verb and a number where you can, refresh your skills and keywords to match today's job titles, and make sure it exports as a clean PDF that parses correctly in applicant tracking systems. A resume from five years ago usually needs a format refresh as much as a content one — rebuilding it in a modern template is often faster than patching the old file.

Should you keep old jobs on your resume when you update it?

Not all of them. As you add newer, more relevant experience, older roles should shrink or drop off. A good rule is to cover roughly the last 10–15 years and let anything older go, unless a much older role is directly relevant to the job you're targeting. Trim the bullet count on early-career jobs to one or two lines and give the most space to your recent, on-target work.
