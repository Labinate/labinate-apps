How to end a cover letter

The last thing a hiring manager reads is the thing they remember walking into your interview — or not calling you for one. A strong ending does two small jobs: a closing paragraph that asks confidently for the next step, and a sign-off that's professional without being stiff. Most people fumble both by getting either too meek ("I hope to hear from you") or too casual ("Cheers!"). Here's exactly how to land it.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

The guide in one line: close with a confident call to action — offer to discuss the role and thank them for their time — then sign off with Sincerely, or Best regards, and type your full name underneath. Everything below is the wording that makes the closing paragraph land, the sign-offs ranked, the ones to avoid, and how email endings differ from printed ones.

The two parts of a good ending

Every cover letter ending is really two pieces, and people usually get one right and one wrong:

  • The closing paragraph — two or three sentences that reaffirm your interest, make a call to action, and thank the reader. This is where the persuasion happens.
  • The sign-off (valediction) — the single word or phrase before your name: "Sincerely," "Best regards," and so on. This is just etiquette; it only has to not be wrong.

Get the paragraph confident and the sign-off professional and you're done. Let's take them in order.

The closing paragraph: ask, don't hope

The single most common mistake is ending on a passive, hopeful note — "I hope to hear from you soon." It's not wrong, exactly, but it puts all the power in their hands and sounds like you're waiting by the phone. A strong close does the opposite: it's a confident call to action that points at their needs, not yours.

A good closing paragraph does three things, in this order:

  1. Reaffirm the fit in one short clause — tie yourself to what they need, not what you want.
  2. Make the ask — say plainly that you'd welcome the chance to talk.
  3. Thank them — a single courteous line for their time and consideration.
Weak — passive and about you "Thank you for taking the time to read my application. I really hope to hear back from you and would love the opportunity. Please let me know if you need anything else."
Strong — confident and about them "I'd welcome the chance to discuss how my five years in customer operations could help your support team cut response times as you scale. Thank you for your time and consideration — I look forward to speaking with you."

Notice the strong version names a concrete contribution, asks for the conversation without begging, and still closes politely. It reads like someone who expects to be taken seriously.

Don't apologise, don't pad, don't repeat

Three things quietly weaken a close: apologising ("Sorry for the long letter"), padding with filler ("As you can see from my attached resume…"), and restating your whole pitch again. The reader just read it. One clean call to action plus a thank-you is stronger than a paragraph of recap.

Confident call-to-action lines you can adapt

  • "I'd welcome the opportunity to discuss how I can contribute to [team/goal] — thank you for your consideration."
  • "I'd love to talk through how my [skill] maps to what you're building. Thank you for your time."
  • "I'm excited about [company]'s work on [thing] and would be glad to discuss the role further whenever it's convenient."
  • "Thank you for reviewing my application — I look forward to the chance to speak with you about the [role]."

The sign-off: what to write before your name

Once the paragraph is done, drop a line and add a sign-off. This is low-stakes — you only need to avoid the ones that read as too casual or too odd. Work down this list and the first one you're comfortable with is fine.

  1. 1. Sincerely, The universal default. Professional, neutral, never wrong for any company or industry.
  2. 2. Best regards, Warm but still professional. A great everyday choice that works almost everywhere.
  3. 3. Kind regards, Slightly more formal cousin of "Best regards," common and safe, especially outside the US.
  4. 4. Regards, / Best, A touch more relaxed. Good for startups or a company whose tone is clearly informal.
  5. 5. Respectfully, More formal and traditional — fits government, legal, academic, or senior-level roles.

Any of these is defensible. If you can't decide, use "Sincerely," — it fits every situation and no reader has ever docked a candidate for it.

Sign-offs to avoid

A handful of closings send the wrong signal on the last line, which is the worst place to send it. Each of these has a clean replacement.

✕ Don't sign off with

  • Cheers,
  • Thanks! / Thx,
  • Warmly, / XOXO,
  • Love,
  • Yours truly, / Yours faithfully,
  • Talk soon,
  • -Your Name (no sign-off at all)

✓ Use instead

  • Best regards,
  • Sincerely,
  • Best regards,
  • Sincerely,
  • Sincerely,
  • Best regards,
  • Sincerely,

The pattern: "Cheers," and "Talk soon," are too casual for a first contact; "Warmly,", "Love," and anything affectionate is too personal; and "Yours truly / faithfully," reads as dated and stiff in most of today's job market. When in doubt, "Sincerely," or "Best regards," covers you.

The small mechanics

Comma and capitalization

Capitalize only the first word of the sign-off and follow it with a comma: Best regards, — not "Best Regards," and not "Best regards" with no punctuation. Then your name goes on the next line.

Your name — and where it sits

Type your full name on the line after the sign-off. On a printed or PDF letter, leave two or three blank lines between the sign-off and your typed name so a handwritten signature can sit in the gap. In an email or an online application box, skip the handwritten space and just type your name — optionally followed by your phone number and email, so your contact details are right there at the bottom.

Printed / PDF letter

  • Sincerely,
  •  
  • (signature)
  • Jordan Lee

Email / online box

  • Best regards,
  • Jordan Lee
  • (555) 123-4567
  • jordan.lee@email.com

Where the ending sits in the whole letter

The closing paragraph is the last of a cover letter's three or four short paragraphs — opening hook, one or two body paragraphs of evidence, then this close. Quick reference

  • Closing paragraph → reaffirm fit + confident call to action + thank them, in 2–3 sentences.
  • Best last line → "I'd welcome the chance to discuss…" not "I hope to hear from you."
  • Safe sign-off → Sincerely, or Best regards,.
  • Mechanics → capitalize the first word only, add a comma, then your full name below.
  • Email vs printed → email skips the handwritten space and adds phone + email under your name.
  • Never → beg, apologise, restate the whole letter, or sign off with "Cheers," "Thanks!," or "Love,".

Frequently asked questions

How do you end a cover letter?

End with two things: a short closing paragraph and a sign-off. The paragraph reaffirms your interest and makes a confident call to action — say you'd welcome the chance to discuss the role, and thank them for their time. Then drop a line, write a professional sign-off like Sincerely, or Best regards,, and type your full name underneath. Keep the whole ending to two or three sentences plus the sign-off — no begging, no apologising, no restating your entire resume.

What is the best way to sign off a cover letter?

"Sincerely," is the safe, universal choice and is never wrong. "Best regards," and "Kind regards," are warm but still professional and work everywhere. "Best," and "Regards," are fine for a slightly less formal company. Capitalize only the first word and always follow the sign-off with a comma. Avoid casual or affectionate closings — "Cheers,", "Thanks!,", "Warmly,", "Love,", or "Yours truly," — which read as too familiar for a first contact.

What should the last line of a cover letter say?

Make it a confident, forward-looking call to action rather than a passive hope. Instead of "I hope to hear from you," write something like "I'd welcome the chance to discuss how I can help your team hit its goals — thank you for your time and consideration." It signals initiative, points at the reader's needs rather than yours, and closes on a courteous note without begging for the job.

Do you put your name at the end of a cover letter?

Yes. Type your full name on the line below the sign-off. On a printed or PDF letter, leave a few blank lines between the sign-off and your typed name for a handwritten signature; in an email or an online application box, you skip the handwritten space and just type your name, optionally followed by your phone number and email so your contact details sit right at the bottom.
