How to list per diem work on a resume

The instinct is to apologize for it — to split it into pieces, bury it, or file it under "part-time" because the hours were low and irregular. Don't. Per diem is one continuous job at one employer — not a gap, and not a temp churn — so it gets a single date range and proves its weight through shift volume and breadth, not tenure. Label it right and an as-needed schedule reads as exactly what it is: a flexible, in-demand professional who can be dropped into any assignment and deliver.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

Per diem — Latin for "per day" — means you're on an employer's roster and pick up individual shifts as they come up, with no guaranteed hours. It's most common in healthcare, where it's often called PRN (Latin pro re nata, "as needed"), but the same arrangement shows up as substitute teaching, event and hospitality staffing, and "casual" roles across industries. The listing problem is always the same: an as-needed schedule can look, at a glance, like underemployment or a job that quietly pushed you out. This guide fixes that — the label, the single date range, and the metrics that make per diem argue for you.

The core idea: per diem is one ongoing employment relationship, not a string of shifts and not a temp assignment. List it as a single role with one continuous date range, then measure it by how many shifts you took and how many settings you covered — because versatility and reliability, not hours, are what an as-needed role is built to prove.

First, what "per diem" is — and what it isn't

Per diem gets lumped in with every other non-standard arrangement, which is why people mislabel it. Pin down what you actually had, because the word you choose changes the signal a recruiter reads.

Not the same as…How per diem differs
Part-time workPart-time is a fixed reduced schedule — the same set hours every week, just fewer of them. Per diem has no guaranteed hours at all; you pick up shifts as they're available or as the employer needs coverage. Calling per diem "part-time" implies a schedule you didn't have and understates the self-direction the arrangement actually shows.
Temp workTemp work runs through a staffing agency that employs you and places you at a client with a planned end date — so the key temp move is grouping multiple assignments. Per diem is usually direct W-2 employment by the facility itself: one employer, no end date. The grouping technique doesn't apply, because there's nothing to group — it's a single continuous role.
Contract / freelanceContract and self-employed work means you were an independent contractor (1099) engaged for a defined scope and billing your own rate. Per diem is normally on the employer's payroll as a W-2 employee — you just aren't scheduled. Labeling per diem "contract" overstates your independence.
Seasonal workSeasonal work is tied to a predictable calendar peak — holidays, summer, tax season — and ends when the season does. Per diem is year-round and on-call, with no seasonal pattern. If your role was really about the peak, list it as seasonal; if it was ongoing as-needed coverage, it's per diem.

List it as one role — the single most important move

Here's the mistake that makes per diem look worse than it is: treating each block of shifts, or each month you happened to work, as a separate entry — or, worse, leaving the low-hours stretches out and creating gaps. Per diem is one job. You joined the roster on a date and you're either still on it or you left it on a date. That's your date range, and it's continuous even in the weeks you picked up nothing.

The clean per diem format

Regional Medical Center — Registered Nurse, Per Diem
Mar 2023 – Present

One employer, the label Per Diem (or PRN) right in the title so no one reads low hours as part-time underperformance, and a single date range that runs the full length of the relationship. This is the whole scaffold — everything else is the bullets that prove weight.

The single continuous range does real work: it tells the reader you've held an active, ongoing role — kept your credentials current, stayed embedded in a workplace — the entire time. Break it into fragments and you throw that away and invite the "why so many short stints?" misread that per diem never earned. It's not temp work; don't format it like temp work.

Prove its weight with shift volume and breadth — not hours

The reader doesn't discount per diem because it was as-needed; they discount it when it's written as a thin duty list with nothing to grab. The fix is to measure the two things per diem is uniquely good at showing: how much you picked up and how wide a range you covered. Those beat any hours figure, because they prove demand and adaptability at the same time.

Notice what the strong version quantifies: units covered, shifts per month, times specifically requested, crises covered. None of that is tenure, and none of it is hours — it's the metrics that fit the arrangement. A per diem professional's value proposition is "drop me anywhere and I deliver on short notice," so the resume should show exactly that range.

Label and language that do the work for you

Put "Per Diem" or "PRN" in the title, not hidden in a bullet

The label is what converts "why so few hours?" into "flexible, on-call professional." Set it right in the role line — Pharmacist, Per Diem or RN, PRN — so the reader frames the whole entry correctly before they reach the bullets. In clinical settings PRN is instantly understood; in other fields, "Per Diem" or "On-Call" reads more clearly.

Lead with what you did, in strong verbs

Treat each line like any real role: lead with the action and the outcome, not the arrangement. "Floated," "stabilized," "covered," "trained" — a per diem line written like a job description reads as filler; written like accomplishments, it reads as range.

Keep dates honest and current-tense when active

Month-and-year dates, and if you're still on the roster, write it in the present tense — Mar 2023 – Present. An active per diem status is a live signal that you're employed and keeping skills current, which matters most if you're searching from a slow patch.

A few common situations

Per diem alongside a full-time job

Many people work per diem on top of a primary role to pick up extra income or hours. List both — the per diem line shows initiative and a second skill set, and there's nothing to hide about it. If the per diem work is off-target for the job you're seeking, keep it brief; if it's directly relevant, give it real bullets.

Per diem covering a career break or job search

This is per diem's quiet superpower. Staying on a roster during school, caregiving, or a search means you were never actually unemployed — you maintained active per diem status and kept your license and skills current. It closes what would otherwise be a gap and answers the "what were you doing?" question before it's asked.

Per diem through an agency, not direct

Some per diem work is booked through a staffing agency rather than by the facility. If so, name the agency as your employer of record — it's what employment verification will confirm — and note the settings you covered as placements. It's still one continuous relationship, so resist the urge to fragment it into separate jobs.

Per diem that kept your credential live

For licensed roles, per diem work is often what keeps a credential active and current. Pair the role with your license or certification so the reader sees an unbroken, in-practice professional — not someone whose credential has gone dormant.

The bright line — label it accurately, don't dress it up. Two temptations backfire. Padding a per diem entry to look like full-time hours falls apart the moment anyone asks about your schedule or checks with the employer. And re-labeling it "part-time" or "contract" to sound more conventional actually weakens it — it trades away the flexibility-and-demand story that is per diem's real strength. The honest version — "Per Diem," one date range, quantified by shift volume and breadth — is both truer and stronger than any disguise.

Quick reference

  • One role, one date range → per diem is a single ongoing job at one employer. Never fragment it into shifts or months, and never let the low-hours stretches become gaps.
  • Label it in the title → "Per Diem" or "PRN" right in the role line, so low hours read as flexibility, not underperformance.
  • Quantify range, not hours → units/facilities covered, shifts per month, times requested, crises covered. Versatility and reliability are the point.
  • It's not temp or part-time → no agency grouping, no fixed schedule. Direct, ongoing, as-needed W-2 work. Use the accurate word.
  • Use it as an anti-gap → "maintained active per diem status" means you were never unemployed and kept your credential live.

Keep building the resume

Per diem is one piece of a work history that should read as intentional. Make sure it sits cleanly against any part-time or temp roles (in the UK and Commonwealth, per diem's closest relative is the zero-hours contract — same as-needed idea, listed the same way), that any gaps around it are handled honestly, and that your licenses and certifications back it up. One caution on wording: per diem's "on-call" means available for a shift — different from carrying an incident-response pager inside a salaried role, which is how to list on-call experience. Frequently asked questions

How do you list per diem work on a resume?

List it as a single, continuous role at one employer, exactly like a regular job — employer name, title with the label "Per Diem" or "PRN," and one date range from when you joined the pool to when you left (or to Present). Do not break it into separate entries for each shift or each month you happened to work; per diem is one ongoing employment relationship, not a series of jobs. Then quantify it by shift volume and breadth — how many shifts a month, and how many units, departments, or facilities you covered — because an as-needed role proves its value through range and reliability, not through hours logged.

What is the difference between per diem and part time on a resume?

Part-time means a fixed, reduced schedule — the same set hours every week, just fewer of them. Per diem means no guaranteed hours at all: you pick up individual shifts as they become available or as the employer needs coverage. On a resume the distinction matters because it changes the signal. Labeling a per diem role "part-time" implies a set schedule you didn't have and quietly understates the flexibility and self-direction that per diem work actually demonstrates. Use the accurate label — "Per Diem" or "PRN" — so a reader reads an on-call professional, not a reduced-hours employee.

Should you put per diem or PRN jobs on your resume?

Yes, in almost every case. Per diem work is real, verifiable, ongoing employment — it keeps your license and skills current, fills your timeline, and often shows a breadth of settings a staff role can't. It's especially valuable when it runs alongside school, caregiving, or a job search, because an active per diem status means you were never actually unemployed. The only per diem role you might cut is a very old or completely off-target one that adds nothing and isn't covering a gap. The usual question isn't whether to include it — it's how to format an as-needed schedule so it reads as flexible and in-demand rather than as underemployment.

How do you quantify a per diem job with irregular hours?

Stop counting hours and start counting shifts and range. "Picked up 6-10 shifts per month across 5 units (ICU, ED, med-surg, telemetry, PACU)" says far more than any hours figure, because it proves adaptability and demand at once. Other strong metrics: number of facilities floated to, high-acuity or specialty assignments taken, times you were specifically requested by a charge nurse or manager, and any coverage you provided during short-staffing or crises. Per diem's whole strength is versatility and reliability on short notice — quantify those, not tenure.

Is per diem the same as temp or contract work on a resume?

No. Temp work runs through a staffing agency that is your legal employer and places you at a client with a planned end date, so the key move there is grouping multiple assignments. Contract work usually means you were an independent contractor (1099) engaged for a defined scope. Per diem is typically direct W-2 employment by the facility itself — one ongoing employer, no end date, no guaranteed hours. That's why the temp technique of grouping assignments does not apply: you have a single employer and a single continuous date range. If your per diem work was through an agency rather than direct, note the agency as the employer, but it's still one continuous relationship, not a churn of jobs.
