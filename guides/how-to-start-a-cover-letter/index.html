How to start a cover letter

A recruiter decides whether to keep reading in about the length of your first sentence. Most cover letters waste it on "I am writing to apply for the position of…" — a line that says nothing and reads like every other letter in the stack. A strong opening does the opposite: it earns the next sentence with something specific, confident, and impossible to copy-paste. Here's exactly how to write one.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

The guide in one line: after the greeting, open with a specific hook — genuine enthusiasm for the company, a quantified win, a mutual connection, or a direct match to the job's top need — name the exact role in the first two sentences, and keep the whole opening to two to four sentences. Everything below is the four hook types with examples, the cliché openers to delete, and how to start when you don't have a name.

Why the first sentence carries the whole letter

A hiring manager screening dozens of applications isn't reading your cover letter — they're skimming it, and the first sentence decides whether skimming turns into reading. Open with a generic line and you've told them, in your own words, that this letter is interchangeable with the others. Open with something concrete and specific and you've bought yourself the rest of the paragraph.

So the opening has one job: be un-copy-pasteable. If you could send the exact same first sentence to a different company without changing a word, it's too generic. The fix is always the same — swap the throat-clearing for a real detail.

The four opening hooks

There are really only four strong ways to open, and you pick by what you've got: a reason you love this company, a result you're proud of, a person you both know, or a skill they clearly need. Any one of them beats a generic opener.

1. The enthusiasm hook — a specific reason for this company

Not "I've always admired your company" (everyone says it), but a concrete detail that proves you actually looked. Reference a product, a blog post, a value, a recent launch — then tie it to why you want in.

Enthusiasm hook "When your team open-sourced its design system last spring, I rebuilt my side project on it in a weekend — so applying to be a front-end engineer at Northwind feels less like a job application and more like joining a project I already use."

2. The achievement hook — lead with a result

Open with a single quantified win that maps to what the role needs. It shows, in one sentence, that you do the kind of work they're hiring for.

Achievement hook "Last year I cut our support team's average response time from 14 hours to under 3 by rebuilding our triage flow — the kind of operational fix I'd love to bring to your Customer Experience Lead role."

3. The connection hook — a mutual name (with permission)

If someone referred you or you share a genuine contact, lead with it. A name the reader recognises is the single strongest opener there is — just make sure you have the person's OK first.

Connection hook "Priya Nadar, who leads your data team, mentioned you're looking for an analyst who can own reporting end-to-end — that's exactly the gap I filled at Acme for the last two years, so I was glad she pointed me to this role."

4. The skills-match hook — mirror their top requirement

When you don't have a story or a contact, go straight at the job description. Find the one requirement they clearly care most about and open by matching it head-on.

Skills-match hook "Your posting for a Growth Marketer asks for someone who has scaled paid acquisition past $1M/month profitably — I've run exactly that budget at two startups and kept CAC flat while doubling spend."
Whichever hook you use, name the role

Somewhere in the first two sentences, state the exact job title you're applying for. A hiring manager may be filling three openings at once; don't make them guess. You usually don't need to say where you found the posting — that detail is rarely interesting and eats your best sentence. Name the role, skip the job board.

The clichés to delete

Four openers show up in a huge share of cover letters, and every one of them wastes the first sentence. Each has a better move.

✕ Don't open with

  • "I am writing to apply for the position of…"
  • "My name is ___ and I am a…"
  • "To Whom It May Concern,"
  • "I found your job posting on Indeed…"
  • "Webster's dictionary defines leadership as…"

✓ Do this instead

  • Lead with a hook; name the role inside it
  • They can see your name — open with a result
  • "Dear Hiring Manager," (or a real name)
  • Where you found it isn't the story — cut it
  • Open with a specific, real detail

The pattern: each cliché is about the mechanics of applying rather than about you or them. Anything the reader already knows — that you're writing to apply, what your name is, where the job was posted — is a wasted first sentence.

Weak vs strong, side by side

Weak — generic and about the process "Dear Hiring Manager, I am writing to express my interest in the Marketing Coordinator position I found on your careers page. I believe my skills and experience make me a strong candidate for this role."
Strong — specific and about the fit "Dear Ms. Alvarez, your latest campaign turning a dry compliance product into a genuinely funny TikTok series is exactly the kind of work I want to make — which is why I'm applying to be your next Marketing Coordinator, after three years running social for a fintech in the same tight-regulation corner."

Same greeting, same role — but the second one proves the writer looked, states a real angle, and gives the reader a reason to keep going. It could not have been sent to any other company.

Opening lines you can adapt

  • "When I saw [company] was hiring a [role], I [specific reaction tied to a real detail] — here's why I think I'm a strong fit."
  • "Over the last [time] at [company], I [quantified result]. I'd like to do the same as your [role]."
  • "[Referrer] suggested I reach out about your [role] — after hearing what the team is building, I understand why."
  • "Your posting for a [role] asks for [their #1 requirement]; that's the part of the job I've spent [time] doing well."

How to start when you don't have a name

Two different questions get tangled here: how to greet the reader, and how to open the paragraph. A missing name only affects the greeting. Do a quick hunt first — the job posting, the company's LinkedIn and team page, the recruiter's email signature — and if you still come up empty, use "Dear Hiring Manager,". It's the modern, professional fallback and reads far better than the dated "To Whom It May Concern." Then open your first paragraph with your hook exactly as you would otherwise. Full detail on the greeting itself is in how to address a cover letter.

Where the opening sits in the letter

The opening is the first of a cover letter's three or four short paragraphs: this hook, one or two body paragraphs of evidence, then a confident close. It should run two to four sentences — long enough to land the hook and name the role, short enough that the reader reaches your evidence fast. Quick reference

  • Job of the opening → earn the next sentence; be impossible to copy-paste to another company.
  • Pick a hook → enthusiasm, achievement, connection, or skills-match — whichever you've got.
  • Name the role → state the exact job title in the first two sentences.
  • Length → 2–4 sentences, then move into your evidence.
  • No name? → "Dear Hiring Manager," then open normally.
  • Never open with → "I am writing to apply…", "My name is…", "To Whom It May Concern," or where you found the posting.

Frequently asked questions

How do you start a cover letter?

Start with the greeting, then open the first paragraph with a specific hook — not a throat-clearing "I am writing to apply for…". The strongest openers lead with genuine enthusiasm for the company, a quantified achievement, a mutual connection, or a direct match to the job's top requirement. Name the exact role you're applying for somewhere in the first two sentences, keep the whole opening to two to four sentences, and make the very first sentence earn the second one — recruiters skim, and the opening decides whether the rest gets read.

What is a good opening line for a cover letter?

A good opening line is specific to this job and this company, and it says something a hundred other applicants can't. For example: "When your engineering blog described cutting deploy times from an hour to nine minutes, I recognised the exact problem I spent last year solving at Acme — which is why I'm applying to be your next platform engineer." It names a real detail, ties it to a concrete result of yours, and states the role — all in one confident sentence. Avoid generic openers like "I am writing to express my interest in the position."

What should you not start a cover letter with?

Avoid the four clichés that make a reader skim past your opening: "I am writing to apply for the position of…" (says nothing), "My name is… and I am…" (they can see your name), "To whom it may concern" (dated — use "Dear Hiring Manager," instead), and "I found your job posting on Indeed…" (where you found it isn't interesting). Also skip opening with your life story or a dictionary-definition quote. Lead with something specific to the company or a concrete result instead.

How do you start a cover letter without a name?

If you can't find the hiring manager's name after checking the job posting, the company's LinkedIn and team page, and the recruiter's email, use "Dear Hiring Manager," — it's the modern, professional fallback and reads far better than the dated "To Whom It May Concern." You can also address the team or department, e.g. "Dear Marketing Team,". Then open the first paragraph with your hook exactly as you would otherwise; a missing name changes only the greeting, not the opening line.
