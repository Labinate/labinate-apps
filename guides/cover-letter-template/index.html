Cover letter template: 4 free fill-in-the-blank templates

The hardest part of a cover letter is the blank page. Start from one of these four copy-ready templates instead — a standard letter, a career-change letter, a referral letter, and an entry-level letter — each with bracketed placeholders you swap in about five minutes.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

A template isn't a shortcut to a lazy letter — it's a shortcut past the blank page to the part that actually matters: filling in your specifics. Every template below is built on the same proven shape (hook → proof → close), so all you have to do is replace what's in [brackets] with real detail. Copy the one that fits your situation, paste it into your editor, and swap the placeholders one by one.

If you want the reasoning behind the structure, read how to write a cover letter; for finished, filled-in versions to model, see the cover letter examples; and for the exact spacing, font and margins, see the cover letter format guide. This page is purely the copy-ready templates.

Before you fill it in: the 4 things to swap

Every template hinges on four specifics. Have these ready and the fill-in takes minutes:

  1. The exact job title and company — copied from the posting, not paraphrased.
  2. One achievement with a number — pulled straight from your resume. (Stuck? See how to quantify achievements.)
  3. One specific reason you want this company — a product, a value, a piece of their work — not "your reputation."
  4. A named person to greet — the hiring manager or recruiter where you can find them; "Dear Hiring Manager" only when you truly can't.

Template 1 — The standard cover letter

The all-purpose template. Use this when you're applying to a role in a field you already work in.

Copy from here

[Your Name] · [Email] · [Phone] · [City]

[Date]

Dear [Hiring Manager Name],

I'm applying for the [Job Title] role at [Company]. [One sentence naming a specific reason you want this job or company — a product you admire, a problem they're solving, a value that matches yours.]

In my current role as [Your Title] at [Current Company], I [achievement with a number — e.g. "cut onboarding time 40% by rebuilding the setup flow"]. [One more sentence of proof that maps to what this job needs.] That's the kind of work I'd bring to [Company].

[Optional: one sentence on why you fit the team or mission specifically — the "this company, not just any company" line.]

I'd welcome the chance to talk about how I can help [Company] [do the thing the role exists to do]. Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]

Template 2 — The career-change cover letter

Use this when you're moving into a new field. The job is to reframe your existing experience as transferable, not to apologize for the switch. (Pairs with the career-change resume guide.)

Copy from here

[Your Name] · [Email] · [Phone] · [City]

[Date]

Dear [Hiring Manager Name],

I'm writing to apply for the [Job Title] role at [Company]. I'm moving into [new field] from [current field], and [one sentence on why — a genuine, forward-looking reason, not a complaint about the old field].

The switch is less of a leap than it looks. In [current field] I [transferable achievement with a number — the skill this new role needs, proven in your old one]. [One more sentence connecting a second transferable skill to this job's requirements.]

I've backed the move with [course, certification, side project, or volunteer work relevant to the new field], so I'm arriving ready to contribute, not just to learn.

I'd love to talk about how a [current-field] background can be an asset in this role. Thank you for considering my application.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]

Template 3 — The referral cover letter

Use this when someone inside the company pointed you to the role. Naming the referral in the first line is your strongest possible hook — lead with it.

Copy from here

[Your Name] · [Email] · [Phone] · [City]

[Date]

Dear [Hiring Manager Name],

[Referrer's Name], [their role at the company], suggested I apply for the [Job Title] role — we [how you know them: worked together at X, met through Y] — and after reading the posting I could see why they thought of me.

At [Current Company] I [achievement with a number that maps directly to this role]. [One more sentence of proof.] It's the same kind of work [Referrer's Name] saw me do firsthand.

I'd be glad to talk about how I can bring that to [Company]. Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]

Template 4 — The entry-level / no-experience cover letter

Use this for a first job, an internship, or a role where you're light on direct experience. Lead with enthusiasm and evidence of capability — coursework, projects, part-time work. (Pairs with how to write a resume with no experience.)

Copy from here

[Your Name] · [Email] · [Phone] · [City]

[Date]

Dear [Hiring Manager Name],

I'm excited to apply for the [Job Title] role at [Company]. [One specific sentence on why this company or role — tie it to something real, like a product you use or a mission you follow.]

I'm early in my career, but I've already [most relevant proof: a class project, a part-time job, a volunteer role, a personal project — with a concrete outcome or number]. Through it I learned to [skill the job needs], and I'm ready to apply that from day one.

[Optional: one sentence on a soft skill backed by an example — "I kept a five-person group project on schedule by…"]

I'd welcome the chance to show what I can do for [Company]. Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]

The test before you send: read your filled-in letter and ask, "could I paste this to a different company by swapping only the name?" If yes, it's still too generic — add one detail that only fits this job. That single specific line is what separates a template from a form letter.

Filling it in: dos and don'ts

  • Do replace every bracket. A stray [Company] left in the final version is the fastest way to the reject pile.
  • Do match the font and margins to your resume. See the cover letter format guide for the exact settings.
  • Do keep it to one page and three or four short paragraphs.
  • Don't open with "I am writing to apply for…" — the template's first line already does that job with a reason attached.
  • Don't restate your whole resume. Pick the one or two achievements that matter most for this role.
  • Don't use "To Whom It May Concern." Find a name; fall back to "Dear Hiring Manager."

Frequently asked questions

How do I use a cover letter template without it looking generic?

Replace every bracketed placeholder with something specific — the exact job title, the company name, one achievement with a number, and one detail about the company that isn't on their About page. The template gives you the shape; the specifics make it yours. A letter still full of [brackets] or vague filler reads as a mail-merge and gets skipped.

Is it okay to use a free cover letter template?

Yes — recruiters can't tell you started from a template, only whether the finished letter is specific and well written. A good template saves you the blank-page problem and enforces the right structure. What gets a letter rejected is copying it word for word without swapping in real detail, not the fact that you used a starting point.

Should the cover letter template match my resume?

Yes. Use the same header style, font and margins on both so they read as one application. The fastest way to look polished is to build the resume and letter with the same tool and export both to matching PDFs — a mismatched font pair is the giveaway that the two documents were thrown together.

How long should a cover letter from a template be?

One page, and usually less. Three to four short paragraphs — an opening hook, one or two paragraphs of proof, and a brief close — is plenty. If filling in the template pushes you past one page, cut a paragraph rather than shrinking the font. Half a page that makes one sharp point beats a full page that hedges.
