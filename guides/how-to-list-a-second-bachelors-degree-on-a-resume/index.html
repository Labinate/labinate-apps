How to list a second bachelor's degree on a resume

You already had a bachelor's — then you went back and earned another, usually in a different field, usually years later. That's a real and increasingly common move, but it puts an awkward question at the top of your resume: why two undergraduate degrees? Answer it well and it reads as deliberate reinvention. Leave it unanswered — or hide the first degree — and it reads as a false start. This is how you frame the switch so it works for you.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Here's the whole thing in one sentence. A second bachelor's degree is a sequential reinvention, not a stacked credential — two separate degrees earned at different times — so you list both, lead with the one the job wants, and frame the switch as a deliberate pivot rather than a do-over. The two failure modes are opposite: quietly dropping the first degree to avoid the "why two?" question leaves an unexplained gap and discards relevant background, while listing both with no story forces the reader to assume the first was a mistake. The honest version — both degrees, ordered for relevance, tied together by one clear line about the pivot — is both truthful and stronger than either.

The core idea: a second bachelor's is two separate degrees earned years apart, not a double major earned at once. List both in Education, put the most job-relevant one first, and use a one-line summary to make the switch read as an intentional pivot. Present a reinvention, not a do-over.

1. List both degrees — never drop the first

The single most common mistake is deleting the first degree to avoid the awkward question. It backfires two ways. It leaves an unexplained gap in your timeline that an interviewer will notice and ask about anyway — so you gain nothing and look evasive. And it throws away relevant background: your first field is often part of what makes you distinctive in the new one. Give each degree its own entry in your Education section, with its own institution and graduation year.

Two honest entries

B.S. in Nursing — State University, 2025

B.A. in Psychology — City College, 2018

Two separate lines, two real years. Nothing to explain away — the timeline is simply true.

The one nuance is depth. If your first degree is decades old and completely unrelated, you can list it briefly — institution and degree, no GPA or coursework — while keeping the new, relevant one detailed. But brief is not the same as absent. Keep it.

2. Order for the job, not just for chronology

The default is reverse-chronological: newest degree first. Conveniently, that usually also puts the new-field, more-relevant degree on top — which is exactly what a skimming recruiter should see first. In the common case, you don't have to think about it.

Override the default only when the target role genuinely values your first field more. Relevance to the posting beats strict chronology in the education section.

Target roleWhich degree leads
Nursing positionB.S. Nursing (newest and most relevant — default order works).
Health-economics analystB.A. Economics may lead, even though it's older — it's what the job wants.
Unrelated to eitherNewest first (default); let the rest of the resume carry relevance.

Whatever order you choose, aim it the same way the rest of the resume is aimed — a focused summary and a tailored experience section should all point at the same target.

3. Frame the switch as a deliberate pivot

Two bachelor's degrees sitting silently next to each other invite the reader to guess — and the default guess is "the first one didn't work out." Don't leave that to chance. A single summary line at the top does the framing before the question is asked, and turns a potential red flag into a story of intentional reinvention.

Reads as intentional

  • "Psychology graduate who retrained in nursing — a people-first foundation plus clinical skill."
  • Names the throughline between the two fields where one exists.
  • The first degree becomes an asset that differentiates you.

Reads as a false start

  • Two degree lines, no summary, no connection drawn.
  • An apologetic note in Education ("changed direction after…").
  • First degree hidden — leaving a gap the reader fills in badly.

Where the two fields share a real throughline — psychology into nursing, English into UX writing, biology into data science — name it, and the older degree stops looking like a detour. Where they don't share one, that's fine too: "made a considered switch into X and committed to it fully" is a perfectly credible line. The point is that you write the story, rather than letting a recruiter invent a worse one.

4. Keep the dates and status honest

A second bachelor's has a distinctive timeline — two separate, non-overlapping chapters — and that timeline is where honesty lives. Show the real years for each degree, gap and all. If the second degree is still in progress, mark it "expected [date]" rather than implying it's finished, and see the in-progress degree guide for the exact phrasing. Above all, never format the two as though they were earned at the same time — that's a dual degree, a different thing, and a transcript check would contradict it.

Accurate timeline

  • "B.S. Nursing, 2025" and "B.A. Psychology, 2018" — real, distinct years.
  • In-progress: "B.S. Computer Science — expected 2027."
  • A gap between the two dates, shown plainly, needs no apology.

Blurred timeline

  • Overlapping dates that imply the two were concurrent.
  • A second degree marked as complete before it is.
  • Dropping the first year to hide the years-apart gap.
The one bright line. Present a deliberate reinvention, not a do-over. List both degrees, order them for the job, connect them with one honest line about the pivot, and show the real timeline — including the gap. Two truthful, well-ordered degrees framed as an intentional switch read as commitment and range; a hidden first degree or a blurred timeline reads as evasion. You don't need to disguise anything — the honest version is the stronger one.

What this is not

A second bachelor's is easy to confuse with three neighbours that sit differently on a resume. Separating them keeps the formatting — and the timeline — honest.

Not the same as…How a second bachelor's differs
A double majorA double major is two fields under one degree — one diploma, one graduation date — so it's one education line. A second bachelor's is two separate degrees with two diplomas, so it's two lines.
A dual degreeA dual (or joint) degree is two degrees earned concurrently through one combined program in the same era — two lines, but one timeframe. A second bachelor's is earned sequentially, years apart, in two distinct chapters — never format it as concurrent.
A master's degreeA graduate degree is a vertical step up on an existing foundation. A second bachelor's is a lateral move into a new field at the same level — the route you take when the graduate program isn't open without the undergrad foundation (nursing, CS, engineering).
A career-change resumeThe career-change resume is the whole-page pivot strategy built on transferable skills. A second bachelor's is one education entry inside it — the formal-retraining route that gives the pivot a credential to stand on.
The bottom line. A second bachelor's degree is a deliberate pivot into a new field, not a false start you need to hide. List both degrees, put the job-relevant one first, tie them together with one clear line about why you switched, and show the real, years-apart timeline. Framed that way, two undergraduate degrees stop being a question mark and become exactly what they are — proof you committed to a new direction and did the work to get there.

Quick reference

  • List both → never drop the first degree; a hidden gap looks worse than the switch.
  • Order for the job → newest usually leads and is usually most relevant; override only when the first field fits better.
  • Frame the pivot → one summary line makes "why two?" read as intentional reinvention.
  • Name the throughline → where the two fields connect, the first degree becomes an asset.
  • Honest timeline → real years, shown gap, "expected [date]" if in progress — never concurrent dates.
  • Not a double major → two separate degrees earned sequentially, not two majors in one program.

Frequently asked questions

Should I list both bachelor's degrees on my resume?

Yes — list both. Dropping the first degree is the most common mistake, and it backfires two ways: it leaves an unexplained gap in your timeline that an interviewer will ask about anyway, and it throws away background that is often still relevant. Two bachelor's degrees is not something to hide; it signals commitment, range, and the willingness to retrain — all of which employers read positively when the switch is framed clearly. Give each degree its own entry in your Education section, with its own institution and graduation year. The only real question is order and emphasis, not whether to include them. The exception is deep history: if your first degree is decades old and completely unrelated, you can list it more briefly (institution and degree, no extras) while keeping the new, relevant one detailed — but still list it.

Which bachelor's degree should come first?

Default to reverse-chronological order, which puts your most recent degree first — and because a second bachelor's is usually the newer, new-field, more-relevant credential, that ordering typically also leads with the degree the employer cares about. That is the common case and it needs no special handling. Override it only when the target role actually values your first field more than your second: relevance to the posting wins over strict chronology in the education section. So if you earned a B.A. in Economics, then a B.S. in Nursing, and you're applying for nursing roles, the nursing degree leads. But if you're applying for a health-economics analyst role, the Economics degree might legitimately lead. Pick the order that puts the most job-relevant degree where a skimming recruiter sees it first, and keep it consistent with how the rest of the resume is aimed.

How do I explain why I have two bachelor's degrees?

Frame it as a deliberate pivot, not a do-over — and do the framing in a one-line summary at the top of the resume rather than in an apologetic note in the education section. Something like "Psychology graduate who retrained in nursing — combining a people-first foundation with clinical training" answers the "why two degrees?" question before it's asked and reframes it as intentional reinvention. Where the two fields share a real throughline, name it: the first degree becomes an asset that differentiates you, not a detour to explain away. Where they don't, that's fine too — "made a considered switch into X and committed to it fully with a second degree" is a perfectly credible story. What you should not do is leave the two degrees sitting next to each other with no narrative, forcing the reader to guess whether the first was a false start. You control that story; write it.

Is a second bachelor's degree the same as a double major or a dual degree?

No — all three are different, and the difference decides the formatting. A double major is two fields of study completed under a single degree — one diploma, one graduation date — so it goes on one education line ("B.A. in Economics and Political Science"). A dual degree (or joint degree) is two separate degrees earned concurrently through one combined program in the same era, so it takes two lines but shares a timeframe. A second bachelor's degree is two separate degrees earned sequentially — you finished one, then went back later for another, usually in a different field — so it takes two lines with genuinely different, non-overlapping date ranges. The key tell is time: a double major and a dual degree happen at once; a second bachelor's happens in two distinct chapters. Never format a second bachelor's as though the two degrees were concurrent — that misrepresents your timeline and a transcript check would contradict it.

Should I get a second bachelor's or a master's degree to change fields?

That's a decision to make before the resume, but it shapes how the resume reads, so it's worth being clear on. A master's degree is a vertical step — building depth on top of an existing foundation — while a second bachelor's is a lateral move into a new field at the same level. People choose the second bachelor's route when the graduate program in the new field isn't open to them without the undergraduate foundation first: fields like nursing, computer science, or engineering often require specific prerequisite coursework or licensure that a bachelor's provides and a random master's can't shortcut. If a master's in the new field would accept you directly, that's usually the faster credential and reads as an advancement. If it wouldn't — if you genuinely need the undergraduate foundation to enter or to be licensed — a second bachelor's is the right and defensible path, and you list it as the deliberate retraining it is. On the resume, the second bachelor's is presented as a pivot; a master's is presented as a level-up.
