How to list a job share on a resume

You split one full-time role with a partner — you worked part of the week, they worked the rest, and together you covered the whole job. Now you're wondering how to put that on your resume without it reading as "half a job." Here's the freeing answer: it isn't half a job. It's a full-scope role you co-held. List the title and scope in full, keep the hours off the page, and claim the results that are genuinely yours.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Here's the whole thing in one sentence. A job share is a full-time, full-scope role that happens to be staffed by two people — so on a resume you present it as the full role you held, not as a fraction you did part of. The resume records the title you held, the scope you owned, and the results you delivered; the internal staffing model isn't part of that. The instinct to flag "(job share)" so you're not "taking credit for the whole thing" is understandable, but it backfires: it tells the reader to halve everything below it, when in fact you were accountable for the full role's outcomes alongside a partner.

The core idea: a job share is one full role, not half a job. List it with its real title and full scope, don't tag it "(job share)" or "(0.5 FTE)", own the results that are demonstrably yours without claiming your partner's, and — if it ever needs mentioning — frame it as proof of collaboration, not as a caveat.

1. It's one full role, not half a job

Start by naming what a job share actually is: an arrangement about staffing. Two people split one full-time position — each works part of the week, and together they cover the whole role. Crucially, the position is full-scope. You held the full title and were accountable for the full mandate; you shared the weekly delivery, not the responsibility.

And resumes record the role, not the staffing model. A full-time employee doesn't annotate their entry "(one of 40 people in the department)", and you shouldn't annotate yours "(job share, 0.5 FTE)" either. The title you held and the scope you owned are what belong on the page — both fully intact.

What people think they need to write

Operations Manager (job share, 0.5 FTE — role split with a colleague) · 2021–2024

What actually belongs on the page

Operations Manager · 2021–2024

Same title, same dates, full scope. How the role was staffed is between you and your employer.

2. Don't tag the arrangement — it invites a discount you don't deserve

Labels like "(job share)", "(0.5 FTE)", "(part-time)", or "(shared role)" on the entry line do one thing: they tell the reader to halve everything below. That's the opposite of what's true. You were accountable for the full role's outcomes, and your bullets describe real work you delivered. Tagging the hours understates the scope you actually carried.

If your role really was genuinely part-time by nature — smaller in scope and hours by design, not a full role split with a partner — that's a different situation, and you'd handle it the way you'd list any part-time job. The test is simple: was the position full-scope (a job share) or actually reduced in scope (part-time)?

3. Own the results that are genuinely yours

A job share is precisely the situation where honest, self-attributed results matter most. The scope was shared — but your accomplishments are yours. So quantify what you personally delivered, and never present your partner's numbers as your own. Where a metric was jointly owned, describe your contribution to it rather than claiming the whole figure.

Attribute your half of a shared mandate — specifically

Led the client-retention side of a shared portfolio, lifting renewals 18% year over year.

Owned the vendor-negotiation half of the function, cutting supplier costs $120K annually.

Your contribution, stated plainly. This quietly answers "but did you do the whole job?" without your ever raising it.

This isn't about diminishing the shared mandate — it's about giving the reader a true picture of what you contributed. Framing each bullet around what you owned and delivered does the persuading for you. See quantifying achievements and responsibilities vs. accomplishments for how to make each line land.

4. Mention the share only if it helps — and only as a strength

In the vast majority of cases the arrangement never needs to appear at all — the title, scope, and results carry the role. But if it genuinely comes up — it explains a working dynamic, or an interviewer asks directly how the team was structured — mention it in the cover letter or the conversation, and frame it as evidence of a real skill.

Frame it as this

  • Proven collaboration and seamless handovers.
  • Shared accountability for a full-scope role's outcomes.
  • Coordination discipline — keeping a role running across two people.
  • Or nothing at all, if it doesn't add anything.

Never this

  • "I was only responsible for half the role…"
  • A hedge or apology attached to the entry.
  • "(Part-time, job share)" on the title line.
  • Claiming your partner's results as your own.

You co-held a full role and delivered against it. Keep the framing forward and confident: a job share is a coordination achievement, not a caveat — and it belongs off the resume entry regardless.

The one bright line. A job share is a full role you co-held with results that are demonstrably yours. Present the title and scope in full, keep the hours off the page, attribute your own accomplishments honestly, and let a shared mandate read as a strength rather than a fraction.

What this is not

A job share sits near several other situations, and it's easy to reach for the wrong playbook. Each carries a different concern — getting the category right tells you what to show and what to leave alone.

Not the same as…How a job share differs
A part-time jobA part-time job is a role that's genuinely smaller in scope and hours by design, and you label it as such because that is the job. A job share is a full-scope role split between two people — same full title, same full mandate, shared delivery. Don't call it part-time; don't tag the hours.
A phased returnA phased return is a temporary, solo ramp of your own hours back to full within a single role after leave. A job share is a permanent, structural two-person split. One is a schedule you're easing through alone; the other is how the role is staffed.
MoonlightingMoonlighting is a second, separate role you hold on top of a main job — two roles, one person. A job share is the inverse: one role, two people. You're not stacking jobs; you're co-staffing a single one.
Remote workRemote work is a work-arrangement detail about location that can be worth signaling — it's a fit and a skill. A job share is a staffing detail that generally doesn't belong on the page at all. Both are "how the work happens," but only one earns a mention.
The bottom line. The question "how do I list a job share on my resume?" has a confidence-restoring answer: as the full role it was. List your real title and full scope with continuous dates, skip the "(0.5 FTE)" tag entirely, lead your bullets with the results that are genuinely yours, and mention the arrangement only in conversation and only as a strength. You co-held a full role and did the work — let the page say exactly that.

Frequently asked questions

Do I have to say a role was a job share on my resume?

No — and in almost every case you shouldn't. A job share is an arrangement about how a role is staffed: two people split one full-time position, each working part of the week, and together they cover the whole job. What the resume records is the title you held, the scope you were responsible for, and the results you delivered — not the internal staffing model. Tagging the entry "(job share)" or "(0.5 FTE)" does nothing but invite the reader to halve everything below it, which misrepresents both the role and your accountability, because you owned the full role's outcomes alongside a partner. List it exactly as you'd list any role: title, employer, dates, and results. The one time it's worth mentioning is if it genuinely comes up — an interviewer asks, or it explains a specific dynamic — and even then you frame it as a strength (collaboration, handover discipline), not a caveat.

Isn't a job share just a part-time job?

No, and the distinction matters on a resume. A part-time job is a role that is genuinely smaller in scope and hours by design — the job itself is part-time, and you label it as such because that's what it is. A job share is a full-time, full-scope role that happens to be staffed by two people. You held the full title and were accountable for the full role's mandate; you simply shared the delivery with a partner. So you don't call it part-time and you don't tag your hours — doing so would understate the scope you actually carried. If your role really was part-time by nature rather than a shared full-time role, then it's a different situation and you'd handle it the way you'd list any part-time job. The test is simple: was the position itself full-scope (job share) or genuinely reduced in scope (part-time)?

How do I show my accomplishments if the work was shared with a partner?

Attribute honestly and specifically — a job share is exactly the situation where self-attributed results matter most. The scope was shared, but your accomplishments are yours, so quantify what you personally delivered and never present your partner's numbers as your own. If you jointly owned a metric, describe your contribution to it rather than claiming the whole figure ("led the client-retention half of a shared portfolio, lifting renewals 18%"), and lean on results that were unambiguously yours where you can. This isn't about diminishing the shared mandate — it's about giving the reader a true picture of what you contributed. Framing your bullets around what you owned and delivered also quietly answers the "but did you do the whole job?" question without your ever having to raise it. See quantifying achievements and responsibilities vs. accomplishments for how to make each bullet land.

Should I ever mention the job share, and where?

Rarely, and never on the resume entry itself. In the vast majority of cases the arrangement never needs to appear — the title, scope, and results carry the role. If it genuinely helps — because it explains a working dynamic, or an interviewer asks directly how the team was structured — mention it in the cover letter or the conversation, and frame it as evidence of a strength: seamless handovers, shared accountability, keeping a full-scope role running across two people is a real coordination skill. What you never do is present it as an apology or a hedge ("I was only responsible for half…"). You were accountable for the full role's outcomes alongside a partner. Keep the framing forward and confident: you co-held a full role and delivered.

How do I list the dates and title for a job share?

Exactly as you would for any role: the real title, the employer, and honest continuous dates for the period you held the position — "2021–2024", or "2021–Present" if it's ongoing. A job share doesn't change your title, doesn't shorten your tenure, and doesn't split into "my half" and "their half" on the timeline. You held the role for its full duration; you shared the weekly delivery, not the calendar. Don't add a partial-year or fractional-tenure note, and don't annotate the hours. If the share ended and you moved to a solo full-time role — whether the same job or a new one — just show that as the next entry or a continuation, the way you'd handle any role change. Keep dates consistent with the rest of the resume; the dates guide covers the formatting.
