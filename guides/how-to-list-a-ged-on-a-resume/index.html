How to list a GED on a resume

A GED is a completed high-school-equivalency credential — so list it in Education like a diploma: cleanly, without apology, and never as "some high school." Its weight is all about rank — it matters most when it's your top credential, and comes off once something above it covers the tier.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

You earned your GED and you're staring at the Education section wondering whether to include it, how to word it, and whether it'll count against you. The anxiety is understandable and mostly misplaced. A GED — General Educational Development — is a state-recognized credential that certifies high-school-level academic skills, accepted by employers, colleges, and the military as satisfying a high-school-completion requirement. On a resume it isn't a confession or a gap to explain; it's a finished credential that you list plainly and move past. The two things that actually go wrong are wording it apologetically — "some high school," "partial diploma" — which turns a completed credential into an unfinished one, and keeping it on the page long after a higher credential has made it redundant. Get those two right and the GED does exactly one clean job and gets out of the way.

The core idea: a GED sits on the axis of equivalency — a non-traditional path to the same educational tier a high-school diploma marks, not a lesser one. That drives one governing rule: rank. List the GED in Education, formatted like a diploma, when it's your highest credential and a job asks for high-school completion. The moment something above it appears — college coursework, a degree, a strong work history — it becomes redundant and either shrinks to one low line or comes off, exactly as a diploma would.

First: treat it as a diploma, not a disclosure

Your Education section lists the credential that marks each level of schooling you completed. A GED is the credential for the high-school level, so it goes there, on the same footing a diploma would — one clean entry, no special "equivalency" heading, no explanation attached. The single most common mistake is wording that describes not finishing: "some high school," "attended [high school] 2019–2021," "partial high school." Those phrases undersell a completed credential into a gap, which is the opposite of what a GED is. You finished; the resume should say so in the plainest possible terms.

Nor does a GED owe a backstory. Whatever route you took — leaving school early, testing out, earning it later — is not something a reader needs, and volunteering it only invites a question the credential itself already answers. State the credential, the issuing authority, and the year, and stop. Confidence here is a formatting choice: the entry that reads as strongest is the one that treats the GED as unremarkable, because a completed high-school credential is unremarkable — it's the baseline, and you have it.

How to write the line

Keep it to a line or two in Education: the credential, the state or issuing authority, and the year you earned it. Spell out the acronym once if you have room — everyone knows "GED," but the full name reads a touch more formal.

Clean and finished

  • GED (General Educational Development), State of Texas, 2023
  • GED, 2022
  • High School Equivalency (GED), Ohio, 2024

Apologetic or unfinished

  • "Some high school" — describes not finishing
  • "Did not graduate; later earned GED" — volunteers a non-issue
  • "Equivalent to a diploma (GED)" — over-explains what's understood

Two refinements. If you're a current student or very recent completer with little else on the page, a brief supporting detail is fair game — a strong test score, or "in progress, expected [month year]" if you haven't finished yet — because Education is doing more of the work at that stage. And if you're also enrolled in further schooling, list that program (in progress) alongside the GED; when the higher credential lands, it takes the GED's place.

Second, and the real call: rank decides whether it stays

Because a GED sits on the high-school tier, its resume life is governed by what sits above it. It's essential when it's your top credential and invisible once it isn't — the same subsume logic that quietly retires a high-school diploma once you have a degree.

It's your highest credential → keep it, and let it check the box

Entry-level jobs and application systems routinely require high-school completion; the GED is what satisfies that requirement. List it plainly in Education — for a light-experience resume it can sit higher on the page.

You now hold something above it → drop it or shrink it

Any college coursework, an associate or bachelor's degree, or a substantial professional certification subsumes the high-school tier. Listing both is redundant and points at the tier you've already passed — keep the higher credential, cut the GED.

You have years of relevant experience → it comes off

An experienced candidate's resume leads with what they've done, and the high-school credential — GED or diploma — quietly leaves the page. Your work history is the stronger evidence; let it carry the education question.

Where it goes on the page

Placement follows the same logic as any education entry: how much of your case it's carrying.

Experienced → Education (and the GED) sits low

Beneath Experience, present but not prominent — a reader confirms the credential and moves on to what you've done.

Entry-level or a thin work history → Education can sit higher

When the GED is one of the main things you're offering and it clears an explicit requirement, surface it. Pair it with a strong objective or a no-experience resume structure so the whole page reads as ready, not thin.

The through-line: a GED is a completed high-school-level credential earned by an alternative route — not an unfinished degree, not a higher tier, and not the absence of a credential. Present it as exactly that, and it reads as the box-checked baseline it is.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode isn't listing a GED — it's listing it as though it were a wound. Stated as the finished credential it is, it clears a requirement and disappears; hedged and over-explained, the same credential reads as a gap you're bracing against.

Reads as credential earned

  • One clean line in Education
  • Named plainly: GED, state, year
  • Dropped once a higher credential covers the tier
  • Experience leads; the GED just checks the box

Reads as a gap

  • Written as "some high school"
  • A backstory about why you left school
  • Kept on the page next to a degree
  • Given its own apologetic "equivalency" heading

A GED is a completed credential that satisfies a high-school-completion requirement — the resume's job is to state it plainly and let your experience do the rest. One clean line in Education, no apology, no story; and the moment a college credential or a real work history arrives, let it take the GED's place. Do that and the GED reads as exactly what it is: a box checked, cleanly, on the way to everything you've actually done.

Quick reference

  • In Education → a GED is a high-school-level credential; list it there like a diploma, not in a separate "equivalency" section.
  • Name it cleanly → GED (General Educational Development), state, year — one line, no backstory.
  • Never "some high school" → that describes not finishing; a GED is finished, so say so plainly.
  • Rank decides → keep it while it's your top credential; drop it once college, a degree, or a strong work history covers the tier.
  • Place by weight → low for experienced candidates; higher for entry-level resumes where it clears a real requirement.
  • Know your axis → GED (equivalency) ≠ high-school resume (a whole resume) ≠ incomplete degree (unfinished) ≠ associate degree (a tier up).

Frequently asked questions

Do you put a GED on a resume?

Yes, when it's your highest credential. A GED is a completed, state-recognized high-school-equivalency credential, and many entry-level jobs and application systems explicitly ask for high-school completion — the GED is exactly what checks that box, so listing it clears a real requirement. Put it in your Education section, formatted like a diploma. The one time you leave it off is when something above it already covers the same tier: if you hold any college coursework, an associate or bachelor's degree, or a strong work history, the GED becomes redundant and either drops to a single low line or comes off entirely. Think of it the way you'd think of a high-school diploma — essential early, quietly retired once a higher credential carries the education question for you.

How do you write a GED on a resume?

Keep it plain and put it in Education: the credential, the state or issuing authority, and the year — for example, "GED (General Educational Development), State of Texas, 2023," or simply "GED, 2023." Spell out the acronym once if you have room, since "GED" is universally understood but the full name reads slightly more formal. Do not write "some high school," "partial high school," or anything that describes not finishing — a GED is a finished credential, and those phrasings undersell it into a gap. Don't add the story of why you earned it by an alternative route; the credential is what a reader needs, not the path. If you scored well and are a current student or very recent completer with little else on the page, a brief note is fine, but for most candidates the clean one-line entry is stronger.

Is a GED as good as a high-school diploma on a resume?

For resume purposes, treat them as equivalent. A GED is a recognized high-school-equivalency credential accepted by employers, colleges, and the military as satisfying a high-school-completion requirement, so on a resume you list it the same way you'd list a diploma: one clean line in Education. A few employers and programs still express a preference for a diploma, and you can't control that, but the resume's job is simply to state the credential accurately and let it check the box — not to litigate the comparison. What weakens a GED on a resume isn't the credential itself; it's presenting it apologetically or as "some high school." Stated plainly as the completed credential it is, it does its job, and after your first year or two of work experience, what you've done matters far more than which route you took to high-school completion.

When should you take the GED off your resume?

Take it off — or shrink it to a single low line — once you hold a credential above it. The GED sits on the high-school tier, so any college coursework, an associate degree, a bachelor's, or even a substantial professional certification subsumes it: listing both the higher credential and the GED is redundant and can actually draw attention to the tier you've already surpassed. The same is true once you have several years of relevant work history — an experienced candidate's resume leads with what they've done, and the high-school credential, GED or diploma, quietly comes off the page. The rule is the same one that governs a diploma: keep it while it's your top education credential and it's answering a real requirement; retire it the moment something higher answers that question for you.

Where does a GED go on a resume?

In the Education section, formatted like any other education entry. For an experienced candidate, Education — and the GED within it — sits low on the page, beneath your work experience, because your experience is the stronger evidence. For an entry-level candidate with little work history, Education can sit higher, since the GED may be one of the main things you're offering, and clearing an explicit high-school-completion requirement is worth surfacing. Don't invent a separate "Equivalency" or "Certifications" section for it — a GED is a high-school-level education credential and belongs in Education, next to where a diploma or degree would go. If you're also currently enrolled in further schooling, list that in progress above or below the GED as appropriate, and once the higher credential is earned, let it take the GED's place.
