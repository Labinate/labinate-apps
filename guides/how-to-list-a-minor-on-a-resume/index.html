How to list a minor on a resume

A minor is optional supporting information, not a credential of its own — which settles two things at once. It lives on the same line as your degree, never in its own section and never dressed up as a second degree. And the only real decision is whether it earns its place: a minor that signals a second, relevant skill belongs on the page; one that just says "I also took some classes in this" is a line of attention you can't afford to waste.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

People overthink the minor in two opposite directions. Some hide it — a genuinely relevant minor that would have strengthened the application, left off because "it's just a minor." Others inflate it — giving the minor its own section, its own bullet points, sometimes a made-up minor GPA, as if it were a second major. Both miss what a minor actually is: a short, formally recognized block of study in a second subject, worth exactly one clause on your education line when the second subject is relevant, and worth nothing when it isn't. This guide covers the judgment call — include it or drop it — and the one correct way to format it.

The core idea: a minor is a modifier on your degree, so it's formatted as one — inline, one clause, in the education entry. Include it when the second subject is relevant to the target job; cut it when it isn't. Relevance is the whole test.

First, what a minor is — and what it isn't

"Minor" gets used loosely for any second subject you studied, which is exactly why people format it wrong. Pin down which of these you actually earned, because the label you use has to match what the school conferred.

Not the same as…How a minor differs
A double majorA double major means you completed the full requirements of two majors and the school conferred both — you list them co-equal ("B.A. in Economics and Political Science"). A minor is a smaller, secondary block of study. Calling a minor a double major overstates the credential, and an education check confirms exactly what was granted.
A concentration or trackA concentration (specialization, track, emphasis) is a focus within your major — it signals depth in your own field. A minor is a second, different field. Both are listed inline after the degree, but they say different things: "I went deeper in my subject" versus "I have a relevant second subject." Use the word the school uses.
A certificationA certification is a standalone professional credential (PMP, AWS, CPA) that often deserves its own section. A minor is part of your degree — it never leaves the education line. Don't promote a minor into the certifications section; it isn't one.
Relevant courseworkCoursework is individual classes you can list even without a formal minor — useful when you took the classes but didn't complete a minor, or want to surface specific skills. A minor is the formally conferred version. If you didn't finish the minor, list the coursework instead; don't call it a minor.

The one test: does the minor earn its place?

A minor takes up a line of the reader's limited attention. It earns that line only when the second subject helps your case for this job. Run the minor through one question — does it signal a skill this role wants? — and let the answer decide.

Keep the minor when…

  • It's relevant to the target role (CS major + Business minor for a product job)
  • It rounds out a one-note profile (Engineering major + Technical Writing minor for a dev-docs role)
  • It signals a language or skill the job names (Biology major + Spanish minor for bilingual clinical work)
  • You're early-career and education is doing real work on the page

Drop the minor when…

  • It's unrelated to the role (CS major + Music minor for a backend job)
  • It dilutes a sharp, focused pitch
  • You're years into a career and experience is the headline
  • You're only including it to fill space — that's not a reason

Notice that the same minor can be a keep for one job and a cut for another. A Statistics minor is a strong signal for a data-analyst role and irrelevant clutter for a graphic-design role. That's why the minor is a tailoring decision, not a fixed fact — you turn it on when the target job makes it relevant and off when it doesn't.

How to format it: one line, one clause

When a minor earns its place, it goes exactly one place — inline in your education entry, as a short clause after the major. No separate section, no bullets, no heading of its own.

The clean format

B.S. in Computer Science, Minor in Business Administration
University of Michigan · May 2026

Degree first (it's the credential that matters most), minor as a modifying clause, then school and dates. The reader takes in "technical degree, business awareness" in a single glance — which is the entire point of including it.

Two relevant minors? Combine them

B.A. in Economics, Minors in Statistics and Spanish. Two can share one clause if both are relevant. Past two, it starts to read as unfocused — pick the ones that support the job and let the rest go.

Don't invent a minor GPA

List one GPA at most — overall or major — and only if it's strong and you're early-career. A separate minor GPA is unusual, easy to misread, and adds nothing; the minor's value is that the subject appears next to your degree, not that you scored well in it.

A few common situations

You're 10 years into your career

Your minor almost certainly comes off. Once experience is your headline, the whole education section shrinks to degree, school, and maybe dates — the minor is a detail no one at that stage is weighing. Exception: if the minor's subject is now central to your work and nothing in your experience shows it, one clause can stay.

The minor is more relevant than the major

It happens — a Philosophy major with a Computer Science minor applying for a dev role. You still list the degree accurately (you can't relabel a minor as a major), but you lean on the minor's subject everywhere else: in your summary, your skills, and especially your projects. The resume argues for the skill through work; the minor just confirms it's real.

You took the classes but didn't finish the minor

Don't call it a minor — a minor is formally conferred, and claiming an unearned one is a credential misstatement a check will catch. List the subject honestly under relevant coursework instead: Coursework in Statistics: regression, experimental design, R. You get full credit for what you studied without claiming a credential you don't hold. The same honesty rule applies to an incomplete degree.

No degree, just a strong minor-level focus

If you don't hold a degree, there's no degree line to hang a minor from — so lead with the skills and work themselves. The no-degree guide covers how to build a resume that competes on capability rather than credentials.

The bright line — never inflate the credential. A minor is not a double major, a concentration is not a minor, and coursework you didn't complete is not a minor at all. Every one of these is confirmed by a routine education verification, and relabeling a smaller credential as a bigger one reads as dishonesty even when you meant it as shorthand. List exactly what the school conferred — the honest version is always safer, and a relevant minor stated plainly already does everything you need it to.

Quick reference

  • Include only if relevant → a minor earns its line when the second subject signals a skill the target job wants; if it doesn't, cut it.
  • Format inline → one clause after the degree ("B.S. in Computer Science, Minor in Business Administration"), in the education entry. Never its own section, bullets, or heading.
  • It's a tailoring call → the same minor can be a keep for one job and a cut for another; turn it on when the posting makes it relevant.
  • Skip the minor GPA → one GPA at most, and only early-career; the value is the subject appearing, not the grade.
  • Bright line → don't relabel a minor as a double major or claim an unfinished minor — an education check confirms exactly what was granted.

Keep building the resume

The minor is one small decision inside a well-built education section. Make sure the rest of it is right too — GPA only when it helps, coursework to surface specific skills a minor can't, and honors where you earned them. Frequently asked questions

Should you put your minor on your resume?

Only if it earns its place. A minor is optional supporting information, not a required credential, so the test is simple: does it signal a second, relevant skill the target job actually wants? A Computer Science major with a Business minor applying for a product role should keep the minor — it says "technical and commercial." A Computer Science major with a Music minor applying for a backend engineering role can drop it; it's a nice fact that adds nothing to the pitch and costs a line of attention. When in doubt for an entry-level resume, include a relevant minor and cut an irrelevant one. Later in your career, when experience is the headline, most minors quietly fall off.

How do you write a minor on a resume?

Inline, on the same line as your degree, as a short clause after the major: "B.S. in Computer Science, Minor in Business Administration." That's it — one line, in the education entry, with your school and graduation dates. A minor never gets its own section, its own bullet points, or its own heading. It's a modifier on your degree, so it's formatted as one. If you have two relevant minors, you can list both in the same clause ("Minors in Statistics and Economics"), but past two it starts to read as unfocused, so pick the ones that matter.

Is a minor the same as a double major or a concentration?

No, and the words are not interchangeable. A double major means you completed the full requirements of two majors and the school conferred both — you list them as co-equal ("B.A. in Economics and Political Science"). A concentration (or specialization/track) is a focus within a single major, listed inline like a minor but signaling depth in your field rather than a second field. A minor is a smaller, formally recognized block of study in a second subject. Use the accurate term for what the school actually granted: calling a minor a "double major," or a concentration a "minor," is a credential overstatement that an education check will catch.

Do you include your minor GPA on a resume?

Almost never. A separate minor GPA is unusual, easy to misread as your overall GPA, and adds clutter for no gain. List one GPA at most — your overall or major GPA — and only if it's strong and you're early enough in your career for GPA to matter at all. The minor's value on a resume is that the subject appears next to your degree, not that you scored well in it. If a specific minor course is genuinely impressive and relevant, surface it under relevant coursework rather than inventing a minor GPA line.

Can you list a minor you didn't finish?

No — not as a minor. A minor is a credential the school formally confers, so listing one you didn't complete is the same kind of misstatement as claiming a degree you didn't earn, and an education verification confirms exactly what was awarded. If you took most of the coursework but didn't finish the minor, don't call it a minor. You can instead surface the subject honestly under relevant coursework ("Coursework in Statistics: regression, experimental design, R") which gives you full credit for what you actually studied without claiming a credential you don't hold.
