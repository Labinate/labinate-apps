Words to avoid on a resume

Certain words make a resume invisible. Not because they're wrong, but because every other applicant uses them too — so they carry no information and quietly blur you into the pile. The fix is never a thesaurus; it's replacing empty claims with specific facts. Here are the words and phrases to cut, why they weaken a bullet, and exactly what to write instead.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

Recruiters skim. In the few seconds a resume gets, filler words and buzzwords are pure friction — they take up line space, delay the fact that actually matters, and read like every other resume in the stack. The words below are the most common offenders, grouped by why they fail. In each case the fix is the same move: cut the claim, show the evidence.

1. Empty self-descriptions

These are adjectives you use about yourself that anyone could — and does — claim. They're unverifiable, so a recruiter reads right past them. The rule: never tell the reader you have a trait; show a bullet that proves it.

AvoidBecauseSay instead
Hard-workingEveryone claims it; proves nothingA bullet with a real result and a number
Team playerUniversal filler"Coordinated 4 departments to ship on deadline"
Detail-orientedSelf-assessment, not evidence"Reduced data-entry errors by 30%"
Motivated / drivenAdds no informationA promotion, an initiative you started
Results-orientedIronically, has no result in itThe actual result

If you want to convey that you're detail-oriented, the way to do it is a bullet that could only be written by someone detail-oriented — not the adjective itself. This is the single highest-leverage edit on most resumes, and it's closely tied to avoiding the most common resume mistakes.

2. Filler verbs that describe involvement, not results

These phrases tell the reader you were near the work without saying what you did. They're the biggest source of weak bullets because they open with a shrug instead of an achievement.

AvoidSay instead
Responsible for…The verb for what you did — Managed, Led, Owned
Helped with / assisted inName your specific contribution
Worked onBuilt, Designed, Shipped, Fixed
Involved inThe concrete role you played
Duties includedNothing — delete it and start with the verb
Weak

Responsible for helping with the company's social media accounts.

Strong

Grew the company's Instagram following from 2,000 to 15,000 in six months by launching a weekly content series.

Every bullet should open with a strong action verb and, wherever one honestly exists, carry a number. That's what turns "involved in" into evidence.

3. Overused buzzwords & clichés

These were once useful and are now noise from overuse. They signal that you reached for the nearest stock phrase rather than describing your own work.

  • Synergy, leverage, dynamic, go-getter, thought leader, ninja, rockstar, guru — corporate or gimmicky filler; describe the actual work plainly instead.
  • Think outside the box, hit the ground running, wear many hats, self-starter — dead metaphors a recruiter has read a thousand times.
  • Proven track record, best of breed, value-add, results-driven — claims that a specific number would make for you.

You don't need a fancier word — you need the plain one plus the fact. "Leveraged data to drive results" says nothing; "Used A/B testing to raise signup conversion 18%" says everything.

4. Vague intensifiers & unquantified claims

Words like various, several, numerous, significantly, substantially, many stand in for a number you should just give. "Managed various projects" is weaker than "Managed 12 concurrent projects." If you know the figure, use it; if you don't, estimate honestly. Precision reads as competence.

5. Pronouns, articles & the word "etc."

Resumes use an implied first person with the pronoun dropped. Cut I, me, my — start with the verb. Trim leading articles ("a," "the") where a bullet reads fine without them. And never end a list with etc. — it signals you ran out of things worth naming. List your best two or three and stop.

One test for every word: could the applicant right after you write the exact same thing? If yes, it's not describing you — it's filler. Replace it with something only your work could produce: a number, a named tool, a specific outcome. Do this once for the whole resume and it will already beat most of the stack.

Frequently asked questions

What words should you avoid on a resume?

Avoid empty self-descriptions everyone uses — hard-working, team player, detail-oriented, motivated, dynamic — and filler verbs like responsible for, helped with, worked on, and assisted in. Both describe involvement rather than results. Replace them with a concrete action verb plus a measurable outcome: not "responsible for social media" but "grew Instagram following from 2,000 to 15,000 in six months."

Why are buzzwords bad on a resume?

Because they're unverifiable and universal. "Team player" and "results-driven" tell the recruiter something rather than showing it, and every other applicant says the same — so the words carry no information. Specific facts, with a number, do the persuading that adjectives only claim.

What should I say instead of "responsible for"?

Use the action verb for what you actually did. "Responsible for managing the budget" becomes "Managed a $400K annual budget." Lead every bullet with a verb — built, led, cut, grew, launched — and let the result follow. "Responsible for" describes a job description; a verb describes an achievement.

Should I use "I" on a resume?

No. Resumes use an implied first person with the pronoun dropped — "Led a team of five," not "I led a team of five." Pronouns add words without adding information and break the clean rhythm of a bullet list. Start each bullet with the verb; the reader already knows it's about you.
