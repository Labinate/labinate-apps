How to put a career break on a resume

You stepped away on purpose — to care for a family member, recover your health, take a planned sabbatical, study, or travel — and now you're staring at the hole it left in your timeline, wondering whether to explain it or bury it. The answer is neither of the extremes people reach for. A deliberate career break is a line item, not a secret and not a confession. Name it, date it, show you stayed sharp, and move on. Here's how to do exactly that — without hiding it and without apologizing for it.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Career breaks have gone mainstream. Enough people have taken planned time away — and enough companies now run "returnship" programs to hire them back — that in 2022 LinkedIn added an official Career Break profile entry. The stigma is fading, but a lot of advice hasn't caught up, and it pushes you toward one of two mistakes: pretending the gap isn't there, or over-explaining it until a non-issue looks like a wound. This guide walks the line between them. The move is simple and confident: treat the break like a brief, honest entry on your work history, front-load your value so the reader meets your strengths first, and keep your story to one calm sentence.

The one bright line: name the break plainly, but never apologize for it and never fabricate work to fill it. Those are the two ditches. A resume that hides the break invites the worst assumptions; a resume that over-justifies it signals you think it's a problem; a resume that invents a fake "consulting" role converts an honest pause into a lie that unravels on the first date-check. The center line is a dated Career Break entry, one neutral line of context, real evidence of anything you did to stay engaged — and then straight back to your career.

First, know what this is not

A career break is easy to confuse with three neighboring situations. They share a hole in the timeline, but the right story is different for each — and picking the wrong frame is what makes people either hide the truth or apologize for it.

SituationHow it differs
A general employment gapThe broad case — any stretch without a job, often unplanned (a slow search, an illness). The task there is usually to minimize and briefly explain. A career break is the deliberate subset you can own as a decision, not smooth over as an accident.
A stay-at-home-parent resumeThat's the parenting-specific version of re-entry, with its own framing around raising children. A career break covers the wider set of planned pauses — caregiving for a parent, health recovery, a sabbatical, study, travel — including but not limited to parenting.
Explaining being laid offThat's an involuntary event — the job ended, not by your choice — and the frame is "it wasn't performance, here's the context." A career break is a choice you made and would stand behind, so you own it rather than explain it away.

The through-line: if the time off was something you chose and would defend, treat it as a career break and own it. If it was involuntary or a short search gap, use the employment-gap or laid-off frame instead. Same blank space, different — and always truthful — story.

Step 1: list the break as a dated line item

The single most effective move is to stop treating the break as an absence and start treating it as an entry. In your work history, right where it falls chronologically, add a short block:

  • Give it a title. "Career Break" or "Planned Career Break" is clean and self-explanatory. You can add the type in plain words — "Career Break — Full-Time Caregiving" or "Career Break — Planned Sabbatical."
  • Date it like a job. Month and year to month and year, matching the format of your other entries — see how to list dates. This is what closes the gap: the reader sees an accounted-for stretch, not a mystery.
  • Add one neutral line of context if you're comfortable — "caring for a family member," "health recovery," "extended travel and language study." Keep it factual; you owe no more detail than that.
  • Show anything you did to stay sharp — freelance or contract work, volunteering, a certification or course, a personal project, a skill you built. One or two lines, quantified where you can. If there's genuinely nothing, that's fine too; don't invent.

That's the whole entry. A break doesn't need the depth a real job gets — the point is to answer the obvious question ("what happened here?") on your terms, in four lines, and let the reader move on.

Step 2: front-load your value so the gap isn't the headline

Naming the break isn't the same as leading with it. You want a reader to meet your strengths before your timeline, so the break lands as a footnote to a strong candidate rather than the first thing they see.

  • Open with a strong summary. Two or three lines up top stating who you are, your best proof, and what you're targeting now — see summary examples. This sets the frame before the dates do.
  • Consider a combination or functional layout. Leading with a skills or accomplishments section, then the dated history below, puts your capability first. (Use this deliberately — a purely functional resume can itself look like it's hiding something, so the combination format is usually the safer choice.)
  • Keep the history honest underneath. Whatever layout you choose, the dated work history — including the break — still appears. Front-loading reorders emphasis; it never erases the timeline.

Step 3: keep LinkedIn and the interview consistent

Your resume isn't the only place the gap shows, and mismatched stories invite exactly the doubt you're trying to close.

  • Use LinkedIn's official Career Break entry. Since 2022 you can add a "Career Break" to your experience with a type (caregiving, health, travel, professional development, and more) and dates. It normalizes the pause and keeps your profile from showing a bare gap — and it should match your resume's dates. More on aligning the two: LinkedIn on your resume.
  • Prepare one calm interview sentence. What the break was, that it's over, and that you're ready — for example: "I took a planned year to care for a family member; that's resolved now, I've kept my skills current, and I'm excited to get back to it." Then pivot to why you fit the role.
  • Let the interviewer follow your lead. Candidates who treat a break as normal almost always find the interviewer treats it as normal too. Confidence and a forward pivot do more than any justification.

Step 4: update the rest of the resume for re-entry

Coming back after time away, the break gets all the attention — but the ordinary work of a strong, current resume matters just as much.

  • Refresh dates, tools, and titles so nothing reads as stale — a quick pass with how to update a resume.
  • Re-tailor to each role. Match your summary and skills to the posting; tailoring to the job description matters more when you're arguing you're ready to return.
  • Lead with recent proof. Any freelance, volunteering, or study during the break belongs near the top of its section — it's your evidence that you didn't go dormant.

Don't undo it — the two failure modes

There's a ditch on each side of this road.

Hiding or faking the break

  • Leaving a bare, unexplained gap in the dates
  • Stretching your last job's end date to cover it
  • Inventing a "consulting" or "freelance" role you didn't do
  • Using years-only dates to blur the timeline

Apologizing or over-explaining

  • A paragraph justifying why you took the time
  • An emotional or defensive tone ("unfortunately I had to…")
  • Framing a deliberate choice as a mistake
  • Volunteering private detail no employer needs

The center line is a resume that names the break plainly and stands behind it: a dated Career Break entry, one neutral line of context, real evidence of anything you did to stay engaged, and a summary that leads with your value. Honest because it's the truth, confident because the break was a choice — never hidden, never apologized for.

Quick reference

  • Name it, don't hide it → a bare gap invites the worst guess; a dated line answers it on your terms.
  • List it as an entry → title ("Career Break"), month/year dates, one neutral line, anything you did to stay sharp.
  • Front-load your value → strong summary and a combination format so strengths come before the timeline.
  • Stay consistent → use LinkedIn's official Career Break feature and match the dates.
  • One calm interview line → what it was, that it's over, that you're ready — then pivot.
  • Never apologize, never fabricate → the two ditches; the truth, stated confidently, beats both.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put a career break on a resume, or just hide it?

Name it. A deliberate career break — time you chose to step away for caregiving, health, a sabbatical, study, or travel — is best handled as an honest, dated line item rather than an unexplained hole. Hiding it almost always reads worse than the truth: a reader who sees your last job ending in 2023 and your next starting in 2025 will notice the gap regardless, and left to guess, they tend to imagine the worst (fired, unemployable, something you're concealing). A one-line entry that says "Career Break — caregiving; completed two online certifications" closes that question instantly and on your terms. The instinct to bury a break by stretching job dates or leaving years blank backfires, because it trades a normal, explainable pause for the appearance of dishonesty. The goal isn't to draw a spotlight to the break; it's to answer the obvious question before it becomes a doubt.

How do you list a career break on a resume?

Treat it like a short work-history entry. Give it a clear title — "Career Break" or "Planned Career Break" — with month-and-year dates just like a job, a brief neutral reason if you're comfortable ("full-time caregiving," "health recovery," "planned sabbatical"), and one or two lines on anything you did that kept you sharp: freelance or contract work, volunteering, courses or certifications, a personal project, or skills you built. Keep it factual and short; a break doesn't need the detail a job gets. If the break is recent, a strong summary at the top and a combination or functional layout can put your skills and best work first, so the reader meets your value before they meet the gap. What you're avoiding is a bare blank space that forces the reader to interpret it — the dated line removes the mystery without dwelling on it.

Is a career break different from an employment gap?

They overlap, but the framing is different, and getting it right changes how you write. An employment gap is the general case — any stretch without a job, often unplanned or involuntary (a layoff, a slow job search, an illness), where the task is usually to minimize and explain briefly. A career break is the specific, increasingly normalized case where you chose to step away for a defined reason and a defined stretch of time. Because it was deliberate, you can own it as a decision rather than an accident: name it, date it, and show what you gained. That's a stronger posture than the apologetic "sorry about the gap" framing. If your time off was a layoff you're bridging, or a short search gap, handle it as an employment gap instead; if it was a planned pause you'd stand behind, treat it as a career break. Same hole in the timeline, different story — and the right story is the one that's true.

What should you not do when explaining a career break?

Don't over-explain, and don't fabricate. Over-explaining turns a non-issue into a liability: a paragraph justifying your break, an emotional backstory, or a defensive tone signals that you think it's a problem, and the reader takes the cue. One neutral line is enough. The opposite failure is papering over the break with invented work — a fake "consulting" role, stretched dates on your last job, or a project you didn't really do. That converts an honest, explainable pause into a misrepresentation that unravels the moment someone checks a date or asks a follow-up question. Also avoid apologizing: "unfortunately I had to take time off" frames a legitimate choice as a mistake. The center line is a resume that states the break plainly, shows you stayed engaged where you genuinely did, and moves on — confident, brief, and truthful.

How do you handle a career break on LinkedIn and in the interview?

On LinkedIn, use the official Career Break feature: since 2022 you can add a "Career Break" entry to your experience with a type (caregiving, health, travel, professional development, and more) and dates, which normalizes it and keeps your profile from showing a suspicious gap. Keeping LinkedIn and your resume consistent matters — mismatched timelines invite questions. In the interview, prepare one calm sentence: what the break was, that it's over, and that you're ready and eager to be back — for example, "I took a planned year to care for a family member; that's resolved now, and I've been sharpening my skills and I'm excited to get back to the work." Deliver it without apology, then pivot to why you're a fit for the role. Interviewers rarely dwell on a break that the candidate treats as normal; they follow your lead. Confidence, brevity, and a forward pivot do more than any justification.
