How to write a cover letter for an internal position

You're applying for a promotion or a transfer where you already work — and you're about to make the most common mistake, which is to write the same letter you'd send a stranger. Don't. The person reading this already knows your work, and that single fact inverts the whole strategy. You don't have to prove you're competent; that's settled. Your job is to answer the one question the hiring manager is actually weighing: is this person ready to step up, or are they simply good where they are? Everything below is built to answer it.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

An internal candidate starts with an advantage no outsider can match — and a trap most people walk straight into. The advantage is that your track record is visible and your fit is proven. The trap is assuming that visibility does the work for you, so the letter either recaps a resume the reader already knows or leans on entitlement ("I've been here five years"). Neither makes the case. This guide turns your insider position into the argument: lead with what only an insider can offer, and prove you already operate at the level you're applying for.

Step 1: understand what actually changes

An internal letter isn't a lightly-edited external one — three things flip, and each changes what belongs on the page.

What changesExternal letter → Internal letter
The readerA stranger who needs convincing you're credible → a colleague who already knows your work, so basic credibility is settled.
The question"Can this person do the job?" → "Is this person ready to level up, or good at their current role and that's it?"
Your evidenceClaims a stranger must take on faith → results the reader can verify by asking one person down the hall.
Your edgeNone inherent — you start even → institutional knowledge, ramp speed, and proven culture fit no outsider can claim.
The riskBeing unknown → reading as entitled, or as "great where they are" and not a level up.

This is a different letter from a career-change cover letter, where you're a relative outsider translating transferable skills to strangers. Here you translate nothing — you leverage what everyone already knows and point it at the next level.

Step 2: lead with the advantage an outsider can never claim

Your institutional knowledge is the one thing an external candidate — however impressive — cannot bring on day one. Open the letter by naming it, because it's your strongest and most defensible argument.

  • You already know the company. The product, the customers, the systems, the internal politics, the roadmap — you're productive in week one, not month three.
  • You already know the people. The cross-functional relationships that take an outside hire months to build, you have today.
  • Your fit is proven, not projected. A hiring manager betting on an outsider is betting on culture fit; with you, it's a known quantity.
Turn it into a line, not a boast: "Because I already own the billing integration and work weekly with the finance team, I'd step into the Senior PM role without the usual ramp — and start on the Q3 migration immediately." That's institutional knowledge stated as concrete value, which is exactly how the reader hears it.

Step 3: prove you're ready for the next level, not good at this one

This is the heart of the letter. The hiring manager isn't unsure you can do your current job — they're deciding whether you can do a bigger one. So don't argue that you've excelled where you are; show that you've already been reaching above your title.

  • Cite scope you've taken beyond your grade. Led a cross-team project, mentored newer hires, owned a decision above your pay level, drove a result the target role would own.
  • Map each example to a requirement of the new role. Don't make the reader connect the dots — say "the new role owns X; last quarter I already did a version of X when…".
  • Frame it as recognition, not a request. The message is "I'm already doing pieces of this job — this move makes it official," which turns a bet on potential into acknowledgment of something happening.

Note the difference from showing a promotion on your resume: that's how you document a step up you've already earned. This letter is how you win the next one — you're building the case, not recording the result.

Step 4: use verifiable wins — don't recap your resume

Because the reader can check anything you claim, specificity is a superpower here that it never is externally. Vague self-praise falls flat; a concrete, checkable result lands with unusual force.

Resume recap — skip it

  • "I'm a hard-working team player with strong communication skills."
  • Re-listing every duty already on your resume
  • Generic claims the reader can't tie to anything

Verifiable win — do this

  • "I cut onboarding time 40% with the checklist the whole team now uses."
  • Name the project, the number, the stakeholder
  • Pick results the reader can confirm in one Slack message

Turn duties into outcomes the same way you would anywhere — see responsibilities vs. accomplishments and how to quantify achievements. The internal twist is only this: choose the wins your reader can personally vouch for, and name the people or teams who saw them.

Step 5: handle the internal politics with transparency

An internal move has a social dimension an external application never does. Navigate it openly — it protects your standing whether or not you get the role.

  • Loop in your current manager, ideally before you apply. Internal moves surface fast; a manager who hears it from you can become an advocate, while one who's blindsided can quietly become an obstacle. A short "I'm seeking more scope and I'm excited about this opening" is enough.
  • Follow the process. Some companies require manager sign-off or a minimum time in role — check before you submit, not after.
  • Stay gracious about your current team. Never frame the move as escaping a bad situation; frame it as growing toward a bigger one. Badmouthing your current role or manager travels fast internally and costs you.
  • Don't assume it's yours. "I've earned this" is the fastest way to read as entitled. You're making a case, not collecting a debt.

Step 6: the structure — tight and forward-looking

Keep it to half a page. You're not introducing yourself, so cut the throat-clearing and get to the argument. A clean three-part shape:

  • Open: name the specific role and that you're a current member of [team] — one warm, direct sentence. See how to start a cover letter; internally you can skip the "please consider my application" throat-clearing entirely.
  • Middle: two or three verifiable wins mapped to the new role's requirements, plus one line of institutional advantage. This is 70% of the letter.
  • Close: look forward — a sentence on what you'd tackle first, respect for the process, and a confident sign-off. See how to end a cover letter.

On length overall, an internal letter runs shorter than the norm in how long a cover letter should be — the reader knows you, so respect their time.

Don't undo it — the two failure modes

There's a ditch on each side of this road. Steer between them.

Too entitled

  • Leaning on tenure ("I've been here five years")
  • Treating the promotion as owed
  • Skipping the case because "they know me"

Too humble

  • Only proving you're good at your current job
  • Recapping your resume instead of arguing readiness
  • Under-claiming the above-grade work you've done

The center line is confidence backed by evidence: you've already been operating at the next level, here are the checkable results, and here's the institutional edge you'd bring on day one. That's neither entitled nor timid — it's the case, made.

Quick reference

  • The reader knows you → the question isn't "can you do it" but "are you ready to level up"; write to that.
  • Lead with institutional advantage → the ramp speed and proven fit an outsider can't claim.
  • Prove above-grade work → cite scope you've already taken beyond your title, mapped to the new role.
  • Use verifiable wins → name the project, number, and stakeholder; skip the resume recap.
  • Handle the politics openly → loop in your manager, follow process, stay gracious, don't assume it's yours.
  • Keep it short → half a page, forward-looking, no throat-clearing.

Frequently asked questions

Do you need a cover letter for an internal position?

Usually yes, and it matters more than people assume. Your internal reputation and resume already show what you've done at your current level — but neither of them makes the argument the hiring manager actually needs, which is that you're ready to operate one level up. That case is exactly what a cover letter is for. It's also where you convert your institutional knowledge into a stated advantage, address why you want to move rather than stay, and show you understand the new role. Even when a form doesn't strictly require one, attaching a short, specific letter signals that you take the move seriously — and gives a decision that may hinge on "is this person ready?" the evidence to say yes.

How is an internal cover letter different from a regular one?

The reader already knows you, and that single fact inverts the strategy. An external letter has to establish that a stranger is competent; an internal letter can skip that entirely and spend its space on the real question — whether you're ready for a step up. So you don't sell yourself from scratch or recap your resume. Instead you cite specific results the reader can verify, lead with the institutional advantage no outside candidate can claim (you know the company, the tools, the people, the roadmap), and prove you already operate above your current grade. You also have to handle a social reality an external letter never touches: you may know the hiring manager, and your move affects your current team.

Should you tell your current manager you're applying internally?

In most healthy organizations, yes — and often before you apply, not after. Internal moves surface quickly, and a manager who hears it from someone else can feel blindsided; one you loop in early can become an advocate and a reference. You don't have to over-explain: a brief, professional conversation that you're seeking more scope and are excited about a specific opening is enough. Check your company's process, too — some require manager sign-off or a minimum time in role. Whatever the norm, don't route around people or keep it secret in a way that reads as going behind their back; transparency is both the professional move and the one that protects your standing if you don't get the role.

How long should an internal cover letter be?

Short — half a page to three tight paragraphs, shorter than you'd write for an external role. You're not introducing yourself or establishing basic credibility, so you can cut all the throat-clearing and get straight to the argument: the specific role, two or three verifiable wins that map to it, your institutional edge, and a forward-looking close. Because the reader knows you and is busy, a focused half-page reads as respectful and confident; a long letter that recaps your whole tenure reads as if you don't trust them to remember your work. Say the new thing — that you're ready for this level — and stop.

How do you prove you're ready for a role above your current one?

Show that you already do the next job in pieces. The most convincing evidence isn't a claim of readiness — it's a record of having reached beyond your title: leading a cross-team project, mentoring newer teammates, owning a decision above your pay grade, or driving a result that a person in the target role would own. Name two or three of these specifically, with numbers or named outcomes the reader can check, and connect each to a requirement of the new role. That reframes the decision from a bet on potential to a recognition of something already happening. Pair it with your institutional advantage — you'll ramp faster than any outside hire — and the letter answers the only question that matters: is this a step up they're ready for, or are they simply good where they are?
