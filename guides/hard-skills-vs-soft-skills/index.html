Hard skills vs. soft skills on a resume

Every job posting asks for both, but they don't belong in the same place — or get treated the same way by the software and the recruiter. Hard skills get your resume found. Soft skills get you hired once a human is reading. Here's the real difference, dozens of examples of each, and exactly where to put each one so it actually counts.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The two words get used loosely, so start with a clean definition. Hard skills are specific, teachable, measurable abilities tied to doing the work — a programming language, a piece of equipment, a certification, a second language. You can prove them with a test, a credential, or a portfolio. Soft skills are personal and interpersonal traits that shape how you work — communication, leadership, adaptability, problem-solving. They can't be certified, and everyone claims them, which is exactly why listing them plainly does so little.

Hard skills examples

Hard skills are usually nouns — tools, systems, methods, credentials. The right ones depend entirely on the role; pull them from the job description and match only what you truly have.

Technical & data

  • Python, SQL, JavaScript
  • Excel / Google Sheets (pivot tables, VLOOKUP)
  • Data analysis, Tableau, Power BI
  • Cloud platforms (AWS, Azure)
  • SEO, Google Analytics, A/B testing

Business & trade

  • Financial modeling, bookkeeping (QuickBooks)
  • Project management, Agile / Scrum
  • Salesforce, HubSpot, CRM administration
  • Bilingual fluency (e.g. Spanish, Mandarin)
  • Welding, CNC machining, phlebotomy, CDL

Notice they're all things you can be tested on or certified in. That's the tell for a hard skill — and the reason they double as resume keywords the ATS and recruiters search for.

Soft skills examples

Soft skills describe how you operate. They're valuable, but as a plain list they're invisible — so treat these as things to demonstrate, not just declare.

Working with people

  • Communication (written & verbal)
  • Leadership & mentoring
  • Collaboration / teamwork
  • Conflict resolution
  • Customer empathy

Working with problems

  • Problem-solving & critical thinking
  • Adaptability & resilience
  • Time management & prioritization
  • Attention to detail
  • Ownership & initiative

Where each one goes on the page

This is the part most people get wrong. The instinct is to pile every skill, hard and soft, into one list. Don't — split them by what each is good for.

Hard skills
Put them in the skills section — and repeat them in bullets

Your dedicated skills section is a scannable, ATS-friendly home for 8–12 concrete hard skills matched to the job. Then reinforce the most important ones inside your experience: "Built the reporting dashboard in Tableau" proves the skill far better than the word sitting alone in a list.

Soft skills
Prove them in your experience bullets, not a list

Instead of writing "leadership," write the evidence: "Led a 5-person team through a platform migration, delivered two weeks early." Instead of "communication," show it: "Presented quarterly results to 40+ stakeholders." The soft skill is implied by the accomplishment — which is credible in a way the bare word never is. See how to write strong bullet points and how to quantify achievements.

The rule of thumb: if a skill can be tested or certified, it's a hard skill — put it in your skills section as a keyword. If it can only be observed, it's a soft skill — prove it with a result in your experience. Hard skills get you retrieved from the database; soft skills close the deal once a person is reading. You need both, in the right places. Not sure yours match the job? Check whether your resume is ATS-friendly first.

What recruiters actually want to see

  • Hard skills tailored to the posting. A generic skills list is filler; a list that mirrors the job's must-haves reads as a match. Tailor it per application.
  • Soft skills backed by evidence. Claims are free. A recruiter believes "adaptable" when they see you shipped through a reorg, not when you type the word.
  • No keyword stuffing. Listing skills you can't back up gets exposed in the interview. Only claim what you can discuss.
  • Balance for the role. Engineering and trades lean hard-skill-heavy; management and client-facing roles weight soft skills more. Read the posting and mirror its emphasis.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between hard skills and soft skills?

Hard skills are specific, teachable, measurable abilities tied to a job — Python, financial modeling, welding, Spanish fluency — and you can prove them with a certificate or portfolio. Soft skills are personal traits like communication, leadership, and adaptability that shape how you work and are shown through behavior and results. A strong resume needs both: hard skills get you found and shortlisted, soft skills get you hired.

Should I list soft skills on my resume?

List only a few, and don't fill your skills section with them. A bare list of "team player, hard-working, detail-oriented" means nothing because anyone can claim it. Prove soft skills inside your work bullets instead — show leadership by the team you led — and reserve the skills section mostly for hard skills the ATS can match.

Which skills matter more to an ATS?

Hard skills. An ATS and the recruiter searching it look for concrete terms — tools, technologies, certifications, languages — that map to the job. Soft-skill words like "motivated" are rarely search terms. Mirror the exact hard skills from the job description where you have them, in both your skills section and your bullets.

How many skills should I put on a resume?

Aim for about 8 to 12 in a dedicated skills section, weighted toward job-relevant hard skills. Tailor the list to each application rather than dumping everything — a focused, matched list reads as expertise; a 30-item wall reads as filler.
