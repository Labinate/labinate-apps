How to list an MBA on a resume

An MBA is one of the few lines on a resume that earns its own strategy. Most degrees just sit in the education section and do their quiet verification job. An MBA gets two real decisions the rest of your schooling never does: where it sits on the page, and whether the letters belong after your name. Get those two right and the same degree reads as a deliberate signal instead of a footnote.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The reason an MBA is different is that it's expensive, deliberate, and repositioning. Nobody stumbles into one — it usually means you paused a career or spent nights and weekends to change your trajectory, and recruiters read it that way. So the question isn't just "how do I format the entry" (though we'll cover that precisely); it's "what am I asking this degree to do for this specific application?" A career-changer uses the MBA to say I belong in this new field. An experienced operator uses it as one more credential behind a track record. Those two goals call for two different layouts, and copying the wrong one wastes the most expensive line on your page.

The core idea: decide what the MBA is for before you place it. If it's your bridge into a new field, lift education near the top so it's read before your older experience. If your work history is the headline, keep the normal order — experience first, a tight education section below with the MBA on top of it. Placement is a strategy call, not a default.

Decision one: where the MBA goes

This is the choice that changes the whole read of your resume, and it comes down to one question — which is your stronger current credential, the degree or the track record?

Lead with education

  • You're a career changer and the MBA is the bridge into the new field
  • You're early-career or a recent full-time MBA grad
  • The school or degree is your single most impressive line
  • → Move education just under your summary, above experience

Keep education below experience

  • You have years of relevant results the MBA supports rather than replaces
  • You're a senior operator, manager, or executive
  • Your work history is the reason you'd get the interview
  • → Normal order: experience first, a compact education section under it

In both cases the MBA sits above your undergraduate degree, because education is listed in reverse-chronological order. The trap is placing on autopilot: a fresh MBA who buries a top-program degree at the bottom of the page throws away their best asset, while a VP with fifteen years of wins who opens with "Education" reads as if they have nothing more recent to lead with. Ask what earns this interview, and put that first. (More on the mechanics of the section: how to list education, and if the degree is a pivot, the career-change resume.)

Decision two: "MBA" after your name?

The instinct to write Jordan Lee, MBA at the top of the page is understandable — you earned it — but whether it helps depends entirely on your field. A degree is not a professional license the way RN, CPA, or PE is; those are earned rights with legal meaning, and appending them is standard. A master's degree is expected to live in the education section, so post-nominals for it are a style choice, not a convention.

Post-nominal "MBA" reads fineIt reads as trying too hard
Management consulting, investment banking, corporate strategy, private equity — credential-driven fields where "MBA" is a recognized ticketEngineering, design, most tech IC roles, creative fields, startups — where a degree after the name signals status over substance
Executive and board-level resumes where the letters are part of the professional identityAny role where the letters would be the flashiest thing about your header
Matching your LinkedIn, where it's more accepted as a searchable tagStacked with other letters into Jordan Lee, MBA, PMP, Six Sigma alphabet soup

The safe default when you're unsure: leave it off your name and let the education section carry it. You never lose credit for an MBA that's clearly listed under education — the recruiter sees it either way — but you can lose a sliver of credibility for post-nominals that read as signaling in a field that doesn't use them. Pick one placement (name or education, not both prominently) and move on.

Formatting the entry itself

Once placement is settled, the entry is simple and should be consistent with how you list every other credential. The full form spells out the degree; the short form uses the initialism. Pick one style and use it throughout.

The complete, done degree

Master of Business Administration (MBA), Concentration in Finance
The Wharton School, University of Pennsylvania — 2025
Add the concentration only when it's relevant to the job; a general-management MBA needs no concentration line.

Honors — better signal than GPA

A short honors line ages far better than a number: Graduated with distinction · Beta Gamma Sigma, a named fellowship, or a case-competition win. These are worth one line and stay credible for years, where a graduate GPA quickly starts to look like you have nothing more recent to point to.

GPA — only when it's strong and you're early

Include it only if it's roughly 3.5+ and you're a recent grad or early-career, where it's real evidence. Drop it entirely once you have a few years of post-MBA results — at that point your work speaks louder than a school metric. (The general rule: GPA on a resume.)

Relevant coursework — for pivots only

If the MBA is repositioning you and specific classes map to the target job, a tight relevant-coursework line can carry keywords an experienced hire wouldn't need. Skip it if your experience already proves the skill.

The in-progress MBA

An MBA you haven't finished — most often a part-time or executive MBA you're doing while working full time — is a genuine asset, not something to hide until it's conferred. It signals ambition and current, relevant coursework, and plenty of strong candidates list one. The entire rule is the label:

Honest and clear

  • MBA, Expected May 2027
  • MBA Candidate — [School], expected 2027
  • Optionally a concentration + one coursework line if it maps to the job

A claim you can't back

  • MBA with no qualifier — asserts a conferred degree you don't hold
  • Listing it in a way that implies you've graduated
  • Omitting the expected date so the reader assumes it's done

Whether to distinguish an executive or online MBA from a full-time one: usually you don't need to — "MBA" from an accredited program is an MBA, and an EMBA in particular is a mark of seniority, not a lesser degree. Note "Executive MBA" only if it adds context (it explains doing the degree alongside a senior role); never dress a part-time program up as a full-time one, but never apologize for it either.

What to do with your undergrad

Keep it — but trim it. Your bachelor's stays in the education section below the MBA (reverse-chronological), because it completes your academic record and background checks expect to see it. What changes is the detail: once the MBA is your headline credential, the undergrad usually sheds its GPA, coursework, and activities and shrinks to one clean line — degree, major, school, year.

  • Keep undergrad detail when the field itself sells the job — an engineering or CS bachelor's paired with the MBA for a technical product or strategy role tells a two-sided story worth showing.
  • Keep it while you're early-career, when a strong undergrad record still adds real weight.
  • Otherwise trim to a line and let the MBA lead. (If you're weighing which credential earns the top slot at all, see how much a degree carries once experience piles up.)
The bright line — never claim a degree you didn't earn. The one unforgivable move is writing "MBA" for a program you started and didn't finish, or coursework that never became a conferred degree. Degree verification is a routine part of hiring, and the registrar confirms exactly what the school granted and when — a gap between your resume and that record is a firing-offense misrepresentation, not a rounding error. Unfinished study has an honest home: "MBA Candidate, expected 2027," or, if you've stopped, a line under relevant coursework or an incomplete-degree entry. Real credentials, stated exactly.

Quick reference

  • Placement is strategy → education near the top when the MBA is your pivot; below experience when your track record leads. Always above your undergrad.
  • "MBA" after your name is field-specific → fine in consulting and finance, try-hard most everywhere else; when unsure, leave it off and let education carry it.
  • Concentration only when relevant → and honors beat GPA, which you include only if strong and early-career.
  • In-progress = label it → "Expected 2027" or "MBA Candidate," never a bare "MBA."
  • Keep the undergrad, trim it → one line, unless the field itself sells the job.
  • Never claim an unearned degree → verification confirms exactly what was conferred.

With the MBA placed, tighten the rest: lay out the full education section, decide on GPA, add relevant certifications and coursework, and if the degree is a pivot, build the whole thing as a career-change resume. Frequently asked questions

Where do you put an MBA on a resume?

It depends on which is your stronger current credential. If the MBA is a pivot — you're changing fields or industries and the degree is the bridge — move your education section near the top, just under your summary, so the reader sees the MBA before they weigh your older experience. If your work history is your headline (you're an experienced professional and the MBA supports rather than redefines you), keep a normal layout: experience first, a compact education section below it with the MBA at the top of that section. Either way the MBA sits above your undergraduate degree in reverse-chronological order. The mistake is defaulting: an early-career MBA who buries a Wharton degree at the bottom wastes their best asset, and a VP with fifteen years of results who leads with school reads as thin. Match the placement to what actually makes your case for the specific job.

Should you put "MBA" after your name on a resume?

Only where the credential is genuinely valued, and even then sparingly. In consulting, finance, corporate strategy, and some executive tracks, "Jordan Lee, MBA" at the top of the resume and on LinkedIn is normal and can help. In most other fields it reads as trying too hard, because a degree isn't a professional license the way "RN", "CPA", or "PE" is — those you earn the right to append and they're legally meaningful, whereas a master's degree is expected to live in the education section. A safe rule: if you're unsure, leave it off your name and let the education section carry it. You never lose credit for an MBA that's clearly listed under education; you can lose a little credibility for post-nominals that read as status-signaling in a field that doesn't use them. Never stack it with other letters into an alphabet soup after your name.

How do you list an MBA that's in progress?

List it honestly as unfinished, and make the expected date visible. Write it as "Master of Business Administration (MBA), Expected May 2027" or "MBA Candidate, [School], expected 2027" — never just "MBA", which claims a conferred degree you don't hold yet. An in-progress MBA, especially a part-time or executive one you're doing while working full time, is a genuine asset: it signals ambition and current, relevant coursework, and it's completely legitimate to feature. You can add a concentration and a line of relevant coursework if the classes map to the target job. The one unbreakable rule is the label: "expected" or "candidate" must appear, because the moment you write "MBA" with no qualifier you've asserted a completed degree, and degree verification during hiring will catch the gap between what you claimed and what the registrar confirms.

Do you include your MBA concentration and GPA?

Include the concentration when it's relevant to the job — "MBA, Concentration in Finance" helps for a finance role and is noise for an unrelated one — and skip it otherwise; a general-management MBA needs no concentration line at all. On GPA, the same logic as any degree applies but skewed higher: include it only if it's strong (roughly 3.5 or above on a 4.0 scale) and you're early-career or a recent graduate, where it's real evidence. Drop it entirely once you have a few years of post-MBA results, because at that point your work speaks louder than a school metric and listing a graduate GPA starts to look like you have nothing more recent to point to. Honors are better signal than GPA anyway: "with distinction", "Beta Gamma Sigma", a named fellowship, or a case-competition win are worth a short line and age more gracefully than a number.

Should you still list your undergraduate degree after earning an MBA?

Yes — keep it, but trim it. Your undergraduate degree stays in the education section below the MBA in reverse-chronological order, because it confirms your full academic record and background checks expect to see it. What changes is the detail: once the MBA is your headline credential, your bachelor's usually drops its GPA, coursework, and activities and shrinks to a single clean line — degree, major, school, and year. Two exceptions to keep undergrad detail: when the undergraduate field itself is a selling point for the job (an engineering bachelor's for a technical product role, plus the MBA for the business side), or when you're early enough in your career that a strong undergrad record still adds weight. Otherwise, let the MBA lead and let the bachelor's quietly do its verification job in one line.
