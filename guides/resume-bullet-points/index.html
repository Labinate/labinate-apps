How to write resume bullet points

Almost everything a recruiter actually reads on your resume is a bullet point. Get these right and the rest of the document takes care of itself. Here's a simple formula, how many to use, and a pile of before-and-after examples.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Recruiters don't read resumes — they skim them, in seconds, and what their eye lands on is the bullet points under each job. That means your bullets are doing nearly all the persuading. The difference between a resume that gets a callback and one that gets skipped usually isn't the jobs you held; it's how you described what you did in them.

The formula: verb → what → result

Every strong bullet is a version of the same shape. Start with a specific past-tense verb, say what you did, and — as often as you can — finish with the result. Skip the leading "I," skip "responsible for," and lead with the action.

Responsible for managing the company's social media accounts
Grew the company's Instagram from 4k to 22k followers in a year through a weekly content plan
Helped reduce customer complaints
Cut repeat support tickets 30% by rewriting the top 10 help articles
In charge of onboarding new employees
Onboarded 25+ new hires, building a checklist that cut ramp-up time from 3 weeks to 2

None of these invent anything — they surface an action and an outcome that were already true. For the verb slot, our 185 action verbs gives you strong openers grouped by what you did; for the number slot, how to quantify achievements shows where metrics hide.

Duty vs outcome — the one shift that matters

Most weak bullets describe a duty — the thing you were assigned. Strong bullets describe an outcome — what changed because you did it. Same job, completely different signal.

Duty (what you were told to do)

"Answered customer emails." Fine, but it says nothing you couldn't guess from the job title.

Outcome (what your work produced)

"Handled 60+ customer emails a day at a 96% satisfaction rating." Now the reader knows your scale and your quality.

You won't have a dramatic outcome for every line, and that's fine — but the first bullet under each job should almost always be an outcome, because it's the one most likely to be read.

How many bullets, and in what order

  • 3–5 bullets for recent, relevant roles; 2–3 for older or less relevant ones.
  • Best first. Lead each job with your single strongest accomplishment. Don't bury it at the bottom.
  • One line each, ideally. A bullet that wraps to three lines is a paragraph in disguise — tighten it.
  • Consistent tense. Past tense for previous jobs, present tense for your current one.

Openers to retire

These phrases start a bullet with a shrug. Cut them and lead with the verb instead.

  • "Responsible for…" → just state what you did.
  • "Helped with / assisted in…" → name your actual contribution.
  • "Tasked with / duties included…" → describe the outcome, not the assignment.
  • "Worked on…" → say what you built, fixed, or moved.
The bullet audit. Read each bullet and ask three questions: Does it start with a strong verb? Does it describe an outcome, not just a duty? Could a number make it more concrete? Rewrite the two weakest bullets in each job first — that's where the biggest gain is. A few sharp, results-first lines beat a dozen vague ones every time.

Frequently asked questions

How many bullet points should each job have?

Three to five for recent, relevant roles; two or three for older ones. Beyond about five, recruiters stop reading and your strongest points get diluted. Lead each job with your best accomplishment — the first bullet is the one most likely to be read.

Should resume bullet points be full sentences?

No. Drop the leading "I" and write tight fragments that start with a strong past-tense verb: "Rebuilt the onboarding flow, cutting drop-off 30%." Full sentences waste space and read slower. Keep tense consistent — past for previous roles, present for your current one.

What makes a resume bullet point strong?

It leads with a specific verb, describes what you did, and ends with a result or number where possible. Weak bullets describe duties; strong ones describe outcomes. That shift from duty to outcome is what separates a forgettable resume from a memorable one.

Do all bullet points need a number?

No — aim for one or two quantified bullets per role, ideally the first. A resume where every line strains for a metric reads as forced. Let the rest describe scope and responsibility in plain, active language.
