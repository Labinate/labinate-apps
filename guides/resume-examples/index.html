Resume examples that actually work

A good example is the fastest way to see what “good” looks like — the order of sections, how a strong bullet reads, how much to say. The trap is copying one so closely that your resume reads like everyone else’s. Here’s how to use examples to learn the shape without losing your own story.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Searching “resume examples” is the right instinct: seeing a finished, working resume teaches you more in two minutes than a list of rules. But most example galleries hand you a pretty template and hope you’ll paste your details into it — which is how thousands of near-identical resumes end up in the same pile. Use examples for the pattern, and write the content from your own real work.

What a good example actually shows you

Look past the styling. The useful signal in any strong example is in four places:

Structure and order

Contact header, then a short summary, then experience (most recent first), then skills and education. A good example makes the reading order obvious — see the full breakdown in how to write a resume.

How a bullet is phrased

Strong bullets lead with an action verb and end with a result: “Cut onboarding time 40% by rebuilding the setup flow.” Notice they describe impact, not duties — the pattern behind good bullet points and quantified achievements.

Restraint

The best examples say less than you’d expect. One page for most people, wide margins, no wall of text. That’s a choice you can copy directly — see how long a resume should be.

A clean, machine-readable layout

Notice what a good example doesn’t do: no photo, no two-column table, no text in graphics. Those look nice but break the software that reads your resume first — the ATS checklist explains why.

How to use an example without sounding like it

  • Copy the skeleton, not the sentences. Borrow the section order and the bullet shape (verb + what + result). Never keep the example’s wording — a recruiter who has read a hundred resumes recognises a template phrase instantly.
  • Match the example to your situation, not just your job title. A career-changer needs a different shape from a 15-year veteran. Pick an example that mirrors your stage — see career-change and no-experience guides.
  • Swap in real numbers. The example’s “increased sales 30%” is a placeholder. Yours has to be true and yours. If you don’t know your numbers, here’s how to find them.
  • Then tailor to the job. An example is generic by definition. The version you send should answer one specific posting — how to tailor a resume.

Worked snippets by stage

First job / student

Summary: “Recent CS graduate who shipped three full-stack class projects and led a 4-person capstone team. Looking to start as a junior developer.” Bullet: “Built and deployed a course-scheduling app used by 60 classmates, cutting sign-up time from days to minutes.”

Mid-career

Summary: “Marketing manager with 6 years growing B2B pipeline through content and paid search.” Bullet: “Grew organic leads 3× in 18 months by rebuilding the content strategy around buyer-intent keywords.”

Career changer

Summary: “Operations lead moving into data analytics, with a completed certificate and two shipped SQL dashboards.” Bullet: “Automated weekly reporting in SQL, saving the ops team ~5 hours a week — the project that led me into analytics.”

Notice the pattern across all three: level, then specialty, then proof. That’s the formula behind every good summary — copy the formula, fill it with your truth.

The one rule. Examples are for learning the shape, never for borrowing the words. Take the structure, the bullet pattern, and the restraint — then write every line from your own real work and tailor it to the job in front of you.

Frequently asked questions

Where can I find good resume examples?

You’ll find them across career sites, but the more useful move is to study what makes an example good — a clear section order, bullets that lead with a verb and end with a result, restraint (usually one page), and a clean single-column layout with no photo or tables. Use an example for that pattern, then write your own content, since a resume copied word-for-word reads as generic to recruiters.

Can I copy a resume example?

Copy the structure and the bullet shape — action verb, what you did, the result — but never the actual sentences. Recruiters read hundreds of resumes and recognise template phrasing instantly, and a copied example won’t reflect your real work or match the specific job. Borrow the skeleton, fill it with your own true numbers, then tailor it to the posting.

What does a good resume example look like?

A contact header, a short summary, experience in reverse-chronological order with impact-focused bullets, then skills and education — usually on one page, with generous margins and a single-column layout. Crucially it avoids photos, two-column tables, and text inside graphics, all of which break the applicant tracking systems that read your resume before a human does.

How do I make my resume different from the example?

Keep only the structure and the bullet pattern from the example, then replace every line with your own real experience and true numbers. Choose an example that matches your career stage rather than just your job title, and finish by tailoring the resume to one specific job posting — that combination is what makes it read as yours, not a template.
