How to list a double major on a resume

A double major is a genuine advantage — proof of range across two fields — but it's easy to format in a way that either undersells it or, worse, quietly inflates it into something you didn't earn. The whole thing turns on one fact: a double major is one degree with two fields, not two degrees. Get that right, lead with the field the job wants, and it lands exactly as it should.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

Most of the education section is mechanical — degree, school, year. A double major adds one wrinkle: you have two fields of study to name, and how you name them decides whether the entry reads as impressive breadth or as a credential stretched to look like more than it is. It's a smaller, cleaner problem than it feels like. We'll settle what a double major actually is (and how it differs from a dual degree and a minor), then the one decision that's fully yours — which major leads — then the clean one-line format and how to handle two GPAs honestly.

The core idea: a double major is a single degree covering two majors — one diploma, one graduation date. It goes on one education line with both fields named, and the field most relevant to the job comes first. Splitting it into two entries claims two degrees you don't have; that's the one mistake to avoid.

First: a double major is one degree, not two

The mistake that undoes everything is treating a double major like two degrees. It isn't. You completed the requirements for one degree and, within it, the coursework for two majors — the school printed one diploma with both fields on it. Three credentials live near each other here, and they're formatted differently:

CredentialWhat it actually is — and how it's listed
Double majorOne degree, two fields. One diploma, one graduation date. Listed on one line: "BA in Economics and Political Science."
Dual / double degreeTwo separate degrees (often a BA and a BS, sometimes from two schools). Two diplomas. Listed on two lines, one per degree.
Major + minorOne major plus a smaller, subordinate concentration. The minor is listed after the major, not as a co-equal field.

The double-major-versus-dual-degree line is the one you can never blur, because it's factual and checkable against your transcript. Listing a double major as two degrees inflates the credential; listing a genuine dual degree as one understates it. If your school issued one diploma, it's one entry — full stop. If it issued two, it's two.

The one decision that's yours: which major leads

Because a double major is a single degree, the order of the two fields is entirely your choice — and reordering them misrepresents nothing. So use it. Put the major most relevant to the job you're applying for first, because the reader scans the first field they see.

Lead with what the job wants

  • Data role, majored in Statistics + History → "BA in Statistics and History"
  • Museum role, same degree → "BA in History and Statistics"
  • Same tailoring logic you apply everywhere else on the page
  • → Surface the most relevant evidence first

Where the line actually is

  • You can't rename a major to fit the job
  • You can't drop one major to fake a single-focus degree
  • You can't promote a minor to a major
  • → Order is flexible; the facts are fixed

This is the same move you make when you tailor a resume to the job description: the facts stay constant, the emphasis follows the reader. Reordering two majors is one of the few places you get that flexibility for free.

The anatomy of a clean double-major entry

Once you know the order, the line itself is simple and consistent. Name the degree once, join the two majors, then school and year.

The standard one-line form

Bachelor of Arts in Economics and Political Science — University of Michigan, 2025
One degree, both majors, one school, one year. This is the complete, honest form. Note the single "Bachelor of Arts" — you don't repeat the degree for each field.

When the two fields are different degree types

If one major led to a BA and the other to a BS, that's usually a dual degree, not a double major — and it takes two lines, one per degree. Check your diploma: one diploma means one line; two diplomas mean two.

Adding a minor or honors

A minor comes after both majors: "BA in Economics and Political Science, minor in Statistics." Honors like cum laude attach to the degree, not to one major: "…Political Science, magna cum laude, 2025."

Still in school?

List an in-progress double major honestly, exactly like any unfinished degree: "BA in Economics and Political Science (expected 2027)." Name both majors and the expected date — never imply it's already conferred.

Two majors, two GPAs — how to be honest about it

A double major sometimes means two very different major-specific GPAs. The rule is the same one that governs every GPA question: report exactly what's true, clearly labeled.

Honest and clear

  • Lead with the overall (cumulative) degree GPA if it's strong
  • Add a major GPA only if labeled: "Economics major GPA 3.8"
  • If overall is weak but a relevant major is strong, label the major GPA

A number the transcript contradicts

  • Presenting the flattering major GPA as your overall GPA
  • A bare "3.8" that implies cumulative when it isn't
  • Averaging or inventing a number that's on no transcript

Cherry-picking one major's GPA and passing it off as your cumulative figure is a misstatement a transcript check exposes in seconds. Labeling it — "Economics major GPA 3.8" — is the honest way to show a strong, relevant number even when the overall is only average. When in doubt, follow the general GPA-on-a-resume rule and state the true, labeled figure.

Quick reference

  • One degree, not two — a double major is a single degree with two fields. List it on one education line, both majors named.
  • Double major ≠ dual degree → a dual degree is two separate diplomas and takes two lines. Your diploma count settles which you earned.
  • Lead with the relevant major → order is fully your choice; put the field the job cares about first.
  • Entry format → Bachelor of Arts in Economics and Political Science — University, Year. Degree named once; minor and honors after.
  • GPA → lead with the overall degree GPA; cite a major GPA only when clearly labeled, never disguised as cumulative.
  • Don't pad → never split a double major into two degree entries; it claims a credential your transcript contradicts.

With the double major placed, tighten the rest of the page: format the full education section at the right depth, decide whether a minor earns a mention, place any relevant coursework that maps to the job, and turn both fields of study into quantified achievements where you can. Frequently asked questions

How do you list a double major on a resume?

List it as one degree with both fields named, on a single education line — never as two separate degrees. The clean form is "Bachelor of Arts in Economics and Political Science, University of Michigan, 2025." Because a double major is one degree with one diploma and one graduation date, splitting it into two entries misrepresents your credential and looks like padding. The one choice that's fully yours is order: put the major most relevant to the job first, since the reader scans the first field they see. If the two fields sit under different degree types — say one leads to a BA and one to a BS — that's usually a dual degree, not a double major, and is listed differently. Keep it to one line in the education section, lead with the field the role cares about, and let the second major read as added breadth.

What's the difference between a double major and a dual degree?

A double major is one degree that covers two fields of study — you graduate with a single diploma (for example a BA) that names both majors, one graduation date, one set of degree requirements plus the coursework for each major. A dual degree (also called a double degree or joint degree) is two separate degrees — often a BA and a BS, sometimes from two different schools or colleges within a university — each with its own diploma and its own requirements, usually earned over more time. On a resume the distinction is factual, not stylistic: a double major goes on one line ("BA in Economics and Political Science"), while a dual degree goes on two lines, one per degree ("BS in Computer Science" and "BA in Economics"). Listing a double major as two degrees inflates the credential; listing a dual degree as one understates it. Your transcript and diploma settle which you actually earned, so match the resume to the record.

Which major should come first on a resume?

Put the major most relevant to the job you're applying for first. Because a double major is a single degree, the order of the two fields is entirely your choice and changing it misrepresents nothing — so use it strategically. If you're applying for a data role and majored in Statistics and History, lead with Statistics: "Bachelor of Arts in Statistics and History." Applying to a museum with the same degree, lead with History. This is the same tailoring logic you apply to the rest of the resume when you match it to the job description: surface the most relevant evidence first. The only limit is honesty — you can reorder the two majors freely, but you can't rename a major, drop one to imply a single-focus degree when a job wants that, or promote a minor to a major. Order is flexible; the facts are not.

Do you need two separate education entries for a double major?

No — a double major is one degree and belongs in one education entry. Write the degree once and name both majors within it: "Bachelor of Science in Biology and Chemistry, Ohio State University, 2024." Creating two separate lines — one "BS in Biology," one "BS in Chemistry" — falsely implies you earned two degrees, which a hiring manager or background check can contradict against your transcript. The exception is a genuine dual degree, where you really did earn two distinct degrees; that legitimately takes two entries. If your school printed one diploma, it's one entry. Keeping it on a single line also reads cleaner and keeps your education section from looking padded, which matters because reviewers are quick to notice a credential stretched to look like more than it is.

How do you list a double major with two different GPAs?

Default to the overall (cumulative) degree GPA if it's strong — that's the number employers expect and the one your transcript reports as your GPA. If your two majors have noticeably different major-specific GPAs and the higher one is in the field the job cares about, you may add it, but you must label it precisely so it can't be mistaken for the overall figure: "Economics major GPA 3.8" alongside or instead of the cumulative number, never a bare "3.8" implying it's your overall GPA. What you can't do is cherry-pick the flattering major GPA and present it as your cumulative GPA — that's a misstatement a transcript check exposes. If your overall GPA is weak but one major's GPA is genuinely strong and relevant, labeling the major GPA is the honest way to show it. When in doubt, follow the same rule that governs every GPA question: report exactly what's true, clearly labeled.
