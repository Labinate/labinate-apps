23 resume tips that actually get you interviews

Most resume advice is noise. These are the tips that change outcomes — grouped by what you're doing: writing the content, passing the applicant tracking system, tailoring to the job, and the final check before you send. Each one links to a deeper how-to if you want to go further.

Updated July 2026 · ~8 min read · by Labinate

A resume gets two reads: a machine skims it for keywords and structure, then a human skims it for evidence — usually in well under a minute. Almost every good tip is really about surviving one of those two skims. Work through the list once and your resume will read cleanly to both.

Content: the words recruiters actually read

Lead with results, not duties. "Responsible for managing social media" says nothing. "Grew Instagram from 4k to 30k followers in 8 months" says everything. Turn each line into an outcome — the method is in how to write bullet points.
Put a number on it. Percentages, dollars, counts, and time saved make claims believable and skimmable. You have more numbers than you think — here's how to find them.
Start every bullet with a strong verb. "Led," "built," "cut," "launched" — not "helped with" or "worked on." Pull from this list of 185 action verbs.
Write a 2–3 line summary, not an objective. Open with a short professional summary of who you are and what you deliver. See the difference in objective vs summary and copy from 15 summary examples.
Cut the buzzwords. "Hardworking team player with a passion for excellence" is filler every recruiter skips. Replace adjectives about yourself with evidence of what you did.
List the right skills, and cut the rest. Mirror the hard skills the posting names; drop generic soft skills that everyone claims. What to keep is in skills to put on a resume.
Know what belongs — and what doesn't. Contact, summary, experience, education, skills. Leave off photos, age, marital status, and "references available on request." Full list: what to put on a resume.

Formatting: pass the applicant tracking system first

Use a single-column layout. Two-column designs with sidebars and icons look modern but frequently scramble when software parses them. Simple wins. Here's why most templates backfire.
Use standard section headings. "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills" — not "Where I've Made an Impact." Parsers look for the expected labels. The full rulebook is the ATS checklist.
Pick a clean, standard font. A common sans-serif at 10–12pt for body text. Details in best resume font and size.
Keep it to one page (usually). One page under ~10 years of experience; two only if a long career genuinely fills it. The rule: how long should a resume be.
Use reverse-chronological order. Most recent job first — it's what recruiters and parsers both expect. When to break from it: the resume format guide.
Skip tables, text boxes, and images. They hold your content hostage from the parser. Use real text and simple bullets. Not sure yours is safe? Run the ATS resume checker.
Export a text-based PDF. A PDF preserves your layout everywhere — unless the posting explicitly asks for Word. The trade-off: PDF or Word.

Tailoring: stop sending the same resume everywhere

Tailor to each posting. The single highest-leverage move: match your summary and top bullets to the specific job. The 6-step method is in how to tailor your resume.
Mirror the job's keywords. Use the same terms the listing uses for tools and skills — without stuffing. How to find them: resume keywords.
Put the most relevant experience up top. Reorder bullets within a job so the ones matching this role come first. The recruiter reads top-down and rarely reaches the bottom.
Address the obvious question head-on. Changing fields or explaining a gap? Frame it, don't hide it — see career-change resumes and employment gaps.
Send a matching cover letter when asked. A short, tailored letter reinforces the fit. Start from how to write one or a fill-in template.

Before you send: the final check

Proofread out loud, twice. Typos in a document about your attention to detail are the easiest reason to get cut. Read it aloud — you'll catch what your eyes skip.
Run it against the mistakes list. A fast pass over the 15 most common resume mistakes catches the errors nearly everyone makes.
Name the file professionally. Jordan-Lee-Resume.pdf, not resume-final-v3.pdf. It's the first thing the recruiter sees in their inbox.
Get the email itself right. The subject line, the short body, and which file to attach all matter — how to email a resume covers the send.
If you only remember one thing: a resume is evidence, not a list of duties. Every line should answer "so what?" with a result — ideally a number. Do that, keep the layout ATS-simple, and tailor it to the job, and you're ahead of most of the pile.

Frequently asked questions

What is the single most important resume tip?

Tailor the resume to the specific job. A generic resume sent to every posting loses to one that mirrors the language and priorities of the listing — match your summary and top bullets to the job before you send it. The method is in how to tailor your resume.

How do I make my resume stand out in 2026?

Not with design — with evidence. Replace duty statements with quantified results. Recruiters skim for numbers and outcomes, so a resume full of measurable wins stands out far more than one with colors and columns, which can also break applicant tracking systems.

How long should a resume be?

One page for most people, especially under about 10 years of experience; two pages for long or senior careers. What fills the space matters more than the count — see how long should a resume be.

Should I use a resume template?

Use a simple, single-column one — not a decorative multi-column design. Fancy templates look impressive but frequently scramble in a parser, which can filter you out. Here's why most templates backfire.
