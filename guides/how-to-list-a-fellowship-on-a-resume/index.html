How to list a fellowship on a resume

A fellowship is one of the hardest lines to place, because it can look like three different things at once — a job, an award, or a degree — and each wants a different section. The confusion clears the moment you answer one question. Was the fellowship a role you performed, or money and recognition you received? That single call decides whether it goes under experience or under honors, and everything else follows.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A fellowship is a competitive, funded appointment — and that hybrid nature is exactly what makes it awkward on a resume. It's not simply an award (a one-time honor), not a publication (written output), and not a degree (a credential), yet it can resemble all three. People either bury a research fellowship in an awards line where its real work disappears, or they inflate a funding award into a job it never was. The fix is a single decision, and this guide walks through how to make it, how to format each type, and how to keep the whole thing honest.

The core idea: a fellowship is a funded appointment, so the only decision that matters is work vs. recognition. If you performed a role — research, teaching, clinical, postdoc — it goes under experience, formatted like a job with quantified bullets. If it was primarily money and a prestigious name — Fulbright, Rhodes, a graduate stipend — it goes under honors as the name and year, and any real work it funded graduates up into experience or projects on its own merits.

First: was it a role, or a recognition?

Every good decision about a fellowship starts here. Ask one thing: would you describe it as "work I did" or "an award I won"? The answer picks the section, and getting it wrong either hides real accomplishments or overstates a stipend.

What the fellowship wasWhere it goes
A role — you had duties, a project, output (research, teaching, clinical, postdoc)Experience section, formatted like a job: title, institution, dates, quantified bullets.
Recognition — a competitive named honor or funding for your own study (Fulbright, Rhodes, a stipend)Honors & awards section: the fellowship name and year, one line.

Most fellowships are genuinely both — the name is the recognition, the project is the work. That's not a problem; it's permission to list the honor for its prestige and the work for its substance, in the two sections where each belongs.

When it was a role: format it like a job

A research, postdoctoral, teaching, or clinical fellowship was a position. Treat it as one, so a hiring manager who doesn't recognize the fellowship's name still sees that you held a role and delivered.

Lead with a title, not the brand

Research Fellow — Stanford Immunology Lab, 2022–2024. The title tells the reader it was a role; the institution grounds it. Anchor with the same structure any job entry uses — title, place, dates, then bullets.

Quantify the work, spend one clause on prestige

Don't write "held a prestigious fellowship." Write what came of it: "Led a 14-month study on X, producing two first-author papers and a dataset now used by three labs." The prestige of the name earns one clause; the results earn the rest.

Let publications and projects stand on their own

If the fellowship produced publications or a substantial project, those get their own lines — under publications or projects — rather than being crammed into the fellowship bullet. One appointment can feed several sections.

Translate it for a non-academic reader

Moving into industry? A fellowship reads as research and self-direction. Describe the transferable competence — project ownership, funding, mentoring, delivery — the way you'd describe any professional strength, not in field jargon a hiring manager won't parse.

When it was recognition: name the honor

A named prestige fellowship — Fulbright, Rhodes, Marshall, Gates, Knight-Hennessy — is recognition first. Its selectivity is the signal, and the name carries weight on its own, so it belongs in honors and awards as a single clean line.

The standard honor line

Fulbright Scholar, [country/program], 2024 — or Rhodes Scholarship, University of Oxford, 2023. Name, awarding body, year. You don't need to explain a Rhodes; the reader who knows it is already impressed, and the one who doesn't will look it up.

State selectivity when you can support it

If the fellowship's competitiveness isn't obvious from the name, a short qualifier helps — "1 of 40 selected nationally" — but only if it's accurate. Selectivity is a transferable signal that reads as capability regardless of field.

Let the funded work graduate

The honor is the name; the substance is what the money bought — a thesis, fieldwork, teaching, a research project. Name the fellowship under awards and put the concrete work in experience or projects as its own quantified bullet. Listing it in both places is correct when each entry says something different.

The honesty rules

Fellowships are easy to list honestly and easy to overstate, and every overstatement is checkable through a reference call or a public record. Stay on the right side of each:

Honest and precise

  • A role you performed listed as experience, with real bullets
  • A named honor as "[Name] Fellowship, year" under awards
  • Status stated exactly: awarded / incoming / current / completed
  • An offer you declined as "Offered [Name] (declined)", or simply omitted

A claim the record contradicts

  • Experience bullets for a fellowship you never started
  • A completed fellowship still reading as current
  • A funding stipend inflated into a job title with duties you didn't have
  • Overstated selectivity you can't back up

A fellowship, placed right, is one of the strongest lines on a page. It signals that a competitive body chose you and funded your work — capability and independence in one line. The only failure modes are hiding the work in an awards line where no one sees it, or dressing a stipend up as a role it wasn't. The same honesty logic that governs an unfinished degree governs this: state exactly what it was and where you stand with it right now.

Quick reference

  • One decision rules it → was it a role you performed (experience) or recognition you received (honors)? Answer that first.
  • Role-type → format like a job: Research Fellow, [institution], dates, plus quantified bullets. Title first, prestige second.
  • Honor-type → one clean line under awards: [Name] Fellowship, awarding body, year. The name carries the weight.
  • It's often both → name the honor under awards and let the funded work graduate into experience or projects.
  • Selectivity travels → a competitive named fellowship is worth keeping even when the subject is unrelated to the job.
  • Status is checkable → awarded, incoming, current, or completed — never list an unstarted or declined fellowship as work you did.

Frequently asked questions

Does a fellowship go under experience or awards on a resume?

It depends on what the fellowship actually was, and this is the only decision that matters. If it was a role you performed — a research fellowship, postdoctoral fellowship, teaching fellowship, or clinical fellowship where you had responsibilities, produced output, and worked on a defined project — it belongs in the experience section, formatted exactly like a job: title, institution, dates, and achievement bullets. If it was primarily money and recognition — a named prestige fellowship like a Fulbright, Rhodes, or Marshall, or a graduate funding award that let you pursue your own study — it belongs under honors and awards, listed as the name and year. The test: ask whether you'd describe it as "work you did" or "an award you won." Many fellowships are both — the recognition is the name, the work is the project it funded — in which case you can name the honor under awards and let the concrete work graduate into experience or projects as its own quantified line.

How do you format a research or postdoctoral fellowship on a resume?

Format it like a job, because it was one. Use a title line — "Research Fellow" or "Postdoctoral Fellow" — followed by the institution or lab, the location, and the dates, then two to four achievement bullets underneath. The bullets should read the way any strong experience bullet reads: what you did, in what context, and what came of it, quantified wherever possible. "Led a 14-month study on X, producing two first-author publications and a dataset now used by three labs" beats "Held a prestigious research fellowship." The prestige of the fellowship's name is worth one clause; the work is worth the rest. Anchor the entry with a title the reader recognizes as a role, not just the fellowship's brand, so a non-academic hiring manager immediately understands you held a position and delivered results.

How do you list a named fellowship like a Fulbright on a resume?

A named prestige fellowship — Fulbright, Rhodes, Marshall, Gates, Knight-Hennessy, and the like — is recognition, so the name and year belong in an honors and awards section: "Fulbright Scholar, [country/program], 2024" or "Rhodes Scholarship, University of Oxford, 2023." The name carries weight on its own; you don't need to explain a Rhodes. But the fellowship almost always funded real work — a research project, a graduate degree, teaching, or fieldwork — and that work is often more valuable to a hiring manager than the label. So name the honor under awards for the recognition signal, and let the substance graduate into experience or projects as a quantified bullet on its own merits. Listing the same fellowship in both places is fine when one entry is the honor (the name) and the other is the work (the project) — they're saying different things.

Should you put a fellowship on a resume if it's not related to the job?

Usually yes for a competitive named fellowship, because selectivity is a transferable signal — winning a Fulbright or a national fellowship tells any employer you were chosen from a large, strong pool, and that reads as capability regardless of field. Keep it as a one-line honor even when the subject is unrelated. For a role-type fellowship, apply the same relevance test you'd apply to any job: if the work maps to the target role, frame the bullets around the transferable skills and keep it; if it's an old, unrelated research post crowding a page full of stronger recent experience, it can shrink to a line or come off entirely. The deciding factor is what the fellowship signals to this employer — prestige and selectivity travel well even across fields, but a detailed, off-topic project description competing for space on a crowded page often does not.

How do you list a fellowship you were awarded but haven't started or declined?

Honestly, and never as experience you haven't performed. A fellowship you were selected for but have not yet started is an honor, not a role — list it under awards as "Awarded" with the year, and add "incoming" or an expected start date only if it's genuinely upcoming and relevant. A fellowship you were offered and declined is still a legitimate honor — the selection happened — so "Offered [Name] Fellowship, 2024 (declined)" is honest and still credits the achievement, though many people simply omit a declined award unless the name is notable. What you cannot do is describe a fellowship you never performed as though you did the work, list an unstarted appointment as current experience, or leave a completed fellowship reading as ongoing. State the real status — awarded, incoming, current, or completed — because dates and appointments are exactly the kind of claim a reference call confirms.
