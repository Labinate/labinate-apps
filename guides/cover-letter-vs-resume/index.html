Cover letter vs resume

They land in the same application, but they do different jobs. A resume is the evidence — a fast, scannable record of what you've done. A cover letter is the argument — a short, tailored case for why that record fits this exact role. Confusing the two, or making one a copy of the other, is where most applications quietly go wrong. Here's what each is for, how they differ, and whether you actually need both.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The whole distinction in one line: your resume says what you did; your cover letter says why it matters here. The resume is read in seconds and answers "is this person qualified?" The letter is read in a minute and answers "why this person, for this job, over the others?" Keep those two jobs separate and both documents get stronger.

What each document is actually for

They're not two versions of the same thing. They're two tools that do complementary work:

  • The resume is a reference document. It's structured, skimmable, and factual — bullet points, dates, metrics, keywords. A recruiter scans it in six to ten seconds to decide whether you clear the bar. It's largely the same across similar applications, with light tailoring. Its voice is impersonal: no "I," just verbs and results.
  • The cover letter is a persuasive document. It's written in full sentences, in the first person, and it's built for one specific job. It doesn't re-list your history — it interprets it: it picks one or two things worth expanding on, tells the reader why they matter for this role, and adds the context a bullet point can't (a career change, a gap, an unusual but strong fit, real enthusiasm for the company).

Do you need both?

Short version: you basically always need a resume, and you should usually send a cover letter too. The resume is the non-negotiable core — very few applications skip it. The cover letter is technically optional far more often, but the safe default is to include one unless the posting says not to, there's no field to attach it, or a recruiter has told you resume-only.

So a "complete" application is normally both documents: the resume as the required foundation, and a tailored cover letter as the companion that makes your case. The one time you send a resume alone is when the process genuinely leaves no room for a letter. We break the send-or-skip call down fully in do you need a cover letter?

The #1 mistake: a cover letter that just repeats the resume

This is the trap that makes a cover letter worthless. If the letter simply re-narrates your job history — "In my role at X I did A, B and C" — the reader has already seen all of that on the page next to it. You've spent your one chance to speak in your own voice saying nothing new.

Repeat vs interpret

✕ "I worked at Acme for three years as a marketing coordinator, where I managed campaigns and social media." (The resume already says this.)

✓ "At Acme I inherited a stalled email programme and grew it from 4% to 11% open rates in a year — the kind of neglected-channel turnaround your posting says this team needs." Same job, but now it's an argument, not a restatement.

The rule of thumb: the resume lists what, the cover letter explains so what. Pick your one or two strongest, most relevant items and give them the story, the significance, or the connection to this role that a bullet point has no room for.

Which comes first?

Write the resume first — always. Building it forces you to assemble your history, quantify your achievements, and decide what your strongest selling points are. Those selling points are exactly the raw material the cover letter draws on. Once the resume is solid, the letter almost writes itself: you're just choosing the one or two things worth expanding and connecting them to the specific job. Write the letter first and you'll usually rewrite it once the resume clarifies your story.

What goes where

On the resume

  • Contact details & a headline/summary
  • Work history with dated bullets
  • Quantified results and metrics
  • Skills, tools and keywords
  • Education and certifications
  • The full, scannable record

In the cover letter

  • Why this company / this role
  • One or two experiences, expanded
  • Context for a change or a gap
  • How your background connects to their need
  • Genuine, specific enthusiasm
  • A clear closing ask

Test for overlap. Read your cover letter and ask: "Could someone learn all of this from the resume alone?" If yes, the letter isn't earning its place — rewrite it to add the interpretation, story, and fit that only prose can carry.

Quick reference

  • Resume → the evidence: what you've done, scannable, factual, almost always required.
  • Cover letter → the argument: why you fit this job, written fresh, usually optional but recommended.
  • Both? → yes, in most complete applications — resume as the foundation, letter as the tailored companion.
  • Don't → let the letter restate the resume. Interpret, don't repeat.
  • Order → resume first; it becomes the raw material for the letter.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a cover letter and a resume?

A resume is a structured, scannable summary of what you've done — your work history, skills, education and achievements, written in tight bullet points and read in seconds. A cover letter is a short, written argument for why you're the right fit for one specific job — it adds context, connects your background to the role, and shows genuine interest in a way a bulleted resume can't. Put simply: the resume is the evidence, the cover letter is the argument that interprets it.

Do you need both a cover letter and a resume?

You almost always need a resume — it's the core document nearly every application requires. A cover letter is usually optional but recommended: the safe default is to send one unless the posting says not to or gives you no way to attach it. So in practice, most complete applications include both, with the resume as the required foundation and the cover letter as the tailored companion that makes your case for that role.

Should a cover letter repeat what's on your resume?

No — that's the single most common mistake. If your cover letter just re-lists the jobs and skills already on your resume, it gives the reader nothing new and wastes the one chance you have to speak in your own voice. Instead, pick one or two of your strongest, most relevant experiences and explain their significance: the story behind a result, why it matters for this role, or how a career change connects. The resume states the facts; the cover letter tells the reader what to make of them.

Which should you write first, the resume or the cover letter?

Write the resume first. It forces you to gather your history, quantify your achievements and decide what your strongest selling points are — and those points become the raw material for the cover letter. Once the resume is solid, the letter is easy: you're just choosing the one or two things worth expanding on and connecting them to the specific job. Trying to write the letter first usually means rewriting it once the resume clarifies your story.
