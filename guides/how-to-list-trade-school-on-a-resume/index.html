How to list trade school on a resume

Trade, vocational, and technical school is real, occupation-specific education — for a skilled-trade job, often the most relevant line on the page. List it in Education with the exact credential and trade, name what you actually earned without inflating it to a degree, pair it with the license it maps to, and place it by relevance.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

A diploma from a welding program, an HVAC/R certificate, a medical-assisting or cosmetology credential — trade school is real education that trains you for a specific job, and yet it's one of the most undersold things on a resume, because people measure it against a four-year degree and find it wanting. That's the wrong yardstick. For the work it prepares you for, a trade credential is frequently more relevant than a bachelor's would be. The two failure modes both come from misjudging what it is: underselling it — burying it, or apologizing for it because "it's not a real college" — which throws away the single most job-relevant thing you have; or mislabeling it — calling a diploma a "degree," or writing a vague "attended trade school" with no credential and no trade named. The fix is one reframe: it's real, occupation-specific education. Name it precisely, pair it with the license, and place it by relevance.

The core idea: a trade-school credential belongs in your Education section with the school, the exact credential and trade, and the year — Diploma in HVAC/R Technology, not "attended trade school" and not an inflated "degree." Add the substance an employer wants: hands-on hours, equipment and processes, and the license it prepares you for (listed separately under Licenses & Certifications). Then match visibility to relevance — central and near the top for an in-trade job, brief and lower for an out-of-trade one, and shrinking to a single line once years of relevant work take over.

Treat it as real education — and place it by relevance

The first move is to stop hedging. A trade, vocational, or technical school is a post-secondary institution, and a diploma or certificate from an accredited one is a genuine credential that goes in your Education section, formatted exactly like any other education entry: school name, the credential, and the year. It sits above a GED or high-school line, not below it, and it should never be tucked away as if it were something to get past.

Where it sits is a question of relevance, never embarrassment. When the target job is in or near the trade you trained for, the credential is a headline qualification — put it at the top of Education, and for an early-career tradesperson it can sit near the top of the whole resume. When the job is unrelated, the completed program still shows discipline and follow-through, so keep it — just briefly and lower down. The rule is the same one every credential follows: it leads while it's your strongest evidence, and recedes as real work outgrows it.

Name the exact credential and the trade

Precision is what separates a credential from a vague claim. Say what you earned, and in what.

Name the credential — diploma, certificate, or completion

Write "Diploma in Welding Technology" or "Certificate in Medical Assisting," not a bare "attended trade school" that names nothing. The credential type is information the reader needs; leaving it out reads as if there's nothing to name.

Don't inflate it into a degree

A diploma is not a "degree," and calling it one is the kind of overstatement a background or reference check on the school unravels. Name it accurately — the honest credential is strong on its own, and precision reads as confidence, not modesty.

Always name the trade

"Certificate program" tells a reader nothing; "Certificate in HVAC/R Technology" tells them exactly what you can do. The trade is the value of the credential — never drop it to sound more general.

Pair it with the license and the hands-on hours

Trade credentials matter because they map to the things that let you legally do the work and prove you've actually done it. Surface both.

List the license separately — the school prepares you, the license lets you practice

Many trades gate the work behind a license or certification — a journeyman card, EPA 608, a cosmetology or barbering license, a CDL, a CNA, an ASE certification. The school proves the training; the license proves you can legally do the job. List the license under a Licenses & Certifications section, not merged into the school line, so a reader (and the ATS) catches both.

Quantify the hands-on hours and the equipment

Trade programs are measured in supervised hours and real equipment: "900 hours of supervised shop training," "600 clinical hours across two rotations," "trained on MIG, TIG, and stick welding to AWS standards." That's the concrete evidence an employer in the field reads first — far stronger than the program's name alone.

Note what it prepared you for, even before you sit the exam

If the license is pending, say so honestly: "Program aligned to the EPA 608 certification (exam scheduled)." It shows the reader you know the credential path and are on it — without claiming a license you don't yet hold.

Match visibility to relevance — lead with it, or let it recede

The last move is judging how much room the credential earns for the job in front of you.

Applying in or near the trade

  • Lead with it — top of Education, near the top for early-career
  • Name the credential, trade, and hands-on hours
  • Surface the license prominently
  • It outweighs a general degree for this work

Applying outside the trade

  • Keep it brief — one line, lower on the page
  • It still shows a completed program and discipline
  • Translate transferable skills into the summary
  • Don't over-explain a credential the job doesn't use

And over time the same rule that governs every credential applies: once you have several years of relevant hands-on work, let the experience take the lead and shrink the school to a single clean line. It never disappears — it just stops being the headline once the work speaks louder.

Trade school isn't the same as an associate degree, an apprenticeship, or a license

Several credentials get tangled together, and each belongs in a different section with a different move. Sort out which one you actually hold before you place it.

The credentialWhat it really is — and where it goes
Trade schoolClassroom-and-lab education earning a diploma or certificate in a specific trade. Goes in Education; name the exact credential and trade, never a "degree."
Associate degreeA two-year academic degree from a community or junior college, with transferable general-education credits. It's a degree; trade school isn't — the test is simply what you earned. See associate degree.
ApprenticeshipPaid on-the-job training under a journeyman — that's work experience, so it goes in Work Experience, not Education. Many trades combine both. See apprenticeship.
License / certificationThe credential to legally practice — journeyman, EPA 608, cosmetology, CDL, CNA, ASE — earned by exam or hours. The school prepares you for it. List it under Licenses & Certifications.
BootcampAn intensive, often non-accredited program, usually in tech. Trade school is an accredited vocational institution for skilled trades; placement instincts differ. See bootcamp.

The clean way to keep them straight: trade school proves the training, the license proves you can do the work, and an apprenticeship is the work. A single path often includes all three — list each in its right section rather than collapsing them into one line.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode isn't dishonesty — it's letting a real credential either shrink into an apology or blur into a vague claim. Handled well, trade school is a direct answer to "can this person do the job."

Reads as a qualification

  • In Education with the exact credential and trade
  • Hands-on hours and equipment quantified
  • License listed separately and clearly
  • Placed by relevance — leads for an in-trade job

Reads as a hedge

  • "Attended trade school" — no credential, no trade
  • A diploma inflated into a "degree"
  • Buried or apologized for as "not real college"
  • License and hours left off entirely

Trade school is real, occupation-specific education — so claim it as the qualification it is. Measured against the work it prepares you for, a trade credential isn't a lesser version of a degree — it's the more precise answer to the question the employer is actually asking.

  • Name the exact credential and trade → "Diploma in Welding Technology," never "attended trade school" and never an inflated "degree."
  • Pair it with the license → list the license or certification separately; the school trains, the license authorizes.
  • Quantify the hands-on hours → supervised shop/clinical/lab hours, equipment, and processes trained on.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put trade school on a resume?

Yes — trade school is real, occupation-specific education, and for a skilled-trade job it's often the single most relevant credential you have, more relevant than a four-year degree would be. The mistake is undervaluing it because it "isn't a real college." It absolutely belongs on the page. Put it in the Education section with the school name, the exact credential, and the year — for example, "Lincoln Technical Institute — Diploma in HVAC/R Technology, 2024." The only time it recedes is by relevance, not by embarrassment: when the target job is entirely outside the trade, keep the entry brief and lower on the page; and once you have several years of relevant hands-on work, let the experience lead and shrink the school to a single line. But for anyone applying in or near the trade they trained for, trade school is a headline qualification, not a footnote.

How do you list a trade school or vocational program on a resume?

Format it like any education entry, with three things named precisely: the school, the exact credential and trade, and the year. Write "Diploma in Welding Technology" or "Certificate in Medical Assisting" — not a vague "attended trade school" and not an inflated "degree" it wasn't. Under the entry, add the substance an employer in that field wants: the hands-on hours you logged ("900 hours of supervised shop training"), the equipment and processes you trained on (MIG/TIG/stick welding, EPA 608 refrigerant handling, EKG and phlebotomy), and any license or certification the program prepared you for. List that license separately under a Licenses & Certifications section, because the school proves the training and the license proves you can legally do the work. If the program is central to the job, it can sit at the top of Education or even near the top of the whole resume for an early-career tradesperson; if it's tangential, keep it to one clean line.

Does trade school count as education on a resume?

Yes. A trade, vocational, or technical school is a post-secondary educational institution, and a diploma or certificate from an accredited one is a real credential that goes in your Education section. It is not a college degree — you should never label a diploma as a "degree" — but it is genuine, structured, often-accredited training, and it sits above a GED or high-school line, not below it. For skilled and technical trades, employers frequently weight the trade credential and its associated license more heavily than a general four-year degree, because it maps directly to whether you can do the work. So don't hedge it, don't apologize for it, and don't bury it under unrelated coursework: name the school, name the credential and the trade, and let it stand as the qualification it is.

How is trade school different from an associate degree, an apprenticeship, and a license on a resume?

They're four different things and each lands in a different place. Trade school is classroom-and-lab education that earns a diploma or certificate — it goes in Education, and you name the exact credential without calling it a degree. An associate degree is a two-year academic degree from a community or junior college, with general-education credits that often transfer; it's a degree, trade school isn't, so the test is simply what you earned — a "degree" or a "diploma/certificate." An apprenticeship is paid on-the-job training under a journeyman: that's work experience and belongs in your Work Experience section, not Education — and many trades combine the two, so you may list a trade-school diploma under Education and an apprenticeship under Work Experience. A professional license or certification — a journeyman card, EPA 608, a cosmetology license, a CDL, a CNA, an ASE certification — is the credential that lets you legally practice; the trade school prepares you for it, and you list the license separately under Licenses & Certifications. School proves the training; the license proves you can do the work; the apprenticeship is the work itself.

Should you list trade school if you didn't finish or didn't earn the credential?

You can, but be honest about what it was, and only include it if it earns its place. If you completed real coursework and it's relevant, list it without claiming a credential you didn't get: "Coursework in Automotive Technology, [School], 2023" or "Completed 2 of 3 terms toward a Diploma in Electrical Technology." Never write a clean completion line or imply a diploma you didn't earn — a background or reference check on the school will surface it, and being caught overstating a credential is far more damaging than an unfinished program. If the training you did complete built real, relevant skills, describe those skills honestly; if it was brief and unrelated to the job, it's fine to leave it off entirely rather than raise a question it doesn't answer. And if you're currently enrolled, mark it as in progress with an expected completion date rather than a past-tense year.
