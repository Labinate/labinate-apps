How to make a resume stand out

Not the way most advice implies. You don't stand out with a colorful template, a photo, icons, or a "creative" design — those break the software that reads your resume first, and to a human they read as compensating for thin content. You stand out by being the most relevant and most specific candidate on the page. This guide covers the three levers that actually work — relevance, specificity, and signal over noise — and the decorative traps to skip.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

"How do I make my resume stand out?" almost always gets answered with decoration: a splash of color, a headshot, a two-column layout, a skill-rating bar. It's the intuitive answer — in a stack of documents, the eye-catching one wins, right? On a resume, no. The first reader is usually an applicant tracking system that turns your design into scrambled text, and the human after it has seen a thousand resumes and is unmoved by flourish. What makes yours the one they shortlist is substance: it's visibly the best fit for this job, and the proof is specific enough to remember.

The core idea: standing out is a positioning problem, not a design problem. Among 200 competent resumes, yours gets remembered because the recruiter can see in seconds that you're the right fit and you back it with concrete numbers — not because it's the prettiest. Three levers do this: relevance, specificity, and signal over noise.

The instinct that backfires: decoration

Before the three levers, kill the reflex. The things people reach for to "stand out" mostly cost you — they add risk and noise without adding information.

Looks like standing out — isn't

  • Colorful / two-column "creative" template
  • A photo or headshot
  • Skill rating bars, star ratings, dials
  • Icons, timelines, sidebars, tables
  • A long "about me" personal statement
  • An unusual font to look different

Actually stands out

  • Targeted to the exact role, top to bottom
  • Specific, quantified results in every bullet
  • Strongest, most relevant win in the top third
  • Clean single column an ATS reads perfectly
  • A two-line summary of specialty + results
  • Ruthless cuts of anything off-target

Why decoration hurts, concretely: most resumes are parsed by an ATS before a human sees them, and multi-column layouts, text-in-graphics, and tables routinely get scrambled — your content can literally vanish. And to the human who does read it, heavy design signals a candidate dressing up weak substance. The one real exception is a visual-design role where the resume is a portfolio piece — and even those designers keep a clean text version for the ATS.

Lever 1 — Relevance: be the obvious fit

The single biggest reason a resume gets remembered is that it reads as if it were written for this exact job. A generic resume sprayed at 40 postings stands out to no one. Relevance means two moves:

Tailor to the posting

Mirror the language and priorities of the job description — the same skills, tools, and outcomes it names, in your own true words. This is also how you clear keyword filters. See how to tailor a resume and resume keywords.

Front-load the most relevant material

Reorder so the experience, projects, and skills closest to the target sit highest. A recruiter reading top-down should hit your most relevant proof first, not dig for it. If a role or bullet doesn't serve this application, shorten or cut it.

Lever 2 — Specificity: numbers they remember

Generic duties blur together; specifics stick. "Responsible for social media" is on a hundred resumes. "Grew Instagram from 2k to 40k followers in 9 months, lifting referral traffic 3×" is on one — and the reader remembers it. The move is turning duties into accomplishments and attaching a number wherever one honestly exists.

Blends in (duty)Stands out (specific accomplishment)
Responsible for improving site performanceCut page load time 2.4s → 0.9s, lifting conversion 18%
Managed the customer support queueCleared a 400-ticket backlog and held CSAT at 94% across a team of 5

Lever 3 — Signal over noise: win the top third

A recruiter's first pass is about six or seven seconds, spent almost entirely on the top third of page one. Standing out means that region answers one question instantly: is this person right for this role? Everything else is in service of not slowing that scan down.

A targeted headline, not a generic one

Lead with the role you're targeting — "Senior Data Analyst · SQL, dbt, Retail" — not "Experienced Professional Seeking Opportunities." See resume headline examples.

A two-line summary that says fit + results

Name your specialty and one or two headline outcomes — not an "about me" paragraph. See resume summary examples.

Your single strongest bullet, first

The first bullet of your most recent role should be your most impressive, most relevant win — the one thing you'd want read if the recruiter read only one line.

Clean, legible, single column

Generous margins, a plain font, consistent spacing. The "design" that stands out is effortless readability, which is also what parses cleanly.

Put the three together

None of the levers works alone. A beautifully specific bullet buried on page two doesn't stand out (signal). A perfectly tailored resume of vague duties doesn't stand out (specificity). A punchy top third for the wrong job doesn't stand out (relevance). Stacked, they compound: the recruiter opens to a top third that's clearly aimed at their role, reads specific numbers that prove it, and never once has to fight the layout to get there. That resume gets remembered — no color required.

Quick reference

  • Standing out ≠ decoration → color, photos, icons, and "creative" templates break the ATS and read as weak content. Skip them.
  • Lever 1 — relevance → tailor to the exact job and front-load your most relevant material.
  • Lever 2 — specificity → replace duties with quantified accomplishments a reader remembers.
  • Lever 3 — signal → win the top third: targeted headline, tight summary, strongest bullet first, clean single column.
  • Fix the basics first → clear the common mistakes; competent is the floor, standout is the layer on top.

Frequently asked questions

How do you make a resume stand out?

By being the most relevant and specific candidate on the page, not the most decorated. Three levers do the work. First, relevance: tailor the resume to the exact job, mirror the language of the posting, and put the most relevant experience first so the reader sees the fit in seconds. Second, specificity: replace generic duties ("responsible for social media") with concrete, quantified accomplishments ("grew Instagram from 2k to 40k followers in 9 months") — a reader remembers a number, not a job description. Third, signal over noise: keep it a clean, legible page with a strong top third (headline plus summary) that a recruiter reads in about seven seconds. Graphics, color blocks, photos, and "creative" templates do the opposite — they break ATS parsing and read as compensating for thin content.

Do colorful or creative resume templates help you stand out?

Almost always no — they hurt. Most resumes pass through an applicant tracking system (ATS) before a human sees them, and multi-column layouts, text inside graphics, sidebars, tables, and icons frequently get scrambled or dropped when the software parses them. Even when a human does read it, heavy design reads as a candidate trying to compensate for weak content — recruiters have seen thousands of resumes and are unimpressed by decoration. The exception is a narrow set of visual-design roles where the resume itself is a portfolio piece, and even there most designers keep a clean text version for the ATS. For everyone else, a clean single-column layout that parses perfectly and puts strong content first stands out more than any template.

What makes a resume stand out to recruiters in six seconds?

Recruiters skim the top third of the page first, so that's where standing out is won or lost. In the first six to seven seconds they want to answer one question: is this person a fit for this specific role? Make that instant by leading with a targeted headline (the job title you're targeting, not a generic "Experienced Professional"), a two-line summary that names your specialty and one or two headline results, and a most-recent role whose first bullet is your single strongest, most relevant accomplishment. If the top third clearly matches the job, the recruiter reads on; if it's generic or off-target, the rest of the page never gets read no matter how good it is.

How is standing out different from just following resume tips?

General resume tips and mistake lists tell you how to avoid looking bad — correct formatting, no typos, consistent tenses, the right length. That gets you to competent, which is table stakes, not standout. Standing out is a positioning question on top of that baseline: among two hundred competent resumes, why does the recruiter remember and shortlist yours? The answer is never decoration; it's being visibly the best fit. That means tailoring to the exact posting, leading with specific quantified results instead of duties, and making the most relevant material impossible to miss. Fix the mistakes first, then use the three levers — relevance, specificity, and signal — to move from acceptable to memorable.

Does adding a photo, personal statement, or design flourish help you stand out?

In most markets, no. A photo can trigger bias-avoidance policies that get a resume screened out before review, and it takes space better spent on content — outside a few countries where photos are conventional, leave it off. A long personal statement or "about me" paragraph usually reads as filler; a tight two-line summary of specialty plus results does more. Design flourishes — icons, rating bars for skills, timelines, color sidebars — mostly add parsing risk and visual noise without adding information. The reliable way to stand out is boringly consistent: cut anything that doesn't help a reader see your fit fast, and pour that space and attention into specific, relevant evidence of what you've actually done.
