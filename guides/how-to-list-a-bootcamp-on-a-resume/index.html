How to list a bootcamp on a resume

A bootcamp isn't a degree and it isn't a certification — it's proof of skills. So the real question isn't "where do I put it?" but "is this bootcamp my main qualification for the job, or a supplement to what I already have?" The answer decides everything: how much space it earns, whether it leads or supports, and how hard the projects it produced have to work. This guide covers both paths — and the one line you must never cross.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

Here's the trap that catches career changers. You finish an intensive coding, data, UX, or cybersecurity bootcamp, you're proud of it, and you drop "Full-Stack Web Development Bootcamp" into the Education section next to your old degree — then wonder why the callbacks don't come. The bootcamp name isn't the argument. What you built during it is. A recruiter skims the program in half a second; what makes them keep reading is a Projects section showing a real, shipped application with a link they can click. This guide sorts the two out: how to list the bootcamp honestly, and how to make the work behind it carry the resume.

The core idea: a bootcamp answers "how did you learn this skill?" — not "how good are you at it?" That makes the program itself context and the projects it produced the evidence. Place the bootcamp so a reader can find it, then spend your best resume real estate on what you can demonstrably do.

First, what a bootcamp is — and isn't

A bootcamp gets filed next to degrees, certifications, and self-teaching, but it behaves like none of them, and the difference decides how you list it.

Not the same as…How a bootcamp differs
A degreeA degree is a multi-year credential from an accredited institution. Most bootcamps are not accredited to grant degrees — they're weeks-to-months of intensive, project-based training. You can list a bootcamp in the Education section, but label it as a bootcamp or program; never format it so it reads like a conferred B.S. or B.A.
A certificationA certification is a single credential earned by passing a standardized exam (AWS, CompTIA, PMP). A bootcamp isn't a pass/fail exam — it's an immersive program that produces a portfolio of work. Some bootcamps issue a "certificate of completion," but that's a receipt for attendance, not a certification, so don't list it as one.
Being self-taughtSelf-teaching shows initiative but is hard for a reader to verify. A bootcamp adds structure, a defined curriculum, and — usually — a cohort capstone. If you're taking the no-degree route, a bootcamp is one concrete, nameable credential within that broader strategy of leading with skills and proof.
A weekend workshop or courseA one-day workshop or a self-paced online course is a line item at most. A full-time, multi-week immersive bootcamp is a real block of your recent history — it can explain a gap, anchor a career change, and justify a Projects section. Don't inflate a short course into "bootcamp," and don't shrink a genuine immersive into a footnote.

The one question that decides placement

Before you format anything, answer this: is the bootcamp your primary qualification for this job, or a supplement to experience you already have? Everything downstream flows from it.

Primary — lead with it

  • You're a career changer moving into the field the bootcamp trained you for
  • It's your most relevant, most recent, job-defining preparation
  • Your work history doesn't yet show the target skills
  • → Give it a clear Education entry and a dedicated Projects section

Supplement — a supporting line

  • You already work in or near the field and have relevant experience
  • The bootcamp added a specific skill (a framework, a data stack)
  • Your experience section already carries the resume
  • → One clean line near education or certifications is enough

Get this wrong in either direction and the resume weakens. A career changer who buries the bootcamp in a one-line footer starves their strongest signal; an experienced engineer who builds a whole section around a weekend course looks like they're padding. Match the space to the role the bootcamp actually plays.

How to format the bootcamp entry itself

Whichever path you're on, the entry itself follows the same honest formula: program name, focus, provider, and dates — stated as what it is.

Name it accurately, with its focus

Full-Stack Web Development Program — [Bootcamp Name], 2026 or Data Analytics Bootcamp — [Provider], Jan–Apr 2026. Include the focus so a non-specialist recruiter and an ATS can both tell what you trained in. The provider name is context, not the headline.

Signal the intensity — briefly

Immersive hours are a real signal that this wasn't a casual course: 600+ hour immersive or full-time, 14 weeks. One short qualifier does it; don't turn the entry into a curriculum dump. What you learned belongs in your skills and projects, not in the education line.

No GPA, no fake honors, no "degree" language

Bootcamps don't issue a GPA, so don't invent one. Skip "graduated with honors" unless the program formally awarded it. And never phrase it to imply an accredited degree — a background or education check verifies exactly what the institution granted.

The projects are the point

This is where a bootcamp resume is won or lost. The program tells a reader how you learned; the projects prove you can actually do the work. For a career changer with no professional experience in the field, the Projects section isn't optional garnish — it's the primary evidence, and it should sit high on the page, right after your summary.

Weak — the program aloneStrong — the work it produced
Completed Full-Stack Web Development Bootcamp. Learned React, Node, and SQL.Built and deployed a full-stack expense tracker (React, Node, PostgreSQL) with auth and a REST API — live at [link], 40+ users in first month.
Tells the reader you attended.Shows the reader you can ship — with a link they can open.

For each capstone or personal project, name the problem, the stack, your role (solo or team), and a quantified result where you can — users, performance, data volume, anything real. Link to a live demo or a public repo; a clickable, working project outweighs any bootcamp brand name. Frame these with strong action verbs (built, deployed, designed, shipped), the same as you would real work — because to a hiring manager, that's what they are.

A few common situations

Career changer, no experience in the new field yet

This is the bootcamp's home turf. Open with a short summary that names the pivot, put a Projects section right below it, then the bootcamp in Education, then your prior work reframed for transferable skills. The old job isn't dead weight — communication, project ownership, and domain knowledge from it can be genuine differentiators. See the career-change resume guide for the full structure.

Already in the field, upskilling

Keep it small. Your experience leads; the bootcamp is a one-line supporting entry showing you added a specific, current skill. If it produced a strong project relevant to the target role, you can mention that in a project or accomplishment line — but don't let a supplement crowd out real work history.

Student or new grad who did a bootcamp alongside a degree

List both in Education, with the degree as the anchor and the bootcamp as the applied-skills complement. For someone early in a career, a bootcamp plus real projects can be exactly what turns a thin resume into a credible one.

You finished the bootcamp but haven't landed a role yet

Nothing to hide and nothing to apologize for. Employers hire on demonstrated skill, so let the capstone and personal projects do the arguing. Keep building and shipping small projects while you search — every new one strengthens the resume more than the bootcamp name ever will.

The bright line — never let a bootcamp masquerade as a degree. Listing an intensive program is honest and increasingly respected; formatting it so it reads as an accredited "B.S. in Computer Science," claiming a degree the school never granted, or inventing a GPA or honors is a fabrication — and one of the easiest to catch, because education verification confirms exactly what an institution conferred. List the bootcamp as a bootcamp. The real version, backed by projects, is a stronger candidate than a fake degree that unravels on the first check.

Quick reference

  • Bootcamp ≠ degree ≠ certification → it's an intensive, project-based program. Label it as one; don't dress it up as either.
  • Primary or supplement? → career changer, lead with it (Education entry + Projects section); already in the field, one supporting line.
  • Format honestly → program name, focus, provider, dates, and a short intensity signal (hours / weeks). No GPA, no invented honors.
  • Projects carry the resume → name the problem, stack, your role, and a quantified result; link a live demo or repo. The work beats the brand.
  • Bright line → never imply an accredited degree the program didn't grant — education checks verify it instantly.

Keep building the resume

A bootcamp is the launchpad, not the whole pitch. Make sure it sits sensibly in your education section, that the projects it produced lead with quantified results, and that your prior work is reframed for the career change you're making. Frequently asked questions

Should you put a coding bootcamp on your resume?

Yes — if it's relevant to the job, a bootcamp belongs on your resume, and for a career changer it's often the qualification that makes you a credible candidate at all. It signals a deliberate, full-time investment in a new skill set and, more importantly, it produced real projects you can show. The only time to leave it off is when it's unrelated to the target role and adds nothing — a three-day weekend workshop or a bootcamp in a field you're not applying to. The mistake isn't listing it; it's either hiding it out of insecurity or overclaiming it as a degree.

Where does a bootcamp go on a resume — Education or a separate section?

It depends on whether the bootcamp is your primary qualification or a supplement. If it's the reason you can do the job (a career changer moving into software, data, or UX), give it real estate: a clear entry in your Education section and, crucially, a dedicated Projects section that shows what you built. If you already have a degree and relevant experience and the bootcamp just adds a skill, a single supporting line near your education or certifications is enough. Either way, don't invent a vague "Training" section to bury it — place it where a reader expects to see how you learned the skill.

Is a bootcamp a degree or a certification?

Neither, and it's important not to imply it's either. A degree is a multi-year credential conferred by an accredited institution; a certification is a single credential earned by passing a standardized exam. A bootcamp is an intensive, project-based training program — typically weeks to a few months — that most schools are not accredited to grant degrees for. List it as what it is: a bootcamp or program, with its real name and dates. Writing it so it reads like a "B.S. in Computer Science" or filing it as an accredited degree is a fabrication a background check will catch.

How do you list a bootcamp if you didn't finish it or it hasn't led to a job yet?

If you completed most of the program and gained real skills, you can list it with the work you actually did — lead with the projects and technologies rather than a completion claim, and don't state "graduated" or "certificate earned" if you didn't. If you barely started, it's usually not worth a line. The fact that it hasn't produced a job yet doesn't change anything: employers hire on demonstrated skill, so let the capstone and personal projects carry the resume. A bootcamp with a strong portfolio behind it is credible whether or not it's landed you a role yet.

Do employers respect bootcamps on a resume?

Increasingly, yes — but they respect the evidence more than the brand. Hiring managers in software, data, and design have hired plenty of bootcamp grads and know the good programs, so the bootcamp opens the door. What actually gets you through it is proof you can do the work: real projects, a public portfolio or GitHub, and quantified results. Treat the bootcamp name as the context and the projects as the argument. A candidate who lists a prestigious bootcamp with no visible work is weaker than one from an unknown program with three solid, shipped projects.
