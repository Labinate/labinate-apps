How to list volunteer work on a resume

Volunteer experience can prove a skill, fill a gap, or show initiative a paid job never captured — but only if you place it right and phrase it like real work. Here's where it goes, how to write the bullets, when to leave it off, and examples by situation.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Volunteer work is real experience. A recruiter doesn't care whether you were paid — they care whether you did something and something changed because of it. Done well, a volunteer entry carries the same weight as an internship: it demonstrates a skill, fills a gap in your timeline, and signals the kind of initiative that's hard to fake. Done carelessly — a bare line reading "Volunteer" with no detail — it just takes up space. This guide covers the difference.

First: should it be on the resume at all?

Include volunteer work when at least one of these is true. If none are, it's usually better to spend the space on stronger paid experience.

Include it when…Leave it off when…
It shows a skill the job needs (led a team, managed a budget, taught, built something)It's unrelated and you already have a full, relevant work history
You're a student, new grad, or career changer with limited paid experienceIt was a one-off afternoon with nothing meaningful to describe
It fills a gap in your employment timelineIt signals a belief a hiring manager might screen on (see below)
It's directly on-topic (shelter volunteering → vet clinic)It would push a two-page resume to three
One caution: volunteer work tied to a religion, political party, or activist cause can invite bias, fair or not. Include it if the skill is what matters (you managed 30 volunteers and a $10K budget); consider generalizing the organization ("a national nonprofit") or leaving it off if the cause itself is the only thing it says about you.

Where to put it

Placement is a signal. The more relevant the volunteering, the higher it goes — the rule is simply how much it reads as real work for this job.

Most relevant
Inside your main Experience section

If the volunteer role directly matches the job, list it right alongside paid roles — same format, dates, and bullets — with "(Volunteer)" in the title so it's honest. A recruiter reads it as experience, because it is.

Relevant but secondary
Its own "Volunteer Experience" section

Give it a dedicated heading — "Volunteer Experience" or "Community Involvement" — placed below your work history. This keeps your paid experience front and center while still crediting the skills you built volunteering.

Little paid experience
As your primary experience

Students and career changers can let volunteer work be the Experience section. Two well-written volunteer entries beat an empty page — and often beat a thin list of unrelated part-time jobs. See how to write a resume with no experience.

How to write the bullets

The mistake that flattens volunteer work is describing it more vaguely than paid work — "helped at events", "assisted with fundraising". Hold it to the same standard: an action verb and a result. For the mechanics, this is the same structure as any bullet — see resume bullet points and how to quantify achievements.

Vague (before)Achievement bullet (after)
Volunteered at a food bankCoordinated weekly distribution for a food bank serving 200+ families, cutting sort time 30% with a new intake system
Helped with the charity 5KRecruited and scheduled 25 event volunteers for a charity 5K that raised $18K, a 40% increase over the prior year
Tutored kidsTutored 8 middle-school students in math weekly for a year; 6 raised their grade by at least one letter
Did social media for a nonprofitRan a nonprofit's Instagram, growing it from 500 to 4K followers and driving 120 event sign-ups in 6 months

What a full volunteer entry looks like

Format it exactly like a job: organization, role (with "Volunteer"), dates, then bullets.

Event Coordinator (Volunteer) — Riverside Community Center · 2024–2025 · Recruited and scheduled 25 volunteers for a charity 5K that raised $18K (+40% YoY)
· Built a shift-signup sheet that cut day-of no-shows from 8 to 1

Don'tDo

Frequently asked questions

Should you put volunteer work on a resume?

Put it on when it shows a skill the job needs, fills a gap in your work history, or is directly relevant to the role. It's especially valuable for students, career changers, and anyone with limited paid experience, because it proves initiative and real skills. Leave it off when it's unrelated, very brief, or would crowd out stronger paid experience on a full resume.

Where do you put volunteer experience on a resume?

If it's directly relevant, list it inside your main Experience section alongside paid roles. If it's relevant but secondary, give it its own "Volunteer Experience" or "Community Involvement" section below your work history. If you have little paid experience, volunteer work can be your primary experience section. The more relevant it is, the higher up it goes.

How do you describe volunteer work on a resume?

Describe it exactly like paid work: organization, role or title, dates, and two or three bullets that start with an action verb and end in a result. Write "Volunteer Tutor" or "Event Coordinator (Volunteer)" rather than just "Volunteer", and quantify what you did — students tutored, funds raised, people served. Treating it like a real job is what makes a recruiter treat it like one.

Should you label volunteer work as unpaid?

You don't need to write "unpaid", but you should make clear it was volunteer work so you're never accused of misrepresenting it. The cleanest way is to add "Volunteer" to the role title or place the entry under a "Volunteer Experience" heading. That signals it honestly without diminishing the skills you demonstrated.

Does volunteer work count as experience on a resume?

Yes. It's legitimate experience, and recruiters treat it as such when it shows relevant skills and measurable results. For students, new graduates, and career changers it can carry as much weight as an internship. What matters is not the paycheck but whether you can point to something you did and something that changed because of it.
