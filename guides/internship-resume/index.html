The internship resume

You're applying for an internship precisely because you don't have much experience yet — so a blank work history isn't the problem it feels like. The trick is to lead with what you do have: your studies, your projects, and the things you've built or done outside a paycheck. Here's exactly how to structure it, section by section, with a full example to copy.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

Recruiters who hire interns already know they're reading resumes from students and recent grads. They aren't scanning for a decade of job titles — they're looking for initiative, relevant skills, and evidence that you'll pick things up fast. That completely changes what a strong internship resume looks like compared to a mid-career one, and it's good news: you almost certainly have more to work with than you think.

Lead with education, not experience

On a normal resume, work experience comes first. On a student internship resume, flip it: put your education section right below your summary, near the top. It's your strongest, most relevant credential, so it earns the prime real estate.

Beef it up beyond a single line. Under your degree, add a relevant coursework line naming the classes that map to the internship, and list any honors, a strong GPA, or a scholarship. A student's education section can reasonably run three or four lines — that's fine here. Not sure whether your number makes the cut? See should you put your GPA on a resume for the threshold and how to format it.

Education section B.S. Computer Science — University of Washington
  • Expected May 2027 · GPA 3.7 · Dean's List (3 semesters)
  • Relevant coursework: Data Structures, Databases, Web Development, Statistics

Turn projects and coursework into experience

This is where most internship resumes are won or lost. A class project, a hackathon entry, a personal app, or a research assignment is real, describable work — treat it exactly like a job. Give the section a heading like Projects and write two or three achievement-style bullets for each: what you built, the tools you used, and the result.

Projects section Campus Events Finder — Personal Project
  • Built a React web app aggregating 200+ campus events into a searchable calendar, used by ~150 students
  • Designed the SQLite schema and a REST API; deployed on a free tier with zero hosting cost

Notice the pattern: an action verb, a concrete tool, and a number wherever one exists. Numbers make a student project sound like output, not a homework assignment. If you can quantify anything — users, hours, percent, dollars, size — do it. See the action-verb list for stronger openers than "worked on" and "helped with."

Count everything that isn't a "real job"

Part-time and summer jobs count — even unrelated ones. A retail or food-service job shows reliability, customer skills, and that you can hold responsibility, and every intern recruiter knows it. Beyond paid work, all of this is fair game for its own section (call it Experience, Leadership & Activities, or Volunteering):

  • Campus roles — club officer, teaching assistant, orientation leader, student government.
  • Volunteering — a tutoring program, an event you helped run, a nonprofit you gave time to.
  • Competitions — hackathons, case competitions, design challenges, sports at a competitive level.
  • Freelance or side work — a logo you designed, a site you built for a local business, tutoring you charged for.

Write each with the same bullet formula. "Treasurer, Robotics Club — managed a $4,000 budget and cut event costs 15%" reads like leadership, because it is. This is the heart of writing a resume with no formal experience.

Open with a short, targeted objective

Because your header can't lean on a job title, a two-line objective at the top does useful work: it says who you are, what you're studying, and what you want. Keep it specific to the internship — a generic "seeking to leverage my skills" line wastes the most valuable space on the page.

Objective
  • Second-year Computer Science student seeking a Summer 2026 software engineering internship. Comfortable with Python, React, and SQL from coursework and three shipped side projects; eager to contribute to a real product team.

Put the whole page together

Here's the section order that works for almost every student internship resume. Keep it to one page.

  1. Contact header — name, email, phone, city, and a LinkedIn or portfolio link. What goes here.
  2. Objective — two lines, tailored to the internship.
  3. Education — degree, expected date, GPA (if strong), relevant coursework.
  4. Projects — two or three, with achievement bullets.
  5. Experience / Activities — jobs, campus roles, volunteering.
  6. Skills — the tools and languages named in the posting that you actually know.
Tailor it to each posting. Internships are competitive and many are screened by an applicant tracking system first. Pull the exact skills and keywords from the listing and make sure they appear where they're true on your resume. Doing this well — matching your resume to the job description — matters more for interns than for anyone, because you have fewer signals to stand on.

Frequently asked questions

How do I write a resume for an internship with no experience?

Lead with what you have. Put education near the top, then fill the page with coursework, class and personal projects, volunteering, clubs, and any part-time jobs — each written as an achievement bullet with an action verb, a tool, and a result. Intern recruiters expect little professional history; they're looking for initiative and evidence you can learn, not a long job list.

What sections should an internship resume have?

Contact header, a short objective, education (near the top for students), then whichever you can fill: relevant coursework, projects, experience/activities, and skills. Order them so your strongest material comes first — for most students that's education and projects ahead of work experience.

How long should an internship resume be?

One page. Recruiters spend seconds on each resume, and weak filler hurts more than a short page does. If you're struggling to fill it, add a relevant-coursework line, a projects section, and more detail on your activities rather than padding margins or fonts.

Should I include a GPA on an internship resume?

Include it if it's about 3.5 or above, or if the posting asks. A strong GPA is real evidence when your work history is thin. If it's lower, leave it off and let projects, coursework, and skills carry the resume — or list a higher major GPA if that's more flattering than your overall one.
