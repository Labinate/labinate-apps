How to list a license on a resume

For a licensed role — nurse, CPA, engineer, teacher, attorney, real-estate agent — the license isn't a bonus credential; it's the thing that legally lets you hold the job. That makes it a gating requirement: both the applicant-tracking system and the human recruiter check for it first, before your experience gets a look. So a license follows a different rule than a certification — surface it in the top third, format it exactly as it verifies, and be scrupulous about its status.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

You're a qualified RN with five years on a med-surg floor, and your license is sitting in a "Credentials" line at the very bottom of page two. To the recruiter skimming forty applicants for one opening — and to the ATS filtering on "RN license required" — you might read as someone who doesn't have it. The license is your strongest single qualifier for a licensed job, and the most common mistake is treating it like a footnote. This guide is about placing it, formatting it, and stating its status so it does the work it should.

The core idea: a license is permission to practice, verified against a public board. That makes it two things at once — your most important qualification for the role, and the most easily fact-checked line on your resume. Play both: make it impossible to miss, and make every word of it true.

First, is it a license or a certification?

These get used interchangeably, but they behave differently on a resume, and the difference drives everything below.

SituationHow it differs
A certificationA voluntary credential from a professional body (PMP, AWS, SHRM-CP, CompTIA) that strengthens you but isn't legally required to do the job. It can usually live in a credentials section lower on the page. A license is legal permission to practice — often a hard gate — so it earns top-third placement the certification doesn't.
Your degreeA license is separate from the degree, even when the degree is a prerequisite for it. Your BSN and your RN license are two different lines; list both. The degree proves you studied; the license proves you're currently cleared to practice.
Awards & publicationsThose are achievements — evidence you did notable work. A license isn't an achievement; it's a permission-to-work status. Don't file it under accomplishments; give it its own credential line where a screener expects to find it.
CourseworkCoursework is a student's stand-in for experience. A license is a professional's proof of legal standing. If you have the license, it outranks coursework entirely — lead with it.

Step 1 — Place it by how much the job needs it

Placement isn't one rule; it depends on whether the license gates the role. Read the posting: if it says "active RN license required" or "must hold a valid CPA," the license is a screening criterion and belongs up top.

License is required for the job → top third, twice

Name it in your headline or summary (Registered Nurse (RN), TX · 5 yrs med-surg) and list it in full in a dedicated Licenses & Certifications section placed high — above or right after experience. The recruiter and the ATS both check for it before reading further; don't make them hunt.

License is a plus, not required → credentials section lower down

If the license strengthens you but isn't demanded (a nurse applying to a non-clinical health role, say), one clean line in a licenses & certifications section further down is enough. Don't crowd the top third with a credential the job doesn't screen on.

Multiple licenses → most-relevant and highest first

Lead with the license the job requires, then others in descending relevance. A multi-state nurse leads with the compact/eNLC privilege; a dual-licensed CPA/attorney leads with whichever the posting names. Don't dump all of them in issue-date order — order by what this employer cares about.

Step 2 — Format it exactly as it verifies

A license entry should give a recruiter — and a verifier — everything the state board record shows, in the order they'd expect. Four fields do the job.

FieldWhat to write
Type / titleRegistered Nurse (RN) · Certified Public Accountant (CPA) · Professional Engineer (PE)
JurisdictionState of Texas · California Board of Accountancy
StatusActive · Active, in good standing
Expiration / renewalExp. 08/2027 · Renews 2026

A clean line reads: Registered Nurse (RN) — Texas, Active, exp. 08/2027. Add the license number only when the field or posting expects it (nursing, real estate, and insurance often do; most others don't) — and think twice before publishing a number on a resume you post openly online, where it can be scraped. License #: available on request is a fine compromise. If you hold a multistate or compact privilege (the nursing eNLC, for example), say so — it means you can work across member states without re-licensing, and that's a real hiring advantage worth surfacing.

Step 3 — Tell the truth about status

This is where a license is unlike almost anything else on your resume: it's verified against a public board in seconds. Inflating it doesn't just risk an awkward moment — in a regulated field it can end the application outright and mark you as a integrity risk. State the real status precisely.

Honest and specific

  • RN — TX, Active, exp. 08/2027
  • CPA candidate — 3 of 4 sections passed
  • License pending — exam scheduled Mar 2026
  • PE — inactive; reinstatement available

Misleading — avoid

  • Listing a pending license as "Active"
  • Showing an expired license with no status
  • Claiming a license in a state where you aren't licensed
  • Implying a certification is a license

A license in progress is common and often welcome — many new-graduate and entry postings expect you to license shortly after hire. Just label it as pending, not active. And if you're relocating and need to license in a new state, note the plan honestly (RN — OH, Active; TX licensure by endorsement in progress); that ties into handling relocation on your resume.

A few special cases

Reciprocity / endorsement when moving states

Many licenses transfer between states by endorsement or reciprocity. If your current license is active but you're applying in a new state, say both: the active license you hold and the endorsement in progress. It reassures the employer you'll be cleared to practice locally without claiming a license you don't yet have.

Expired or inactive license you're not using

If a lapsed license isn't relevant to the job, you can leave it off — a resume is a marketing document, not a full record. If it is relevant and you plan to reinstate, list it with its true status ("inactive; eligible for reinstatement") rather than hiding the lapse or implying it's live.

Roles where the license is the whole ballgame

For nursing, real estate, insurance, cosmetology, teaching, and the trades, the license often is the qualification a recruiter screens on. In those fields, put it in the summary line and give it a prominent section — treat it with the weight you'd give your job title, because to the reader it carries the same information.

The bright line — never list a license you don't currently hold as active. A license record is public and instant to check, so a "CPA" you never earned, an RN license you let lapse, or a state you're not actually licensed in doesn't survive first contact — and in a regulated profession a false credential claim can end the application and follow you professionally. The honest versions do the same job: "pending," "candidate," "in progress," "inactive," or "by endorsement in progress" are all legitimate and often expected. State what's true, precisely — that's not a weakness here, it's the credential doing its job.

Quick reference

  • License ≠ certification → a license is legal permission to practice and often gates the role; a certification strengthens you but usually sits lower.
  • Placement → if the job requires it, name it in the summary and a dedicated section in the top third; if it's only a plus, a credentials line lower down is fine.
  • Format → type, jurisdiction, status, expiration — e.g. RN — Texas, Active, exp. 08/2027.
  • License number → include only when the field expects it; be cautious about publishing it on open job boards.
  • Multistate / compact → surface it; it's a genuine advantage.
  • Status honesty → "pending," "candidate," "inactive," "by endorsement" are all legitimate; never label a non-active license "Active."
  • Bright line → never claim a license you don't hold — a board lookup is instant.

Frequently asked questions

Where do you put a license on a resume?

If the license is required to do the job — a nursing, CPA, teaching, real-estate, or engineering license, for example — put it where it's seen first: name it in your headline or summary at the top of the page, and list it in full in a dedicated "Licenses & Certifications" section near the top. A recruiter screening a stack of candidates for a licensed role is scanning for that credential before reading your experience, and the ATS may be filtering on it too, so burying it at the bottom is a real risk. If the license is only a nice-to-have for the role, a single line in a credentials section lower down is fine.

What information should you include when listing a license?

Format it the way it verifies against the issuing board: the license type or title (e.g., Registered Nurse, Certified Public Accountant, Professional Engineer), the state or jurisdiction that issued it, the status (Active), and the expiration or renewal date. Add the license number only when the field or posting expects it — nursing and real-estate listings often do; many others don't — and avoid posting a number on a public job board where it can be scraped. If you hold a multistate or compact privilege (such as the nursing eNLC), note it, because it's a genuine hiring advantage.

Should you put your license number on a resume?

Only when it helps and it's safe. In some fields — nursing, real estate, insurance — employers expect the number and it speeds verification, so include it. In many others it adds nothing a recruiter needs at the resume stage. Be cautious about publishing a license number on a public job board or a resume you post openly online, since it can be used to look you up or impersonate you; it's fine to write "License #: available on request" or to share it once you're in a real hiring conversation.

How do you list a license that's still in progress or pending?

Label its real status honestly and specifically — never imply it's already active. Write "License pending — exam scheduled March 2026," "Eligible for licensure, application submitted," or "CPA candidate (3 of 4 sections passed)." This is common and often acceptable for entry-level and new-graduate roles where employers expect you to license shortly after hire. What you can't do is list a pending or expired license as "Active," because the status is verified against the state board in seconds and any inflation collapses immediately.

What's the difference between a license and a certification on a resume?

A license is a legal permission to practice, issued by a government or state board — without it you can't legally hold the job (a registered nurse, a CPA signing audits, a licensed teacher, a practicing attorney). A certification is a voluntary credential from a professional body that strengthens your candidacy but isn't legally required. That difference changes placement: a required license often gates the role, so it belongs in the top third where it can't be missed, while most certifications can sit in a credentials section lower down. Verification stakes are higher too — a license is checked against a public board, so honesty about type, jurisdiction, and status matters more here than almost anywhere on the resume.
