Resume templates: why most backfire

A polished template feels like a shortcut to a professional resume. But the features that make templates look impressive — sidebars, two columns, icons, text boxes — are the exact things that scramble the software reading your resume first. The best “template” is often the plainest one.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Here’s the uncomfortable truth about most downloadable resume templates: the prettier they are, the more likely they are to hurt you. Recruiters rarely see your resume in its original form — an applicant tracking system (ATS) reads it first by extracting the text into a database. A design-heavy template can turn that extraction into garbage before a human ever opens it.

Why most templates backfire

Two columns and sidebars

The most common “modern” template layout. A parser reads left-to-right, top-to-bottom, so a two-column design gets interleaved into nonsense — your job titles land inside your skills list. The single most reliable way to break an ATS.

Text boxes and tables

Templates use these to place elements precisely. Many parsers skip the contents of a text box entirely, so your contact details or a whole section can silently vanish from the machine-read copy.

Icons, graphics, and skill bars

An icon next to your phone number is an image with no text. A “90% proficient” skill bar carries zero information a computer can read. They look modern and communicate nothing to the system doing the first screen.

Photos and unusual fonts

A photo is an unreadable image that also forces a column layout to fit around it (see photo on a resume). Non-standard embedded fonts can extract as garbled characters (see resume font and size).

What a genuinely safe template looks like

Plainer than you’d guess, and that’s the point:

  • One column, full width. Everything reads top to bottom in the order a parser expects.
  • Real text, everywhere. Name, headings, contact details — all selectable text, never inside an image or box.
  • Standard section headings. “Experience,” “Education,” “Skills” — the words parsers are built to find. Not “Where I’ve Made an Impact.”
  • A common font at a readable size, generous margins, and clear whitespace between sections. That’s the whole design — the format guide covers the rest.

A resume like this looks less flashy on screen — and gets read by both the software and the human. Design is not what gets you the interview; a clean, relevant, machine-readable resume is.

Free vs paid templates

You don’t need to pay for a resume template. The free Google Docs and Word templates are fine if you pick a simple single-column one and strip anything decorative. Paid template marketplaces mostly sell visual polish — the two-column, icon-heavy designs that are exactly what you should avoid for any resume going through an online application. Spend the money you’d spend on a template on nothing, and put the time into the words instead.

The test before you use any template. Open it, select all, and copy it into a plain text editor. If the result reads in a sensible order with nothing missing or scrambled, the template is safe. If your columns interleave or a section disappears, so will it in the ATS — pick a plainer one.

Frequently asked questions

Are resume templates bad?

Not all of them, but most of the popular ones are. The two-column, sidebar, icon-heavy templates that look impressive are exactly what breaks applicant tracking systems, because a parser reads top-to-bottom and interleaves columns into nonsense or skips text boxes entirely. A plain single-column template with real text is safe; a design-heavy one can get your resume misread before a human sees it.

What is the best resume template to use?

The plainest one that works: a single full-width column, real selectable text everywhere, standard headings like Experience and Education, a common font at a readable size, and generous margins. It looks less flashy than a designer template but reads cleanly for both the ATS and the recruiter — and that’s what actually gets you the interview.

Do I need to pay for a resume template?

No. Free single-column templates in Google Docs or Word are perfectly good once you strip anything decorative, and a tool that builds a parser-safe layout for you is free too. Paid marketplaces mostly sell visual polish — often the exact two-column, icon-heavy designs you should avoid for online applications. Put the effort into the wording instead.

Why does my resume look wrong after using a template?

Usually because the template uses columns, text boxes, or tables to position elements. When the file is opened, copied, or read by an ATS, those structures don’t hold — columns interleave, boxed text drops out, and the layout collapses. Test any template by selecting all and pasting into a plain text editor: if the order is scrambled or content is missing, switch to a simpler single-column layout.
