How to list a co-op on a resume

A co-op isn't a class — it's a full-time, paid, months-long job that happened to alternate with your degree. Yet the reflex is to tuck it under Education as a "Cooperative Education Program" line, quietly demoting real professional work into a school detail. For a new grad, the co-op is often the single strongest thing on the page. The fix: put it in Work Experience with a real title and quantified bullets — and consolidate repeat terms at one employer into one entry.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Cooperative education — a co-op — sends you into a company full-time for four to eight months, then back to school, then out again, often two or three times before you graduate. Schools built around it (Northeastern, Waterloo, Drexel, Cincinnati, Georgia Tech) turn it into a credential. But on a resume it lands in an awkward spot: it's part of your degree, so it feels like education, yet it's paid full-time work, so it belongs with your jobs. Put it in the wrong place and you throw away the best evidence you have that you can do the work. This guide shows where a co-op goes and how to format it so it reads as the professional experience it is.

The core idea: a co-op is employment, not a school line. It goes in Work Experience with a real title, real dates, and quantified bullets — exactly like any job. Burying it under Education demotes months of real work; splitting several terms at one employer into separate short entries manufactures job-hopping out of a program designed around coming back.

First, know what this is not

A co-op sits next to several student-experience situations, and treating it like one of them is how it ends up in the wrong section. The distinction that matters: a co-op is full-time, paid employment that you return to school between — it produces real work to show, not credit to report.

An internshipAn internship is usually shorter — often a single summer — and sometimes part-time or unpaid. A co-op is typically a full-time, paid, multi-month placement built into your degree, often repeated. Both go in Work Experience, but a co-op usually gave you deeper ownership of real work, so lean into the scope rather than flattening it into "intern duties."
An apprenticeshipAn apprenticeship is structured training toward a trade qualification or license — you earn a credential in the field. A co-op is a degree-program work placement for degree-track students; there's no trade license at the end, just real professional experience and your diploma.
A rotational programA rotational program is a post-graduation corporate track — you're a permanent hire moving through functions after your degree. A co-op happens during your degree: you return to school between terms, and you're a student on a placement, not a full-time permanent employee.
Study abroadStudy abroad earns academic credit inside your degree and belongs in Education. A co-op is work, not coursework — even though it's degree-integrated, the placement produced a job's worth of results, so it goes in Work Experience.
A part-time student jobA part-time campus job fits around classes. A co-op is the opposite arrangement: you pause school and work full-time for the term. That full-time, focused block is exactly why a co-op carries more weight than the hours you picked up on the side.

Step 1 — Put the co-op in Work Experience, not Education

This is the whole decision. A co-op is a job you did full-time for a real employer, so it goes in your Work Experience section, formatted like any other role. Demoting it to a one-line note under your degree is the costly, common mistake — it takes the strongest evidence a new grad has and hides it where a reader skims past.

Instead ofWrite
EDUCATION
BS Computer Science, Northeastern
 • Cooperative Education Program, 2022–24		WORK EXPERIENCE
Software Engineering Co-op — Acme Robotics
Boston, MA · Jan–Aug 2023
 • Shipped X, cutting Y by 30%…

The left version reads as "a student who took part in a program." The right version reads as "someone who built and shipped real software for eight months." Same eight months — but only one of them lets a reader see you doing the job. Keep a brief mention of the co-op program under your Education if you like, but the work itself lives up in Work Experience.

Step 2 — Use a real title with "Co-op" in it, and format like a job

Give the placement the actual title you held, with the word "Co-op" in it — "Software Engineering Co-op," "Marketing Co-op," "Mechanical Engineering Co-op." That one word does real work: it tells a reader that a four-to-eight-month full-time stint was a structured, time-boxed student placement, so a short tenure reads as the program working as designed rather than a job you quit. Then format it exactly like any role — employer, location, dates, and two or three bullets.

Reads as real experience

  • "Software Engineering Co-op — Acme Robotics · Jan–Aug 2023"
  • Full-time dates and location, like any job
  • Bullets on what you built, shipped, or improved
  • Numbers that show the scope you owned

Undersells it

  • A bare "Co-op, 2023" line with no title or employer
  • Hiding it under Education as a program note
  • "Assisted with…" duty bullets instead of results
  • Dropping "Co-op," so a 5-month stint reads as churn

The point of naming it "Co-op" isn't to diminish the work — it's to protect it. Without that word, a reader glancing at a run of short full-time roles might read job-hopping; with it, they read a student who kept landing real placements.

Step 3 — Quantify what you actually delivered

A full-time co-op usually hands you a real slice of production work — a feature to ship, a process to fix, a dataset to build — which is exactly what separates a co-op from "just a student thing" in a reader's mind. So treat the bullets like any job's: lead with a strong verb, say what you did, and put a number on it.

Show the real work, with real numbers

A co-op is your best chance to prove you can operate in a workplace, not just a classroom. "Built and shipped an internal dashboard used by 40 warehouse staff, cutting daily reporting time by 2 hours" says far more than "helped the analytics team" — the numbers show you owned something that mattered.

Keep the title and scope honest

Use the title you genuinely held and describe the scope you genuinely owned. If you were a co-op on the platform team, say so — inflating a co-op into "Senior Engineer" or claiming you "led" a team you supported turns a real, verifiable strength into a claim that collapses on one interview question. The placement is impressive without padding.

Step 4 — Consolidate repeat terms at one employer

Co-op programs are built around going back — many students do two, three, or four terms, often returning to the same company. That return is a strength: it means they kept wanting you. But if you split each term into its own short entry, you manufacture the exact impression a co-op is designed to prevent — a stack of four-month "jobs" that looks like churn.

Instead ofWrite
Co-op, Acme · Jan–Apr 2022
Co-op, Acme · Sep–Dec 2022
Co-op, Acme · May–Aug 2023		Acme Robotics — Engineering Co-op
2022–2023 · 3 terms
 • Progressed from X to owning Y…

One employer, one entry, with the full span and a "3 terms" note — then let the bullets show how your responsibility grew each time. If your terms were at different employers, that's genuine breadth, so each gets its own entry; the "Co-op" label on each keeps the pattern legible as a program rather than instability. Either way, you're presenting a coherent story, not a scatter of short stints.

The bright line — never bury a co-op in the Education section. A co-op is full-time paid employment, and for most new grads it's the strongest proof on the page that they can do the work. It belongs in Work Experience with a real title, real dates, and quantified bullets. Name the co-op program in Education as context if you want, but don't let that note stand in for the real entry — and don't double-count the same work in both places.

Quick reference

  • Work Experience, not Education → a co-op is a real job; list it where your jobs go.
  • Real title with "Co-op" in it → the word signals a structured, time-boxed placement, so a short full-time stint doesn't read as churn.
  • Quantify like any role → strong verb, what you did, a number; a co-op hands you real work to show.
  • Consolidate repeat terms at one employer → one entry, full span, "3 terms," progression in the bullets — being invited back is a strength.
  • Program in Education is context, not the entry → mention it under your degree, but keep the work up top; don't double-count.
  • The bright line → never demote a co-op to a school line; it's employment, and often your best.

Once each co-op reads like the job it was, the rest is ordinary craft: turn duties into accomplishments, quantify them, and lead with strong action verbs. Frequently asked questions

Where does a co-op go on a resume — Work Experience or Education?

Work Experience. A co-op is full-time paid employment where you did real work for a real employer over several months — that is a job, and it belongs in the Work Experience section with a title, an employer, dates, and quantified bullets like any other role. Burying it under Education as a one-line "Cooperative Education Program" note is the most common and most costly mistake, because it quietly demotes months of professional work into a school detail. For a new graduate, a co-op is frequently the single strongest thing on the page — the one place a reader can see you delivering in a workplace rather than a classroom — so it should be presented at full weight, not tucked away. You can still mention the co-op program in Education as context, but the work itself lives in Work Experience.

How do I format a co-op entry?

Format it exactly like a job, with one small signal added. Put the real title you held, with the word "Co-op" in it, then the employer, location, and dates: for example "Software Engineering Co-op, Acme Robotics, Boston MA, Jan–Aug 2023", followed by two or three accomplishment bullets with numbers. The word "Co-op" in the title matters because it tells a reader that a four-to-eight-month full-time stint was a structured, time-boxed student placement rather than a job you left quickly — so a short tenure reads as the program working as designed, not as instability. Then treat the bullets like any role: what you built, shipped, or improved, and the measurable result.

Is a co-op the same as an internship on a resume?

They're listed the same way — both go in Work Experience with a real title and quantified bullets — but a co-op usually carries more weight, and it's worth letting it. An internship tends to be shorter, often a single summer, and sometimes part-time or unpaid; a co-op is typically a full-time, paid, multi-month placement built into your degree, and students frequently do more than one. That means a co-op often gave you deeper ownership of real production work than a summer internship, so lean into the scope and results rather than flattening it into "intern duties". Using "Co-op" in the title, rather than relabeling it "Intern", preserves that distinction for a reader who knows what a co-op involves.

I did several co-op terms at the same company — separate entries or one?

One consolidated entry. If you did two, three, or four work terms at the same employer, splitting them into separate short entries manufactures the exact impression a co-op is built to prevent — a run of four-month "jobs" that looks like churn even though you never really left. Instead, give the employer one entry with the full span ("Acme Robotics — Engineering Co-op, 2022–2024, 3 terms") and let the bullets show progression, because being invited back term after term is itself a strength: it says the company kept wanting you. If your terms were at different employers, then each gets its own entry — that's genuine breadth, and the "Co-op" label on each keeps the pattern legible as a program rather than instability.

Should I name the co-op program, like Northeastern or Waterloo?

You can, in the Education section, as one line of context — but don't let it replace the Work Experience entry. Naming a recognized co-op program ("Cooperative Education Program" under your degree) tells a reader the placements were part of a structured, often competitive track, which adds a little credibility, especially at schools known for co-op. The mistake is double-counting: putting the program in Education and also treating that Education line as where the co-op "lives", so the actual work never gets a real entry. Keep the division clean — the program is a note under your degree, and each co-op placement is a real job in Work Experience with its own title, dates, and results.
