What is a resume?

A resume is a concise, targeted document — usually one page — that summarizes your relevant skills, experience, and education for a specific job. But the most useful way to understand it isn't by its parts; it's by its purpose. A resume is a marketing document, and its one job is to win you an interview — not to get you the job outright, and not to tell your whole story. Grasp that, and every other resume decision gets easier.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Almost everyone will write one, and almost no one is taught what it actually is. People treat a resume like an autobiography, a list of every job they've held, or a form to fill in — and then wonder why it doesn't work. A resume isn't any of those. It's a short, deliberately selective argument, aimed at one reader who will spend seconds on it, that says: I can do this specific job, and here's the evidence. Once you see it as a targeted pitch rather than a complete record, the rules that seem arbitrary — keep it to a page, tailor it to each job, cut the old stuff, quantify everything — all start to make sense. This guide gives you the plain-English definition, what goes in one, and how it differs from the documents people confuse it with.

The core idea: A resume is a concise, targeted marketing document that summarizes your relevant skills, experience, and education for one specific job — and its single job is to earn you an interview. It is selective, not exhaustive; a pitch, not an autobiography. That one idea explains every other rule about resumes.

The definition, unpacked

Take the definition one piece at a time, because each word is doing work:

  • Concise — usually one page (two if you're genuinely experienced). A resume earns attention by being short and relevant, not by being complete. See how long a resume should be.
  • Targeted — aimed at a specific job. The same person writes a different resume for a different role, leading with whatever that role cares about. A generic copy sent everywhere is the most common mistake there is.
  • Summarizes — it condenses and selects. You choose the experience that proves you can do this job and leave out the rest; you're not obligated to include everything you've ever done.
  • Skills, experience, education — the substance a hiring reader is scanning for: what you can do, what you've done, and how you're trained.
  • To win an interview — the purpose. A resume is a screening document. It doesn't get you hired; it gets you a conversation.

What a resume is for (the part most people miss)

A resume has exactly one job: to get you an interview. When you apply, an employer — often with applicant-tracking software doing a first pass — skims your resume to decide, quickly, whether you're worth a closer look. That's the whole function. Two consequences follow, and they settle most of the arguments people have about resumes:

Because its job is to earn a "yes, let's talk"

  • Include what proves you can do this job
  • Lead with your strongest, most relevant evidence
  • Tailor it to each role you apply for
  • Write results, not duties — give a reader something to react to

Because it is not your life story

  • Cut jobs and details that don't help the case
  • Don't try to be complete — be relevant
  • It won't get you hired; the interview does that
  • Length is a tool, not a virtue — shorter and sharper wins

This is the shift that makes everything click. A resume isn't a record you owe the world; it's a pitch you make to one reader who is asking a single question — "can this person do this job?" — and who will decide in seconds. Everything on the page either helps answer that question or gets in its way.

What goes in a resume

A standard resume is built from a small set of expected sections. You don't need all of them — you need the ones that make your case — but a reader knows where to look for each:

SectionWhat it does
Contact informationName, email, phone, city, and links (LinkedIn, portfolio). Sits at the top so a reader can reach you — the one section that must be complete and correct.
Summary (optional)Two or three lines that frame who you are for this role. Useful when your fit isn't obvious from the job titles alone; skippable when it is.
Work experienceThe core of the resume. Each job listed newest-first with title, employer, dates, and a few accomplishment bullets — what you did and what resulted, ideally with numbers.
EducationDegree, school, and year. Leads the resume for students and new grads; drops below experience once you have a work history.
SkillsThe tools and abilities the job asks for — a scannable list that also helps you pass automated keyword screens.
Optional sectionsProjects, certifications, volunteer work, publications — added when they strengthen the case for a particular role.

The order and emphasis shift with your situation — that's what resume format is about — but this is the shared vocabulary. If you know these sections, you know the anatomy of a resume.

What a resume is not

A lot of resume trouble comes from confusing it with a neighboring document. Here's the difference between a resume and the four things people most often mix it up with:

A CVA CV is long and exhaustive — a complete academic and professional record (publications, research, teaching, grants) that can run many pages. A resume is short and selective. In the US, "resume" is the default for industry jobs and "CV" is mainly academic; in much of the world "CV" is the everyday word for a resume. Use a CV only when a research, academic, or international application asks for one.
A cover letterA cover letter is the narrative — a short message that argues, in prose, why you fit this role. The resume is the evidence that backs the argument up. They work as a pair: the letter makes the case, the resume proves it.
A job applicationAn application is the employer's own form — the fields and questions they make you fill in. Your resume is the document you bring to it. The application is their paperwork; the resume is your pitch.
A LinkedIn profileLinkedIn is public, always-on, and general — how people find you and get the broad picture. A resume is private and targeted — tailored to one job and submitted for it. Keep both: the profile for discovery, the resume for the application.
The bright line — a resume is a targeted pitch, not a complete record. It exists to make one fast reader want to interview you for one specific job. So it's concise, it's tailored, it leads with relevant evidence, and it leaves out whatever doesn't help. Every rule people find fussy — one page, tailor it, quantify it, cut the old stuff — is just this one idea applied. Get the purpose right and the rest is mechanics.

Quick reference

  • Definition → a concise, targeted document summarizing your relevant skills, experience, and education for a specific job.
  • Its one job → win you an interview. It's a screening document, not the thing that gets you hired.
  • Its nature → a marketing pitch, selective and tailored — not an autobiography or a complete history.
  • What's in it → contact info, optional summary, work experience (accomplishment bullets), education, skills, plus optional sections that help.
  • How long → usually one page; two only if you're genuinely experienced.
  • Not a CV → a CV is long and exhaustive; a resume is short and selective.

Frequently asked questions

What is a resume, in simple terms?

A resume is a short document — usually one page — that summarizes your relevant skills, work experience, and education so an employer can quickly decide whether to interview you for a specific job. The clearest way to think about it is by its purpose: a resume is a marketing document, and its one job is to win you an interview, not to get you the job outright and not to tell your entire life story. Everything a good resume does follows from that. It's targeted to the role you're applying for, selective about what it includes, and structured so a recruiter can scan it in seconds. If you remember one thing, remember that a resume argues "I can do this specific job, here's the evidence" — and then hands the decision to a human who reads it fast.

What should a resume include?

A standard resume has a small set of expected sections. Contact information at the top (name, email, phone, city, and links like LinkedIn or a portfolio). An optional summary — two or three lines that frame who you are for this role. Work experience, the core of the resume, listed newest-first with each job's title, employer, dates, and a few accomplishment bullets that say what you did and what resulted, ideally with numbers. Education (degree, school, year). And a skills section listing the tools and abilities the job asks for. Depending on your situation you might add projects, certifications, volunteer work, or publications. You don't need every section — you need the ones that prove you can do the job you're applying to, arranged so the strongest evidence is easy to find.

What is the difference between a resume and a CV?

A resume is short and targeted — usually one page, tailored to a specific job, and deliberately selective. A CV (curriculum vitae) is long and exhaustive — a complete record of your academic and professional history, including publications, research, teaching, grants, and presentations, and it can run many pages. In the United States, "resume" is the default for most industry jobs and "CV" is used mainly for academic, scientific, and medical roles. In much of Europe and the rest of the world, "CV" is often the everyday word for what Americans call a resume. The practical rule: for most jobs you want a concise, tailored resume; reach for a CV when a research, academic, or international application specifically asks for one.

What is a resume used for?

A resume is used to get you an interview. When you apply for a job, an employer — often with help from applicant tracking software — reviews your resume to decide, quickly, whether you're worth a closer look. That's the entire function: it's a screening document. It is not the thing that gets you hired (interviews and offers do that), and it is not a place to record everything you've ever done. Understanding this narrows every decision: you include what helps a reader say "yes, let's talk," you cut what doesn't, and you tailor it to each role because the reader is asking "can this person do this job?" — not "what has this person done in general?"

What makes a good resume?

A good resume is targeted, evidence-based, and easy to scan. Targeted: it's tailored to the specific job, leading with the experience and skills that role cares about instead of a one-size-fits-all copy. Evidence-based: its bullets describe accomplishments with results and numbers ("cut processing time 30%") rather than listing duties ("responsible for processing"). Easy to scan: clean formatting, standard sections, and consistent structure so a recruiter who spends seconds on the first pass still finds the important things. Honest: everything on it is true and defensible in an interview. And appropriately short: usually one page (two if you're experienced), because a resume earns attention by being selective, not exhaustive.

Do I still need a resume if I have a LinkedIn profile?

In most cases, yes. A LinkedIn profile and a resume tell the same story, but they do different jobs. LinkedIn is public, always-on, and general — it's how people find you and get a broad sense of your background. A resume is a private, targeted document you submit for a specific role, tailored to that job and often required by the application itself or parsed by the employer's applicant tracking system. Many applications still ask for a resume file directly, and even when they let you "apply with LinkedIn," a focused, tailored resume usually makes a stronger case than a general profile. Keep both: the profile for discovery, the resume for the actual application.
