How does an ATS work?

You hit "apply," and your resume disappears into something called an applicant tracking system. The internet is full of scary claims about it — that a robot scores you, that 75% of resumes are auto-rejected, that one wrong keyword sinks you. Most of that is wrong. Here's what an ATS actually is, the four things it really does with your resume, and how to format so the software reads you correctly instead of garbling you.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

An applicant tracking system (ATS) is database software that companies use to collect and manage job applications. Think of it as a CRM for candidates: it's where recruiters keep every applicant, search through them, and move people along the hiring pipeline. It is not, for the most part, an AI that judges your worth and deletes you. Understanding the difference is what turns a resume from "invisible" to "found."

The four things an ATS actually does

Whatever the brand — Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, Taleo, iCIMS — the core job is the same. Here's the real lifecycle of your resume after you apply.

Step 1
Parse — it reads your file into fields

The moment you upload, the ATS tries to break your resume into structured data: name, contact info, each job title, employer, dates, and skills. This is where formatting matters most. If your resume is a clean, single-column document with standard headings, the parser maps everything correctly. If it's a two-column design, a table, or a scanned image, the parser can scramble your work history — putting your dates in the wrong field or losing a job entirely.

Step 2
Store — your data goes into a searchable database

The parsed fields are saved as a candidate profile. Your original file is kept too, but the searchable version is the parsed text. This is why a resume that looked perfect to your eye can still fail: if the parser couldn't read a section, that content simply isn't in the database to be found.

Step 3
Search — a recruiter queries the database by keyword

This is the real "filter," and it's a human doing it. A recruiter with 400 applicants doesn't read 400 resumes; they search the database for the terms that matter — "registered nurse," "Python," "CPA," a specific city. Candidates whose parsed resume contains those terms surface; those who used different wording, or whose text didn't parse, don't. You aren't rejected — you're never retrieved.

Step 4
Rank — some systems show a match score

Some (not all) ATS platforms show recruiters a rough match percentage between a resume and the job description. It's a convenience for sorting, not an automatic gate — the recruiter decides who to read. A few systems also apply knockout questions you answered in the application ("Do you have a valid license?") that can filter you before a human looks. Those are answers you gave, not a secret judgment of your resume.

The myth vs. the reality

The mythWhat actually happens
A robot scores my resume and rejects itA recruiter searches a database; unmatched resumes are just never seen

I need to stuff keywords to beat the botKeyword stuffing is obvious to the human who reads the shortlist; use real terms in real bullets
White-text hidden keywords trick the ATSRecruiters see the rendered resume; it reads as deception and gets you cut

The takeaway: the ATS isn't your adversary. Bad formatting and vague wording are. Fix those two things and the software works for you.

How to format so the ATS reads you correctly

Everything that helps a parser also helps the human who reads you later. There's no trade-off.

  • One column. Multi-column and table layouts are the single biggest cause of scrambled parsing. Keep the whole resume in a single flowing column.
  • Standard section headings. Use "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills" — not clever labels like "Where I've Made an Impact." The parser looks for the standard ones.
  • A text-based PDF or .docx. Never a scanned image or a screenshot — the parser can't read pixels. Export real, selectable text.
  • Real keywords in real bullets. Mirror the exact terms from the job description where they honestly apply. Say "Managed Google Ads campaigns," not "handled digital marketing," if the job asks for Google Ads.
  • No critical text in headers, footers, or graphics. Many parsers ignore the header/footer region; keep your name and contact info in the body.
  • Standard fonts, simple bullets. Skip icons, symbols, and decorative characters that a parser may drop or mangle.
The one-line rule: a resume built to be read clearly by a busy person is automatically the one an ATS parses best. You don't format for the machine and the human separately — clean, single-column, keyword-honest wins both. If you want to check whether your resume is ATS-friendly, the fastest test is whether you can copy-paste it into plain text and still read every section in order.

Frequently asked questions

How does an applicant tracking system work?

An ATS is database software recruiters use to manage applications. When you apply it parses your resume into fields, stores that data in a searchable database, lets recruiters search and filter it by keyword, and sometimes shows a match score. It's a filing and search tool, not an AI judge — a human still reads the resumes a search surfaces.

Does an ATS automatically reject resumes?

Almost never. What filters you is usually a knockout question you answered, or a recruiter searching for keywords and never seeing resumes that don't match or didn't parse. You're overlooked, not robot-rejected — and clean formatting plus relevant keywords fixes it.

How do I make my resume ATS-friendly?

Single column, standard font, standard section headings, saved as a text-based PDF or .docx (never an image). Put real keywords from the job into your actual bullets, and avoid tables, text boxes, and header/footer text for anything the parser must read.

Do all companies use an ATS?

Most medium and large ones do; very small or referral hires may not. But a parser-safe resume also reads well to a human, so assume one's in the pipeline — it costs you nothing and protects you either way.
