How to list an apprenticeship on a resume

The instinct is to file it under Education, next to your schooling. That's the one mistake to avoid. An apprenticeship is paid, supervised, on-the-job employment that also earns a credential — so it leads under Work Experience, with a real job title and bullets, and the credential gets its own line.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

An apprenticeship confuses people on a resume because it does two things at once: it teaches you a trade and it employs you to do it. Faced with that, most people default to the "learning" half and drop it under Education — right beside a degree or a bootcamp. But that buries the part an employer actually wants. In a registered apprenticeship you held a real job title, drew a real wage on a rising scale, and logged thousands of hours of supervised work. That's not a class you attended — it's a job you did, with a credential attached. Placed as a job, it's one of the strongest entries a working candidate can put on a page.

The core idea: an apprenticeship is experience + credential, in that order. The work goes under Work Experience with a genuine job title (Electrical Apprentice), employer or union sponsor, dates, and quantified bullets. The credential you earn by finishing — journeyworker status, a completion certificate, a registration number — goes on its own line under Licenses & Certifications. Lead with the job, not the schooling.

Why it goes under Work Experience, not Education

The placement decision is the whole game, and it turns on one fact: you were paid to do the job. A degree and a bootcamp are things you studied; an apprenticeship is something you did, for money, under a supervisor, for a long time. Recruiters read the two sections differently — Education answers "what were you trained in," Work Experience answers "what can you already do." An apprenticeship answers the second question, so it belongs there.

FormatWhat it is, and where it goes
DegreeAcademic study, no wage, a diploma at the end. Goes under Education.
BootcampShort, usually self-funded, classroom-based, no wage, no formal credential. Goes under Education or Training.
ApprenticeshipLong, paid on a rising scale, structured on-the-job work plus instruction, earns a recognized credential. Goes under Work Experience.
InternshipShort, exploratory, sometimes unpaid. A brief experience entry — see internship resume.

The tell is simple: if it paid you a wage and you clocked real hours doing the work, it's experience. The classroom component (the "related instruction" that comes with a registered program) can earn a short Education note, but it never leads — the same way you'd list a job first and its training second, not the reverse.

How to write the job entry

Treat it exactly like any other job, because it was one. Give it a title, a sponsor, dates, and bullets that quantify the work — apprenticeships are unusually easy to quantify because they run on logged hours and graded competencies.

Use the real occupational title — with "Apprentice" in it

Electrical Apprentice, Apprentice Machinist, Plumbing Apprentice, Apprentice Software Developer. If it was a registered program, add that as a qualifier — Registered Electrical Apprentice (IBEW/JATC) — because "registered" signals a formal, credentialed track, not an informal on-the-job arrangement.

Name the sponsor — employer, union, or JATC

Apprenticeships are sponsored by an employer, a union, or a joint apprenticeship & training committee. Put that name where the employer normally goes: Local 3 IBEW / Joint Apprenticeship Training Committee, or the contractor you worked under. It anchors the entry as real employment.

Bullets = the work, quantified

Lead each with an action verb and a number: "Logged 6,400+ OJT hours across commercial and industrial installs", "Wired and terminated panels on 12 build-outs under journeyman supervision", "Completed 288 hours of related classroom instruction with a 3.9 GPA." Skills, scale, safety record — the concrete work, not "learned about wiring."

If you're still in it, state the stage plainly

Registered Apprentice, Year 2 of 4 — or note hours toward completion: "3,200 of 8,000 OJT hours logged toward journeyworker." An in-progress apprenticeship is a strength, not a gap; it tells an employer you're on a credentialed track and already productive.

The credential is a separate line

Finishing an apprenticeship earns you something distinct from the work itself, and it deserves its own place on the page. Keep three ideas separate so none of them gets muddied.

The job → Work Experience

The apprenticeship entry, with title, sponsor, dates, and bullets. This is the experience.

The completion credential → Licenses & Certifications

When you complete, you become a journeyworker (journeyman) and receive a Certificate of Completion of Apprenticeship, often with a state or national registration number. That goes on its own line among your certifications — or folds into your new title: Journeyman Electrician. Your title graduates the day you finish; lead with it from then on.

Any state license → its own credential

Many trades require a state license (journeyman or master) that's distinct from the apprenticeship credential. If you hold one, list it as the license it is — number, issuing state, status — not as the same thing as the completion certificate.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode isn't padding — it's underselling. Filed as a course, the apprenticeship shrinks; filed as the job it was, it stands up next to any experience entry.

Reads as real experience

  • Under Work Experience, with a job title
  • Sponsor named; dates shown
  • Bullets with hours, counts, systems
  • Credential on its own Certifications line

Sells itself short

  • Buried under Education like a class
  • Titled vaguely — "trade training"
  • Duties, not results: "learned wiring"
  • Journeyworker status implied before earned

An apprenticeship is proof you can already do the work — paid, supervised, credentialed, and logged in hours. That's a claim most entry-level resumes can't make, so don't let it hide under Education. Put it where a hiring manager looks for what you can do, quantify the hours, and let the credential close the case.

Quick reference

  • It's experience, not a class → paid, supervised work goes under Work Experience, never Education.
  • Real job title → "Electrical Apprentice," add "Registered" if it was; sponsor as the employer.
  • Quantify the hours → OJT hours logged, systems worked, classroom hours — apprenticeships are easy to put numbers on.
  • Credential is separate → journeyworker status / completion certificate on a Certifications line; any state license is its own thing.
  • Mid-program is a strength → "Year 2 of 4" or hours toward completion; state the stage, don't hide it.
  • Don't overstate → never imply completion or a journeyman card you haven't earned — trade status is registered and checkable.

Keep sharpening the resume

With the apprenticeship placed as experience, tune the rest: keep a bootcamp and any certifications in their own lanes, list a trade license as the credential it is, and quantify every entry using achievement math. Frequently asked questions

Does an apprenticeship count as work experience on a resume?

Yes — that's the whole point, and it's what makes an apprenticeship different from a class or a bootcamp. During a registered apprenticeship you held a real job title, drew a real wage on a progressive scale, and logged thousands of hours doing the actual work under supervision. So it belongs under Work Experience, not Education: list it with a genuine job title like "Electrical Apprentice," the sponsoring employer or union, your dates, and accomplishment bullets that quantify what you did. Treating it as a course sells short the most valuable thing about it — that it's paid, credentialed, hands-on experience an employer can trust.

How do you write an apprenticeship job title on a resume?

Use the real occupational title with "Apprentice" in it: "Electrical Apprentice," "Apprentice Machinist," "Plumbing Apprentice," "Apprentice Software Developer." If it was a registered program, you can add that as a qualifier — "Registered Electrical Apprentice (IBEW/JATC)" — because "registered" signals a formal, credentialed program rather than an informal on-the-job arrangement. If you're still in it, state the stage: "Registered Apprentice, Year 2 of 4." Once you complete, your title graduates — you're a "Journeyman Electrician" or "Journeyworker," and that's the title to lead with going forward.

Where does the apprenticeship credential go on a resume?

The credential is separate from the work. The apprenticeship itself goes under Work Experience as a job; the credential you earn by completing it — a Certificate of Completion of Apprenticeship, journeyworker or journeyman status, or the state/national registration number — goes on its own line in a Licenses and Certifications section, or is folded into your job title. Many trades also lead to a state license after the apprenticeship (a journeyman or master license), which is a distinct credential of its own. Keep the three ideas distinct: the job (experience), the completion credential (certification), and any license (license).

How is an apprenticeship different from an internship or a bootcamp on a resume?

All three teach you a job, but they carry very different weight and sit in different places. A bootcamp is short, usually self-funded, classroom-based, and produces no wages and no formal credential — so it goes under Education or Training. An internship is a short, often exploratory stint, sometimes unpaid, meant to try out a field. An apprenticeship is longer, always paid on a rising wage scale, formally structured, and it earns a recognized credential — it is a real job with a training arc built in. That's why an apprenticeship leads under Work Experience with quantified bullets, while a bootcamp is a training line and an internship is a shorter experience entry. Don't flatten them into the same thing.

Should you list an apprenticeship you didn't finish?

Yes, list it — but as the paid work experience it was, not as a completed credential. If you left partway through, keep it under Work Experience with the job title, employer, and dates, and let the bullets show the skills and hours you did accumulate. Don't imply completion or claim journeyworker status you didn't earn, because in the trades that status is registered and verifiable. If you logged a meaningful number of hours, you can note them ("Completed 3,200 of 8,000 OJT hours toward journeyworker"), which reads as concrete progress rather than a gap. The work was real; present it honestly and it still counts.
