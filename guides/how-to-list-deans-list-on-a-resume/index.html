How to list Dean's List on a resume

You made the Dean's List, and it feels like something a resume should say. It is — but only in the right form and at the right stage. Dean's List is a per-term honor for a top GPA, so it's a verifiable signal of consistent academic performance while you're a student or recent grad, and it ages out like GPA once you have real work experience. The fix: put it as one line in your Education section, consolidate the semesters instead of listing each one, use your school's exact name for it, and let it retire once your job history carries the story.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Dean's List is one of the most common honors a student can put on a resume, and also one of the easiest to handle badly — repeated term by term until it eats half the Education section, kept on the page a decade after graduation, or listed so vaguely that a reader can't tell whether it was one semester or eight. It's genuinely worth including when you're early in your career: it's checkable on a transcript, it says you performed near the top of your class consistently, and for a candidate without much work history that's real evidence. The craft is in listing it cleanly, honestly, and only while it's still your strongest academic proof. This guide shows exactly how.

The core idea: Dean's List is a per-term academic honor for a top GPA (the threshold is set by your school — often the top 10-25% of the class or a fixed cutoff like a 3.5 semester GPA). It's verifiable and stage-dependent: strong evidence for a student or new grad, but it ages out like GPA once you have work experience. First, know what this is (and isn't)

Dean's List sits next to a few other academic lines, and it's easy to confuse what each one proves. The distinction that matters: Dean's List is recognition for a top GPA in a given term — a proxy for strong grades, tied to specific semesters, awarded by your institution on its own standard.

GPAA GPA is the raw number — your average across courses. Dean's List is a per-term honor a school grants for a top GPA that term. It's essentially a proxy for a strong semester GPA, which is why it's useful when your cumulative number is only okay but your best terms were excellent.
Awards & achievementsAwards is the broad category of recognition — competition wins, honor societies, departmental prizes. Dean's List is one common academic honor within it. List it under Education by default; only pull it into a dedicated Awards section once you have several honors to group.
ScholarshipsA scholarship is money awarded — often merit- or need-based, tied to a fund or sponsor. Dean's List is a GPA-based recognition with no money attached. Both can sit in Education, but they're different signals: funding vs. performance.
The Education entryYour Education entry is the whole block — school, degree, dates, and honors. Dean's List is one line within it, attached to the degree it belongs to, not a section of its own.

Step 1 — Decide whether it still belongs (the stage test)

This is the first and most-missed question. Dean's List is a college honor, and its value on a resume decays with time exactly the way a GPA does. Ask where you are.

List it — while it's your strongest academic proof

  • Current student or recent grad (roughly 0-3 years out)
  • Applying to internships or your first full-time roles
  • Work history is thin, so academics carry weight
  • Grad-school or fellowship applications that read for academic record

Retire it — once experience carries the story

  • You have 2-3+ years of relevant work experience
  • Your job accomplishments are the stronger evidence now
  • Senior or mid-career roles where a college honor reads as reaching
  • The Education section has shrunk to school + degree + year

There's no hard cutoff — it's about what's carrying your candidacy. As long as your academic record is a real part of the case (student, new grad, career pivot that leans on a degree), Dean's List earns its line. Once you can fill the page with quantified work results, let the honor retire; keeping it around signals you couldn't find something more recent to say.

Step 2 — Home it on one line in Education

The default and correct home is a single line inside your Education section, attached to the degree — that's where a reader looks for academic honors, and it keeps the honor in context.

Step 3 — Consolidate the terms and name it honestly

Two formatting rules do almost all the work here, and both are about telling the exact truth compactly.

Do

  • Consolidate: "Dean's List (5 semesters)" or "Dean's List, 2022-2024"
  • Qualify one term honestly: "Dean's List (Fall 2024)"
  • Use your school's exact name — Honor Roll, President's List
  • Pair it with a strong GPA when you have one

Don't

  • Repeat it as a separate line for every semester
  • Write a bare "Dean's List" when you earned it once
  • Invent a name your transcript won't back up
  • Keep listing "every semester" if it wasn't every semester

Everything about Dean's List is verifiable on a transcript, so the entire honesty game is naming exactly what you earned. If you made it once, say the term — that's still real and still counts. If you made it across most of your degree, "Dean's List, all semesters" is a genuinely strong line, but only if it's true. And match your school's actual term: some institutions call it the Honor Roll or the President's List, and using the wrong name is a small flag that you weren't careful.

Step 4 — Decide it against your GPA

Because Dean's List is a proxy for a strong term GPA, it interacts with whether you list the number itself. Play the pairing to show the truest, strongest picture.

Strong cumulative GPA (3.5+)

List both. The GPA is the most direct proof, and Dean's List reinforces that the performance was consistent across terms, not a single spike — "GPA 3.8 · Dean's List (6 semesters)" reads stronger than either alone.

Mediocre cumulative GPA, standout semesters

Lead with Dean's List, and consider omitting the cumulative number. If a rough first year dragged your average down, the honor speaks to your best terms without forcing a so-so figure onto the page — an honest way to surface your academic strength rather than your weakest average.

Both are weak

List neither, and let something else carry the academics. A relevant project, coursework, or an internship is a better use of the space than a thin honor — don't manufacture academic signal that isn't there.

The bright line — list Dean's List only if you actually earned it, name exactly what's true, and let it retire once your work experience carries the story. It's checkable on a transcript, so a bare "Dean's List" for one term, an invented honor name, or an "all semesters" that wasn't is a needless risk for a small reward. Named honestly and homed in Education while you're early-career, it's a clean, verifiable signal — and knowing when to drop it is part of listing it well.

Quick reference

  • Stage decides inclusion → list it as a student or recent grad; retire it once you have 2-3+ years of work experience, like GPA.
  • Home it in Education → one line under the degree, not a standalone section (unless you have several honors to group).
  • Consolidate the terms → "Dean's List (5 semesters)," never a separate line per semester.
  • Name it honestly → qualify a single term ("Fall 2024"), use your school's exact name, never imply more than you earned.
  • Play it against your GPA → both when your average is strong; Dean's List alone when standout semesters beat a mediocre cumulative number.
  • The bright line → only if you earned it, exactly as it was, and only while it's your strongest academic proof.

Keep building the resume

Once Dean's List sits cleanly in Education, tune the rest of the academic story: decide the GPA question, format the full Education entry, and group any other awards and achievements or a scholarship if you have them. Frequently asked questions

Should I put Dean's List on my resume?

Yes, if you're a current student or a recent graduate — roughly your first zero to three years out — and you actually earned it. Dean's List is a verifiable signal of consistent academic performance that a reader can check against a transcript, and for a candidate without much work history it's real evidence of discipline and ability. But it ages out the same way GPA does: once you have two or three years of professional experience, your job accomplishments carry the story, and a term-based college honor starts to look like you're reaching back for something to say. So the honest rule is stage-based — list it while it's the strongest academic proof you have, and quietly retire it once your work experience outweighs it. And only list it if you made it; it's checkable, so inflating it is a needless risk.

How do I format Dean's List on a resume?

Consolidate it into a single entry rather than repeating it term by term. If you made the list across several semesters, write "Dean's List (5 semesters)" or "Dean's List, 2022-2024" — one clean line that shows the pattern. Listing "Dean's List — Fall 2022," "Dean's List — Spring 2023," "Dean's List — Fall 2023" as separate lines wastes space and reads as padding. Put the line inside your Education section, under the degree it belongs to, for example: "B.S. Computer Science, State University, 2024 — Dean's List (5 semesters), GPA 3.8." Use whatever your school actually calls it — some use Honor Roll or President's List — because that's the name that will match your transcript. If you only earned it once, still list it, but qualify it honestly with the term so it doesn't imply every semester.

Where does Dean's List go — the Education section or a separate Honors section?

The default home is a single line inside your Education section, attached to the degree it belongs to. That's where a reader expects academic honors, and it keeps the honor tied to its context (which school, which program, when). Don't build a standalone "Honors" or "Awards" section for Dean's List alone — one honor with a section header on it draws the eye and then underdelivers, which reads as thin. A dedicated Honors or Awards section is only worth it once you have several things to group there — Dean's List plus a scholarship, a departmental award, an honor society, a competition win — enough that the section reads as a genuine record. Until then, one line under Education does the job cleanly.

Should I list Dean's List if I only made it one semester?

Yes, but qualify it so it's honest. One semester of Dean's List is still a real, verifiable achievement, and for a student or new grad with a short resume it's worth the line. The only trap is implying it was more than it was: writing a bare "Dean's List" when you earned it once can read as if you made it every term, and a hiring manager who checks will feel misled. So name the term — "Dean's List (Fall 2024)" — which is completely honest and still counts. As you accumulate more terms, switch to the consolidated form ("Dean's List, 4 semesters"). The principle is the same throughout: say exactly what's true, no more, because it's all checkable on a transcript.

Dean's List vs. GPA — which should I put on my resume?

They're related signals, and Dean's List is essentially a proxy for a strong term GPA — so you can list one, the other, or both depending on what flatters the truth. If your cumulative GPA is strong (generally 3.5 or above), listing the number is the most direct proof, and Dean's List reinforces that it was consistent, not a one-time spike. If your cumulative GPA is only okay but you had several standout semesters — common if a rough first year dragged the average down — Dean's List lets you show academic strength without leading with a mediocre number, since the honor speaks to your best terms rather than your overall average. What you shouldn't do is omit a genuinely strong GPA and hope Dean's List carries it, or list a weak GPA next to the honor in a way that undercuts it. Pick the framing that's both true and strongest: often that's Dean's List plus a strong GPA together, or Dean's List alone when the cumulative number isn't your best asset.
