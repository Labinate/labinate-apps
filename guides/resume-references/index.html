Should you put references on a resume?

Short answer: no — and you can skip "references available upon request" too. Here's why the space is worth more to you empty, what to do instead, and how to build the proper reference sheet an employer will actually ask for.

Updated July 2026 · ~4 min read · by Labinate

References matter — but not on the resume. By the time an employer wants to call your former manager, you're deep in the process, and they'll ask for the list directly. Putting it on the resume itself spends prime space on information nobody needs yet, exposes your contacts' details to every place your resume lands, and dates the document. This guide settles the question, then shows you the reference sheet to have ready instead.

Why references don't belong on the resume

Nobody asks this early

Reference checks happen near the end, after interviews. Listing them upfront answers a question no one is asking yet — and buries the answers they are.

It wastes your best real estate

Three references eat a big block of a one-page resume. That space carries far more weight as another results-driven experience bullet. See how to write bullet points.

It exposes your contacts

Your resume gets forwarded, uploaded, and stored in databases you'll never see. Your references' names, employers, and phone numbers shouldn't ride along for the trip.

"References available upon request" — retire it

This is the phrase everyone still types out of habit, and it's pure filler. Every employer already assumes you can produce references when asked; stating it tells them nothing and consumes a line you could give to a real accomplishment. Leave it off. It's on most lists of common resume mistakes for exactly this reason.

What to have ready instead: a reference sheet

Keep a separate one-page document so that the moment references are requested, you reply within the hour and look organized doing it. Build it to match your resume — same header, same fonts — so the two read as one package.

How to format each reference

For every person, list five things: name, job title, company, your relationship to them, and current contact details.

Reference entry Dana Ruiz
Director of Marketing, Acme Corp
Former manager (2021–2024)
dana.ruiz@example.com · (555) 013-2288

Who to choose

  • Aim for three to four professional references — enough range, not a crowd.
  • Prioritize former managers — someone who directed your work is the most credible voice. Close colleagues and clients work too.
  • Pick people who can be specific — a reference who can name what you actually did beats a senior title who barely remembers you.
  • Ask first, every time — get permission, and give them a heads-up (plus a copy of the job description) when a call is likely, so they can speak to the right things.
The rule, start to finish. Keep references off the resume and drop "available upon request" — both just cost you space. Instead, keep a separate, resume-matched reference sheet with three to four people, each listed with name, title, company, your relationship, and contact details. Ask permission before you list anyone, and warn them before the call comes. Then spend the space you saved on one more line of proof.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put references on a resume?

No. Employers ask for them later in the process, and the space is far better spent on your experience and results. Keep a separate reference sheet ready to send the moment it's requested.

Should I write "references available upon request"?

No — it's outdated and adds nothing. Every employer already assumes you can provide references, so the phrase just uses a line that could carry a stronger bullet. Leave it off.

How many references should I have ready?

Three to four professional references. Favour people who managed or worked closely with you and can speak specifically to your work — a former manager is the strongest kind. Always ask permission first.

How do I format a reference list?

A separate one-page document with the same header as your resume, listing each reference's name, job title, company, relationship to you, and current contact details. Match the fonts to your resume so it reads as one package.
