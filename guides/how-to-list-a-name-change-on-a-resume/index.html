How to list a name change on a resume

The instinct is to document the change on the page — "née," "aka," a maiden name in the header. The move is the opposite: your resume shows your current name, full stop. The real work is continuity, and it happens off the page.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

You changed your name — you married, you divorced, you made a legal change, you transitioned — and now you're looking at your resume wondering how to reconcile the person on the page with the references, diplomas, and old employer records that still carry your previous name. Do you write "formerly known as"? Do you add your maiden name in parentheses so a reference check lines up? The unlock is to separate two things people jam together: the resume and the paper trail. The resume is a marketing document about who you are now — it carries only your current name and has no field for name history, so there's nothing to "list." The continuity problem is real, but it lives everywhere except the resume: with your references, on the background-check form, and at the offices that verify a degree or license. Solve it there, and the name change becomes a non-event on the page itself.

The core idea: a name change is a question of identity and continuity, not resume formatting. On the resume: current name only — no "née," no "aka," no maiden name in the header. Off the resume, bridge the three places your past is on record under the old name: give references a heads-up, put your legal former name in the application's background-check field, and supply the issued name for a degree or license only at the verification step. The one exception on the page is a fixed publication byline. And you never owe the reason for the change.

First: the resume shows your current name, full stop

Whatever name you go by now — a new legal name, a married name, a preferred name you use professionally — that is the name in your header and throughout the document. A resume presents who you are today; it has no line for "and I used to be called…," so there is nothing to add and nothing to explain. Leaving a former name off is not hiding it — the page simply has no field for it. Adding one does real harm: "Jordan Rivera (née Patel)" clutters the header and quietly announces a marriage, a divorce, or a transition to a stranger who has no reason to know. Use the name you'll be addressed by, and let the rest of the page do its job.

The real problem is continuity — and it lives off the resume

Here's the legitimate worry underneath the question: your past is on record under the old name. Old managers remember you as someone else; your diploma prints a name you no longer use; a background check searches records filed under a name that isn't on your resume. None of that is fixed on the resume — each has its own correct home.

Your references and former colleagues

The people who can vouch for you knew you under your previous name, so a reference call or a recruiter's back-channel can come back "no record of that person." Give each reference your current name plus a quick heads-up — "at [Company] I worked as [former name]" — so they recognize the call and connect it to the right person. This is a two-line message, not a resume edit. See resume references.

The background check & employment verification

This is where a legal former name actually belongs. The job application — not the resume — usually has a dedicated "former names / also known as" field, and the verifier searches employment, education, and public records under the name they were filed under. Fill that field truthfully with your legal former name. Omitting it there is what causes a delayed or failed check — the exact failure people wrongly try to prevent by crowding their resume header.

Degrees, licenses & certifications issued under the old name

List them under your current name, like everything else — the credential is yours no matter what name is printed on the certificate. The issued name only matters when someone verifies it: a transcript request, a licensing-board lookup, a certification registry check. Supply the issued name at that step, not as a standing label on the resume.

The one exception: a fixed publication byline

There is exactly one place a former name can legitimately sit on the resume itself — a publications byline. A byline is a fixed historical record: the paper, article, or book was published under a specific name, and the goal is attribution, not identity. So a researcher, writer, or academic can keep the published name with a short note — "Smith, J. (published as J. Patel), 2022" — or list the work under the current name with the same (published as …) tag, so their body of work stays findable and correctly credited. This is the narrow exception, and it's about a citation, not your header. Everywhere else, the former name stays off the page.

Don't clutter the header — "formerly" belongs off the resume

The tempting move is to preempt every possible mismatch right at the top of the page. Resist it: the header is prime real estate for your name, contact, and links, and a parenthetical maiden name buys you nothing while costing clarity and privacy. The mismatches get solved where they actually occur — the reference heads-up, the application's former-names field, the credential verifier — each of which reaches exactly the person who needs the old name, and no one who doesn't.

Handled cleanly

  • Current name only in the header and throughout
  • Legal former name in the application's former-names field
  • References told your current name + a one-line heads-up
  • Publication bylines noted (published as …) where relevant

Overshared

  • "(née …)" or "aka …" crowding the header
  • The reason for the change explained anywhere on the page
  • A former name left off the background-check field instead
  • Every diploma tagged "issued as …" as standing noise

You never owe the reason

Marriage, divorce, a legal change, a gender transition — all of them are handled identically: current name on the resume, no explanation anywhere. You are not obligated to surface a former name, and no employer is entitled to the reason behind the change. For a transition in particular, there is no requirement to expose a former "dead" name anywhere it isn't strictly needed — and the only place it's ever strictly needed is a background check that legally requires prior legal names to match records. Answer that field truthfully and nothing more. Your name history is yours; the resume and the interview stay on your current name and your fit for the role.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode isn't the name change itself — it's treating the resume as the place to reconcile your paper trail. Solved where each mismatch lives, the change is invisible and every record still lines up; crammed into the header, it costs privacy and clarity while still leaving the background-check field — the one that matters — unaddressed.

Frequently asked questions

How do you handle a name change on a resume?

Put only your current name on the resume — full stop. A resume presents who you are now and has no field for name history, so there is nothing to "list": you use your current legal or preferred name in the header and throughout, exactly as you'll be addressed. Don't add "née," "aka," "formerly," or a maiden name to the header; it clutters the page and volunteers personal history (a marriage, a divorce, a transition) that the resume doesn't owe anyone. The actual work happens off the resume, where your past is on record under the old name: give references a heads-up, supply your legal former name on the application's background-check field, and provide the issued name for any degree or license only at the verification step. Handle those, and the name change is a non-event on the resume itself.

Should you put your maiden name or former name on a resume?

Generally no. The resume carries your current name only, and a maiden or former name in the header — "Jane Smith (née Doe)" — adds nothing a hiring manager needs and quietly discloses personal history you're not required to share. There is one legitimate exception: a publication byline. If you have published work under your former name, a byline is a fixed historical record and the point is attribution, so you may keep the published name with a short "(published as [former name])" note, or list the work under your current name with that same note, so your body of work stays findable and correctly credited. Everywhere else, the former name belongs off the resume: on the job application's "former names / also known as" field for the background check, and in a quiet heads-up to your references — not on the resume face.

How do you make sure references and past employers still match after a name change?

Bridge it deliberately, off the resume. Your former managers and colleagues knew you under your previous name, so a reference check or a recruiter's back-channel can come back "no record of that person" if you don't connect the dots. Two moves cover it. First, when you give someone as a reference, tell them your current name and remind them that at [Company] you worked as [former name], so they recognize the call. Second — and this is the one that prevents a failed background check — put your legal former name in the application's dedicated "former names / also known as" field, truthfully, because the verifier searches employment and education records under the name they were filed under. That field, not the resume, is the correct home for a legal former name.

How do you list a degree, license, or certification issued under a former name?

List it under your current name, just like everything else on the resume — the credential is yours regardless of the name printed on the certificate. The name it was issued under only matters at the verification step, so supply it there: on a transcript or degree-verification request, on a licensing-board record lookup, or as a brief parenthetical where a board or employer cross-checks the credential against its registry. You don't add "issued as [former name]" as a standing label on the resume; it's noise to a hiring manager and only relevant to the office that verifies the credential. If you're worried a specific check (a nursing license, a bar admission, a security clearance) won't reconcile, resolve it proactively with the issuing body or in the application's former-names field, not by cluttering the resume.

Do you have to disclose a gender transition or the reason for a name change on a resume?

No. You use your current name on the resume and you owe no one the reason for the change — marriage, divorce, and a gender transition are all handled identically: current name only, no explanation. A resume has no field for a former name, so leaving a prior name off is not concealment, and you are never obligated to surface a former "dead" name anywhere it isn't strictly required. The single place a legal former name may be legally required is a background check, where the application asks for prior legal names so records can be matched; answer that field truthfully with the legal former name, and nothing more. Beyond that, your name history is yours, and the resume — and the interview — stay focused on your current name and your fit for the role.
