How to list research assistant experience on a resume

A research assistantship is real professional work — not "just helping a professor." The whole job is translation: turn the research into plain, quantified results a non-academic recruiter and an ATS can read, place it as the job it was, and claim your share of the work honestly.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

A research assistant role — helping a professor, lab, or institution with study design, data collection, analysis, or writing, whether it was paid, for course credit, or on a stipend — is genuine experience that builds exactly the skills employers want: methodology, data analysis, tools, technical writing, and the discipline to work independently. Yet it's routinely mishandled at two opposite extremes. The first is underselling it — dismissing it as "just helping a professor," burying it, or describing it as a vague "assisted with research," which throws away everything you actually learned to do. The second is walling it behind academic jargon — dense sub-field language and citation-speak that a non-academic recruiter (and the applicant-tracking system reading first) simply cannot parse, so your best work stays invisible outside a narrow field. Both failures have the same cure.

The core idea: a recruiter is reading for what you can do, stated in language they understand. Your research topic is context; your contribution is the point. So translate the work into plain, results-first bullets, quantify the operation you ran, and attribute your part honestly — then place the role by how relevant it is to the job you're chasing now.

Move 1 — Treat it as a real job, and place it by relevance

A research assistantship belongs in your Work Experience section, formatted like any other role: a real title, the lab or department as employer, and honest dates. "Undergraduate Research Assistant, Cognitive Neuroscience Lab, University of X" is a job entry, not a footnote. Paid, credit-bearing, or stipend — the arrangement affects your taxes, not whether the work counts.

Research-adjacent target — lead with it

Applying to R&D, data, analyst, or science roles? The RA work is your strongest evidence — give it a full entry near the top with three or four quantified bullets.

For a recent graduate, a substantial research role often outweighs unrelated part-time jobs. Let it carry the page.

Unrelated target, or a one-off — shrink it

Chasing a job the research doesn't speak to? Keep the entry but compress it to a line or two of transferable skills. And if it was genuinely a small, single-semester contribution rather than an ongoing role, it can sit under a Projects or Education subsection instead — match the weight to the substance.

The test is relevance and scale, not prestige. A semester of light help is a project; two years running a study's data pipeline is a job. Size the entry accordingly, and don't apologize for either.

Move 2 — Translate the work, don't recite the topic

This is where most research resumes fail. The instinct is to describe the study — its hypothesis, its sub-field, its citations — when the reader needs to know what you did. Lead every bullet with an action and a result in plain professional language, and keep the topic to a short bit of context. The person reading first may not share your field, and the ATS reading before them definitely doesn't.

Topic-first — invisible outside the field

  • "Assisted with research on X"
  • "Investigated the epigenetic modulation of hippocampal plasticity"
  • Recites the study, not your skill
  • An ATS can't match it to a job

Work-first — legible to anyone

  • "Ran statistical analysis on a 12,000-record dataset in R, cutting processing time 40%"
  • "Recruited and screened 120 study participants"
  • Names transferable skills plainly
  • Reads as capability, not an abstract

Name the portable skills explicitly so a non-specialist and the parser both catch them: experimental design, data collection and cleaning, specific lab techniques, literature review, statistics, coding, and outcome-focused technical writing. The topic tells a reader what the study was about; the verbs and numbers tell them what you can bring to their work.

Move 3 — Quantify the operation, and attribute your share honestly

Research is full of numbers, which makes it easy to quantify — do it. Give the reader a sense of scale and rigor: dataset and sample sizes, participants recruited or run, experiments or trials conducted, tools and instruments and languages used, and any papers, posters, or presentations you contributed to. "Processed and cleaned a 12,000-record dataset in Python, reducing analysis turnaround from three days to one" tells a story that "helped with data" never will.

The one rule that protects your credibility

Describe your part of a team effort — never claim the whole study or the principal investigator's finding as your own.

Research is collaborative, and a recruiter who reads "discovered a new treatment" from a junior RA discounts the entire resume. Write what you personally owned — "built the analysis pipeline," "coded 300 interview transcripts," "managed the participant database" — and describe your contribution to a shared result rather than annexing it. Credit authorship precisely too: "co-authored one conference poster; second author on one journal paper" is exact and checkable. Honest attribution isn't modesty — it's the judgment employers are screening for.

Move 4 — Show independence and rigor

Anyone can hold a pipette; employers are screening for the person who can be trusted to run something. Surface the signals that separate a real contributor from a pair of hands: work done with minimal supervision, a protocol you designed, a sub-project you owned end to end, a grant deadline you met, new assistants you trained or onboarded. These translate directly into the workplace — they're the same traits that make someone reliable on a team that isn't a lab.

  • Ownership. "Independently ran the weekly data collection for a 3-year longitudinal study" reads as trust earned, not tasks assigned.
  • Rigor and process. "Standardized the lab's data-entry protocol, reducing coding errors by ~30%" shows you improve systems, not just follow them.
  • Leadership, even junior. "Trained and supervised 4 incoming undergraduate RAs each semester" is people management by another name.
  • Deadline discipline. Delivering under a grant timeline or before a conference is exactly the pressure a hiring manager wants proof you can handle.

The bright line

Present a research assistantship as real, quantified professional work, translated into skills a non-academic can read. Put it in Work Experience with a real title and honest dates; lead each bullet with what you did and the result in plain language, not the sub-field topic; quantify the operation you ran — datasets, participants, tools, papers contributed to; and attribute your share honestly, never claiming the team's study or the PI's finding as your own. Show independence and rigor to prove you can be trusted to run something. Never bury it as "just helping a professor," never wall it behind jargon, and never inflate your slice of a group project into the whole thing.

Research assistant vs. the things it's confused with

"How do I list research" overlaps with several nearby situations. They're genuinely different — and you often hold more than one at once, so list each for what it actually was.

The situationWhat it really is — and how it differs
Thesis / dissertationYour own independent capstone project, authored by you. An RA role is assisting on someone else's research agenda — the professor's or lab's. The test: whose research question and whose project is it? Your thesis is yours to claim in full; the lab's study is a shared effort where you claim your part.
InternshipA structured, fixed-term, often-corporate training program you're placed into. An RA role is an ongoing academic position assisting a specific lab or PI, often across multiple semesters, and paid by credit or stipend. The test: a structured program with a defined cohort and end date, versus an ongoing lab role tied to a supervisor.
PhD (the degree)The credential itself, which belongs in Education. An assistantship may fund a PhD, but the job you did (research or teaching) goes in Work Experience while the degree goes in Education. You list both, separately. The test: a degree earned versus a job performed.
PublicationsThe outputs — papers and posters — listed in their own section. An RA role is the experience that produced them. List the role in Experience and the papers under Publications; don't collapse one into the other. The test: the paper (an output) versus the job that made it (experience).
Teaching assistantAn instructional role — teaching sections, grading, holding office hours. An RA does investigative work — running studies and analyzing data. The same person often holds both; list each for what it was. The test: did you teach students (TA) or run research (RA)?

Quick reference

  • It's a real job → Work Experience, real title, lab/department as employer, honest dates; paid, credit, or stipend all count.
  • Translate, don't recite → lead with what you did and the result in plain language; the topic is context, not the headline.
  • Quantify the operation → datasets, samples, participants, experiments, tools, papers/posters contributed to.
  • Attribute honestly → describe your part of a team study; never claim the whole finding or the PI's work as your own.
  • Show you can be trusted to run something → independence, a protocol you owned, RAs you trained, a deadline you met.
  • Reweight for the target → foreground the skills the new field cares about; let the research topic recede to a line.

Frequently asked questions

Does research assistant experience count as real work experience on a resume?

Yes. A research assistantship is a real job — you contributed to a genuine project under a supervisor, built skills, and produced results — so it belongs in your Work Experience section like any other role, with a title, the lab or department as employer, and honest dates. Whether it was paid, for course credit, or on a stipend doesn't change that; the pay structure affects taxes, not whether the work happened. The one time it sits elsewhere is a very small, one-off contribution — a single semester of light help — which can go under a Projects or Education subsection instead. Match the placement to the substance, but don't dismiss real research work as "just helping a professor."

How do you describe research assistant work without academic jargon?

Lead each bullet with what you did and the result in plain professional language, and keep the sub-field topic to a short bit of context. A non-academic recruiter and an applicant-tracking system can't parse "investigated the epigenetic modulation of hippocampal plasticity," but both understand "ran statistical analysis on a 12,000-record dataset in R, cutting processing time 40%." Name the transferable skills explicitly — experimental design, data collection and cleaning, specific lab techniques, literature review, coding, technical writing — so the resume reads as capability rather than a paper abstract. The topic tells them what the study was about; the verbs and numbers tell them what you can do.

How do you show your own contribution when the research was a team effort?

Describe your specific part, not the whole study. Research is collaborative, and a recruiter who reads "discovered a new treatment" from a junior RA will discount the entire resume. Instead write what you personally owned — "recruited and screened 120 participants," "built the analysis pipeline," "coded 300 interview transcripts" — and describe your contribution to a shared result rather than claiming the principal investigator's finding as your own. Credit authorship accurately too: "co-authored one conference poster; second author on one journal paper" is precise and verifiable. Honest attribution isn't modesty; it's exactly the judgment employers are screening for.

What should you quantify in a research assistant role?

Quantify the operation you ran so a reader can gauge scale and rigor: dataset or sample sizes, participants recruited or run, experiments or trials conducted, tools and instruments and programming languages used, and any papers, posters, or presentations you contributed to. Add efficiency or quality gains where they exist — time saved, error rates reduced, a protocol you standardized. A bullet like "processed and cleaned a 12,000-record dataset in Python, reducing analysis turnaround from three days to one" beats "helped with data." Numbers turn "assisted with research" into evidence of what you can actually do.

Where does a research assistant role go if you're changing careers out of academia?

Keep it in Work Experience, but reweight it toward the transferable skills the new field cares about. An RA role is full of portable strengths — data analysis, project management, technical writing, working independently to a deadline, handling ambiguity — so lead with those and translate the domain out of the bullets. For a data or analyst role, foreground the datasets, tools, and statistics; for an operations or project role, foreground the coordination, recruitment, and process work you owned. Tailor the emphasis to the target job the same way you would any role, and let the research topic recede to a single line of context.
