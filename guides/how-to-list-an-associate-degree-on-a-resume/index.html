How to list an associate degree on a resume

An associate degree is a finished credential — list it fully, with the real degree name and college, never as vague "some college." The one decision that actually matters is rank: once you hold a bachelor's in the same field, the associate that fed into it is usually redundant and comes off.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Two years of college, a completed A.A., A.S., or A.A.S. — and yet the associate degree gets undersold on resumes more than almost any credential. People bury it under "some college," hedge it as "just a two-year degree," or agonize over whether it even belongs next to real work experience. All of that misreads what the credential is. An associate degree is a complete, accredited qualification; the only questions worth your attention are how to name it so it reads as one, and — the real decision — whether to keep it once you've earned something higher. Get those two right and it stops being a thing to apologize for and becomes exactly what it is: a qualification that satisfies the many jobs that ask for it.

The core idea: an associate degree sits on the axis of credential rank — a complete credential at a lower tier than a bachelor's. That single fact drives everything. Name the real degree fully (Associate of Applied Science (A.A.S.) in Nursing, Houston Community College, 2024) and never downgrade it to "some college." And cut it only when a higher degree makes it redundant — a bachelor's in the same field subsumes the associate that led to it.

Name the real credential — never "some college"

The most common failure isn't leaving the degree off; it's shrinking it. "Some college," "two years of college," "college coursework" — every one of those phrases describes not finishing, which is the exact opposite of what an associate degree is. You completed a program and were awarded a credential, so write the credential: the specific degree name, the field, the college, and the year.

Use the correct degree abbreviation rather than a generic "associate degree," because the exact name tells a reader what kind of program it was: A.A. (Associate of Arts), A.S. (Associate of Science), or A.A.S. (Associate of Applied Science, typically a career-focused track). A clean line looks like Associate of Science (A.S.) in Business Administration, Santa Monica College, 2023. That's it — no hedging, no scare-quotes, no "only."

The real decision: keep it, or does a higher degree make it redundant?

This is where the credential-rank axis does its work. An associate degree is often the first rung of a ladder you kept climbing — and once you're standing on a higher rung, showing the lower one can be redundant. The rule is about information, not pride: keep the associate only if it says something the higher degree doesn't already imply.

Bachelor's in the same field → drop the associate

If you hold a B.S. in Nursing, the A.S. in Nursing that fed into it repeats the same track and spends a line for nothing. List the bachelor's alone. A reader already knows you did the foundational years; you don't need to show the intermediate credential.

Associate in a distinct, relevant specialty → keep both

A B.A. in Communications and an A.A.S. in Web Development, for a role that values the technical training, are two different statements — so both earn a line. The test: does the associate add information the bachelor's doesn't already carry? If yes, keep it.

Associate is your highest degree → it's the headline, list it proudly

No bachelor's? Then the associate is your top credential and gets full, unhedged billing — and for the many jobs that ask for exactly an associate (nursing, paralegal, IT support, dental hygiene, skilled trades), it's the right and sufficient qualification. Don't undersell the thing the job is literally asking for.

Where it goes on the page

Placement follows relevance and career stage — not the degree's tier. The credential stays fully named wherever it sits; only its position on the page moves.

Early-career or degree-is-the-qualification → Education near the top

If the associate is your highest credential and it's relevant, or your work history is still thin, put Education high, so the qualification is seen before a reader reaches a short experience section.

A few years of relevant experience → Education drops below

Once you've done real work in the field, employers weigh what you've done over where you studied. Move Experience up and let the associate keep its full line lower on the page.

Unrelated degree, strong experience → compact line at the bottom

If the associate isn't relevant to the target job and your experience carries the resume, it can shrink to a single tidy Education line at the foot of the page. Still named in full — just out of the spotlight.

Still working toward it? List it in progress

An associate you haven't finished is listed as in progress with an expected date — never as awarded. Write Associate of Science (A.S.) in Computer Science, Portland Community College — expected May 2027. If you're using it as a stepping stone, you can say so: A.S. in Biology (transferring toward a B.S.), 2025–present. The line that must never be crossed is presenting an unearned degree as conferred; expected [date] keeps you on the honest side of it. This is the same discipline that separates an in-progress credential from an incomplete degree you've stopped pursuing.

An associate degree isn't the same question as these

Four situations get tangled together because they all touch the Education section — but each sits on a different axis, with a different fix. Sort out which one you're actually in before deciding how to present it.

The situationWhat it's really about — and the resume move
Associate degreeCredential rank — a complete credential at a lower tier than a bachelor's. Name it in full; cut it only when a higher degree makes it redundant.
Incomplete degreeTenure — you started but didn't finish. The question is how to show earned coursework without implying a degree. See incomplete degree.
Minor / double majorScope — extra fields inside one bachelor's. The question is when the second field is worth the space. See double major.
MBA / PhDHigher tier — a graduate credential above the bachelor's. Different placement and emphasis rules. See MBA and PhD.

The through-line: an associate degree is finished (unlike an incomplete degree) and free-standing (unlike a minor, which lives inside a bachelor's). Present it as the complete, lower-tier credential it is — no more, and no less.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode isn't lying — it's letting an inferiority reflex shrink a real qualification into an apology. Named in full and placed by relevance, an associate degree reads as a credential; hedged and buried, the same degree reads as a gap.

Reads as a qualification

  • Full credential: A.A.S. in Nursing, HCC, 2024
  • Placed by relevance and stage, not tier
  • Redundant associate dropped once a bachelor's covers it
  • In-progress shown as "expected [date]"

Reads as a gap

  • Hedged as "some college" / "2 years"
  • Both a bachelor's and its feeder associate, same field
  • Apologetic framing: "only an associate"
  • Unfinished degree listed as if it were awarded

An associate degree is a completed credential, and the resume's job is to let it read as one. Name the real degree in full, place it by how relevant it is and how far along your career is, and remove it only when a higher degree in the same field genuinely makes it redundant. Do that and you stop apologizing for two real years of work — and start getting credit for the qualification they earned you.

Quick reference

  • Name the real credential → A.A. / A.S. / A.A.S. in [field], [college], [year] — never "some college."
  • Keep-or-cut is about rank → a bachelor's in the same field subsumes the associate; drop it. A distinct, relevant associate stays.
  • Place by relevance and stage → Education high early-career or when the degree is the qualification; below Experience once you have a track record.
  • In progress → "expected [date]" → never list an unfinished associate as awarded.
  • Don't apologize → for the many roles that ask for an associate, it's the right and sufficient credential.
  • Know your axis → associate (rank) ≠ incomplete degree (tenure) ≠ minor/double major (scope) ≠ MBA/PhD (higher tier).

Frequently asked questions

How do you write an associate degree on a resume?

Write it out fully in your Education section, exactly like any degree: the real credential name, the field, the college, and the graduation year. For example, "Associate of Applied Science (A.A.S.) in Nursing, Houston Community College, 2024," or "Associate of Arts (A.A.) in Business, Santa Monica College, 2023." Use the correct name for the specific degree — A.A. (Associate of Arts), A.S. (Associate of Science), or A.A.S. (Associate of Applied Science) — rather than a generic "associate degree," because the exact credential tells a reader what kind of program it was. Don't write "some college," "two years of college," or "college coursework" — that language describes not finishing, and it undersells a credential you actually earned. It's a completed qualification; list it as one.

Should you put an associate degree on a resume if you have a bachelor's?

Usually no, when the bachelor's is in the same or a closely related field. A bachelor's degree subsumes the associate that fed into it, so listing both just repeats the same academic track and spends space a reader doesn't need — list the bachelor's alone. The clear exception is when the associate is in a distinct, relevant specialty the bachelor's doesn't cover: someone with a B.A. in Communications and an A.A.S. in Web Development, applying for a role that values the technical training, has a real reason to show both, because each says something different. The test is whether the associate adds information the bachelor's already implies. If it does, keep it; if it's just the earlier rung of the same ladder, cut it.

Does an associate degree count as a college degree on a resume?

Yes. An associate degree is a completed, accredited college credential and counts as a degree — it's simply a lower tier than a bachelor's. When a job asks for "an associate degree or equivalent," or lists an associate as the minimum, yours satisfies it fully, and you should present it plainly and without hedging. It does not meet a requirement that specifically says "bachelor's degree required," and no formatting changes that; don't dress an associate up to look like a four-year degree. But within its own tier it's a genuine qualification, and for the many roles that ask for exactly it — nursing, paralegal, IT support, skilled trades, dental hygiene, and more — it's the right and sufficient credential.

Where should an associate degree go on a resume?

Placement follows relevance and career stage, not the degree's tier. If the associate is your highest credential and it's relevant to the job — or you're early-career with limited work history — put the Education section near the top, so the qualification is seen before a reader reaches thin experience. Once you have a few years of relevant work, Education drops below Experience, because employers weigh what you've done over where you studied; the associate still gets its full line, just lower on the page. If the degree is unrelated to your target job and you have strong experience, it can move to a compact Education line at the bottom. The credential stays fully named wherever it sits; only its position moves.

How do you list an associate degree you're still working toward?

List it as in progress with an expected date, never as complete. Write "Associate of Science (A.S.) in Computer Science, Portland Community College — expected May 2027," so the reader knows you're enrolled and on track without implying the degree is already conferred. If you're using the associate as a stepping stone and plan to transfer, you can note it: "A.S. in Biology (transferring toward a B.S.), 2025–present." Include relevant completed coursework if the field is directly on point and your experience is thin. What you must not do is list an unfinished associate as if it were awarded — that's the line between an in-progress credential and a misrepresentation. "Expected [date]" keeps you on the right side of it.
