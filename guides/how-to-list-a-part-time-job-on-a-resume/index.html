How to list a part-time job on a resume

The instinct is to flag it — to tack (Part-Time) onto the title as if reduced hours were a lesser grade of employment you're obligated to disclose. Skip that. A part-time job is listed exactly like a full-time one — same title, employer, dates, and quantified bullets — and the label goes on only when it actually explains something.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Part-time work trips people up because it feels like a caveat — something that needs an asterisk. So they append (Part-Time) to every such title by reflex, quietly telling the reader to discount the entry before they've read a word of it. But a hiring manager isn't grading your schedule; they're grading what you did and how well you did it, and neither of those shrinks because you worked twenty-four hours a week instead of forty. The schedule is almost never the question. Listed plainly, a part-time job stands beside a full-time one — same format, same weight, same quantified bullets. The label is a tool you reach for in a few specific situations, not a disclosure you owe.

The core idea: hours-per-week is not a status class. Put a part-time job under Work Experience with a real title, employer, dates, and results — no annotation. Add (Part-Time) only when it does explanatory work: concurrent roles (two overlapping jobs that would otherwise look like an error), an apparent gap or lighter output the reader might question, or genuinely useful context like "while in school." If the label doesn't answer a question the reader would otherwise ask, leave it off.

Why the "(Part-Time)" reflex backfires

Adding the label everywhere does the opposite of what you hope. You mean it as honesty; the reader takes it as a hint to expect less. It pre-frames the entry as a lesser job, so the accomplishment bullets underneath have to fight their own header. And it's rarely even relevant — the schedule doesn't change whether you cut the checkout queue in half or trained the three hires who came after you. Say what you did; let the reader draw their own conclusion about the hours if they even care.

The exception is the whole point of this guide: a label earns its place the moment it answers a question the resume would otherwise raise. Two jobs with overlapping dates raise "is this a typo?" A thin-looking stretch raises "what happened here?" A role held during a degree raises "how did they do both?" In each of those, (Part-Time) is the fastest answer. Everywhere else, it's noise.

Part-time isn't the same question as short-term or temp

Three different words get treated as interchangeable, and they aren't — each is a different axis, and each has a different resume fix. Sorting out which one actually applies to your job saves you from solving the wrong problem.

The labelWhat it's really about — and the resume question
Part-timeSchedule — fewer hours per week, but the role can run for years. The question is mostly whether to annotate it at all (usually: no).
Short-termTenure — you were there only briefly, at any hours. The question is keep it or cut it, and how to frame a short stay. See short-term job.
TempEmployer structure — a staffing agency placed you at a client. The question is naming the agency vs. the client and grouping assignments. See temp job.
Contract / freelanceEngagement type — you were the vendor, not an employee. See contract work and freelance work.

A single job can sit on more than one axis at once — a brief part-time role placed by an agency is all three. Name the axis that a reader would actually wonder about and solve for that; don't reflexively stamp "part-time" on something whose real story is that it was short, or agency-placed, or a contract.

How to write the entry

Format it like any job, because it is one. Title, employer, dates, bullets — and quantify, because part-time work is just as easy to put numbers on as full-time.

Use the real job title, unadorned

Barista, Sales Associate, Bookkeeper, Marketing Assistant — not Barista (Part-Time) by default. The title is the title. You reach for the parenthetical only in the specific cases below, not as standard punctuation.

Bullets = results, not hours

Lead each with an action verb and a number: "Served 120+ customers per shift with a 98% satisfaction score," "Reconciled weekly cash drawers with zero shortfalls," "Trained 3 new hires on POS and opening procedures." Nothing here references the schedule — and nothing needs to.

When the label helps: concurrent roles

Holding two jobs at once? Add (Part-Time) to each so the overlapping dates read as deliberate, not a mistake or job-hopping: Barista (Part-Time), Blue Cup — Mar 2024–Present alongside Front-Desk Assistant (Part-Time), City Gym — Jun 2024–Present. Two concurrent roles signal drive; the label is what makes the overlap legible.

When the label helps: context or a gap

If part-time work explains an apparent gap, a lighter output a full-time reader might question, or was done alongside school, say so once: "Retail Associate (Part-Time, while completing B.S.)." That earns its space — it answers "how did they fit this in," which is a fair question.

Two part-time jobs at once — an asset, if you signal it

Concurrent part-time work is the situation people fear most and mishandle most, so it's worth its own beat. Left unlabeled, two overlapping date ranges look like a copy-paste error or, worse, like you're padding. Labeled and ordered well, they read as exactly what they are: someone who took on more than one commitment and delivered on both.

Two separate entries, real overlapping dates

Don't merge them or fudge the dates. List each as its own entry with its true range and (Part-Time) on each title so the overlap is unmistakably intentional.

Order by relevance, not strictly by date

Put the role closest to the job you want first, even if the other started earlier. Reverse-chronological is the default, but relevance wins when two entries share a time window.

Differentiate the bullets

Give each entry distinct, non-overlapping accomplishments so they don't read as one job typed twice. Different skills under each is what makes two concurrent roles look like range rather than repetition.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode here isn't dishonesty — it's discounting yourself. Stamped "(Part-Time)" everywhere, the work reads as lesser; formatted like any job and labeled only where it clarifies, it reads as work.

Reads as real work

  • Under Work Experience, formatted like any job
  • Plain job title; no reflexive tag
  • Bullets in numbers, results — not hours
  • (Part-Time) only where it clarifies overlap or a gap

Discounts itself

  • "(Part-Time)" stamped on every title
  • Apologetic framing: "just a part-time gig"
  • Duties, not results: "helped out at the café"
  • Concurrent dates left unlabeled and confusing

A part-time job is real work, and a resume that treats it that way makes the reader treat it that way too. Give it the same format and the same quantified bullets you'd give a full-time role, drop the reflexive label, and reach for (Part-Time) only in the handful of spots where it answers a real question. The schedule is your business; the results are theirs.

Quick reference

  • Format it like any job → real title, employer, dates, quantified bullets under Work Experience.
  • Drop the default label → don't append (Part-Time) by reflex; hours aren't a status you owe.
  • Label when it clarifies → concurrent roles, an apparent gap, lighter output, or "while in school."
  • Two jobs at once → separate entries, true overlapping dates, (Part-Time) on each, ordered by relevance.
  • Bullets = results → numbers and outcomes, never "helped out"; the schedule never appears in a bullet.
  • Know your axis → part-time (schedule) ≠ short-term (tenure) ≠ temp (employer structure).

With the part-time role placed like any job, tune the rest: if it was also brief, read how to list a short-term job; if an agency placed you, see temp jobs; if you were the vendor, contract work and freelance work cover it. Make overlapping or stacked entries read as range, not churn, with job-hopping, and quantify every entry using achievement math. Frequently asked questions

Do you have to write "(Part-Time)" on a resume?

No — and most of the time you shouldn't. A part-time job is listed like any other job: real title, employer, dates, and quantified bullets. The number of hours you worked is not a status a resume is obligated to disclose, and adding "(Part-Time)" by reflex only invites the reader to discount the entry. Add the label only when it does actual explanatory work: when two part-time jobs overlap in time and the label prevents the dates from looking like a mistake; when a part-time stretch explains a gap or a lighter output; or when "while in school" or "while running my own business" is genuinely useful context. If the label doesn't answer a question the reader would otherwise ask, leave it off.

How do you list two part-time jobs at the same time on a resume?

List them as two separate entries with their real, overlapping date ranges, and here is the one place the "(Part-Time)" label earns its keep: adding it to each title tells the reader the overlap is intentional, not an error or job-hopping. So "Barista (Part-Time), Blue Cup Coffee, Mar 2024–Present" and "Front-Desk Assistant (Part-Time), City Gym, Jun 2024–Present" read as one person holding two concurrent roles, which is a sign of drive, not instability. Order them by relevance to the job you want, not strictly by date, and let the bullets under each show distinct skills so the two entries don't feel redundant. Concurrent part-time work is an asset on the page — you just have to signal that it's deliberate.

Does a part-time job count as work experience on a resume?

Yes, fully. Part-time work is real employment and belongs under Work Experience exactly like a full-time role — with a genuine job title, the employer, dates, and accomplishment bullets. What matters to a hiring manager is what you accomplished and the skills you demonstrated, and those don't become less real because the schedule was reduced. Quantify the work the same way you would for any job — customers served, sales handled, shifts covered, a process you improved — and it stands on its own. The only time part-time hours are worth mentioning is when they add useful context (you did this alongside school or another job); otherwise, let the results speak and don't apologize for the schedule.

How is a part-time job different from a temp job or a short-term job on a resume?

They answer three different questions, so don't conflate them. "Part-time" is about the schedule — fewer hours per week, but the role can run for years. A "short-term job" is about tenure — you were there only briefly, regardless of hours — and the resume question there is whether to keep it at all and how to frame the short stay. A "temp job" is about who employed you — a staffing agency placed you at a client — and the question is how to name the agency versus the client and how to group multiple assignments. A single job can even be more than one of these at once. Figure out which axis actually applies before you decide how to present it: part-time changes little about the format, short-term is a keep-or-cut call, and temp is a naming-and-grouping call.

Should you list a part-time job that's unrelated to the job you want?

Often yes, especially if leaving it off would create a gap or if it demonstrates transferable strengths. An unrelated part-time job — waiting tables, retail, a weekend gig — still shows reliability, customer handling, cash accountability, and the plain fact that you were working. If you have plenty of relevant full-time experience, you can drop an unrelated part-time role or compress it to a single line to save space. If you're early-career, changing fields, or filling a thin history, keep it and write the bullets toward the transferable skills the target job cares about rather than the tasks themselves. The test is whether the entry earns its space by adding evidence or continuity — not whether the title matches.
