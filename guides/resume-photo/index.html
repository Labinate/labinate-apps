Should you put a photo on your resume?

It feels personal and modern to add a headshot — but in most of the English-speaking job market a photo works against you, and in nearly every market it can trip the software that reads your resume first. Here’s when to include one and when to leave it off.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

The short answer for most applicants: leave the photo off. The two forces that decide this — hiring law and resume-parsing software — both point the same way in the US, UK, Canada, Australia, and increasingly across Europe. There are real exceptions, and they’re worth knowing, but the default is no photo.

The rule depends on where you’re applying

US, UK, Canada, Australia, Ireland — no photo

To avoid even the appearance of bias, most employers here prefer resumes without photos, and some larger companies discard any that include one so they can’t be accused of discriminatory screening. A photo gives the reader information about your age, race, and gender that has no business in a hiring decision — and legally shouldn’t be there.

Parts of continental Europe & elsewhere — traditionally yes, but shifting

In Germany, France, and some other markets a photo has historically been expected on a CV. That norm is fading as anti-discrimination practice spreads and international companies standardise. When in doubt in these markets, a clean, professional headshot is acceptable — but a resume without one is rarely penalised.

Genuine exceptions — photo expected

Acting, modelling, and some client-facing performance roles where appearance is a bona fide part of the job. Here a headshot belongs — often as a separate file or on a portfolio, not embedded in the resume text.

The ATS problem nobody mentions

Even where a photo is culturally fine, there’s a technical cost. Applicant tracking systems read your resume by extracting its text. A photo is an image with no text to extract — and worse, the layout tricks people use to place a photo (text boxes, columns, tables wrapping around the image) are exactly the structures that scramble a parser. Your name and contact details can end up next to the photo in a box the system skips entirely, so a machine-read version of your resume arrives with the top missing.

What to do with the space instead

A photo takes prime real estate at the top of the page — the spot a recruiter looks first. Give that space to something that actually advances your case: a sharp professional summary or a clear header with your name, title, and contact details. If you want your face on file, that’s what LinkedIn is for; link to your profile from the resume and let the photo live there.

The safe default. Applying in the US, UK, Canada, Australia, or through any online application portal? No photo. Applying in a market where it’s still expected, or for a role where appearance is genuinely part of the job? A clean, professional headshot is fine — but keep the layout parser-safe so the software can still read your text. When unsure, leaving it off is never the wrong call.

Frequently asked questions

Should I put a photo on my resume?

In the US, UK, Canada, Australia, and Ireland, no — most employers prefer resumes without photos to avoid any appearance of bias, and some discard resumes that include one. In parts of continental Europe a photo is still expected, though that norm is fading. The safe default, especially for online applications, is to leave it off.

Why do employers not want a photo on a resume?

A photo reveals your age, race, and gender — information that shouldn’t factor into hiring and that, in many countries, employers are legally wary of seeing. To protect against discrimination claims, some companies automatically reject resumes with photos, so including one can quietly remove you from consideration.

Do photos cause problems with applicant tracking systems?

Yes. An ATS reads your resume by extracting its text, and a photo has no text to extract. The layout structures used to place a photo — text boxes, columns, tables — also scramble parsers, which can push your name and contact details into a section the system skips. The machine-read copy then arrives with the top missing.

When is a photo on a resume okay?

When appearance is a genuine part of the job — acting, modelling, some client-facing performance roles — or when you’re applying in a market where a CV photo remains the norm. Even then, use a clean professional headshot and keep the surrounding layout parser-safe so your text still reads correctly.
