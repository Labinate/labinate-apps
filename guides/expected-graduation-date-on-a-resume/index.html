How to list an expected graduation date on a resume

A degree you haven't finished yet still belongs on your resume — you just date it with the future. List it in Education with "Expected [Month Year]," which shows the degree is in progress and on track without pretending it's already conferred.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

You're a semester or two from finishing and you're not sure how to handle the degree on your resume. Do you leave it off until it's official? Put a date on it? Which date? The answer is simpler than the worry: a degree you're actively pursuing and on track to complete goes in your Education section like any other — you just replace the completion year with an anticipated one, marked Expected. That single word carries the whole thing. It tells a reader the credential is real and coming, on a known schedule, without claiming you already hold it. Get that word and the date right, and an unfinished degree reads as momentum instead of a question mark.

The core idea: an expected graduation date sits on the axis of timing — a degree in progress that you will complete, shown with an anticipated completion date. Two rules follow: state the future date honestly (the word "Expected" is what keeps a not-yet-earned degree from reading as conferred), and never promise a precise month you might miss — when the date is uncertain, drop to the year or write "In Progress." Everything else is just standard Education formatting.

First: list the degree, then date it to the future

Write the entry exactly as you would a finished degree — the full degree name, your field, and the institution — and then, where a completed resume would show the year conferred, put the date you expect to finish, labeled so there's no ambiguity. The label is the load-bearing part. "B.S. in Computer Science, University of Texas — Expected May 2027" is instantly clear; the same line with a bare "2027" reads as if you already graduated in the future, which is confusing at best and misleading at worst. Employers read hundreds of student resumes and parse "Expected" without a second thought, so lean on it rather than inventing workarounds.

Don't leave the degree off until it's official, either. An in-progress degree is one of the strongest lines an early-career candidate has, and omitting it both undersells you and opens an unexplained gap where your schooling should sit. The degree is doing real work on the page — put it on, and let the word "Expected" tell the truth about where it stands.

How to write the line

A month and year is the cleanest form; drop to a year alone when the month is genuinely uncertain. Keep the label present and keep it plain.

Clear and honest

  • B.S. in Nursing, Univ. of Washington — Expected Dec 2026
  • B.A. in Marketing, Boston University, Expected 2027
  • Candidate for M.S. in Data Science, NYU — Anticipated May 2027
  • Expected graduation: June 2026

Ambiguous or overstated

  • "B.A. in Marketing, 2027" — reads as conferred in the future
  • "Graduated 2027" — states a completion that hasn't happened
  • "Expected May 2027" when you're part-time and unsure — a month you might miss
  • Leaving the degree off — undersells you and opens a gap

Two refinements. If the page is light on experience, Education is carrying your case, so it's fair to add a strong GPA, a few lines of relevant coursework, or an honor beneath the degree — while in school, that supporting detail earns its place. And if you're within a month or two of finishing, you don't need "Expected" at all once the credential is real; list the conferred date and move on.

Second, and the real call: honesty about the date

A date on a resume is a small commitment. Print "Expected May 2027" and a reader may plan a start date around it or ask about it in an interview, so the date has to be one you can stand behind. Match the specificity to your actual certainty.

On a firm schedule → give the month and year

Full-time, on track, finishing on a known term. "Expected May 2027" is precise, confident, and helpful — it lets an employer plan around your availability.

Year known, month uncertain → give the year

You'll finish that year but the exact term could shift. "Expected 2027" commits to what you're sure of and to nothing you're not.

Genuinely uncertain or part-time → "In Progress" or years enrolled

Stretching the degree out, or unsure you'll finish on the current pace. Write "In Progress," or show "2024–present," rather than promising a date that may slip — an honest status beats a confident guess.

Where it goes on the page

Placement follows who you are — specifically, how much of your case the in-progress degree is carrying.

Current student or new grad → Education sits high

The degree is your headline credential, so put Education near the top, above or just under a short objective. Pair it with relevant coursework, projects, and any internship so the page reads as ready, not thin.

Working professional finishing a degree → Education moves down

Once real experience is the stronger evidence, the in-progress degree still belongs on the page — dated "Expected" — but below your experience, where a completed degree would also sit.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode isn't putting an unfinished degree on the page — it's handling the date badly. Marked "Expected" and dated to your real schedule, it reads as a credential in motion; stated as if already earned, or hidden entirely, the same degree reads as a misrepresentation or a gap.

Reads as momentum

  • Degree in Education, dated Expected [Month Year]
  • Date specificity matches real certainty
  • Education placed high for a student, low for a pro
  • Supported by coursework, projects, GPA while the page is light

Reads as a problem

  • Bare year that looks already conferred
  • A precise month you later miss
  • Degree left off until it's official
  • Written as "Graduated" for a date in the future

An expected graduation date is a real credential in motion — the resume's job is to state it honestly and let it signal momentum. Put the degree in Education, mark it "Expected," match the date to what you can actually stand behind, and place it by how much of your case it's carrying. Do that and an unfinished degree stops reading as unfinished and starts reading as almost done.

Quick reference

  • In Education → list the degree normally; replace the conferred year with an anticipated one.
  • Use the label → Expected May 2027 (or "Anticipated"); a bare year reads as already earned.
  • Match certainty → month + year if firm; year alone if unsure; "In Progress" if genuinely uncertain.
  • Never overstate → don't write "Graduated" for a future date, and don't promise a month you might miss.
  • Place by weight → Education high for students, low once experience leads.
  • Know your axis → expected date (timing) ≠ incomplete degree (unfinished) ≠ listing dates (formatting) ≠ coursework (classes).

Frequently asked questions

Should you put an expected graduation date on a resume?

Yes. If you're actively pursuing a degree and on track to finish, list it in your Education section and date it to the future with the word "Expected" — for example, "B.A. in Marketing, Boston University — Expected May 2027." A degree in progress is a real credential in motion, and leaving it off makes you look less qualified than you are; hiding it also creates an unexplained gap where your schooling should be. The word "Expected" is what keeps it honest: it tells the reader the degree is underway and on schedule without implying it has already been conferred. The only time you wouldn't date it to the future is when you've actually stopped and aren't finishing — that's an incomplete degree, a different situation listed differently.

How do you write an expected graduation date on a resume?

Put the degree in Education exactly as you would a finished one — degree name, field, and institution — then replace the completion year with an anticipated date labeled "Expected" or "Anticipated": "Bachelor of Science in Nursing, University of Washington — Expected December 2026." A month and year is the cleanest form; a bare year ("Expected 2026") is fine when the month is uncertain. Some writers use "Expected graduation: May 2027" or "Candidate for B.S. in Biology, expected 2027" — all read clearly as long as the "Expected" signal is present. Don't write a future date as if it were the past ("Graduated 2027" or a bare "2027" that reads like a conferred year), and don't invent a precise month you're not confident you'll hit.

What if you don't know your exact graduation date?

Use the least specific form you can stand behind. If you know the year but not the month, write "Expected 2027." If even the year is genuinely uncertain — you're part-time, stretching the degree out, or unsure you'll finish on the current schedule — write "In Progress" or show the years you've been enrolled ("2024–present") instead of promising a date. The rule is that anything you print becomes a small commitment a reader may ask about, so never state a precise graduation month you might miss; an honest "In Progress" is stronger than a confident date that slips. If you're within a month or two of finishing, you can also simply list the conferred date once it's real and drop "Expected" entirely.

Should you list a degree you haven't finished yet?

Yes, as long as you're on track to complete it. A degree in progress is one of the strongest things a student or recent-starter can put on a resume, and dating it "Expected [date]" presents it as the real, on-schedule credential it is. This is entirely different from an incomplete degree — one you started and aren't finishing — which is a tenure question handled a different way (often listing the institution, dates attended, and credits or relevant coursework, without an "Expected" date). The dividing line is intent and trajectory: if you're still enrolled and moving toward the credential, date it to the future; if you've stopped, don't imply a completion that isn't coming.

Does an expected graduation date look bad to employers?

No — for students, recent starters, and career-changers finishing a degree, it's completely standard and reads as momentum. Employers hiring for internships and entry-level roles expect to see in-progress degrees, and a clear "Expected May 2027" actually helps them plan around your availability. What looks bad is not the future date but mishandling it: hiding the degree entirely (which creates a gap), writing it so it reads as already earned (which is a misrepresentation that surfaces on a transcript check), or promising a precise month you then miss. Stated plainly and honestly, an expected graduation date signals a candidate who's investing in themselves and will soon hold the credential — that's a positive, not a liability.
