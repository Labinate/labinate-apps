Should you put hobbies and interests on a resume?

Usually skip them — the space is worth more spent on skills and results. But there are real cases where a short interests line helps, and specific hobbies that add genuine signal. Here's how to tell the difference, and how to phrase them so they earn their place.

Updated July 2026 · ~5 min read · by Labinate

A hobbies section is the most optional thing on a resume. For an experienced candidate with a full page of achievements, it's usually the first thing to cut. But used deliberately — the right hobby, in the right situation, phrased well — it can round out a candidate and give an interviewer something human to open with. The trick is knowing when it's signal and when it's filler.

The default: leave them off

For most applicants, hobbies don't help. A hiring manager decides on your skills, experience, and results — not on the fact that you enjoy hiking. Every line spent on interests is a line not spent on an achievement, and on a one-page resume that trade rarely pays off. If your page is already full of strong, relevant content, skip the section entirely and don't feel you're missing anything.

When to include them

You're early in your career

Little work history means genuine empty space. A short interests line — especially one showing initiative or a relevant skill — beats a half-empty page. Pairs well with our no-experience resume guide.

A hobby directly relates to the job

Open-source contributions for a developer, a photography portfolio for a designer, a finance blog for an analyst. When the interest is relevant experience, it's not really a hobby — it's evidence.

The culture clearly values personality

Some startups and creative teams openly hire for cultural add. If the posting and company voice invite it, a well-chosen interest can help you connect.

Which hobbies actually help

The good ones do a job: they demonstrate a skill or a quality that matters for the role. Be specific — a concrete detail is what makes an interest credible instead of generic.

Interests: reading, music, sports, traveling
Interests: open-source contributor (2 published npm packages) · competitive 5-a-side football captain · long-form tech writing (personal blog, ~4k monthly readers)

The second version signals initiative, teamwork, communication, and follow-through — and gives an interviewer three real conversation starters. That's the bar: does it make you a stronger, more memorable candidate for this job?

Which to avoid

  • Generic filler. "Reading, music, traveling" tells a recruiter nothing and reads as padding.
  • Polarizing topics. Politics and religion introduce risk with no upside — leave them off.
  • Risky-sounding activities. Anything that could raise liability or insurance eyebrows is a needless gamble.
  • Passive pastimes. "Watching movies," "browsing the internet" — signals nothing you'd want to signal.
The test. For each interest, ask: "Does this show a skill or quality that helps me get this job, or give the interviewer a genuine reason to be more interested in me?" If yes, keep it and make it specific. If no, cut it and give the space back to your experience.

Where it goes

If you include interests, put them last — a single line at the bottom, after experience, skills, and education. It's the least important section, so it gets the least room, and only when the rest of the page is already doing its job.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put hobbies and interests on a resume?

Usually no — the space is better spent on skills and achievements. Include a short interests line when you're early-career and need to fill genuine space, when a hobby relates directly to the job, or when the culture clearly values personality.

What hobbies look good on a resume?

Ones that show a relevant skill or quality — open-source work for a developer, team sports for a collaborative role, a blog with a real audience for marketing. Be specific: "marathon running (3 completed)" beats "fitness."

What hobbies should you avoid?

Generic filler ("reading, music, traveling"), polarizing topics (politics, religion), risky-sounding activities, and passive pastimes. If it doesn't make you a stronger candidate for this job, cut it.

Where do interests go on a resume?

At the very bottom, as a short one-line section after experience, skills, and education — never near the top, and only if the rest of the page is already strong.
