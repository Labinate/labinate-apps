How to tailor your resume to a job description

The single resume you send to every job matches no posting well — and an applicant tracking system scores that mismatch against you. Tailoring fixes it in about ten minutes per application. Here's the repeatable method, without lying and without a rewrite from scratch.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

Recruiters and their software both ask the same question: does this person's resume answer this posting? A generic resume forces them to guess. A tailored one hands them the answer in the posting's own words. You don't do this by rewriting everything — you keep one strong master resume and adjust the few parts that carry the match. Six steps.

Step 1 · Build one strong master resume first

Tailoring is editing, not writing. Start from a complete master resume that lists every role, project and skill in your history — longer than you'd ever send. Each application then becomes a quick subtraction-and-reordering job rather than a blank page. If you don't have one yet, our ATS checklist and summary examples get you there fast.

Step 2 · Pull the real keywords from the posting

Read the job description twice and mark three things:

  • Named tools and skills — "Figma", "SQL", "Salesforce", "GAAP". These are the highest-value matches.
  • Repeated terms — anything the posting says more than once is a stated priority.
  • The exact title phrasing — if they say "client success" and you wrote "customer support", note the gap.

Ignore filler like "fast-paced environment" and "team player" — it barely registers in parsing and it isn't what gets you scored in.

Step 3 · Match their words to your true experience

This is the whole game and the honest line runs right through it: change the wording, never the facts. If you genuinely did the thing, describe it in the posting's vocabulary.

Handled setup for new customers
Owned client onboarding for 40+ accounts
Made reports in the analytics tool
Built weekly dashboards in Looker for the leadership team

If a required skill is one you truly don't have, don't paste it in — that's the one move that reliably burns you in the interview. Tailoring surfaces real experience; it doesn't invent it.

Step 4 · Rewrite the summary and skills line for this role

Your two highest-value inches are the professional summary and the skills line, and they're the fastest to tailor. Rewrite the summary to name the exact role and lead with the proof this posting cares about. Reorder your skills so the ones the job names sit first — a reader and a parser both weight what comes first.

Step 5 · Rework three or four bullets, not all of them

You don't need to touch every bullet. Pick the three or four most relevant to this job and rewrite them to mirror the posting's language and lead with the result. Cut or demote the bullets that don't support this role — a shorter, on-target resume beats a long, generic one. (Our action verbs list and common mistakes guide help here.)

Step 6 · Re-check length and format, then export

Tailoring often adds a line here and there — confirm you're still at the right length (the rule) and that the layout is one clean column a parser can read. Export a fresh PDF named for the role, and keep the master untouched for next time.

The 10-minute loop. Copy the posting → highlight named tools and repeated terms → rewrite the summary and reorder skills to match → rework 3–4 bullets into their vocabulary → trim anything off-target → export a role-named PDF. Repeat per application from the same master. That's tailoring — fast, honest, and far ahead of the generic resume in the same pile.

Frequently asked questions

Should I really tailor my resume for every job?

For jobs you actually want, yes. A tailored resume matches more of the applicant tracking system's keywords and reads as a direct answer to that posting — raising your parse score and holding a recruiter's attention. It's not a full rewrite each time; the summary, skills line and three or four bullets usually do the work in about ten minutes.

Is tailoring a resume the same as keyword stuffing?

No. Stuffing pastes terms you can't back up and gets caught the moment a human reads it. Tailoring surfaces experience you already have using the same words the posting uses — "client onboarding" instead of "customer setup". Only claim skills you genuinely have.

Which keywords from a job description matter most?

The hard requirements: named tools and technologies, certifications, and the exact titles of core responsibilities — especially any repeated or listed under "required". Match those first. Soft phrases like "team player" carry little weight and shouldn't crowd out real skills.

How long should tailoring a resume take?

About ten minutes once you have a strong master resume. Read the posting, pull the repeated terms, adjust your summary, reorder skills to lead with matches, and rework three or four bullets. A live-preview builder makes the swaps fast and keeps the layout ATS-safe.
