How to list commission on a resume

Commission is how you were paid, not what you achieved — and no employer hires you for a pay structure. Translate the commission into the performance it measured: quota attainment, ranking, revenue, and deal size. Lead with quota and rank, translate earnings into production, use percentages when figures are confidential, and keep every number honest.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

Here's the trap with a commission job: it feels like the commission is the achievement, so people write "commission-based sales role" or "100% commission" and think they've said something impressive. They haven't. A commission structure is a compensation model — it describes how the money reached you, not how good you are. A top performer and a rep who missed quota every quarter are both "commission-only." The label proves nothing on its own. What a hiring manager actually wants to know is a single question — can this person sell? — and the answer lives entirely in the numbers the commission measured. The whole task is to translate the pay into that performance: quota attainment, ranking, revenue closed, deal size, growth. Get those on the page and the commission takes care of itself.

The core idea: put the performance on the page, not the pay structure. Replace "commission-based" with the results the commission reflected — "127% of a $950K quota," "#3 of 42 reps," "grew territory 40% YoY." Lead with quota attainment and ranking because they're self-contextualizing, translate your earnings into what you produced for the company rather than publishing your income, and reach for percentages and awards when the raw figures are confidential. Every number is checked on the reference call — so round honestly and cite the real period.

Put the performance on the page, not the pay structure

The first move is to stop treating the compensation model as the headline. "Commission-based," "100% commission," "draw against commission" — these describe how you were paid, and a reader skims right past them because they carry no evidence of skill. Delete the pay label and put the result it produced in its place. If the role was fully commission-only and you want the reader to know you can sell without a safety net, that's fine as a one-clause aside — but it never substitutes for the numbers.

Sales is the purest case of the accomplishments-over-responsibilities rule, because in sales the outcome simply is a number. "Responsible for selling enterprise software to mid-market accounts" is a job description; "closed $1.4M across 28 new mid-market accounts, 118% of quota" is the same role expressed as what actually happened. The commission was the reward for that second sentence — so lead with the sentence, not the reward.

Lead with quota attainment and ranking — the two metrics recruiters trust

Not all sales numbers are equal. A raw dollar figure is weaker than it looks because it has no context: is $1.2M a lot? For a two-person startup selling six-figure deals, it's modest; for a rep with a $600K quota, it's outstanding. Two metrics fix this by carrying their own context.

Quota attainment — % to plan

"127% of a $950K annual quota" tells the reader you cleared a bar the company itself set, and roughly how high the bar was. It's the single most trusted line on a sales resume because the employer defined "good" and you beat it. Cite the quota and the percentage together — a percentage with no quota behind it invites the question "127% of what?"

Ranking — where you stood among peers

"#3 of 42 reps, 2023" or "top 5% of the sales org" answers the question a hiring manager is really asking: not just did you hit a target, but were you better than the people around you doing the same job. Ranking is powerful precisely because it's relative — it survives even when the whole team had a soft year.

Then the supporting cast — revenue, deal size, cycle, growth

Underneath quota and rank, add the texture an employer reads to picture how you sell: total revenue or units closed, average deal size, sales-cycle length, new accounts won, retention or upsell rate, and growth versus prior year. Each one should read as a result, not a task — quantified the way sales is measured.

Translate earnings into production — don't publish your income

The instinct to write "earned $180K in commissions" is understandable — it's a real, concrete number — but it's the wrong number, for two reasons.

Why the income figure fails

  • Oversharing — your pay is a private negotiation you don't want to anchor before the first interview
  • Ambiguous — a big check could be a huge base of deals or one lucky whale; the reader can't tell
  • Doesn't transfer — your payout measures your comp plan, not your value to a new employer

Translate it to production

  • The revenue you closed for the company
  • The accounts or units you won
  • The quota you hit and where you ranked
  • These measure your value, and they follow you to the next role

Your commission was a function of your production; the production is the underlying truth. Put that on the page and let the reader infer that you were paid well for it. The rare exception is a role where a specific, verifiable earnings figure is genuinely the norm and the headline — even then, pair it with quota or rank so it reads as performance, not a boast.

When the numbers are confidential, use relative framing

Plenty of reps can't publish exact revenue — NDAs, private-company figures, sensitive territories. Relative framing carries the signal without exposing the number.

Percentages and growth instead of dollars

"Grew territory 40% year over year," "increased average deal size 25%." A percentage proves the trajectory without naming the base — and it's often more impressive than the raw figure anyway.

Rank and percentile instead of totals

"Top 10% of a 200-person sales org," "#2 of 42 reps." A standing among peers says you outperformed without disclosing a single confidential dollar.

Awards do the same job, honestly

"President's Club 2022 and 2023," "Rookie of the Year," "Circle of Excellence." A named sales award is both impressive and non-disclosing — it certifies the outcome without the figure.

Show trajectory for a ramp year or an uneven stretch

A short tenure or a first year still-ramping? Lead with the slope, not a raw total: "110% of ramp quota in first 6 months," "climbed from #30 to #6 in the org over 18 months." Direction can be more convincing than a single-year number.

The bright line: every sales number is checkable

Sales is one of the most verified professions on earth. Quota attainment, ranking, President's Club, revenue closed — a hiring manager's reference call goes straight to your former sales manager, who remembers exactly where you finished, and a background check confirms tenure and title. Sales references are specific in a way most aren't.

Never inflate a sales number. Rounding "112% to quota" up to "top performer" when you were middle of the pack, claiming a President's Club you didn't make, implying one great year was your norm, or citing a quota softer than the real one — each of these collapses under one question on the reference call, and being caught overstating results is fatal in a field built on trust and measurement. Cite the real percentage, the real period, and the real ranking. If you had one standout year among average ones, say so plainly ("128% in 2023; 96% and 101% the prior two years") — an honest, uneven record beats a suspiciously perfect one, and it survives the call.

Strong vs. self-defeating

The failure mode isn't dishonesty — it's letting the compensation model stand in for the performance, so a strong track record reads as a shrug. Handled well, a commission role is a direct, quantified answer to "can this person sell."

Reads as a closer

  • Leads with quota attainment and ranking
  • Earnings translated into revenue and accounts
  • Confidential figures shown as % growth, percentile, awards
  • Every number honest and reference-proof

Reads as a shrug

  • "Commission-based sales role" — a pay label, no result
  • A bare "$1.2M in sales" with no quota to size it
  • Personal income published instead of production
  • An inflated quota % or a Club you didn't make

Commission is how you were paid — put on the page what you achieved to earn it. Lead with quota attainment and ranking, translate your earnings into the revenue and accounts you produced, use percentages and awards when the figures are confidential, and keep every number honest enough to survive the reference call. The commission structure is context for you; the performance it measured is the case for the reader.

Commission work isn't the same as freelance or self-employment

One distinction changes where the role goes and how you frame it: were you an employee whose pay was variable, or were you in business for yourself?

The situationWhat it really is — and how to list it
Commission sales employeeAn employee paid a variable rate. You sold the company's product to leads and accounts it largely provided. List it like any job — employer, title, dates — and let quota and revenue carry the bullets. The commission is just the pay model.
Independent / 1099 agentThe gray zone. A real-estate agent, insurance broker, or manufacturer's rep working their own book under a brokerage is often a contractor closer to self-employed. If you built and ran the book yourself, lean toward self-employment — but still lead with production numbers.
Freelance / project workDifferent animal entirely. You find and bill your own clients per project, not paid on commission against a company's sales. See freelance work — it's about client work and gaps, not quota attainment.
Tips / hourly-plus-tipsService, not sales. Tipped restaurant or service work isn't commission and isn't measured on quota; describe it by the operation you ran and the volume you handled, not a sales percentage.

The clean test: if the company supplied the product, the leads, and the structure, it's employment — lead with your sales metrics under the employer's name. If you built and ran the practice yourself, it's closer to self-employment — list it as your own, still leading with what you produced.

Quick reference

  • Put performance, not pay → replace "commission-based" with the result it measured; the pay model is at most a one-clause aside.
  • Lead with quota and rank → "127% of a $950K quota, #3 of 42 reps" — self-contextualizing, the metrics recruiters trust most.
  • Translate earnings to production → revenue closed, accounts won, quota hit — never your personal income.
  • Confidential? Go relative → % growth, percentile, ranking, and President's Club carry the signal without the figure.
  • Ramp or uneven year → show trajectory: "110% of ramp quota in 6 months," "#30 to #6 in 18 months."
  • Keep it reference-proof → sales numbers are checked; cite the real quota, period, and ranking, and never claim an award you didn't earn.
  • Know your status → commission employee (list as a job) ≠ independent agent ≠ freelance.

Keep sharpening the resume

With the sales numbers leading, tune the rest: make every bullet a result with achievement math, and read responsibilities vs. accomplishments to keep the whole resume outcome-first. Put your President's Club and sales awards in their own section, and if you ran your own book, see how to list self-employment. Frequently asked questions

Should you put commission on a resume?

Not the commission itself — the performance it measured. "Commission-based sales role" describes how you were paid, and no employer hires you for a compensation structure; a top rep and a struggling rep are both "commission-only," so the label proves nothing. What belongs on the page is the result the commission reflected: quota attainment ("127% of a $950K quota"), ranking ("#3 of 42 reps"), revenue closed, average deal size, and growth. You can mention the structure in one word if it adds context — a fully commission-only role signals you can sell without a safety net — but it should never stand in for the numbers. The rule is simple: put the outcome the pay produced, not the pay.

How do you show sales numbers on a resume?

Lead with the two metrics recruiters trust most because they are self-contextualizing: quota attainment as a percentage of plan, and ranking among your peers. "$1.2M in sales" sounds good but leaves the reader guessing whether it was strong for the territory; "127% of a $950K quota, ranked #3 of 42 reps" answers that in one line — you beat a defined bar and you can see where you stood. Underneath, add the supporting production numbers an employer reads: revenue or units closed, average deal size, sales-cycle length, new accounts won, growth versus the prior year, and any awards like President's Club. Frame each as an accomplishment ("grew territory revenue 40% YoY," "closed 30+ new accounts averaging $45K") rather than a duty. And translate your pay into what you produced for the company, not what you earned — production is the number that transfers to the next employer.

Should you list how much commission you earned on a resume?

No — don't publish your income. Writing "earned $180K in commissions" is both oversharing (your compensation is a private negotiation you don't want to anchor before you've even interviewed) and ambiguous (a big commission check could reflect a huge base of deals or one lucky whale, and the reader can't tell which). Translate the earnings into production instead: the revenue you closed, the accounts you won, the quota you hit. That is the number that measures your value to a new employer, where your personal payout does not. The one exception is when a specific, verifiable earnings figure is itself the headline achievement in a role where that's the norm — but even then, pairing it with quota attainment or ranking makes it far more credible than the dollar figure alone.

How do you list commission results when the numbers are confidential?

Use relative framing, which carries the signal without exposing a number you're not allowed to share. If exact revenue is under NDA, percentages and rankings do the work: "grew territory 40% year over year," "top 10% of a 200-person sales org," "ranked #2 of 42 reps," "President's Club 2022 and 2023." Awards and percentile standings are especially useful because they're both impressive and non-disclosing — they say you outperformed without naming the dollars. For a ramping first year or an uneven stretch, show trajectory rather than a raw total: "110% of ramp quota in first six months" or "grew from #30 to #6 in the org over 18 months." The goal is the same as always — prove the outcome — just expressed in a form your confidentiality obligations and the honest shape of your record allow.

Is a commission sales job the same as self-employment on a resume?

Usually not, and the distinction changes how you list it. Most commission salespeople are employees of a company — you sell the company's product to leads and accounts it largely provides, and your pay happens to be variable; you list it like any job, with the employer, your title, and dates, and let quota and revenue carry the bullets. Self-employment is different: you run your own business, find and bill your own clients, and carry your own overhead and risk. The gray zone is the independent agent — a real-estate agent, an insurance broker, a manufacturer's rep working their own book under a brokerage — who is often a 1099 contractor closer to self-employed. If the company supplied the product, the leads, and the structure, treat it as employment and lead with your sales metrics; if you built and ran the book yourself, it leans toward self-employment and you list it as your own practice, still leading with production numbers. Either way, the resume shows the same thing — what you sold — just under a different heading.
