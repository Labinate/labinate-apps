Relevant coursework on a resume

When you don't have years of work behind you yet, the right classes are evidence. A "Relevant Coursework" line lets a student, new graduate, or career changer show they've been trained in the exact skills a job needs — before they've had the job. The catch is in the word relevant: list the wrong courses, or too many, and it reads as filler. Here's when to include it, where it goes, and how to format it so it actually helps.

Updated July 2026 · ~6 min read · by Labinate

The one-line version: coursework is a stand-in for experience you don't have yet. If your work history can't fill the page, a tight list of job-relevant classes proves you've covered the ground. The moment real experience can tell that story better, the coursework comes off.

When to include coursework — and when to drop it

This is the whole decision, and it comes down to how much relevant experience you already have:

Include it

  • You're a current student or recent graduate
  • You're changing careers into a field you studied
  • Your work history is thin or unrelated to the target job
  • The role names specific technical skills your classes cover
  • You need to fill a short resume with genuine, relevant substance

Drop it

  • You have ~1–2+ years of relevant work experience
  • Internships or projects already show the same skills
  • The courses are intro-level things every grad in your field took
  • You're mid-career and the degree is old
  • It's padding — you're listing classes just to fill space

The expiry rule. Coursework is the first thing to cut as your career grows. Once you've held a relevant job or two, a recruiter cares far more about what you did than what you studied. If you're at that stage, spend the space on quantified results instead.

"Relevant" is the operative word

The section is called Relevant Coursework for a reason: it's not your transcript. Listing every class you took signals that you couldn't tell which ones matter — the opposite of the judgement employers want. Choose courses that map directly to the job:

  • Match the posting. Read the job description, note the skills and tools it asks for, and list the classes that taught them. This doubles as free keyword alignment for ATS scans.
  • Favour advanced over intro. "Machine Learning" earns its place; "Introduction to Computing" usually doesn't. Specialised, higher-level courses show depth.
  • Cut the assumed. Skip anything a reader assumes every graduate in your field took. If it's implied by the degree, it adds nothing.
  • Keep it to 5–8. Enough to show range, few enough that each one clearly counts.

Where it goes

Coursework lives inside your education section, as a sub-line under the specific degree it belongs to — never as its own floating section detached from your school. If you're a student or recent grad with little work history, education can sit near the top of the resume, above experience, so the coursework is visible. As experience accumulates, education slides down the page and the coursework line is the first to go. For the section around it, see how to list education on a resume.

Three clean formats

Use course names, not catalog codes — write "Data Structures & Algorithms," never "CS 301." Nobody outside your registrar knows what CS 301 is. Pick whichever of these three layouts fits your space:

1. Inline — one compact line

The most space-efficient option. Good when you want coursework to take a single line under the degree.

Under the degree
B.S. in Computer Science — State University, 2026
Relevant coursework: Data Structures & Algorithms, Database Systems, Operating Systems, Machine Learning, Web Development, Software Engineering

2. Two-column — scannable for 4–8 courses

Slightly more prominent and easy to skim. Useful when coursework is doing real work on a thin resume.

Relevant coursework
  • Financial Accounting
  • Corporate Finance
  • Business Statistics
  • Managerial Economics
  • Data Analysis in Excel
  • Marketing Analytics

3. Bulleted with a result — when a class did something

If a course involved a substantial project or outcome, give it a bullet with a one-line result. This is the strongest format because it turns a class into a mini-achievement — a bridge to a real projects section.

Relevant coursework & projects
  • Database Systems — designed and built a normalized PostgreSQL schema and query layer for a 3-person capstone booking app.
  • Machine Learning — trained a churn-prediction model reaching 88% accuracy on a public telecom dataset.

What about grades and honors?

Coursework and GPA are separate calls. A strong GPA can sit on the degree line; a weak one comes off entirely — the threshold and formatting are covered in GPA on a resume. Individual course grades don't belong next to the coursework list; listing "A" after each class reads as trying too hard. If a class produced something substantial, promote it to a bullet with an outcome (format 3 above) rather than tagging it with a grade.

The bigger picture for a light resume

Coursework is one tool for a resume that's short on experience — it's rarely the only one. Internships, part-time jobs, volunteering, clubs and personal projects all count as evidence too. If your work history is thin, read how to write a resume with no experience for the full playbook, and the internship resume guide if you're targeting an internship. Coursework fills a gap; these fill the rest of it.

Quick reference

  • When → student, recent grad, or career changer with thin relevant experience.
  • Where → inside education, under the specific degree.
  • What → 5–8 job-relevant courses, real names not codes.
  • How → inline line, two columns, or bullets-with-a-result.
  • Until → you have ~1–2 years of relevant experience — then cut it.

Frequently asked questions

Should you put relevant coursework on a resume?

Include relevant coursework when you're a student, a recent graduate, or a career changer who doesn't yet have enough directly-related work experience to fill the page. In those cases the right classes act as evidence that you've been trained in the skills the job needs. Drop the section once you have roughly one to two years of relevant work experience — at that point real jobs, internships and projects tell a stronger story, and coursework just takes up space a recruiter would rather see used on results.

How do you list relevant coursework on a resume?

Put it inside your education section, directly under the degree it belongs to, under a short heading like "Relevant Coursework." List courses by their real names, not catalog codes — "Data Structures & Algorithms," not "CS 301." Three clean formats work: a single comma-separated line for compactness, a two-column list for four to eight courses, or a short bulleted list where a course or two carries a one-line result or project. Only include classes that map to the target job, and keep the list to your five to eight strongest.

Where does relevant coursework go on a resume?

It belongs in the education section, as a sub-entry under the specific degree — not as its own top-level section floating away from your school. If you're a current student or recent graduate with little work history, education (and therefore coursework) can sit near the top of the resume, above work experience. Once you have solid experience, education moves down and the coursework line is usually the first thing to cut.

How many courses should you list on a resume?

Aim for about five to eight courses — enough to show depth in the areas that matter, few enough that every one is clearly relevant. Listing your entire transcript signals you couldn't tell which classes count. Choose the courses that most directly match the skills and keywords in the job posting, favour advanced or specialised classes over intro-level ones, and cut anything a reader would expect every graduate in your field to have taken.
