What to put on a resume

A resume is five sections, not a life story. Get those five right — in the right order, with the right things left off — and you have a resume that reads clean and passes an ATS. This is the complete section-by-section checklist: what goes in each, what to add only when it helps, and what to cut.

Updated July 2026 · ~7 min read · by Labinate

Most people overthink this. They add sections that don't belong ("References available upon request"), leave off the one that gets read first (a summary), and bury the strongest material three-quarters of the way down. A resume has a settled shape. Here it is — five required sections, a short list of optional ones, and a clear list of what to leave off entirely. The five sections every resume needs

Every standard resume is built from these five. If yours has all five, done well, you have a complete resume — the rest is optional.

1. Contact information

Goes at the very top. Keep it to what a recruiter actually needs to reach you:

  • Full name — the largest text on the page.
  • Phone number and a professional email (firstname.lastname@, not a nickname address).
  • City and state/country — not your full street address. Location is enough.
  • One relevant link — LinkedIn, a portfolio, or GitHub, if it adds something.

Leave off your full mailing address, date of birth, and marital status — see whether to add a photo (usually no).

2. Professional summary

Two or three lines directly under your contact details that say who you are and what you offer. It's the first thing read and the most valuable real estate on the page. Write it last, once the rest of the resume exists, so it summarizes real material. Full walk-through with 15 examples: resume summary examples. If you're unsure whether to use a summary or an objective, here's when each one fits.

3. Work experience

Usually the biggest section and the one that decides the interview. Each entry: job title, company, dates, and 3–6 bullet points that show results, not duties. List jobs most-recent-first. The complete format — including short stints, gaps, and stacked roles — is in how to list work experience, and the bullet-writing craft is in resume bullet points and resume action verbs.

4. Education

Degree, institution, and graduation year — plus GPA only if it's strong and you're early-career. Experienced professionals keep this short and low; students and recent grads may place it above experience. The format, ordering, and the awkward cases (degree in progress, no degree) are covered in how to list education.

5. Skills

A tight, scannable block of the abilities that matter for the job — technical tools, software, and genuinely job-relevant skills, not filler like "hard worker." Match the language of the posting so the right keywords get picked up. What to list and what to cut: skills to put on a resume.

Optional sections — add only when they earn the space

These aren't required. Add one when it strengthens your case for this specific job, and cut it otherwise.

Certifications & licenses

Include when they're relevant or required (PMP, CPA, AWS, nursing license, etc.). List the name, issuer, and year.

Projects

Powerful when you're light on formal experience — a strong project section can do the work of a job. Especially useful for students and career-changers.

Volunteering

Counts as experience when it's substantial or fills a timeline gap. Format it like a job entry.

Languages

Worth adding if the role is international or bilingual, or if fluency is a genuine asset. State the level (native, fluent, professional working).

Hobbies & interests

Almost always skippable, occasionally useful for culture fit or when they're genuinely relevant. Full call: hobbies and interests on a resume.

What NOT to put on a resume

Cutting the wrong things is half the job. Leave all of these off:

  • Your full home address — city and state is enough; the full address is a privacy risk and adds nothing.
  • Date of birth, marital status, nationality — irrelevant, and they invite bias.
  • A photo — in the US, UK, Canada and Australia, leave it off; it can trip up ATS and introduce bias. (Exceptions by country →)
  • "References available upon request" — assumed, and a waste of a line. Here's what to do with references instead.
  • An objective that's about you — "seeking a role where I can grow" tells the employer nothing. Use a summary.
  • Every job you've ever had — trim anything older than about 10–15 years (how far back to go).
  • Anything untrue — inflated titles or faked dates surface in a background check and cost you the offer.

The order to put it all in

Recruiters read top to bottom and decide fast — so the order is strategic, not decorative. The standard, ATS-safe sequence:

Standard section order
  • 1. Contact information — always at the very top
  • 2. Summary — your pitch, in 2–3 lines
  • 3. Work experience — for experienced candidates, lead here
  • 4. Education — students may move this above experience
  • 5. Skills — plus any optional sections that help

The one rule underneath all of it: lead with your strongest card. Seasoned professional? Experience goes high. Student or career-changer with a light work history? Education or a skills-and-projects block leads instead. Which arrangement is really a question of resume format — chronological, functional, or combination — and the format guide covers when to use each.

The whole thing in one breath. Five sections: contact, summary, experience, education, skills — in that order, strongest card highest. Add certifications, projects, or languages only when they help this specific job. Cut your full address, date of birth, photo, and "references available upon request." Keep it single-column and honest, and you have a resume that reads clean and passes the scan.

Frequently asked questions

What are the five sections every resume needs?

Contact information, a summary, work experience, education, and skills. Those five cover every standard resume. Everything else — certifications, projects, volunteering, languages — is optional and added only when it strengthens your case for the specific job.

What order should resume sections go in?

Contact details at the top, then a summary. After that, lead with your strongest card: experienced candidates put work experience next, then education and skills; students and career-changers may lead with education or a skills block. Recruiters read top to bottom, so put the most convincing section highest.

What should you NOT put on a resume?

Leave off your full home address, date of birth, marital status, a photo (in most countries), an objective statement that only says what you want, "references available upon request," and anything untrue. These waste space or create bias and ATS problems without helping you.

Do I need an objective or a summary?

A summary, in almost all cases. A two-to-three line summary of what you offer works far better than an objective stating what you want. Use an objective only when you're changing careers or have no experience and need to explain your direction.
